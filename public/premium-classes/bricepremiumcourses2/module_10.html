Module Overview

Welcome to Module 10: Communicating with Financial Stakeholders. In finance and accounting, technical expertise alone is not sufficient. The ability to communicate complex financial information clearly, accurately, and persuasively to a range of stakeholders — from board members and investors to analysts, auditors, and regulators — is one of the most valuable and most demanding professional skills in the field.

This module focuses on the English language skills required for high-stakes financial communication. You will learn the vocabulary of stakeholder reporting, investor relations, and board-level financial presentations. You will study the grammar structures used in formal financial documents and oral presentations, including how to express uncertainty, hedge financial forecasts, and present both positive and negative results with appropriate professional register. You will also practise the specific communication challenges that arise in financial contexts: presenting earnings results, handling analyst questions, writing investor letters, and communicating during periods of financial difficulty.

By the end of this module, you will be able to present financial results to a mixed audience, write a professional investor letter and earnings commentary, handle challenging stakeholder questions with composure, and apply the appropriate formal register and hedging language required in regulated financial communication.

Key Vocabulary — 21 Terms
📞
Earnings call
noun phrase
A scheduled conference call in which senior management presents the company's financial results for a given period to analysts and investors, followed by a live question-and-answer session.
💹
Investor relations (IR)
noun phrase
The strategic function responsible for managing communication between a company and its financial stakeholders, including institutional investors, retail shareholders, analysts, and the financial media.
📋
Forward guidance
noun phrase
Statements made by company management about expected future financial performance, including projected revenue, earnings, or margins, used by investors and analysts to form expectations.
📊
EBITDA
noun phrase
Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortisation; a widely used measure of a company's core operating profitability, adjusted to remove the effects of financing and accounting decisions.
📈
Earnings per share (EPS)
noun phrase
The portion of a company's profit allocated to each outstanding share of common stock, calculated by dividing net profit by the number of shares; a key metric for equity investors.
⚠️
Profit warning
noun phrase
A public announcement by a company that its earnings will be significantly below market expectations, required under stock exchange disclosure rules when material adverse information is known.
🏦
Road show
noun phrase
A series of presentations given by senior management to potential or existing investors, typically conducted in multiple financial centres around a capital-raising event or major corporate announcement.
📝
Annual report
noun phrase
A comprehensive document produced by a company at the end of each financial year, containing audited financial statements, a directors' report, a strategic review, and governance information.
🔍
Material non-public information (MNPI)
noun phrase
Information that has not been disclosed to the market and would, if disclosed, be likely to have a significant effect on the price of a company's securities; sharing MNPI is illegal in most jurisdictions.
📉
Going concern
noun phrase
The assumption that a company will continue to operate for the foreseeable future, without the need or intention to liquidate its operations; auditors flag a going concern qualification when this assumption is in doubt.
👔
Sell-side analyst
noun phrase
A financial analyst employed by a brokerage or investment bank who produces research reports and financial models on publicly listed companies, making buy, sell, or hold recommendations to institutional investor clients.
💼
Buy-side analyst
noun phrase
A financial analyst employed by an investment fund, such as a pension fund, hedge fund, or asset manager, who evaluates securities to inform the fund's own investment decisions.
🌱
Organic growth
noun phrase
Revenue or profit growth generated by the company's existing operations, excluding the impact of acquisitions, disposals, or foreign exchange movements; used to assess underlying business performance.
🔗
Inorganic growth
noun phrase
Growth achieved through mergers, acquisitions, or joint ventures rather than through the expansion of the company's existing business activities.
✅
ESG reporting
noun phrase
Disclosure of a company's performance on Environmental, Social, and Governance criteria; increasingly required by investors, regulators, and other stakeholders as part of the broader financial reporting package.
📣
Shareholder letter
noun phrase
A formal letter from the chairman or CEO to shareholders, typically included in the annual report, communicating the company's strategic direction, performance review, and outlook for the coming year.
⚡
Capital markets day
noun phrase
An event hosted by a company at which senior management presents its long-term strategy and financial targets to institutional investors and analysts, typically held once every two or three years.
🔔
Selective disclosure
noun phrase
The practice of sharing material financial information with specific investors or analysts before disclosing it to the broader market, which is prohibited under fair disclosure regulations in most jurisdictions.
🏛️
Audit committee
noun phrase
A committee of the board of directors responsible for overseeing the financial reporting process, including the relationship with external auditors, the integrity of financial statements, and internal controls.
📐
Net debt
noun phrase
The total borrowings of a company minus its cash and cash equivalents; a key measure of a company's financial leverage and its capacity to service or repay its debt obligations.
🎯
Guidance
noun phrase
Short form of forward guidance; the management's published expectations for future financial performance, which the market uses as a benchmark against which actual results are compared.

Text 1: The Language of Financial Reporting — Principles and Pitfalls

Paragraph 1 — Who Are Financial Stakeholders?

Financial stakeholders are the individuals and institutions with a direct financial interest in a company's performance and financial health. For a publicly listed company, this group is extensive and heterogeneous. It includes institutional investors — pension funds, insurance companies, asset managers, and hedge funds — who hold significant positions in the company's shares and expect regular, detailed financial communication. It includes retail investors, who own shares individually and typically receive less detailed engagement. It includes sell-side analysts, who publish research reports and financial models that influence the investment decisions of their institutional clients. It includes debt holders — banks and bondholders — who are primarily concerned with the company's ability to service and repay its borrowings. And it includes regulators, who require accurate and timely disclosure under the rules of the relevant stock exchange and securities law.

Each of these stakeholder groups has different information needs, different levels of financial sophistication, and different expectations about the format, frequency, and depth of financial communication they receive. A sophisticated institutional investor expects granular financial analysis and access to senior management through investor meetings and earnings calls. A retail investor expects a clear annual report and understandable dividend information. A regulator expects timely, accurate, and complete disclosure under the applicable rules. The challenge for the finance and investor relations team is to communicate appropriately with all of these audiences simultaneously, using the right language, the right format, and the right level of detail for each.

Paragraph 2 — The Earnings Announcement

The earnings announcement is the most important regular communication event in the financial calendar for any publicly listed company. It occurs quarterly for US-listed companies and typically semi-annually or annually for UK and European-listed companies. The earnings announcement typically comprises three elements. First, a regulatory announcement (in the UK, a Regulatory News Service or RNS release) containing the statutory financial results in a form required by the stock exchange. Second, a results presentation — a set of slides used by the CFO and CEO to walk analysts and investors through the key financial and operational highlights. Third, an earnings call — a live audio or video conference at which management presents the results and then takes questions from analysts and institutional investors.

The language used across these three components varies significantly. The regulatory announcement must be precise, factual, and legally compliant — every statement must be supportable, and material information must be disclosed completely and without ambiguity. The results presentation can be more narrative in style, using charts, comparisons, and selective emphasis to highlight the most important messages. The earnings call requires the most sophisticated communication skills: the ability to explain complex financial information clearly to a technically literate audience, to handle challenging or hostile questions without becoming defensive, and to convey confidence in the company's strategy and performance without overstating the outlook in a way that creates unrealistic expectations.

Paragraph 3 — Forward Guidance and Its Risks

One of the most delicate areas of financial stakeholder communication is forward guidance: the practice of providing the market with management's expectations for future financial performance. Guidance creates a benchmark against which actual results will be measured — and against which management credibility will be assessed. Consistently delivering results in line with or above guidance builds trust and credibility over time. Consistently missing guidance destroys trust, depresses the company's valuation, and can lead to activist shareholder pressure or board changes.

The language of forward guidance must therefore strike a careful balance. It must be informative enough to help the market form realistic expectations, but sufficiently hedged to reflect genuine uncertainty about future outcomes. Professional financial communicators use a consistent set of hedging expressions: "we anticipate," "we expect," "we are targeting," "subject to market conditions," "assuming the current trading environment continues." These expressions signal management's best current expectations while protecting against accusations of misleading the market if results fall short. Understanding and using these expressions correctly is an essential skill for any finance professional working with English-speaking stakeholders.

Text 2: Presenting Difficult Financial News — Honesty, Structure, and Composure

Paragraph 1 — Why Difficult Communication Is the Hardest Test

Presenting positive financial results to stakeholders is a relatively straightforward communication exercise. Presenting negative results — a profit warning, a significant earnings miss, a write-down, a covenant breach, or a going concern qualification — is one of the most demanding communication challenges in professional life. The stakes are high: financial markets are unforgiving, reputational damage can be permanent, and regulatory consequences for poor disclosure practice can be severe. At the same time, the temptation to soften, delay, or obscure bad news is powerful — and consistently yields worse long-term outcomes than prompt, honest, clear disclosure.

The research on financial communication during periods of distress consistently shows the same pattern: companies that communicate problems early, clearly, and with a credible plan for addressing them fare significantly better in terms of market reaction, stakeholder trust, and eventual recovery than those that delay, understate, or obscure the difficulty. Investors can tolerate problems that are explained; what they cannot tolerate is management that appears to have been caught off guard or that has been less than honest with the market. The phrase that experienced investor relations professionals use is: "bad news does not get better with time." The obligation to disclose material adverse information promptly is both a legal requirement and a strategic imperative.

Paragraph 2 — Structure and Language for Negative Financial Communication

When communicating negative financial results or a profit warning, structure and language both matter enormously. The most effective approach follows a consistent pattern. First: state the news directly and immediately, without burying it in positive context. Audiences that sense they are being led to bad news through a long positive preamble lose trust immediately. Second: provide the factual context that explains what happened and why, using precise financial language without jargon that non-specialist board members or investors may not understand. Third: present the management response — the specific actions being taken to address the situation. Fourth: provide a realistic but measured outlook — neither catastrophising nor minimising the likely trajectory.

The language of negative financial communication must be honest without being alarming, specific without being speculative, and confident without being complacent. Common errors include the use of euphemistic language that obscures the severity of the situation ("we are experiencing some near-term headwinds" when the reality is a 30% profit shortfall), passive constructions that deflect accountability ("revenues were impacted" rather than "we failed to convert the pipeline we guided for"), and premature positive signalling that creates expectations the company cannot yet substantiate. Each of these errors, though they may seem to soften the immediate impact of bad news, invariably creates larger credibility problems when the full picture becomes clear.

Paragraph 3 — The Analyst Q&A: Where Credibility Is Built or Lost

For listed companies, the analyst question-and-answer session that follows an earnings presentation is the moment of greatest scrutiny and greatest risk in the financial communication calendar. Experienced sell-side analysts are highly skilled at identifying inconsistencies in management commentary, pressing on assumptions that appear optimistic, and using their questions to surface information that the company might prefer to keep general. They prepare extensively for earnings calls — modelling the results, comparing them against guidance, and identifying the specific areas where they need more clarity.

The CFO's performance in the Q&A is therefore critical to the company's relationship with the analyst community and, through them, to its share price. The most effective CFOs approach the Q&A with three principles. First: prepare exhaustively for the questions you expect, but remain calm and composed when questions arrive that you did not anticipate. Second: be honest about uncertainty. Attempting to project confidence where none genuinely exists is almost always detected by experienced analysts and reduces rather than builds trust. Third: control the scope of your answers. An imprecise or over-expansive answer to an analyst question can create a new problem. Answer the question asked, completely and honestly, and then stop.

Reference Box 1
Hedging Language in Financial Guidance and Forecasts
Why hedging is essential in financial communication
Financial forecasts involve genuine uncertainty. Using appropriately hedged language signals analytical honesty, protects the company legally if actual results differ from expectations, and aligns with regulatory requirements for fair and accurate disclosure. Overclaiming certainty in financial forecasts is both professionally inappropriate and potentially illegal.
Hedging DeviceStrengthFinancial Communication Example
We anticipate / expect / projectModerate confidenceWe anticipate full-year revenue to be in the range of GBP420m to GBP440m.
We are targeting / aiming forAspiration, not promiseWe are targeting an EBITDA margin improvement of 100 basis points by year end.
Subject to / assumingConditional qualifierSubject to market conditions, we expect to resume the dividend in H2.
We believe / are confident thatManagement opinionWe believe the cost reduction programme will deliver annualised savings of GBP30m.
Could / may / mightWeak possibilityInflationary pressures could impact margins in the second half.
At this stage / at the current timeTemporal qualifierAt this stage, we are not in a position to provide specific guidance for FY2026.
In line with / broadly consistent withApproximate comparisonPerformance was broadly consistent with the guidance we issued in November.
Standard forward guidance phrasing in earnings calls
We are guiding for full-year revenue of GBP850m to GBP880m, assuming no material deterioration in the macroeconomic environment.
At this point in the year, we expect adjusted EPS to be in the range of 42p to 46p, subject to the usual caveats around currency movements and exceptional items.
It would be premature at this stage to provide specific guidance for the next financial year, but we remain confident in the medium-term growth trajectory of the business.
Reference Box 2
Reported Speech & Indirect Questions in Financial Briefings
When reported speech is used in financial communication
Reported speech is used frequently in financial contexts: when summarising what management said on an earnings call, when reporting analyst questions in a briefing document, when documenting board discussions in minutes, and when relaying investor feedback to the executive team. Understanding the grammar shifts in reported speech is essential for accurate financial communication.
Direct SpeechReported SpeechContext
"Revenue grew 8% in Q1."The CFO stated that revenue had grown 8% in Q1.Earnings call summary
"We expect margins to recover."Management indicated that they expected margins to recover.Analyst note
"What is driving the cost increase?"The analyst asked what was driving the cost increase.Q&A transcript
"Will you restore the dividend?"An investor asked whether the company planned to restore the dividend.IR meeting note
"We cannot comment on that."The CEO declined to comment on that matter at that time.Board minutes
Key grammar rules in financial reported speech
Present tense shifts to past. "Will" becomes "would." "Can" becomes "could." Direct questions become indirect, using statement word order: "What is driving costs?" becomes "She asked what was driving costs." Yes/no questions use "whether" or "if": "Are you on track?" becomes "He asked whether the company was on track."
The analyst asked whether the cost reduction programme was on track to deliver the targeted savings. The CFO confirmed that it was, adding that she expected to see the full benefit reflected in the H2 results. She declined to speculate on whether additional measures might be required.
Reference Box 3
Formal Passive Structures in Financial Documents
Why the passive is used in financial reporting
Financial documents — annual reports, regulatory announcements, audit reports, and board papers — use the passive voice extensively to create an objective, authoritative tone; to focus on outcomes rather than actors; to distribute responsibility across the organisation rather than naming individuals; and to comply with the formal register expected in regulated financial communication.
Active (informal)Passive (formal/financial)
We adjusted revenue for one-off items.Revenue has been adjusted to exclude one-off items.
The auditors reviewed the accounts.The financial statements have been reviewed and approved by the external auditors.
We will restate the prior year figures.The prior year comparative figures will be restated to reflect the change in accounting policy.
Management decided to write down the asset.An impairment charge of GBP45m has been recognised in respect of the North American business.
We expect the acquisition to close in Q3.The acquisition is expected to complete in Q3, subject to regulatory approval.
We cannot predict this with certainty.It is not possible at this stage to predict the outcome with certainty.
Common impersonal passive constructions in financial English
It is expected that... / It is anticipated that... / It should be noted that... / It has been determined that...
It is not possible to provide guidance at this stage. / It would be inappropriate to speculate on the outcome of ongoing discussions.
The board has been advised that... / Shareholders are reminded that... / It is recommended that...

Case Study 1: Clarendon Capital — How Transparent Communication Through a Crisis Built Long-Term Investor Trust

Investor Relations · Crisis Communication · Forward Guidance · Earnings Call Management

Background

Clarendon Capital is a mid-cap UK asset management firm that manages approximately £28bn in assets. In the second quarter of 2022, the firm experienced a significant period of performance difficulty: three of its largest flagship funds underperformed their benchmarks by 8–12 percentage points, driven by concentrated positions in a technology sector that corrected sharply. Client outflows accelerated, and assets under management fell 18% over the quarter. The share price dropped 34% between March and June. Institutional shareholders, several of whom had held large positions for over five years, were concerned and demanding answers.

The CFO and Head of Investor Relations faced a choice that many leadership teams face in this situation: manage the communication carefully — release information gradually, avoid specifics, and frame the situation as positively as possible — or communicate fully, honestly, and immediately, with a clear explanation of what had gone wrong and a credible plan to address it. After significant internal debate, the board chose the second path.

The Communication Approach

Within 72 hours of the quarter's end, Clarendon published a detailed interim update acknowledging the underperformance directly. The announcement stated: "Performance in Q2 2022 was materially below the standards we expect of ourselves and below the outcomes our clients have a right to expect. We take full responsibility for the positioning decisions that contributed to this outcome." The update then provided a detailed attribution analysis explaining which positions had underperformed and why, what the portfolio management team's reasoning had been, and why that reasoning had proved incorrect in the circumstances.

The CFO hosted an emergency investor call within five days of the announcement. Rather than opening with a positive framing of the longer-term performance record, he opened by acknowledging the severity of the quarter, expressing regret directly, and then moving to the specific changes being made to risk management procedures and portfolio construction guidelines. He was asked approximately 45 questions by analysts and investors during a 90-minute call. His approach to every question was consistent: direct acknowledgement of the concern, a precise factual response, and a refusal to speculate beyond what the data supported.

The result was counterintuitive to some observers. Despite the scale of the underperformance, net outflows in Q3 were 60% lower than modelled by the sell-side. The share price stabilised within two weeks of the call. Several investors subsequently cited the quality of the communication — its directness, its specific attribution analysis, and the absence of defensive language — as a significant reason for maintaining their positions. By Q4 2022, performance had partially recovered, and the full-year outflows were substantially below the levels initially feared. The CFO's post-mortem observation: "Investors can manage disappointment. What they cannot manage is uncertainty about whether management understands the problem."

Case Study 2: Northgate Retail Group — When Poor Financial Communication Compounded a Business Problem

Profit Warning Communication · Analyst Relations · Selective Disclosure · Regulatory Consequences

Background

Northgate Retail Group is a UK-listed mid-market fashion retailer that had been experiencing structural trading difficulties since 2019. In late 2021, trading deteriorated significantly as the company faced a combination of supply chain disruption, rising wage costs, and a shift in consumer spending away from the mid-market category. By October 2021, the finance team was aware that full-year profit was likely to be significantly below the guidance of £42m that had been issued in June. The actual expected outcome, based on internal projections, was now £24m to £28m — a shortfall of approximately 38%.

The CFO was reluctant to issue a profit warning before the interim results, scheduled for November, arguing that the situation might improve in the final weeks of the period. The legal and company secretary teams recommended immediate disclosure; the executive team overruled them. In the intervening period, the CFO met informally with two major institutional shareholders at an industry conference and, in the course of those conversations, shared information about the likely shortfall that was not yet in the public domain. This constituted selective disclosure, a violation of the UK Market Abuse Regulation.

The Consequences

When the profit warning was finally published alongside the interim results, it came as a severe shock to the market. The company's shares fell 43% on the day of the announcement — significantly more than would typically be expected for a profit shortfall of this magnitude, partly because the delay had created the impression that management was not in control of its financial reporting and partly because rumours of the selective disclosure had reached the broader analyst community. The FCA launched an investigation into the informal conversations at the industry conference, which was eventually settled with a significant regulatory fine and a formal censure of the CFO.

The case became a widely referenced example in investor relations training for what not to do. Several lessons were drawn by practitioners. First: a profit warning issued immediately, when the shortfall first becomes known, will always produce a smaller market reaction than one delayed and then announced alongside financial results. Second: selective disclosure — sharing material information with specific investors before the broader market — is not a favour to those investors but a serious regulatory violation with severe consequences for both the company and the individual. Third: the decision about when and how to disclose material adverse information is not a communication decision — it is a legal decision, and the company secretary and legal counsel must be the primary advisors rather than the executive team's instincts about market timing.
