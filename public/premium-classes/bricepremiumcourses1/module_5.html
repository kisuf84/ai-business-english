Module Overview
B1–B2 · Writing Business Reports

Welcome to Module 5: Writing Business Reports. This module develops the professional English writing skills required to produce clear, credible, and well-structured business reports for a range of contexts — from internal analytical memos to formal reports submitted to boards, clients, and senior stakeholders. You will build specialised vocabulary for report writing, study the grammar structures that characterise formal written English, and practise the planning and drafting skills that distinguish a compelling report from a disorganised one.

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Use precise vocabulary to describe report structure, purpose, and findings.
  • Write a well-organised business report with a clear introduction, body, and conclusion.
  • Use impersonal, formal language appropriate for professional report writing.
  • Apply grammar structures — including the passive voice, nominalisation, and formal connectors — in report contexts.
  • Critically evaluate report language for clarity, objectivity, and professional register.

Text 1 — The Architecture of a Business Report

A business report is not simply a collection of information about a topic — it is a purposeful, structured argument designed to help a specific reader make an informed decision. The most common failure in business report writing is the absence of a clear central question that the report is trying to answer. Before a single word is written, the effective report writer asks: what decision needs to be made, who is making it, and what evidence will they need to make it well? This foundational clarity shapes every subsequent choice — the scope of the research, the selection of evidence, the depth of analysis, and the directness of the recommendations. Without it, even well-researched and well-written reports risk becoming informative but inconclusive — what experienced readers sometimes call "a report that tells me everything except what to do."

The standard structure of a formal business report consists of several interconnected sections, each with a distinct function. The title page and table of contents allow the reader to navigate the document. The executive summary condenses the report's entire argument — purpose, key findings, conclusions, and recommendations — into a single, self-contained section typically no longer than one page; it is often the only part a busy senior reader will read in full, and it must therefore be written last but placed first. The terms of reference formally define the report's scope, audience, and objectives, establishing boundaries that the report's body must not exceed. The methodology section explains how evidence was gathered and analysed, which allows the reader to evaluate the reliability and validity of the findings. The findings section presents the evidence objectively and without interpretation, leaving all analysis to the conclusions section, which draws together what the findings mean. The report closes with recommendations — specific, actionable, and prioritised proposals — followed by an appendix containing any supplementary data referenced in the body.

Formatting and presentation are not cosmetic concerns in business report writing — they are communicative tools. A well-formatted report uses numbered headings and sub-headings to create a clear visual hierarchy, enabling readers to locate specific sections without reading linearly. Tables and graphs should be used to present complex quantitative data more clearly than prose allows, but each must be labelled precisely, referenced in the text, and placed as close as possible to the relevant discussion. Signposting language — phrases such as "As the data in Figure 2 illustrates…", "Turning to the issue of…", or "In light of these findings…" — guides the reader through the report's argument and creates coherence across sections. The overarching principle of formatting is that every structural and stylistic decision should reduce the cognitive effort required of the reader, not display the sophistication of the writer.

Text 2 — Language and Style in Professional Report Writing

The language of a business report is not merely a vehicle for information — it is itself an expression of professional credibility. Readers of business reports — whether they are board members, clients, investors, or regulators — are experienced in evaluating the quality of written analysis, and they will draw inferences about the writer's rigour, objectivity, and judgment from the language choices they observe on every page. The hallmarks of credible business report language are well-established: formal register, impersonal constructions, measured hedging where uncertainty exists, precise vocabulary, and a logical flow from evidence to conclusion. Conversely, a report that uses colloquial language, makes unsupported assertions, shifts between first and third person inconsistently, or deploys vague and inflated language signals not just poor writing, but poor thinking.

Two grammatical features are particularly associated with high-quality formal report writing: the passive voice and nominalisation. The passive voice — "It was found that…", "The data indicates that the following conclusions can be drawn…" — removes the writer as an agent and focuses attention on the process or the finding itself, reinforcing the report's objectivity. Nominalisation transforms verbs and adjectives into nouns, compressing meaning and creating a more formal density of expression: "the company's failure to implement" rather than "the company did not implement"; "a significant reduction in costs" rather than "costs fell significantly". Neither device should be overused to the point of obscuring meaning, but their disciplined deployment marks the register of formal written English at its most professional. Report writers should also develop fluency in formal connectors — "Furthermore…", "In contrast…", "This suggests that…", "It follows that…" — which create explicit logical relationships between sentences and paragraphs and prevent the reader from having to infer connections that the writer has not made clear.

The question of objectivity in business report writing is more nuanced than it might first appear. A truly objective report is not one that refuses to take a position — it is one whose position is transparently supported by evidence and whose limitations are openly acknowledged. A confident, well-evidenced recommendation is not a departure from objectivity; it is its highest expression. Where the evidence is ambiguous or contested, the report writer must use appropriately hedged language — "The data would suggest…", "It is conceivable that…", "Further investigation would be required to confirm…" — rather than projecting false certainty. Where the evidence is robust and the conclusion compelling, the writer must resist the instinct to soften it into meaninglessness. The skill of business report writing, ultimately, is the ability to calibrate the strength of one's language precisely to the strength of one's evidence — no stronger, and no weaker.

Grammar Review
Summary of all 3 grammar points covered in Module 5

1. Passive Voice in Business Report Writing

The passive voice is the cornerstone of formal report language. It removes the writer as a named agent, focuses on the action or finding, and creates an objective, impersonal tone appropriate for professional analysis.

  • Past passive: was/were + past participle — "The data was gathered over six months."
  • Present passive: is/are + past participle — "The following recommendations are made on the basis of…"
  • Future passive: will be + past participle — "The report will be presented to the board on 14 March."
  • Impersonal "It" passive: "It was concluded that…" / "It is estimated that…" / "It has been found that…" — signals objective analysis
  • Modal passive: should/can/must + be + past participle — "These findings should be treated as indicative."
✔ It was concluded that the current pricing structure is no longer competitive. (impersonal past passive)
✔ Three strategies were evaluated against the following criteria. (past passive)
✔ The findings will be presented to the board next week. (future passive)
✔ These recommendations are made on the basis of the analysis in Section 4. (present passive)

2. Nominalisation in Formal Report Writing

Nominalisation — converting verbs and adjectives into nouns — is a defining feature of formal written English. It creates denser, more compressed expression and a professional impersonal tone by shifting emphasis from action to concept.

  • Verb → Noun: investigate → investigation | decide → decision | recommend → recommendation | fail → failure | reduce → reduction | improve → improvement | expand → expansion | analyse → analysis
  • Adjective → Noun: significant → significance | possible → possibility | effective → effectiveness | clear → clarity | complex → complexity
  • Pattern: informal verb clause → formal noun phrase: "costs fell" → "a reduction in costs"; "the company failed to comply" → "the company's failure to comply"
  • Caution: excessive nominalisation can obscure meaning — use it purposefully, not reflexively
✔ "A significant reduction in costs was recorded in Q3." (not: "Costs fell significantly in Q3.")
✔ "The company's failure to implement the strategy…" (not: "The company did not implement the strategy…")
✔ "The recommendation is for a phased approach." (not: "We recommend a phased approach.")
✔ "This report presents an analysis of changes in productivity." (not: "This report analyses how productivity changed.")

3. Formal Connectors and Hedging Language in Reports

Formal connectors create explicit logical relationships between ideas, and hedging language calibrates the confidence of claims to the strength of the evidence — both are essential in professional report writing.

  • Contrast: However / Nevertheless / That said / In contrast / Notwithstanding this
  • Cause and result: Consequently / As a result / Therefore / For this reason / It follows that
  • Addition: Furthermore / Moreover / In addition / Additionally
  • Balanced conclusion: On balance / Overall / Taking all factors into account / Weighing the evidence
  • Hedging (uncertainty): "would appear to suggest" / "it is conceivable that" / "the data indicates a possible…" / "further research would be required to confirm"
  • Strong conclusion (evidence is robust): "the evidence clearly supports…" / "it is concluded that…" / "the data demonstrates…"
✔ "Nevertheless, the overall attrition rate remains above the industry benchmark." (contrast: positive, but problem persists)
✔ "Consequently, the findings should be treated as indicative." (result of limitation)
✔ "The data would appear to suggest a correlation, though causation has not been established." (hedged claim)
✔ "On balance, the evidence supports the phased approach." (balanced conclusion)

Mistake one: mixing up findings and conclusions. Your findings section should be purely factual — you report what the data shows, nothing more. Mistake two: writing recommendations that aren't actually recommendations. I see this constantly. That is a fear of commitment dressed up as professional caution. A recommendation should be direct: "It is recommended that the company initiate a phased restructuring programme by Q2." Mistake three: the invisible executive summary. Many writers treat the executive summary as an afterthought — a brief introduction to the report. But for most senior readers, the executive summary is the report. Write it as though it is the only page anyone will read. Mistake four: passive voice overuse. Yes, reports should be formal and impersonal. But sentences like "It is believed that it was suggested that it may have been determined that…" are not formal — they are evasive. Use the passive purposefully, not reflexively. And mistake five: the conclusion that introduces new information. Your conclusion must draw together what has already been argued in the body. If a reader encounters a new fact or data point in your conclusion, your report has a structural problem.

Case Study 1 · Financial Services · United Kingdom

Ashmore Capital: When a Poor Report Costs a Decision

Ashmore Capital is a London-based investment management firm with assets under management of £4.2 billion. In 2022, the firm's Risk Committee commissioned a formal analytical report to evaluate whether the firm should divest from a portfolio of commercial real estate assets, in light of rising interest rates and shifting post-pandemic office occupancy patterns. The report was assigned to a Senior Analyst, David Brennan, who had extensive expertise in real estate markets but limited experience producing formal board-level reports. David spent eight weeks conducting a thorough investigation — reviewing market data, consulting external economists, modelling multiple scenarios, and producing an analysis that the Head of Risk later described as "genuinely excellent" in terms of its underlying thinking. The report, however, was 78 pages long, had no executive summary, presented findings and interpretations interchangeably throughout the body, and offered no prioritised recommendations — only a four-paragraph section headed "Considerations" that listed possible courses of action without recommending any of them. When it was submitted to the Risk Committee, the chair read the first six pages and set it aside. The firm made no decision on the portfolio for a further nine months, during which time valuations declined by 14%.

A post-mortem review conducted by the firm's Head of Governance identified the precise structural failures that had prevented David's analysis from reaching the people who needed it. The report lacked a clear terms of reference, meaning readers had no initial orientation to the report's scope or purpose. The absence of an executive summary meant that the report's central argument — that a phased divestment strategy would optimise returns before the expected rate peak — was buried on page 43. The intermingling of findings and conclusions meant that the Risk Committee could not distinguish between what the data showed and what David thought it meant. And the absence of direct recommendations left decision-makers without a clear course of action to evaluate or endorse.

Following the review, Ashmore Capital introduced a mandatory Report Writing Standard for all investment analysis reports. The standard required every report to include: a one-page executive summary written last and placed first; clearly separated findings and conclusions sections; and a minimum of three specific, prioritised, and individually owned recommendations with implementation timelines. David Brennan was enrolled in a professional report writing programme, and his subsequent report — a 22-page feasibility study on emerging market reallocation — was described by the Risk Committee chair as "the clearest, most actionable investment analysis I have received in my career." The underlying analysis was of equivalent quality to his first report. The difference was entirely structural.

Case Study 2 · Healthcare · Colombia

SaludPlus: Building a Report Writing Culture Across Languages

SaludPlus is a Bogotá-based healthcare group managing eleven clinics across Colombia, with annual revenues of approximately COP 280 billion. In 2023, the organisation underwent a significant governance transformation, shifting from a family-owned management structure to a professionalised board model with international non-executive directors — four of whom were based in the United States and required all internal reporting to be produced in English. The transition exposed a systemic weakness: while the clinical and administrative teams were technically competent and highly experienced, none of the senior managers had ever written a formal English-language business report. Internal communication had historically been conducted in Spanish, informally, and without consistent structure. The organisation's first English-language Quarterly Performance Report — a 94-page document submitted to the new board in February 2023 — was returned within three days with a three-page list of structural, linguistic, and content concerns.

The board's primary concerns fell into four categories. First, the report had no executive summary — a non-negotiable requirement for any board submission. Second, the language oscillated between formal and informal registers inconsistently, and included several direct translations from Spanish that were grammatically correct but tonally inappropriate for a board context (for example, referring to the clinical quality data as "a little bit lower than expected" rather than "below the projected benchmark"). Third, the report's recommendations were written as questions rather than proposals — "Should SaludPlus consider reviewing its emergency referral pathway?" — which the non-executive directors found confusing and lacking in conviction. Fourth, no individual had been identified as accountable for any of the proposed actions, making implementation planning impossible.

SaludPlus partnered with a business English consultancy to design a twelve-week Report Writing for Healthcare Governance programme, attended by all twelve of the organisation's senior managers. The programme focused specifically on formal English register, report structure, the distinction between findings and conclusions, and the language of direct professional recommendations. By Q3 of 2023, the organisation's Quarterly Performance Report had been reduced to 38 pages, included a structured executive summary, used consistent formal register throughout, and presented eight specific, owned, and time-bound recommendations. The board's response was unambiguous: the report was described as "professional, clear, and decision-ready." The Chief Executive, Dr Isabella Montoya, described the investment in report writing training as "possibly the highest-return professional development initiative the organisation has undertaken."
