Module Overview

Welcome to Module 7: Influencer and Affiliate Marketing. These two channels have transformed the way brands reach new audiences and drive revenue online. Influencer marketing — the practice of partnering with content creators who have established audiences — has grown into a multi-billion-pound industry, with brands investing in everyone from global celebrities to highly targeted niche micro-influencers. Affiliate marketing, meanwhile, enables brands to build performance-based partnerships where publishers, bloggers, and comparison sites earn a commission only when they drive a measurable result.

This module builds the vocabulary and communication skills you need to operate professionally in both areas. You will learn how to evaluate influencer profiles, negotiate partnership terms, brief creators in English, interpret campaign metrics such as earned media value and engagement rate, and discuss the ethical and regulatory dimensions of paid partnerships. You will also master the vocabulary of affiliate networks, commission structures, tracking technology, and programme management.

By the end of this module, you will be able to discuss influencer and affiliate strategy confidently in meetings and presentations, write professional briefs and outreach emails, interpret campaign performance data, and apply the grammar structures used in professional marketing negotiations and evaluations.

Key Vocabulary — 21 Terms
🌟
Influencer marketing
noun phrase
A marketing strategy that involves partnering with content creators who have an established, engaged audience to promote products or services to their followers.
📊
Engagement rate
noun phrase
A metric measuring the level of interaction an influencer's audience has with their content, calculated by dividing total interactions by total followers and expressing the result as a percentage.
🔗
Affiliate marketing
noun phrase
A performance-based marketing model where publishers, bloggers, or comparison sites earn a commission for each sale, lead, or click they generate for a brand via a tracked link.
💸
Commission
noun
The payment an affiliate receives for driving a specific action, typically calculated as a percentage of the sale value or a fixed fee per lead or click.
🍪
Cookie window
noun phrase
The period of time after a user clicks an affiliate link during which a completed purchase or action is attributed to that affiliate and earns them a commission.
🏢
Affiliate network
noun phrase
A third-party platform that connects brands with potential affiliate publishers, managing tracking technology, payment processing, and reporting for all parties.
🧑
Micro-influencer
noun
A content creator with a relatively small but highly engaged following, typically between 10,000 and 100,000, known for niche expertise and strong audience trust.
💼
Brand ambassador
noun phrase
A long-term influencer or public figure contracted to consistently represent a brand across multiple campaigns and contexts, embodying the brand's values.
💡
Earned media value (EMV)
noun phrase
An estimate of the monetary value of organic coverage and influencer content, calculated by comparing it to the equivalent cost of paid advertising with the same reach and engagement.
🎁
Gifting campaign
noun phrase
An influencer marketing strategy where brands send free products to creators in exchange for an honest review or feature, without guaranteeing paid content.
📋
Influencer brief
noun phrase
A document provided by a brand to an influencer that outlines the campaign objectives, key messages, content requirements, deadlines, usage rights, and disclosure obligations.
🔍
Audience fit
noun phrase
The degree to which an influencer's followers match the demographics, interests, and purchasing behaviours of a brand's target customer.
🚫
Influencer fraud
noun phrase
The practice of artificially inflating follower counts, engagement metrics, or reach through the use of bots, purchased followers, or engagement pods.
📈
Cost per engagement (CPE)
noun phrase
A pricing model in which a brand pays for each measurable interaction (like, comment, share, or save) generated by an influencer's content, rather than a flat fee.
🔑
Affiliate link
noun phrase
A unique, trackable URL assigned to each affiliate that records which publisher drove a visitor to the brand's website, enabling accurate attribution of commissions.
📣
Paid partnership
noun phrase
A commercial collaboration between a brand and a content creator where the creator is compensated financially for promoting the brand to their audience.
🏷️
Disclosure
noun
The legal and ethical requirement for influencers to clearly communicate to their audience that a post or piece of content is sponsored or paid for by a brand.
⭐
Nano-influencer
noun
A content creator with a very small following, typically between 1,000 and 10,000, whose high trust level and close community relationships often produce outstanding engagement rates.
📉
Chargeback
noun
In affiliate marketing, a reversal of a previously paid commission, typically occurring when the sale that triggered the commission is later cancelled or refunded.
🌐
Publisher
noun
In affiliate marketing, any website, blog, comparison platform, or content creator that promotes a brand's products via affiliate links in exchange for commission.
🔁
Revenue share
noun phrase
An affiliate commission model in which the affiliate earns a percentage of the total revenue generated by customers they refer, often used for subscription or high-value products.

Text 1: The Rise of Influencer Marketing — Strategy and Evaluation

Paragraph 1 — What Influencer Marketing Is and Why It Works

Influencer marketing is built on a principle that predates social media by centuries: people trust recommendations from individuals they admire, respect, or relate to far more than they trust advertising from brands. What social media has done is scale this principle dramatically. A fitness creator with 800,000 YouTube subscribers can recommend a protein supplement and generate more sales in 48 hours than a television advertisement costing ten times as much. The reason is authenticity: the creator has built a relationship of trust with their audience over years, and when they recommend something, their followers believe it represents a genuine opinion rather than a paid message.

This authenticity advantage is the central value proposition of influencer marketing, and it is also its central challenge. The moment an audience perceives an influencer as purely transactional — recommending anything that pays enough — the trust evaporates and the marketing value with it. The most effective influencer partnerships are those where the product genuinely fits the creator's content and lifestyle, where the creator is given creative freedom to present it in their own voice, and where the audience cannot easily distinguish where the creator's genuine enthusiasm ends and the paid promotion begins.

Paragraph 2 — Influencer Tiers and Audience Fit

The influencer landscape has become highly segmented. At the top are mega-influencers: celebrities, athletes, and global content creators with followings in the millions. They offer enormous reach but typically lower engagement rates and less precise audience targeting. Below them are macro-influencers (100,000–1 million followers), who balance significant reach with stronger niche credibility. Micro-influencers (10,000–100,000 followers) are increasingly favoured by sophisticated marketers because their communities are typically more engaged, their audiences more precisely defined, and their content more trusted. Nano-influencers (under 10,000 followers) generate the highest engagement rates of all tiers, but the small absolute reach means they are typically used in large groups rather than individually.

Selecting the right influencer requires more than simply matching follower counts to budget. The most critical criterion is audience fit: the degree to which the influencer's followers match the demographics, interests, and purchasing behaviours of the brand's target customer. An influencer with 50,000 followers who are predominantly female, aged 25–35, interested in sustainable living, and located in the UK is a far more valuable partner for a sustainable homeware brand than an influencer with 500,000 followers whose audience is diffuse and unqualified. Follower count is a measure of scale; audience fit is a measure of relevance.

Paragraph 3 — Metrics and Measurement

Measuring the success of influencer campaigns has historically been challenging, but a growing set of metrics now allows marketers to evaluate performance with more precision. Engagement rate — the ratio of likes, comments, saves, and shares to total followers — is the most widely used indicator of content resonance and audience quality. An engagement rate above 3% is generally considered strong for larger accounts; micro-influencers regularly achieve rates of 5–10%. Beyond engagement, brands increasingly track reach (the total number of unique users who saw the content), impressions (the total number of times the content was displayed), and earned media value (EMV) — an estimate of the equivalent cost to achieve the same reach and engagement through paid advertising.

For campaigns with a direct sales objective, unique discount codes and trackable affiliate links allow brands to attribute specific revenue directly to individual influencer partners, transforming influencer marketing from a brand-building exercise into a measurable, performance-based channel. This convergence of influencer marketing and affiliate mechanics — sometimes called performance influencer marketing — is one of the most significant trends in the industry today.

Text 2: Affiliate Marketing — Structure, Models, and Programme Management

Paragraph 1 — How Affiliate Marketing Works

Affiliate marketing is one of the oldest performance-based marketing models in digital commerce. Its fundamental logic is simple: a brand (the merchant) allows third-party publishers (the affiliates) to promote its products in exchange for a commission on any sales or leads they generate. Each affiliate is given a unique tracking link. When a user clicks that link and subsequently completes a purchase within a defined time window — the cookie window or attribution window — the affiliate earns their commission. The entire system is tracked automatically through the affiliate network or a brand's own tracking technology.

The appeal of affiliate marketing from the brand's perspective is that it is almost entirely performance-based. Unlike paid advertising, where brands pay for impressions or clicks regardless of whether they convert, affiliate commissions are only paid when a real commercial result is achieved. This makes it a highly capital-efficient channel: the cost is incurred only after the revenue has been generated. For affiliates, the appeal is passive income potential: well-positioned content can generate commission from purchases made weeks or months after the content was originally published.

Paragraph 2 — Commission Structures and Affiliate Networks

Affiliate commissions are structured in several different ways depending on the brand's objectives and the nature of the product. Cost per sale (CPS) — where the affiliate earns a percentage of the sale value — is the most common model for e-commerce brands. Cost per lead (CPL) — where the affiliate earns a fixed fee for each qualified lead generated, such as a form submission or free trial sign-up — is widely used in financial services, insurance, and SaaS. Cost per click (CPC) models, in which affiliates are paid per click rather than per conversion, are less common due to the potential for click fraud, but are used in specific contexts.

Affiliate networks play a central role in connecting brands and publishers. They provide the technological infrastructure for link generation and click tracking, handle payment processing for commissions, offer a marketplace where brands can list their programmes and publishers can discover new opportunities, and provide reporting dashboards for both parties. Major networks in the UK include AWIN, Commission Junction (CJ), and Rakuten Advertising. Brands can also operate in-house affiliate programmes using platforms such as Impact or Partnerstack, giving them greater control over partner selection, commission structures, and creative assets but requiring more internal resource to manage.

Paragraph 3 — Programme Management and Optimisation

Running a successful affiliate programme requires active management rather than passive oversight. Key responsibilities include recruiting high-quality affiliate partners, reviewing and approving applications, providing affiliates with up-to-date creative assets and promotional materials, monitoring for fraudulent traffic or misattributed sales, and optimising commission rates to remain competitive with other programmes affiliates can promote. Affiliate fraud — attempts by unscrupulous publishers to manipulate tracking systems, generate fake clicks, or claim commission for sales they did not genuinely influence — is a significant concern that requires robust monitoring and clear programme terms.

The most important driver of affiliate programme performance is the commission rate. Affiliates operate rationally: they promote the programmes that offer the best combination of commission rate, average order value, and conversion rate. A brand that pays 5% commission on a product with an average order value of £200 (generating £10 per sale) will struggle to attract premium publishers if a competitor offers 8% on a similar product. Understanding the economics of the programme from the affiliate's perspective — and pricing it accordingly — is one of the most critical skills in affiliate programme management.

Reference Box 1
Gerunds & Infinitives in Marketing Writing
Why this matters in professional marketing
Marketing proposals, briefs, and presentations frequently use gerunds and infinitives to describe strategies, goals, and actions. Choosing the wrong form is a common error that makes writing sound unnatural. The choice is often determined by the verb or expression that precedes it.
PatternVerb/ExpressionMarketing Example
Gerund (verb + -ing)consider, avoid, recommend, suggest, involve, risk, enjoyWe recommend partnering with micro-influencers for this campaign.
Infinitive (to + verb)want, plan, decide, agree, aim, need, refuse, expectWe plan to launch the affiliate programme in Q3.
Both (meaning changes)remember, forget, stop, tryStop using follower count as the only metric. / Stop to evaluate the engagement data.
Both (meaning same)begin, start, continue, prefer, like, love, hateWe prefer working with creators who have organic audience growth.
Gerund as subject—Measuring ROI on gifting campaigns remains difficult.
Gerund after prepositionfor, in, by, before, after, withoutBefore briefing the influencer, ensure the creative assets are ready.
Examples in influencer and affiliate contexts
We considered working with a mega-influencer, but decided to invest in twenty micro-influencers instead.
Avoid selecting influencers based solely on follower count without examining the engagement rate.
The brand aims to recruit fifty affiliates before the end of the financial year.
By partnering with niche publishers, the brand improved its conversion rate significantly.
Reference Box 2
Discourse Markers in Marketing Presentations
What are discourse markers and why do they matter?
Discourse markers are words and phrases that signal the relationship between ideas. They help a speaker or writer guide their audience through a presentation or report with clarity. In marketing meetings, pitches, and strategy documents, strong use of discourse markers is a hallmark of professional communication.
FunctionDiscourse MarkersExample
Adding informationfurthermore, in addition, moreover, what is moreFurthermore, micro-influencers generate a higher engagement rate.
Contrastinghowever, on the other hand, by contrast, neverthelessHowever, the reach of nano-influencers is limited in absolute terms.
Giving a reasonbecause of this, as a result, consequently, thereforeThe cookie window was extended; consequently, affiliate revenue increased.
Giving an examplefor example, for instance, such as, to illustrateFor example, travel bloggers make highly effective affiliate publishers.
Summarisingin summary, to conclude, overall, in shortIn summary, the influencer campaign exceeded all key performance targets.
Sequencingfirst, secondly, then, next, finally, lastlyFirst, identify the target audience; then select the influencer tier.
Emphasisingin particular, above all, especially, notablyAbove all, audience fit is the most important selection criterion.
In presentations and reports
There are several reasons to prefer micro-influencers for this campaign. First, their engagement rates are significantly higher. Furthermore, their audiences are more precisely defined. However, the limited absolute reach means we will need to activate a larger number of creators to achieve the desired total impressions.

Digital marketing is a data-rich discipline. Being able to express quantities, proportions, and approximations accurately in English is essential for interpreting reports, presenting results, and making data-driven recommendations. The following expressions are used constantly in professional marketing contexts.
ExpressionUseMarketing Example

Case Study 1: How Blossom Teas Built a Micro-Influencer Programme That Outperformed Paid Social

Influencer Marketing · Micro-Influencer Strategy · Performance Measurement

Background

Blossom Teas is a UK premium tea brand founded in 2018, selling a range of loose-leaf teas, blends, and accessories online and in independent retailers. In early 2022, the marketing team was spending £18,000 per month on Instagram and Facebook paid social advertising, achieving a ROAS of approximately 2.4x. While profitable, the team believed the paid social strategy was beginning to reach diminishing returns as audience fatigue set in and CPCs climbed. They decided to explore influencer marketing as a complementary channel.

Rather than partnering with one or two large influencers, the team made a deliberate strategic choice to build a programme of fifty micro-influencers across three niches: wellness and self-care, sustainability and eco-living, and home lifestyle. Each influencer had between 15,000 and 80,000 followers. The total monthly fee for the programme was £12,000 — significantly less than the paid social budget — with each influencer receiving a flat fee per post plus a unique discount code through which sales could be directly attributed to them.

Results & Analysis

Within three months, the influencer programme was generating results that surprised even the optimistic members of the team. The average engagement rate across the fifty influencer accounts was 5.8% — nearly four times the industry average for Instagram at the time. The unique discount codes generated £41,000 in directly attributable revenue per month, representing an effective ROAS of 3.4x on the influencer investment. Additionally, the brand observed a 22% uplift in organic search traffic for brand name terms in the months following each wave of influencer posts — an indirect benefit that the team attributed to increased brand awareness and word-of-mouth.

Perhaps most significantly, the cost per new customer acquired through the influencer programme was £8.40, compared to £19.80 through paid social. The lower cost reflected both the higher conversion rates driven by trusted recommendations and the lower baseline cost of the programme relative to the media spend it replaced. By month six, Blossom Teas had increased the influencer programme to eighty creators and reduced its paid social budget by 35%. The case illustrated a principle that sophisticated marketers had long suspected: at the right scale and with the right audience fit, micro-influencers can outperform paid media on both ROAS and cost per acquisition.

Case Study 2: Wanderpack Luggage — Building a High-Revenue Affiliate Programme from Scratch

Affiliate Marketing · Programme Design · Commission Optimisation

Background

Wanderpack is a direct-to-consumer luggage and travel accessories brand that launched in the UK in 2021. The founding team had strong experience in product design and supply chain but limited knowledge of digital marketing. In their first year, they relied almost entirely on paid search and paid social, achieving solid but expensive customer acquisition. In year two, the head of growth proposed building an affiliate programme as a way to generate performance-based revenue from content creators, comparison sites, and travel publications without the upfront cost of paid media.

The programme launched on the AWIN affiliate network with an initial commission rate of 6% per sale and a 30-day cookie window. The average order value was £195. In the first month, the programme attracted 34 affiliates and generated £4,200 in affiliate-attributed revenue. These results were disappointing relative to expectations. An audit of the network revealed the problem: several competing travel luggage brands were offering commission rates of 9–12% on similar products. Wanderpack was simply not competitive enough to attract the high-quality publishers who drove the majority of affiliate revenue in the travel category.

Optimisation & Results

Based on the audit findings, the team made three changes. First, the base commission rate was raised from 6% to 10%. Second, a tiered commission structure was introduced: affiliates generating more than £2,000 per month in sales earned 12%, creating a strong incentive for top performers to promote Wanderpack more actively. Third, the cookie window was extended from 30 to 60 days — an important change for a travel accessories brand, where the purchase cycle can be long as customers research and compare before buying.

The team also invested in actively recruiting high-value affiliates: travel bloggers with proven conversion track records, comparison sites with strong domain authority, and travel-focused email newsletters. Within six months of the programme relaunch, the affiliate count had grown from 34 to 312. Monthly affiliate-attributed revenue had risen from £4,200 to £67,000. The top twenty affiliates — representing just 6% of the total partner base — accounted for 74% of total affiliate revenue, a pattern consistent with the Pareto principle commonly observed in affiliate marketing. The cost of affiliate revenue (commission paid as a percentage of sales) was 10.3%, well below the cost of acquiring equivalent customers through paid search.
