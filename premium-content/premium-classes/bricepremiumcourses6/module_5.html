📚 Key Legal Terms
🧩
Problem Statement
A clear, precise articulation of the specific problem a consulting engagement is designed to solve — including what is happening, why it matters, what is causing it (if known), and what a successful resolution would look like. A well-constructed problem statement is the single most important input into a consulting engagement: it focuses the team's analysis, prevents scope creep by defining the boundaries of the work, and ensures the client's actual problem is being addressed rather than a convenient proxy. Problem statements should be specific (not 'our performance is declining' but 'our operating margin has fallen from 18% to 11% over three years, primarily in the SME segment'), causal (not just descriptive), and outcome-oriented (what would good look like?).
🌳
Logic Tree
A visual decomposition of a problem or question into its component parts — structured as a branching tree diagram that shows how the parts relate to the whole. Logic trees can be used for hypothesis testing (what are all the possible explanations for this problem?), for structuring recommendations (what are all the things we need to do to solve this problem?), or for structuring analysis (what questions must we answer to resolve the central problem?). A well-constructed logic tree is MECE at every level: the branches at each node are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive. Logic trees are among the most powerful tools in consulting problem-solving because they make implicit problem structure explicit — forcing the team to confirm that it is addressing the right questions before committing analytical resources.
🔍
Root Cause Analysis
A structured diagnostic process that traces a problem from its observable symptoms back to its fundamental causes — the conditions or decisions that, if addressed, would prevent the problem from recurring. Root cause analysis prevents the common failure of treating symptoms rather than causes: a logistics company with declining margins might implement a cost-cutting programme (treating the symptom) when the root cause is a pricing model that systematically underprices the most complex routes. In consulting practice, root cause analysis is typically conducted through a combination of data analysis, client interviews, and structured frameworks — often using the 5 Whys technique to trace each symptom to successively deeper causes until the fundamental driver is identified.
❓
5 Whys
A root cause analysis technique that traces a problem to its fundamental cause by asking 'why?' five times in sequence — each answer becoming the subject of the next 'why?' question. Developed by Sakichi Toyoda at Toyota, the technique is powerful because it resists the temptation to stop at the first plausible explanation: 'Why are margins declining? Because route costs have increased. Why? Because we've taken on more complex routes. Why? Because our pricing model doesn't differentiate by complexity. Why? Because no one owns the pricing model. Why? Because pricing accountability wasn't included in the recent restructuring.' The root cause — pricing accountability — suggests a different solution from the apparent cause (route cost increases).
🎯
Recommendation
A specific, evidence-based proposal for action — the primary output of a consulting engagement. A consulting-quality recommendation is not a strategic direction ('Meridian should focus on growing its enterprise customer base') but a specific, actionable proposal ('Meridian should introduce a dedicated enterprise account management function of 4 FTEs by Q3, targeting the 50 largest current SME customers as the first enterprise conversion pipeline, with a target of converting 12 to enterprise contracts within 12 months'). The quality of a recommendation is determined by its specificity (what exactly should be done?), its evidence base (why this recommendation rather than an alternative?), and its implementability (is there a clear path from decision to execution?).
⚡
Quick Win
A recommendation that can be implemented rapidly — typically within 90 days — generates visible, measurable impact quickly, and requires relatively limited investment or organisational change. Quick wins serve two strategic purposes in a consulting engagement: they generate early evidence that the engagement is producing value, which builds client confidence and supports the implementation of larger, longer-term recommendations; and they create organisational momentum, demonstrating to the client's team that change is happening and building the appetite for larger transformation. In a multi-initiative recommendation set, quick wins are typically sequenced first — before the more complex strategic initiatives that will take longer to design, approve, and implement.
🏗️
Strategic Initiative
A recommendation that requires significant investment, time, and organisational commitment to implement — and that addresses a fundamental driver of the organisation's performance rather than a surface-level improvement. Strategic initiatives are the core of most consulting recommendations: they address root causes, require cross-functional coordination, and typically deliver their primary value over 12-24 months rather than weeks. In a consulting deliverable, strategic initiatives are presented with a clear rationale (why this, rather than something else?), a sequenced implementation plan, a resource requirement, and a risk assessment. They are typically supported by, but distinguished from, the quick wins that generate early momentum.
📊
Prioritisation Matrix
A two-dimensional analytical tool that evaluates a set of options against two criteria — most commonly impact and feasibility, or impact and effort — to identify which options should be prioritised. Prioritisation matrices are standard tools in consulting recommendation development: when a team has identified ten potential improvement initiatives, a prioritisation matrix allows it to identify the two or three that are both high-impact and high-feasibility — the 'no-brainers' — before developing the full recommendation set. The matrix produces four quadrants: high impact / high feasibility (prioritise), high impact / low feasibility (invest in enablement), low impact / high feasibility (quick wins), and low impact / low feasibility (deprioritise or eliminate).
🗺️
Implementation Plan
A structured document that translates a recommendation into specific, sequenced actions — specifying what will be done, by whom, by when, and with what resources. An implementation plan is the bridge between a recommendation and its execution: without it, a recommendation remains aspirational rather than actionable. A consulting-quality implementation plan typically covers: the phased sequence of activities, the dependencies between activities (what must happen before what?), the resource requirements at each phase, the governance and accountability structure, the key milestones and decision points, and the early warning indicators that will signal if the implementation is off track. Implementation plans are often presented as Gantt charts or sequenced milestone tables.
🔒
Risk Mitigation
The identification of potential adverse outcomes that could prevent a recommendation from achieving its intended impact — and the specific actions that reduce the likelihood or impact of each risk. Risk mitigation in a consulting recommendation is not a list of risks but a structured response to risks: for each identified risk, the mitigation specifies what will be done, by whom, and at what point in the implementation. A common failure in consulting deliverables is identifying risks without providing mitigations — which tells the client that risks exist but provides no basis for managing them. The most credible consulting recommendations include a risk-mitigation table that directly addresses the most material risks and demonstrates that the team has thought through the potential failure modes.
⚖️
Prioritisation
The process of ranking competing recommendations or initiatives by their relative strategic importance — to determine the sequence and level of investment appropriate to each. Prioritisation is necessary because organisations always have more potential improvements available than resources to implement them simultaneously. In consulting practice, prioritisation is typically conducted using a combination of quantitative assessment (financial impact, implementation cost, timeline) and qualitative judgment (strategic alignment, organisational readiness, competitive urgency). The prioritisation output — typically a sequenced initiative roadmap — communicates to the client not just what should be done but in what order, at what pace, and with what level of investment at each stage.
🧪
Proof of Concept
A limited, controlled test of a recommendation — designed to validate the key assumptions before committing to full implementation. A proof of concept is the consulting equivalent of the scientific hypothesis test: rather than recommending immediate full rollout of a new approach, the consulting team recommends first testing the approach in a limited environment where failure is recoverable and learnings can inform the full implementation. Proofs of concept are particularly valuable for recommendations that involve significant implementation risk, novel approaches without comparable precedent, or technology solutions whose performance depends on client-specific factors that cannot be fully assessed through analysis alone. A well-designed proof of concept specifies the success criteria — what results from the PoC would validate proceeding to full implementation?
📋
So What Test
A quality check applied to any analytical finding or recommendation — asking 'so what?' to verify that the finding has a clear, specific implication for the decision or recommendation it is intended to support. The 'so what' test is one of the most important quality disciplines in consulting: a slide that states 'the German logistics market is growing at 4.2% annually' fails the 'so what' test if it does not also state what the team's recommendation implies from this fact. A slide that states 'the German logistics market is growing at 4.2% annually — above Meridian's UK growth rate of 3.1% — supporting the prioritisation of Germany over further UK investment' passes the 'so what' test because it connects the finding to its implication.
🔗
Dependency
A relationship between two activities or recommendations in which one cannot begin or succeed without the other being completed first. Identifying and managing dependencies is one of the most important tasks in implementation planning: a recommendation that assumes a technology system is available before the system has been procured, or that assumes staff are trained before the training programme has been designed, will fail at the dependency point. In consulting implementation plans, dependencies are typically mapped in a dependency chart or noted in the Gantt chart — with the dependent activity explicitly linked to the enabling activity. The critical path of an implementation — the sequence of dependencies that determines the earliest possible completion date — should be identified and monitored as the primary implementation risk indicator.
🏆
Actionable Recommendation
A recommendation that is specific enough to be implemented without further analytical work — providing sufficient clarity on what should be done, who should do it, by when, and with what resources. Actionable recommendations are the highest-value output of a consulting engagement: they move the client from analysis to execution. The most common quality failure in consulting recommendations is the 'strategic direction' — a recommendation that states a general intention ('Meridian should strengthen its enterprise sales capability') rather than a specific action ('Meridian should hire 4 enterprise account managers by Q3, implement a structured account planning process by Q1, and define a target list of 50 enterprise conversion accounts by the end of this month'). The test of an actionable recommendation: could the client's team begin executing it tomorrow without asking the consulting team for clarification?
🗝️
Change Management
The structured process of preparing, equipping, and supporting an organisation's people through a significant change — ensuring that a new strategy, process, or technology is adopted effectively rather than resisted or ignored. Change management is frequently the difference between a recommendation that is implemented and one that is not: the most analytically rigorous recommendation will fail if the organisation's people do not understand why the change is necessary, do not believe it will benefit them, or do not have the skills and support required to execute it. In consulting engagements, change management planning typically covers: stakeholder communication (who needs to know what and when?), training and capability building, resistance management, and the governance structures that will sustain the change after the consulting team has left.
🎭
Stakeholder Map
A structured representation of the individuals and groups who have an interest in, or influence over, a recommendation or change initiative — typically showing their level of support, their level of influence, and the actions required to manage each. A stakeholder map is essential before finalising a recommendation: a recommendation that faces strong resistance from a key stakeholder who has not been engaged may be technically excellent but practically unfeasible. Stakeholder mapping typically classifies stakeholders into quadrants based on their influence (high vs low) and their current support (champion, neutral, sceptic, or opponent) — and then specifies the engagement strategy for each: inform, consult, involve, or co-design.
📅
Implementation Sequencing
The deliberate ordering of implementation activities — determining which actions should come first, which can proceed in parallel, and which must wait for earlier activities to be completed. Implementation sequencing is not simply chronological ordering: it is a strategic choice that reflects the dependencies between activities, the organisational change capacity available, the urgency of specific outcomes, and the risk profile of the implementation. A consulting team that sequences recommendations thoughtfully — starting with the activities that enable other activities, building capability before deploying it, and generating quick wins before committing to large investments — significantly increases the probability that the full recommendation set is implemented successfully.
🧭
Critical Path
The sequence of dependent activities in an implementation plan that determines the minimum time required to complete the overall programme — the chain of activities where any delay directly extends the programme completion date. Identifying the critical path is essential for implementation planning: it reveals which activities must be started immediately (because they are on the critical path) and which have float (they can start later without affecting the overall timeline). In consulting implementation plans, the critical path is typically the most resource-intensive and least controllable sequence — often involving technology development, regulatory approval, or organisational restructuring — and should receive the most active management attention and the earliest escalation triggers.
🌀
Iterative Approach
An implementation philosophy that delivers recommendations in cycles — each delivering a defined increment of value, generating feedback, and informing the next cycle — rather than as a single large-scale transformation. The iterative approach is the alternative to 'big bang' implementation: rather than designing a perfect solution and implementing it all at once, the team designs, implements, learns, and refines in repeated cycles. Iterative approaches are particularly appropriate when: the problem is complex and not fully understood at the start, when user or customer feedback is essential to the final design, or when the organisation's change capacity is limited and needs to build progressively. The iterative approach reduces implementation risk but extends the timeline to full benefit realisation.
🔑
Key Performance Indicator
A measurable metric used to assess whether a recommendation is achieving its intended outcome — the quantitative signal that the implementation is on track or off track. KPIs are defined at the recommendation design stage — not retrospectively after implementation has begun. A well-designed KPI set for a consulting recommendation includes: leading indicators (early signals of progress before the final outcome is achieved), lagging indicators (confirmation that the intended outcome has been achieved), and threshold values (the levels above and below which action is required). A recommendation without KPIs is an aspiration, not an implementation plan: the client cannot monitor progress, identify problems early, or hold the implementing team accountable without a clear and agreed measurement framework.

Text 1: How Consultants Structure Complex Problems — The Discipline of Problem Definition Before Solution Development

The most common reason a consulting engagement fails to produce its intended value is not poor analysis or weak recommendations — it is solving the wrong problem. A client who presents with 'declining margins' may actually have a pricing problem, a cost structure problem, a customer mix problem, or a growth problem — and the solution to each is entirely different. A consulting team that defines the problem as 'declining margins' and proceeds to develop margin improvement recommendations without understanding which of these underlying issues is driving the decline will produce a technically correct but practically irrelevant set of recommendations. The discipline of problem definition — of ensuring the team is working on the right problem before investing analytical resources — is the most valuable and most frequently underinvested discipline in consulting practice.

Problem definition in consulting is a structured process. It begins with the problem statement: a precise articulation of what is happening, why it matters, and what a successful resolution would look like. The problem statement is not a description of the client's complaint but a diagnostic framing of the underlying issue — and it frequently differs from what the client initially presents as the problem. A client who says 'we need to reduce costs' may have a problem that is better framed as 'our cost structure generates margins of 8%, below the 14% required to fund the growth investments needed to prevent market share erosion to a well-funded new entrant' — which immediately suggests a different set of diagnostic questions and a different set of potential solutions. Getting the problem statement right is the consulting team's first and most important analytical task.

Once the problem statement is agreed, the team structures the problem using a logic tree — a visual decomposition that identifies all the possible causes or solutions and confirms that the structure is MECE. The logic tree disciplines the team to be explicit about what it does and does not know: each branch of the tree represents a hypothesis that will either be confirmed or ruled out by the subsequent analysis. The branches that survive — those supported by data and analysis — form the basis for the recommendations. The logic tree prevents two common analytical failures: working on a subset of the relevant hypotheses (missing the actual root cause because it was not included in the initial structure) and doing analysis that is not connected to any hypothesis (answering questions that are interesting but not consequential for the central problem).

Text 2: From Analysis to Recommendation — How Consulting Teams Translate Findings into Actionable Proposals

The transition from analysis to recommendation is the most consequential step in a consulting engagement — and the one that most frequently falls short. Teams that have done rigorous analysis often produce recommendations that are too vague to implement, too ambitious to be realistic, or technically correct but politically impossible. A recommendation that states 'Meridian should strengthen its enterprise sales capability' is not a recommendation — it is an aspiration. A recommendation that states 'Meridian should hire four enterprise account managers by Q3, implement a structured account planning process in Q1, and define a 50-account conversion target list by end of this month, with a goal of converting 12 accounts to enterprise contracts within 12 months' is a recommendation. The difference is specificity: the second version provides sufficient detail that the client can begin execution tomorrow without asking the consulting team for further clarification.

The most reliable test of a recommendation's quality is the 'so what' test applied in reverse: does the recommendation directly address the root cause identified in the analysis? If the analysis identifies that Meridian's margins are declining because the pricing model does not differentiate by route complexity — and the recommendation is to improve the enterprise sales capability — the recommendation fails the reverse 'so what' test. It may be a good recommendation for a different problem, but it does not address the identified root cause. Every recommendation should trace directly back to a specific finding from the analysis, and the rationale should make that connection explicit: 'because the analysis confirms X, we recommend Y, which will produce outcome Z.'

The most common quality failures in consulting recommendations are: vagueness (the recommendation does not specify what exactly should be done), non-sequitur (the recommendation does not address the identified root cause), over-ambition (the recommendation requires resources or capabilities the client does not have), and under-ambition (the recommendation addresses a symptom rather than the root cause and will produce only marginal improvement). The best protection against all four failures is a simple discipline: before finalising any recommendation, ask three questions — Is it specific enough to implement tomorrow? Does it address the identified root cause? Is there a realistic path from decision to execution given the client's constraints?

Hypothesis Language — Stating What Is Being Tested

Hypothesis language makes the team's analytical assumptions explicit and provisional — signalling that conclusions will be drawn from evidence rather than assumption.

'We are testing the hypothesis that [specific claim about cause or relationship].'
'The [branch name] hypothesis is that [specific claim]. If this hypothesis is correct, we would expect to observe [specific data pattern].'

Root cause language explicitly names the difference between what is observable (the symptom) and what is generating it (the cause) — making the distinction visible and actionable.

'[Symptom] is a symptom rather than the root cause. The root cause — confirmed by [evidence] — is [specific structural or accountability condition that, if changed, would prevent the symptom from recurring].'
'The 5 Whys traces the symptom of [symptom] to its root cause through five sequential causal steps: [Step 1] → [Step 2] → [Step 3] → [Step 4] → [Step 5 = root cause].'

Grammar Point 2: Recommendation Language — Specific, Evidence-Based, and Actionable
Stating a Complete, Actionable Recommendation

A complete recommendation specifies: what should be done (specific action), who should do it (named role or function), by when (specific timeline), and with what basis (evidence or rationale). Recommendations missing any of these elements are incomplete.

'We recommend [specific action] — implemented by [named role/function] by [specific date/timeframe] — based on [evidence connecting this action to the identified root cause].'
'The first recommendation is [quick win or strategic initiative]: [specific action with measurable outcome], implementable within [timeframe] at a cost of [resource], projected to recover [specific financial impact] by [date].'
'Three conditions must be met for this recommendation to produce the projected outcome: [condition 1], [condition 2], [condition 3]. The implementation plan addresses each condition explicitly.'
'This recommendation is preferred over [alternative] because [reason A] and [reason B] — specifically, [alternative] addresses [symptom] while this recommendation addresses [root cause].'
The Three-Part Rationale Structure

A complete recommendation rationale has three components that should each appear in the recommendation's supporting slide or section.

Diagnostic basis: 'Because the analysis confirms that [root cause X] is the primary driver of [problem], the recommendation must address [X] directly.'
Comparative basis: 'This recommendation is preferred over [alternative A] because [reason]; and over [alternative B] because [reason]. Both alternatives address symptoms rather than the root cause identified in the analysis.'
Feasibility basis: 'The recommendation is implementable within [timeframe] using [existing or specified resources] — the primary constraints are [constraint A and constraint B], each of which is addressed in the implementation plan.'
Complete rationale example: 'Because pricing undifferentiation is the confirmed root cause [diagnostic], and route rationalisation and cost reduction each address symptoms rather than the root cause [comparative], we recommend the pricing complexity surcharge, which is implementable in 60 days within existing ERP infrastructure [feasibility].'
Quick Win vs Strategic Initiative Language

Distinguishing quick wins from strategic initiatives in a recommendation set requires specific language that communicates the different timelines, investment levels, and strategic purposes of each.

Quick win: 'As an immediate quick win — implementable within [60-90 days] without capital investment — we recommend [specific action] projected to generate [specific financial impact] in year one.'
Strategic initiative: 'The medium-term strategic initiative — to be initiated in [Q/quarter] following [enabling condition] — is to [specific transformational action], requiring [resource level] over [timeframe] to produce [long-term financial impact].'
Sequencing language: 'The quick win is sequenced first to [generate early evidence / build momentum / fund subsequent investment] — creating the conditions for the strategic initiative to be launched from a stronger base.'
Dependency language: 'The strategic initiative cannot begin until [enabling condition] is in place — which the quick win will establish by [date]. The sequence is therefore: [quick win] by [date] → [enabling condition] confirmed → [strategic initiative] launched.'

Grammar Point 3: Language for Implementation Planning and Change Management
Sequencing Language — Describing Dependencies and Order

Implementation sequencing language must make dependencies explicit — not just list activities in chronological order but explain why each must come before the next.

'[Activity A] must be completed before [Activity B] can begin — because [B] depends on [specific output of A].'

Risk and Mitigation Language

Risk mitigation language must specify not just the risk but the specific action that reduces its likelihood or impact — making the mitigation as precise and actionable as the recommendation itself.

'The primary risk to [recommendation] is [specific adverse outcome]. The mitigation has [number] components: [first, specific action]; [second, specific action]; [third, specific action].'

Change Management Language

Change management language frames the people dimension of implementation as a structured requirement — not an afterthought — with specific actions, owners, and success criteria.

'Successful implementation requires that [number] [role] are able and willing to [specific new behaviour]. The change management programme achieves this through [specific training / communication / support structure].'

Case Study 1: Toyota and the 5 Whys — How a Manufacturing Root Cause Technique Became a Consulting Standard
How Sakichi Toyoda's simple technique for identifying machine failures transformed problem diagnosis across industries — and why it remains the most practical root cause analysis tool in consulting practice

Background

The 5 Whys technique was developed by Sakichi Toyoda — founder of the Toyota Group — and became a foundational element of the Toyota Production System (TPS), later popularised in the West as 'lean manufacturing' or the 'Toyota Way'. The technique was originally designed to diagnose machine failures in manufacturing: when a machine stopped working, the standard response was to ask 'why?' five times in sequence until the root cause — typically a specific mechanical failure or process deviation — was identified. The technique was simple, fast, and counterintuitive: in manufacturing, the instinctive response to a machine failure was to fix the observable symptom (restart the machine) rather than diagnose the root cause (why did it stop?). Toyoda's insight was that repeatedly fixing symptoms was more expensive in the long run than identifying and eliminating root causes.

From Manufacturing to Management Consulting

The 5 Whys technique transferred from manufacturing to management consulting because the underlying diagnostic challenge is identical: in business, as in manufacturing, the observable symptom of a problem (declining margins, customer churn, missed deadlines) is almost never the root cause. Consultants apply the 5 Whys to organisational, commercial, and strategic problems rather than mechanical ones, but the logic is unchanged: ask 'why?' five times, and the answers typically lead from observable symptoms through proximate causes to the fundamental structural, accountability, or cultural condition that is generating the problem. The key insight that transferred from Toyota to consulting is the distinction between the 'last nail' (the proximate cause — the thing that directly produced the symptom) and the 'root nail' (the fundamental cause — the thing that, if removed, would prevent the symptom from recurring).

The 5 Whys in Consulting Practice — Strengths and Limitations

The 5 Whys is the most widely used root cause analysis technique in consulting because it is fast (a skilled facilitator can complete a 5 Whys analysis in 30-45 minutes), structured (the sequence prevents the team from accepting the first plausible explanation), and accessible (it requires no specialist tools). Its primary limitation is that it works best for problems with a single causal chain — for complex problems with multiple interacting causes, a full causal map or fishbone diagram may be more appropriate. In consulting practice, the 5 Whys is most commonly used for: diagnosing operational failures, identifying the root cause of metric deterioration (as in the Meridian margin case), and facilitating client discussions about why a previous change programme did not produce the expected results. It is rarely sufficient for genuinely complex strategic problems — but as a diagnostic starting point, it is almost always valuable.

Case Study 2: The McKinsey Report and NHS Change Management — What Happens When Excellent Recommendations Are Not Implemented
How the NHS's experience with major consulting-led transformation illustrates the gap between technically rigorous recommendations and successful implementation — and why change management is not optional

Background

The NHS has commissioned numerous major consulting studies over the past two decades, producing detailed analytical reports with technically rigorous recommendations for improving efficiency, patient outcomes, and organisational effectiveness. Many of these reports — including several produced by McKinsey, KPMG, and Deloitte — have been praised for analytical quality. But the NHS's implementation track record has been consistently below the potential suggested by the quality of the recommendations: a pattern of technically excellent analysis leading to partially implemented or unimplemented recommendations has been documented in multiple independent reviews of NHS management consulting spend. The gap between the quality of the recommendations and the quality of the implementation is not unique to the NHS — but the scale and transparency of the NHS's experience provides a detailed case study in why change management is not optional.

Why Recommendations Fail to Be Implemented

The NHS experience points to three recurring failures in the translation from recommendation to implementation. First, stakeholder management failure: recommendations that affect clinical staff — doctors and nurses — almost always face resistance when clinicians have not been involved in the diagnostic process and do not understand why the recommendation is analytically justified. A recommendation that is developed by a consulting team in isolation and presented to clinical staff as a fait accompli will face resistance regardless of its analytical quality. Second, insufficient change management investment: the budget for change management — training, communication, culture change, and leadership development — is almost always proportionally smaller than the budget for analysis and recommendation development. Third, loss of momentum after the consulting team leaves: recommendations that depend on sustained leadership attention and organisational change management are particularly vulnerable to the withdrawal of the consulting team's presence and energy.

What the NHS Case Teaches About Recommendation Implementation

The NHS experience offers four specific lessons for consulting recommendation development. First, the recommendation design process should involve the people who will implement it: clinical staff, front-line managers, and operational teams who are involved in co-designing recommendations will understand, own, and champion them — those who are presented with recommendations from the outside will resist, adapt, or ignore them. Second, the change management plan should be as specific as the implementation plan: vague change management ('we will communicate the changes to staff') is as inadequate as a vague recommendation. Third, the implementation plan should specify what happens after the consulting team leaves: who owns the change programme, how is momentum maintained, and what is the escalation path when problems arise. Fourth, the consulting team should include implementation success — not just recommendation approval — as a primary measure of engagement quality.
