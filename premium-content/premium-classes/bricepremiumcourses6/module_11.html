📚 Key Legal Terms
📋
Proposal
A formal document submitted by a consulting firm to a prospective client — setting out the consulting team's understanding of the client's problem or opportunity, the proposed approach to addressing it, the team that will conduct the work, the deliverables the client will receive, the timeline, and the commercial terms. Proposals are the primary business development tool in consulting: they translate the consulting firm's capability and approach into a client-specific document that demonstrates fit, analytical rigour, and commercial clarity. A consulting proposal is both a commitment document (the client will hold the consulting firm to what it promises) and a sales document (it must be persuasive enough to win against competitor proposals). The most common failure in consulting proposals: treating the proposal as primarily a sales document without the analytical rigour of a commitment document, or vice versa.
🔍
Request for Proposal
A formal invitation from a client organisation to consulting firms to submit proposals for a specific piece of work — typically specifying the problem to be addressed, the types of deliverables required, the timeline for the engagement, and the criteria that will be used to evaluate proposals. An RFP creates a competitive process: multiple consulting firms submit proposals, and the client evaluates them against explicit or implicit criteria to select a preferred partner. Responding to an RFP requires the consulting team to both answer the client's specified requirements and to differentiate their approach from competitors who are responding to the same brief. RFPs are common in the public sector (where competitive tendering is often legally required) and among large private sector organisations with formal procurement processes.
🎯
Win Theme
The central, differentiated message of a consulting proposal — the one idea that the proposal is designed to leave with the evaluator, explaining why this consulting firm is the right choice for this specific engagement. A win theme is not a generic capability statement ('we have deep expertise in logistics strategy') but a specific, client-relevant argument ('our recent work with three comparable European logistics operators means we can apply validated frameworks and benchmark data to your specific situation, compressing the diagnostic timeline and improving the reliability of our market entry projections'). The win theme should appear in the executive summary, be reflected in the approach section, and be reinforced by the team biographies and the commercial terms. A proposal without a clear win theme reads as a competent but undifferentiated response — often sufficient to make a shortlist but not to win.
📐
Scope of Work
The precise definition of what work the consulting firm will perform within the engagement — including what is explicitly included and (often equally important) what is explicitly excluded. A scope of work that is too vague creates disputes when client expectations exceed what the consulting team planned to deliver; a scope that is too narrow creates frustration when the client cannot get the help they need without a formal change order. Professional scope of work language describes: the specific questions the engagement will answer (not just the general topic area), the data sources and analytical approaches that will be used, the geographies or business units that are in scope, and the specific deliverables that will be produced. Explicit exclusions — 'this engagement does not include implementation support or change management activities' — protect the consulting team from scope creep and set appropriate client expectations.
📦
Deliverable
A specific, tangible output that the consulting firm commits to producing and presenting to the client within the engagement — as opposed to activities (what the team will do) or outcomes (what will happen as a result of the work). Professional consulting deliverables are: specific ('a 25-30 slide strategy recommendation deck' rather than 'a presentation'), timed (associated with a specific date or milestone), and complete (fully finished and presentable, not a draft or interim output unless explicitly labelled as such). The distinction between deliverables and activities is important in scope management: a client who receives a deliverable can evaluate it against the commitment; a client who only knows what activities were undertaken has no clear basis for evaluation. A proposal that lists activities without deliverables is harder to hold to than one that specifies deliverables.
🔄
Workstream
A defined component of a consulting engagement that addresses a specific aspect of the overall question — typically with its own analytical approach, team members, timeline, and deliverables. Workstreams allow large, complex engagements to be managed in parallel: a market entry engagement might have three workstreams — market analysis, competitive assessment, and financial modelling — each producing interim deliverables that feed into the final integrated recommendation. Workstream structure communicates to the client how the consulting team will organise the work, who is responsible for each component, and how the pieces will be integrated into the final output. A proposal that describes workstreams clearly helps the client understand the engagement architecture and provides a basis for managing the work throughout the engagement.
💰
Fixed Fee
A pricing model in which the consulting firm agrees to complete a defined scope of work for a specific total price — regardless of the actual time spent by the team. Fixed-fee pricing transfers volume risk to the consulting firm: if the work takes longer than projected, the consulting firm absorbs the additional cost; if it is completed more efficiently, the consulting firm retains the financial benefit. Fixed-fee pricing is most appropriate when: the scope is clearly defined, the analytical questions are well understood, and the consulting firm has sufficient experience with comparable engagements to price accurately. Clients typically prefer fixed-fee engagements because they provide cost certainty; consulting firms price a risk premium into fixed-fee proposals to reflect the uncertainty in actual time requirements.
⏱️
Time and Materials
A pricing model in which the consulting firm charges based on the actual time spent by team members (at defined day rates or hourly rates) plus any out-of-pocket expenses incurred — rather than a fixed total price. Time-and-materials pricing transfers volume risk to the client: if the work requires more time than anticipated, the client pays more; if less time is required, the client pays less. Time-and-materials is most appropriate when: the scope is difficult to define precisely at the start of the engagement, the work involves significant exploratory phases, or the engagement may need to pivot based on early findings. Time-and-materials engagements require more active budget management by the client but provide the consulting team with appropriate compensation for scope uncertainty.
📝
Statement of Work
A formal document that defines the specific work to be performed, the deliverables to be produced, the timeline, and the commercial terms for a consulting engagement — typically signed by both parties as a legal agreement. The SOW is the binding commercial document that governs the engagement; the proposal that preceded it describes the approach and sets expectations, but the SOW is what both parties are legally committed to. A professional SOW contains: a scope of work section, a deliverables list with dates, a team structure description, a commercial terms section (fee, payment schedule, expense policy), a change order process, and standard legal provisions (confidentiality, IP ownership, limitation of liability). Discrepancies between the proposal and the SOW should be resolved before signature.
🔄
Change Order
A formal document that modifies the scope, timeline, or commercial terms of an existing consulting engagement — required when the client requests additional work beyond what was agreed in the original scope of work, when the timeline needs to be extended, or when the team structure needs to change. Change orders protect both parties: the client receives a formal commitment for the additional work, and the consulting firm receives appropriate compensation for effort beyond the original scope. A professional change order describes: what is changing (additional scope, timeline extension, or team change), why the change is being made, the additional fee or timeline implication, and the revised deliverables list. Change orders should be signed before the additional work begins — not retrospectively.
👥
Team Structure
The description of the consulting team members who will conduct the engagement — their roles, experience, and time commitment. A professional team structure in a proposal includes: the partner (responsible for overall engagement quality and client relationship), the engagement manager or project lead (day-to-day management of the team and work), and the analyst or associate team (conducting the analysis and producing the deliverables). Team biographies in a proposal should be specific to the engagement: not a generic list of credentials but a description of how each person's experience is relevant to the specific problem and why this is the right team for this client. Clients evaluate team structure carefully — particularly the seniority and experience of the people who will actually do the work, not just those presented at the pitch.
📅
Milestone
A significant point in the engagement timeline at which a specific deliverable is completed, a decision is made, or a phase transitions to the next — used to structure the engagement, monitor progress, and provide the client with clear checkpoints. Milestones in a consulting proposal serve two purposes: they demonstrate that the consulting team has a credible plan for delivering the work within the proposed timeline, and they provide the client with clear points at which to assess progress and decide whether to proceed. Professional milestone planning identifies the critical path of the engagement — the sequence of activities and deliverables that determines the minimum time required — and builds in appropriate time for client review, feedback, and direction.
📊
Engagement Economics
The financial structure of a consulting engagement — including the fee, the payment schedule, the expense policy, and the basis on which the fee was calculated. Engagement economics are a critical element of a consulting proposal: the fee must be competitive enough to win the engagement, justified enough for the client to approve it, and profitable enough for the consulting firm to conduct the work at the promised quality. Professional proposals present engagement economics with sufficient transparency that the client understands the basis for the fee — whether it is based on time-and-materials rates, a fixed fee for a defined scope, or a value-based fee reflecting the business impact of the work. Hidden or unclear engagement economics undermine client trust and create disputes.
⚖️
IP Ownership
The legal question of who owns the intellectual property developed during a consulting engagement — including analytical frameworks, proprietary data, custom tools, and the content of deliverables. In most consulting engagements, IP ownership is a negotiated term: the consulting firm typically retains ownership of its pre-existing methodologies and tools (which it may use in other engagements), while the client retains ownership of the specific deliverables produced for their engagement. Disputes about IP ownership arise most commonly when: the consulting firm develops a novel tool or framework during an engagement and wishes to use it with other clients, or when the client wishes to share or publish deliverables without the consulting firm's consent. Clear IP ownership language in the SOW prevents these disputes.
🔐
Confidentiality
The mutual obligation in a consulting engagement to protect sensitive information shared between the consulting firm and the client — typically formalised in a non-disclosure agreement (NDA) signed before substantive discussions begin. In consulting proposals, confidentiality covers two directions: the client's confidential information (strategy, financial data, customer information) shared with the consulting team during the engagement, and the consulting firm's confidential information (proprietary methodologies, pricing, internal processes) that may be shared with the client. The NDA specifies what information is covered, how it may be used, and how long the obligation survives the end of the engagement. Most professional consulting engagements begin with an NDA — particularly when the consulting firm is given access to sensitive client data during the diagnostic phase.
🏛️
Governance
The structure of oversight, decision-making, and communication that will manage the consulting engagement — typically including a steering committee (senior client stakeholders who approve major decisions), a day-to-day project team (operational client contacts who work with the consulting team directly), and regular check-in cadences (weekly working sessions, bi-weekly steering committees, and monthly partner reviews). Governance is specified in the proposal and then agreed formally at the engagement kick-off. A well-designed governance structure ensures: senior client stakeholders are engaged at key decision points (preventing late surprises), day-to-day client contacts can support the consulting team efficiently, and the consulting team can escalate issues quickly when they arise. Poor governance structures — where senior stakeholders are unavailable and day-to-day contacts lack authority — are one of the most common causes of engagement delays and quality problems.
🎪
Pitch
The verbal presentation of a consulting proposal to a prospective client — typically a formal meeting at which the consulting team presents their proposed approach, team, and commercial terms, and answers the client's questions. A pitch differs from the written proposal in that it allows for dialogue: the consulting team can read the client's reactions, address specific concerns, and emphasise the elements of the proposal most relevant to the client's current priorities. Effective pitches are structured around the client's problem rather than the consulting firm's capabilities — leading with the team's understanding of what the client is trying to achieve, then demonstrating how the proposed approach addresses it, and using the commercial terms as a conclusion rather than a central focus. The most common pitch failure: presenting the consulting firm's general capabilities rather than demonstrating understanding of the client's specific situation.
🏷️
Day Rate
The fee charged by a consulting firm for one day of work by a team member at a specified level of seniority — the unit of billing in time-and-materials engagements. Day rates vary significantly by: the level of seniority (partners charge much higher day rates than analysts), the consulting firm's market positioning (top-tier strategy consultancies charge higher day rates than specialist boutiques), the nature of the engagement (complex, high-stakes strategic work commands higher rates than operational support), and the geography (consulting day rates are typically higher in major financial centres than in regional markets). Day rates in proposals should be presented transparently — the client should understand who is billing at what rate and for approximately how many days.
📎
Appendix
The supporting section of a consulting proposal that contains team biographies, firm credentials, sample deliverables, client references, and other supporting material — referenced in the main body but too detailed to include there. A professional proposal appendix is: relevant (every appendix item supports a specific claim in the main body), current (credentials and biographies reflect the most recent and most relevant experience), and concise (team biographies focus on what is relevant to this engagement, not the full career history). The appendix should not be longer than the main body — a proposal where the supporting material overwhelms the core argument has a structural problem. Common appendix contents: team biographies, relevant project examples, firm capability description, and references from comparable engagements.
📏
Scope Creep
The gradual expansion of a consulting engagement beyond its agreed scope — typically through informal additions of work that are not formally documented or compensated. Scope creep is one of the most common commercial problems in consulting engagements: a client who asks for 'just one more analysis' or 'a quick look at the French market too' may not realise they are requesting out-of-scope work; a consulting team that accommodates these requests without flagging the scope implication creates a precedent that makes subsequent scope management more difficult. Professional scope management requires: a clear scope of work that both parties have agreed on, a process for formally requesting scope changes (the change order), and the discipline to have the scope conversation early rather than absorbing additional work silently.
📌
Exclusivity
A contractual provision in which the client requires the consulting firm not to work for the client's direct competitors during the engagement — or for a specified period after the engagement concludes. Exclusivity clauses are most common in engagements where: the client is sharing highly sensitive competitive strategy with the consulting team, the engagement involves access to proprietary business models or customer data that could benefit competitors, or the client is making a major strategic decision and wants to ensure the consulting team's full analytical attention. Consulting firms must weigh exclusivity requests carefully: an exclusivity clause reduces revenue flexibility and may be commercially unacceptable if the firm has existing relationships with the client's competitors. When exclusivity is granted, it typically commands a premium fee reflecting the revenue restriction it imposes.

Text 1: How Consulting Proposals Are Evaluated — What Clients Actually Look For

Most consulting firms believe that a proposal is won or lost on the quality of the proposed methodology — that the client evaluates the approach, compares it to competitors, and selects the most analytically rigorous option. This is broadly wrong. Most clients evaluating proposals already have a strong prior about which consulting firm they prefer — formed from previous relationships, reputation, and the quality of the conversations that preceded the proposal submission. The formal proposal evaluation process serves primarily to validate or challenge that prior: a proposal from the preferred firm that is professionally adequate will win; a proposal from a less preferred firm that is analytically excellent will frequently still lose. The practical implication: understanding the client's priorities and pre-existing perceptions before writing the proposal is more important than the technical quality of the proposed methodology.

Proposals are typically evaluated on four dimensions, in roughly this order of importance: team (who will actually do the work — their experience, seniority, and relevance to the client's specific problem), understanding of the problem (does the proposal demonstrate genuine insight into the client's situation, or does it apply a generic framework?), approach (is the methodology credible and differentiated?), and commercial terms (is the fee competitive and is the engagement structure workable?). Most consulting firms invest most of their proposal-writing effort in the approach section — which is evaluated third. The first thing most clients look at is the team. The second is the executive summary, which communicates the team's understanding of the problem. A proposal with an excellent approach section but a generic team section and a weak problem understanding is unlikely to win.

The single most differentiating element in a successful consulting proposal is the demonstration of genuine client-specific insight: evidence that the consulting team has thought carefully about this client's specific situation, rather than applying a generic capability statement. This insight can come from: specific industry knowledge ('in our experience working with three comparable European logistics operators...'), specific understanding of the client's situation ('the tension between Meridian's growth ambitions and its current margin trajectory suggests the German entry decision is time-sensitive'), or specific analytical hypotheses about the problem ('we believe the primary barrier to German market entry is not market attractiveness but management bandwidth and local relationships — both of which the proposed joint venture structure addresses'). Each of these examples demonstrates insight; each takes the proposal out of the realm of generic capability demonstration and into the realm of genuine advisory value.

Text 2: Scope of Work — The Most Consequential Document in a Consulting Engagement

The scope of work is the most consequential document in a consulting engagement — more consequential than the proposal that secured the engagement, because it is the document to which both parties will return every time a dispute arises. A scope that is too vague ('the consulting team will provide strategic analysis and recommendations') creates a document that can neither be fulfilled nor enforced: the client can always argue that more work is required; the consulting team can always argue that additional work is out of scope. A scope that is too prescriptive ('the team will conduct exactly seven client interviews, produce exactly one market sizing model, and deliver a presentation of exactly 25 slides') creates a document that does not allow the team to adapt to what it learns during the engagement. The professional standard: a scope that is specific enough to set clear expectations and enforceable boundaries, while flexible enough to allow the team to pursue the most valuable analytical path.

The most important element of a professional scope of work is the explicit definition of what is excluded — not just what is included. Exclusions are professionally uncomfortable to write because they can appear defensive or presumptuous — the client has not asked for French market analysis, so why mention that it is excluded? The reason is that scope disputes almost never arise from things that were clearly in scope or clearly out of scope — they arise from the grey area in between, where the client believes something is naturally included and the consulting team believes it is a separate piece of work. Explicit exclusions eliminate a significant portion of the grey area before it becomes a dispute. The professional framing of exclusions is not defensive but clarifying: 'This engagement will address the German market opportunity. The scope does not include analysis of other European markets, which would require a separate phase of work. Should additional European markets become relevant during the engagement, we would address them through a formal change order.'

The deliverables section of a scope of work is the commitment the consulting firm makes to the client about what they will actually receive. Professional deliverable descriptions are: specific (a '25-30 slide strategy recommendation deck' is more defensible than 'a strategy presentation'), dated (associated with a specific delivery date or milestone, not 'at the end of the engagement'), and complete (a final, presentable deliverable, not a draft subject to unlimited revision). The most common scope dispute in consulting involves deliverable quality: the client expects a polished, publication-ready document; the consulting firm believed a working draft was sufficient. This dispute is prevented by describing deliverables precisely enough that both parties have the same expectation of what the consulting firm is committing to produce.

Grammar Point 1: Proposal Executive Summary and Scope Language
Writing the Executive Summary — Client-Specific Insight First

A proposal executive summary must demonstrate genuine understanding of the client's specific situation before describing the consulting firm's approach. The most powerful opening is a client-specific observation — not a generic capability statement.

Strong opening: 'Meridian Logistics stands at a strategic inflection point: UK organic growth of 3.1% annually is approaching its ceiling while Germany presents a €19.2B opportunity growing at 4.2% — and the 2-3 year window before incumbents close Meridian's technology gap is narrowing.'
Weak opening (avoid): 'We are pleased to submit this proposal for Meridian Logistics's consideration. [Firm name] is a leading strategy consulting firm with deep expertise in logistics and European market entry.'
Win theme language: 'Our recent work with Rhein Logistics and two comparable German freight operators means we apply validated frameworks, proprietary benchmarking data, and established partner ecosystem relationships — compressing the diagnostic timeline and improving projection reliability.'
The executive summary should answer in sequence: What is the client's situation? What is the decision they are trying to make? Why is this the right team for this decision? What is the proposed approach? What will the client receive?
Writing Scope Inclusions — Specific and Answerable

Scope inclusions should specify the questions the engagement will answer — not just the topics it will cover. 'Market analysis' is a topic; 'Is the German B2B logistics market sufficiently attractive to justify Meridian's entry investment?' is an answerable question.

Strong scope inclusion: 'The engagement will address: (1) Is the German market sufficiently attractive to justify Meridian's entry investment? (2) Which entry mode — joint venture, acquisition, or organic entry — best resolves the three identified barriers? (3) What are the specific JV terms that maximise execution probability?'
Weak scope inclusion (avoid): 'The consulting team will conduct market analysis, competitive assessment, and financial modelling.'
Workstream structure within scope: 'Workstream 1 (weeks 1-3): Market and Competitive Analysis — market sizing, competitive landscape, customer survey. Workstream 2 (weeks 2-4): Entry Mode Assessment — JV vs acquisition analysis, partner shortlist. Workstream 3 (weeks 3-6): Business Case — financial model, risk register, implementation roadmap.'
Each workstream should have: a clear question it answers, a timeline, a named owner, and a specific deliverable.
Writing Scope Exclusions — Clarifying, Not Defensive

Scope exclusions should be written in a clarifying rather than defensive tone — explaining what is out of scope and why, and proposing how out-of-scope work could be addressed.

Strong exclusion: 'This engagement does not include comparative assessment of European markets beyond Germany — France, the Netherlands, and other markets would require a separate analytical programme. Should additional markets become relevant during the engagement, we would address them through a formal change order.'
Defensive exclusion (avoid): 'We will not conduct any analysis of markets outside Germany and any such requests will be declined.'
The clarifying framing: acknowledge the legitimacy of the excluded topic, explain why it is separate, and propose a path forward — making the exclusion feel like quality commitment rather than service refusal.
Common necessary exclusions: implementation support, legal and regulatory advisory, other geographies, financial due diligence, and operational change management.

Grammar Point 2: Deliverable Descriptions and Commercial Terms
Writing Deliverable Descriptions — Specific, Dated, Complete

Every deliverable should be described with three elements: what it is (format, length, content), when it will be delivered (specific date or milestone), and what it represents (final or interim, presented to whom).

Strong deliverable: 'Week 7 — Final Strategy Recommendation (25-30 slides): a complete market entry recommendation including recommended entry mode, JV partner recommendation, full financial model with base case and downside scenarios, risk assessment and mitigation plan, and implementation roadmap. Presented at Steering Committee 3. This is a final, board-ready deliverable.'
Weak deliverable (avoid): 'Final recommendation at the end of the engagement.' — no format, no date, no content specification.
Interim deliverable language: 'Week 3 — Diagnostic Findings Presentation (15-20 slides, interim): preliminary findings on market attractiveness and entry mode options, presented at Steering Committee 1 for directional input before finalising the financial modelling workstream.'
Labelling interim vs final clearly prevents disputes about deliverable quality — clients who expect a final document and receive an interim draft are rightly disappointed.
Commercial Terms — Transparency and Precision

Commercial terms should be transparent about the basis for the fee, the payment schedule, and the expense policy — creating shared understanding rather than surprises.

Fixed fee structure: 'Total fee: £185,000 fixed. Basis: 240-260 consultant-days across seven weeks. Billing rates: Partner £3,200/day; EM £1,800/day; Senior Analyst £1,100/day; Analyst £750/day. Payment: 40% on commencement, 30% at SC2, 30% on final delivery.'
Time and materials estimate: 'Estimated fee: £180,000-£210,000 (240-280 consultant-days at standard rates). Bi-weekly budget updates provided. Budget implication of any scope change will be confirmed before additional cost is incurred.'
Expense policy: 'Out-of-pocket expenses billed at cost with no mark-up. Client approval required for any single expense above £500. Itemised on monthly invoices.'
Avoid: vague language like 'competitive pricing' or 'fees to be agreed' — these suggest the team has not thought through the commercial terms.
Writing Change Order Language

Change order provisions establish a clear process for managing scope evolution — which is inevitable in most complex engagements. Clear language prevents both scope creep and adversarial disputes.

Standard change order clause: 'Any work beyond this scope will be addressed through a written Change Order specifying the additional scope, fee or timeline implication, and revised deliverables list. Change Orders require written client approval before the additional work commences. Both parties commit to processing Change Order requests within five working days of receipt.'
Triggering language: 'A Change Order will be required when: (1) the client requests analytical work on a topic not specified in this scope; (2) the timeline is extended beyond the agreed completion date; or (3) a key team member is replaced.'
Professional Change Order conversation: 'We would be delighted to address this additional question. I want to flag that it falls outside the agreed scope — I will prepare a Change Order specifying the additional fee and timeline implication within 48 hours for your review before we proceed.'
The Change Order process should feel helpful — not adversarial. The framing is always: 'we want to do this work properly and give it the attention it deserves' rather than 'you are asking for something not in the contract.'

Grammar Point 3: Pitch Language — Persuasion, Differentiation, and Confidence
Opening the Pitch — Client's Problem, Not Firm's Capabilities

A pitch opening that leads with the consulting firm's capabilities ('we are a leading strategy firm...') is less persuasive than one that leads with the client's specific situation — demonstrating that the team has done its homework.

Strong pitch opening: 'Before we walk through the approach, I want to share our hypothesis about what this decision actually hinges on — because we think the key variable is not market size, which is clearly large enough, but timing. The technology window is closing and that changes the urgency of the entry decision. Our scope is designed around that specific observation.'
Weak pitch opening (avoid): 'We are pleased to present our proposal for Meridian's German market entry assessment. Our firm has significant experience in European logistics strategy...'
Demonstrating pre-engagement insight: 'Your UK organic CAGR of 3.1% is below your board's 5% target. Germany grows at 4.2%. Your routing algorithm is 2-3 years ahead of German incumbents. These three facts together make the entry decision look compelling — the question is the structure and timing.'
The client should feel, by the end of the pitch opening, that the team understands their situation better than other firms proposing.
Handling Commercial Challenges — Value, Not Defensiveness

Commercial challenges in a pitch should be met with value framing — connecting the fee to the value of the work — rather than defensive justification or immediate concession.

Value framing: 'The fee of £185,000 represents 2.2% of the projected £8.4M NPV if the entry is approved. If the analysis recommends against entry, it prevents a materially larger investment being made on an insufficient analytical basis. On either outcome, we believe it is proportionate.'
Seniority trade-off: 'I can reduce the fee by approximately £20,000 by moving to one analyst rather than two — but I want to be transparent that I would maintain the partner's involvement at 20% of time, because for a board-ready recommendation the quality oversight is not something I am willing to compromise on.'
Avoid immediate fee concessions: they signal that the fee was padded rather than justified, which reduces confidence in the overall commercial proposal.
Timeline challenge: 'Seven weeks reflects what the analysis needs to be defensible at board level. I can walk you through what each week produces and why removing any week compromises the quality of a specific deliverable — the timeline is not padding.'
Closing the Pitch — Specific Call to Action

The closing of a pitch should include a specific call to action — not just a general expression of interest in proceeding.

Strong close: 'We would like to propose starting on the 15th — which puts the final recommendation in front of your board before the summer break. To make that work we need a decision by the 7th. I want to be transparent that my availability is contingent on confirming by then. Can I confirm that the 7th is feasible for your timeline?'
Weak close (avoid): 'We look forward to working with Meridian and are ready to start whenever you are ready to proceed.'
Reference offer: 'Before you decide, I would be happy to arrange a reference conversation between our client's CFO — who completed a comparable German entry engagement 18 months ago — and Marcus. That conversation is often more useful than a written case study.'
Proposal expiry: if the proposed team has competing commitments, state the expiry date for the proposal: 'The team and fee structure proposed here are available until the 10th — after which we would need to reassess availability.'

Case Study 1: The McKinsey Proposal — How the World's Most Studied Consulting Firm Approaches Business Development
What McKinsey's approach to proposals reveals about the principles of winning consulting business — and why the best analytical proposal does not always win

Background

McKinsey & Company has been the subject of more business school case studies on consulting firm management than any other firm — and its proposal and business development practices have been particularly well-documented through books, interviews, and academic research. The firm's approach to business development is characterised by a paradox: McKinsey invests less in formal proposal writing than most competitors (often producing shorter, less polished proposals) while winning at a higher rate on competitive engagements. The explanation for this paradox lies in McKinsey's investment in what happens before the proposal is submitted: the quality of the client relationship, the specificity of the pre-proposal insight sharing, and the credibility of the team's understanding of the client's situation all develop through conversations that precede and inform the written proposal. By the time a McKinsey proposal is submitted, the client often already has a strong prior that McKinsey is the right choice — the proposal confirms rather than creates that view.

The McKinsey Proposal Approach

McKinsey's documented proposal approach reflects several principles that are directly relevant to consulting proposal practice. First, the proposal is a communication of an already-developed point of view, not the development of that point of view in the act of writing. McKinsey partners typically develop and share a specific hypothesis about the client's situation before writing the proposal — the proposal then articulates that hypothesis with the supporting analytical approach. Second, the team section is given the same importance as the approach section — because clients have learned that the team that is proposed is not always the team that is delivered, and McKinsey works to make the proposed team credible and specific to the engagement. Third, commercial terms are not discussed until there is a strong shared view on approach — beginning a commercial discussion before the client values the engagement sufficiently to commit to a fee is, in McKinsey's experience, counterproductive.

What the McKinsey Approach Teaches

Three lessons from McKinsey's proposal approach are directly applicable to consulting proposal practice. First, relationships built before the proposal are more valuable than polished documents produced after the brief is received — the firm that best understands the client's situation before the RFP is issued has a structural advantage that no amount of proposal writing can overcome. Second, a specific hypothesis in the executive summary — even a provocative or challenging one — is more persuasive than a balanced overview of the engagement approach, because it demonstrates that the team has done enough thinking to have a point of view. Third, competitive proposals are often won or lost on team credibility rather than methodology: a client who trusts that the proposed team will deliver insight they could not generate internally will accept a higher fee and a less polished proposal from that team than a lower fee and a more polished proposal from a team they have less confidence in.

Case Study 2: The Scope Dispute — How Vague Scope Documents Create Expensive Conflicts and How to Prevent Them
How the most common commercial dispute in consulting arises from imprecise scope language — and the specific drafting practices that prevent scope disputes from becoming client relationship problems

Background

Scope disputes — disagreements between a consulting firm and a client about what work was included in the agreed engagement — are the most common source of commercial conflict in professional services. Surveys of consulting firm leaders consistently identify scope management as one of the top three commercial risks in the industry. The most common pattern: a client requests additional analysis during the engagement that the consulting team believed was out of scope; the consulting firm's engagement manager raises a change order; the client is surprised and sometimes offended that work they believed to be naturally included in the engagement would be treated as additional; a dispute about the interpretation of the scope document ensues. The cost of scope disputes is not primarily financial — it is relational: a scope dispute that is handled clumsily is among the most common causes of a client not re-engaging the same consulting firm for subsequent work.

How Scope Disputes Arise

Scope disputes have two primary causes. The first is vague scope language: when a scope of work describes topics ('European logistics market') rather than specific questions ('Is the German B2B logistics market above €20 billion attractive enough to justify Meridian's entry investment, given its current margin position?'), the client and consulting team almost certainly have different mental models of what is included. The second is the absence of exclusion language: when the scope document does not explicitly exclude adjacent topics, the client reasonably assumes they are included. A scope that covers 'the German market' without excluding 'other European markets' creates a reasonable expectation that some consideration of France and the Netherlands is included — even if the consulting team never intended to address them. Professional scope drafting addresses both causes: specific questions rather than topics, and explicit exclusions for the most likely adjacent topics.

How to Prevent Scope Disputes

The most effective prevention for scope disputes is a combination of precise scope drafting and a well-managed scope conversation at engagement kick-off. Precise drafting specifies questions (not topics), deliverables (not activities), and explicit exclusions (not just inclusions). The kick-off conversation reviews the scope document with the client team and explicitly confirms the exclusions — creating a shared understanding before the engagement begins. When a scope dispute arises despite these measures, the most effective resolution approach is to treat it as a misunderstanding rather than a conflict: 'I want to make sure we address this question properly — let me check whether it is within the current scope or whether we should address it through a change order, and I will get back to you within 24 hours.' This framing keeps the client relationship intact while protecting the commercial terms of the engagement.
