📚 Key Legal Terms
📋
Engagement
The standard consulting term for a client project — preferred over 'project' because it implies an active, ongoing relationship and a scope of work rather than a one-time task. An engagement begins with a signed contract (the Statement of Work or SOW) and ends with the delivery of the agreed outputs. Engagements are typically categorised by type (strategy, operations, transformation, due diligence), by duration (sprint, short-term, or multi-phase), and by the team size required. The word 'engagement' also carries a professional connotation of deep client involvement — consultants are engaged, not merely hired.
🎯
Case
The informal consulting term for an engagement — used conversationally within the firm to refer to the client project a team is working on. 'What case are you on?' is a standard first question among consultants. The case typically has a name (often the client's name and the type of work: 'the Meridian growth strategy case') and a team. At the start of a case, the team 'opens' it; at the end, they 'close' it. Case teams are assembled for each specific engagement based on the skills required and consultant availability.
🏢
Partner
The most senior client-facing role at a consulting firm — responsible for originating new business, maintaining client relationships, and providing strategic oversight on engagements. Partners own the client relationship and are accountable for the quality of the firm's overall output to that client. At top-tier firms, becoming a partner typically requires 7-12 years of consulting experience. Partners are typically equity owners of the firm and share in its profits. In day-to-day engagement work, the partner is consulted on major decisions and presents final recommendations to the most senior client stakeholders.
📊
Engagement Manager
The mid-level consulting role that manages the day-to-day operations of a consulting engagement — also called Project Leader at some firms. The Engagement Manager (EM) is responsible for structuring the problem, managing the team's work plan, maintaining the client relationship at the working level, and ensuring the quality of analysis and deliverables before they are presented to the Partner. The EM is the most demanding role in consulting in terms of both breadth and intensity: they must manage up (to the partner) and manage down (to the team of analysts) simultaneously.
💼
Business Analyst
The entry-level consulting role for new hires joining directly from undergraduate study — also called Analyst or Associate Consultant depending on the firm. Business Analysts are responsible for conducting the primary research and quantitative analysis that underpins the team's recommendations: building financial models, conducting market research, performing interviews, synthesising data, and creating the slides that will eventually form the client deliverable. The Business Analyst role is intense, analytical, and primarily execution-focused: the analyst is given a 'module' or 'workstream' to own and is expected to drive it to completion.
📐
Framework
A structured analytical lens — a set of organising categories or principles — used to structure a consulting problem or organise a set of findings. Common frameworks include Porter's Five Forces (for competitive analysis), the 3Cs (Company, Customer, Competitor), McKinsey's 7-S (for organisational alignment), the BCG matrix (for portfolio analysis), and MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive — a logic structure for organising information). Frameworks are tools rather than answers: they help consultants and clients organise complexity, not replace the thinking required to understand it.
💡
Hypothesis
An initial, provisional answer to the engagement's central question — stated before the full analysis is complete, to guide what analysis is actually needed. The hypothesis-driven approach is a defining feature of top-tier strategy consulting: rather than gathering all available data before forming a view, the team states an early hypothesis ('we believe the decline in profitability is driven primarily by pricing rather than volume') and then designs the analysis to test whether that hypothesis is supported or refuted. This approach is significantly faster than data-first analysis and forces teams to be explicit about their assumptions.
📑
Deck
The PowerPoint (or equivalent) presentation document that is the primary output of most consulting work — named for its resemblance to a deck of cards. A consulting deck is not a presentation in the conventional sense: it is a structured analytical document in which each slide makes a single specific point (often stated in the 'headline' at the top of the slide) and the body of the slide provides the evidence for that point. Decks are used for everything: interim findings, final recommendations, status updates, and internal team discussions. Being able to build a rigorous, persuasive, visually clear consulting deck is one of the most important technical skills in the profession.
🔀
Workstream
A defined component of the overall consulting engagement — a sub-project with its own scope, analytical questions, and deliverables that contributes to the overall case output. Large engagements are typically divided into workstreams, each owned by a different team member or small sub-team: for example, a market entry strategy engagement might have a 'market sizing' workstream, a 'competitive landscape' workstream, and a 'go-to-market' workstream. Workstream leads are responsible for driving their component to completion and integrating their findings into the overall team output.
✅
Deliverable
The agreed output of a consulting engagement or a phase within it — the tangible work product that is handed over to the client. Deliverables are specified in the engagement contract (Statement of Work) and typically include: a final recommendations deck, an implementation plan, a financial model, a market analysis, or a set of workshops. The quality of deliverables is the primary basis on which a consulting firm's value is judged by the client. In internal consulting language, a deliverable is also any discrete piece of work that a team member produces and hands off to the broader team.
🧩
MECE
Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive — the foundational logic principle of consulting problem-structuring. A MECE breakdown divides a problem or category into components that do not overlap (mutually exclusive) and together cover the full space (collectively exhaustive). For example, a MECE market segmentation by size would divide all companies into exactly one size category with no company excluded. MECE is used to structure analytical frameworks, organise issues trees, and ensure that a slide's supporting points genuinely cover the full argument without duplication. Mastering MECE thinking is considered a core consulting competency.
👥
Stakeholder
Any individual or group with a material interest in, or influence over, the outcome of a consulting engagement — including the client's internal decision-makers, the employees affected by recommendations, the regulators who must approve them, and the investors who fund them. Stakeholder management is the process of identifying, understanding, and appropriately engaging each stakeholder group throughout the engagement: ensuring decision-makers are aligned, concerns are heard and addressed, and the final recommendations have the buy-in required for implementation. Poor stakeholder management is one of the most common reasons that technically excellent consulting recommendations fail to be implemented.
🏛️
Practice Area
A specialisation within a consulting firm — a defined domain of expertise in which the firm has deep knowledge, proprietary methodologies, and a track record of client work. Common practice areas include: Strategy, Operations, Organisation, Digital, Risk, Financial Services, Healthcare, and Sustainability. Consultants typically develop a primary practice area expertise over time, while remaining generalist enough to contribute across sectors. Practice areas have their own leadership, business development targets, and internal training programmes. Some practice areas are organised by function (what kind of work: strategy vs operations); others by sector (which industry: healthcare, financial services).
📝
Scope
The defined boundaries of what a consulting engagement will and will not address — the explicit agreement between the firm and the client about what work will be done, on what timeline, and at what cost. Scope is documented in the Statement of Work and is one of the most important governance documents in a consulting relationship. 'Scope creep' — the gradual expansion of the work beyond the agreed boundaries — is a common challenge: clients often request additional analyses or topics that were not in the original scope, requiring the engagement manager to either include the work within the existing budget (reducing profitability) or have a conversation with the client about adjusting the scope and the fee.
⏱️
Billable Hours
The hours of consulting work that are charged to a specific client engagement — as opposed to internal hours spent on firm administration, business development, training, or other non-client activities. Consulting firms charge clients based on the number of hours (or, in fixed-fee engagements, a pre-agreed total) and track consultant time in detailed timesheets. Utilisation — the percentage of a consultant's total hours that are billable to clients — is a key performance metric. New consultants typically target 70-80% billable utilisation; more senior consultants, who spend more time on business development and firm management, often have lower billable utilisation.
🧑‍🏫
Subject Matter Expert
An individual with deep specialised knowledge in a specific domain — brought into a consulting engagement to provide technical expertise that the core consulting team does not possess. SMEs can be internal (a specialist within the consulting firm's network, such as a former regulator or a technical expert in a specific industry) or external (an academic, former executive, or industry specialist engaged as an adviser). SMEs are typically not full-time team members but are consulted at specific points in the engagement — for example, to review the regulatory section of a market entry analysis or to validate a technical assumption in a business case.
📣
Workplan
The detailed timeline and task breakdown for a consulting engagement — mapping the work required over the duration of the case to specific days, team members, and outputs. The workplan is the Engagement Manager's primary operational tool: it shows what needs to be done, by whom, and by when, and is updated weekly as the engagement progresses. A well-structured workplan sequences activities so that outputs from earlier workstreams inform later ones, provides sufficient time for iteration and quality review, and builds in buffer for the inevitable unexpected challenges that arise on any complex engagement.
📌
Staffing
The process of assembling the consulting team for a specific engagement — selecting which consultants at which levels will work on the case, for how many hours per week, and for how long. Staffing decisions are made by the Engagement Manager and Partner, often in consultation with a central staffing function. Consultants are staffed based on: their relevant sector or functional expertise, their availability (current engagement commitments), their development needs (what skills they need to build), and their personal preferences. Being 'put on a case' is consulting parlance for being staffed on an engagement.
🗣️
Client Team
The client-side counterpart to the consulting team — the group of internal employees at the client organisation who work alongside the consultants, provide data and context, and are responsible for implementing the eventual recommendations. Client teams typically include a project sponsor (a senior executive who owns the engagement), a day-to-day project manager, and several functional experts or analysts. The quality of the client team and its engagement with the consulting team is one of the most important determinants of a consulting engagement's success: clients who actively participate produce better outcomes than those who treat consultants as external service providers.
🔍
Issue Tree
A structured decomposition of a complex problem into its component sub-questions — organised as a tree diagram in which the root is the central problem and the branches are the subsidiary questions that must be answered to resolve it. Issue trees are used at the beginning of an engagement to structure the problem and design the analytical approach: before the team decides what analysis to do, they map out all the questions that need to be answered and ensure the structure is MECE. A well-constructed issue tree is one of the most valuable tools in consulting practice: it prevents teams from doing analysis that is not needed and ensures no important question is overlooked.
🏷️
Statement of Work
The formal contract document that defines the scope, deliverables, timeline, team, and fees for a consulting engagement — abbreviated as SOW. The Statement of Work is the legal and commercial foundation of the engagement: it specifies exactly what the consulting firm is committed to deliver, by when, and at what cost. Any work performed outside the agreed scope requires a change order to the SOW. For consultants, understanding the SOW is essential: it defines what the client has paid for, what the team is accountable for, and where the boundaries of the engagement lie. Experienced clients always read the SOW carefully; inexperienced clients often discover its importance only when a dispute arises.

Text 1: The Structure of a Consulting Firm — How the Hierarchy Works and Why It Is Designed the Way It Is

The consulting firm hierarchy is designed around a fundamental economic principle: the firm creates value by deploying the analytical capabilities of junior consultants — who are relatively inexpensive to employ — against client problems, under the direction of senior consultants who have the experience and relationship capital to win and retain client work. This structure is often called the 'leverage model': partners generate revenue by leveraging the work of analysts and associates, allowing the firm to charge significantly more than the direct cost of the work performed. A partner who oversees three simultaneous engagements — each staffed with a team of five to eight consultants — generates substantial fee revenue while personally investing perhaps 30-40% of their time in direct client work.

At the entry level, Business Analysts (or Associate Consultants, depending on the firm) are the analytical workhorses of the engagement: they build models, conduct research, analyse data, and create the slides that will eventually be presented to the client. The Business Analyst role is explicitly an apprenticeship: new analysts are expected to develop rigorous analytical skills, learn the firm's problem-solving methodologies, and demonstrate the intellectual capability and professional judgment required for promotion. Most top-tier firms evaluate analysts over a two-to-three year period before making promotion decisions; analysts who progress become Consultants or Senior Consultants, with greater scope responsibility and client exposure.

The Engagement Manager (sometimes called Project Leader) is the role at which the consulting career trajectory changes most significantly. Below the EM level, success is primarily determined by the quality of individual analytical contribution. At and above the EM level, success is primarily determined by the ability to manage: to structure problems, to direct a team, to manage client relationships, to communicate recommendations persuasively to senior client stakeholders, and to anticipate and resolve the inevitable challenges that arise on complex engagements. Promotion to Partner is the most selective step in the consulting career: it requires not just exceptional track record of delivery but demonstrable ability to generate new business — because partners are expected to bring in revenue, not just serve existing clients.

Text 2: How a Consulting Engagement Is Organised — From Kick-Off to Final Presentation

A consulting engagement typically follows a predictable structure, even when the client problem and industry vary enormously. The engagement begins with a kick-off: a first meeting between the consulting team and the client team in which the problem is scoped, the approach is agreed, key stakeholders are identified, and the workplan is shared. The kick-off is one of the most important meetings in the engagement — it sets the tone for the client relationship, establishes working norms, and provides the team with direct access to the client data and context that will shape the subsequent analysis. A poorly run kick-off — one that fails to establish clear working relationships or to clarify the problem statement — can compromise the entire engagement.

The engagement then proceeds through a series of analytical workstreams, each producing interim outputs — typically in the form of slides or analytical documents — that are reviewed by the Engagement Manager and eventually by the partner before being shared with the client. Interim check-ins with the client — variously called 'steering committee meetings', 'progress updates', or 'working sessions' — occur at regular intervals to share findings, validate assumptions, and ensure the team's direction is aligned with the client's evolving priorities. These interim meetings are not just updates: they are opportunities to test hypotheses against the client's knowledge, to surface issues that the data cannot resolve, and to build the trust that makes the final recommendations credible.

The engagement concludes with a final presentation — typically a formal meeting with the most senior client stakeholders at which the team presents its findings and recommendations in a polished deck. The final presentation is the moment at which the engagement's value is most visible: a well-structured, clearly argued, evidence-based deck that addresses the client's central question and provides actionable recommendations demonstrates the full capability of the consulting team. But experienced consultants know that the final presentation should contain no surprises: by the time the final deck is presented, the key findings should have been shared, discussed, and broadly validated through the interim working sessions — ensuring that the recommendations are not rejected because they are unexpected, but rather endorsed because they are correct.

Grammar Point 1: Language for Describing Roles and Hierarchy Accurately
Using Precise Role Descriptions — Avoiding Vague Language

Consulting role descriptions require precision — vague language like 'does the analysis' or 'is senior' does not convey what a role actually involves. Precise role language specifies the responsibilities, the relationships, and the accountability of each level.

Vague: 'The Business Analyst does the analytical work.' → Precise: 'The Business Analyst conducts primary research, builds financial models, and creates the slides forming the client deliverable — under the direction of the Engagement Manager.'
Vague: 'The EM manages the project.' → Precise: 'The EM structures the problem, directs the team's analytical workstreams, maintains the client relationship at the working level, and ensures quality before partner review.'
Vague: 'The Partner is the most senior person.' → Precise: 'The Partner owns the client relationship, provides strategic oversight, presents final recommendations to C-suite stakeholders, and is responsible for originating new business.'
Vague: 'The SME helps with technical questions.' → Precise: 'The SME provides specialist domain expertise at specific points in the engagement — validating technical assumptions or regulatory analysis that the core team lacks the depth to assess independently.'

Describing career progression in consulting requires language that captures both what changes and what stays the same between levels. The key transition is from individual analytical contribution to management and business development.

Grammar Point 2: Vocabulary for Engagement Structure and Process
Using Engagement Vocabulary Accurately

Core consulting vocabulary — engagement, case, workstream, deliverable, deck — must be used accurately in the correct context. These terms are not interchangeable and signal professional fluency when used precisely.

Correct: 'We are on a three-month market entry engagement for a logistics client.' (engagement = formal client project)
Correct: 'The case has four workstreams — market sizing, competitive analysis, customer research, and go-to-market.' (case = informal term; workstream = component of the case)
Correct: 'The team's primary deliverable is a 50-slide recommendations deck for the C-suite presentation.' (deliverable = agreed output; deck = the PowerPoint document)
Incorrect and to avoid: 'We are working on a project for a client' — 'project' is not the preferred professional term; use 'engagement' in formal and client-facing contexts.
Describing Problem-Structuring Vocabulary

The vocabulary of consulting problem-structuring — hypothesis, framework, MECE, issue tree — has specific meanings that differ from everyday usage. Using these terms correctly signals analytical rigour.

'The team opened the engagement with a clear hypothesis: that the profitability decline was driven primarily by pricing rather than volume — a testable claim that structured the subsequent analysis.'
'The team used a three-branch issue tree to structure the problem — each branch MECE with the others and together covering the full space of strategic options.'
'Porter's Five Forces provided the analytical framework for the competitive landscape workstream — organising the assessment of supplier power, buyer power, substitution threat, new entrant risk, and competitive rivalry.'
'The team validated the MECE structure of their issue tree before beginning analysis — ensuring no question was counted twice and no important question was omitted.'
Process Language — Describing What Happens During an Engagement

Describing engagement process in English requires sequential and causal language — how events lead to each other, and what each stage produces.

Sequential: 'The engagement begins with a kick-off, proceeds through a series of analytical workstreams, and concludes with a final recommendations presentation to the C-suite.'
Causal: 'The interim working sessions allow the team to test hypotheses against the client's knowledge — ensuring the final recommendations address the actual business problem rather than the team's initial assumption.'
Conditional: 'If the client raises questions that fall outside the agreed scope, the EM must decide whether to absorb the work, raise a change order, or defer the question to a follow-on engagement.'
Contrast: 'While the first half of the engagement focuses on problem diagnosis — understanding what is driving the performance gap — the second half focuses on solution design and implementation planning.'

Grammar Point 3: Professional Register for Client-Facing and Internal Communication
Formal Language for Client Communication

Client-facing communication requires formal register that demonstrates professionalism, specificity, and respect for the client's time. The difference between informal and formal consulting language is not just tone — it is precision, structure, and completeness of information.

Informal: 'We need your financial data asap.' → Formal: 'To complete the profitability analysis, we will need the last three years of divisional P&L statements by Wednesday. Could your finance team share these by end of day? This will allow us to complete the model in time for the steering committee on Friday.'
Informal: 'That's not in scope.' → Formal: 'That question falls outside the scope defined in our Statement of Work. We could raise a change order to include it, or note it as a potential follow-on topic. Which approach would work better for you?'
Informal: 'We found something interesting.' → Formal: 'I'd like to share a preliminary finding and welcome your reaction: our initial analysis suggests that pricing — not volume — is the primary driver of the profability decline. Does this align with your understanding?'
Informal: 'Thanks everyone, talk soon.' → Formal: 'Thank you for the session. To summarise the agreed next steps: [list of actions with owners and dates]. Are there any other actions or clarifications before we close?'
Specific Language for Presenting Findings

Presenting analytical findings requires language that distinguishes confidence levels, links findings to evidence, and invites client reaction without undermining the team's credibility.

Strong finding: 'The analysis confirms that pricing is the primary driver — across all three product lines, a 5% price reduction in 2023 directly corresponds to the observed 8% margin decline.'
Preliminary finding: 'Our initial analysis suggests that pricing is the primary driver — we are still validating this against the full cost data, but it appears directional.'
Hypothesis to test: 'We are testing whether pricing or volume is the primary driver — the answer will determine whether the recommended solution is a pricing intervention or a volume growth programme.'
Inviting client reaction: 'Does this finding align with your understanding of the business? We want to make sure we're not missing context that would change the interpretation.'
Language for Managing Scope and Workplan Changes

When scope changes or workplan disruptions occur, the language used determines whether the client perceives the team as in control or struggling. Proactive, specific, solution-oriented language maintains client confidence.

'I want to flag that the competitive landscape workstream is tracking three days behind the workplan — due to delayed data access. We are exploring two alternative sources and will confirm the revised date by tomorrow.'
'The request you've raised is outside our current scope. We have three options: include it in the current engagement via a change order, defer it to a follow-on piece of work, or address it at a high level within the existing fee.'
'We've identified a dependency that wasn't visible at the start of the engagement: the go-to-market recommendations require the competitive analysis to be complete first. We are adjusting the workplan to sequence these correctly — this doesn't affect the final delivery date but does change the interim milestone.'
'To maintain the agreed delivery date despite the data delay, we are reallocating two analyst-days from the go-to-market workstream to the competitive analysis — which is the critical path item. We will flag if this creates risk to any other deliverable.'

Case Study 1: McKinsey & Company — The Firm That Defined Modern Strategy Consulting
How McKinsey developed the vocabulary, methodology, and professional norms of strategy consulting — and why its model became the template for the industry

Background

McKinsey & Company is widely regarded as the most influential strategy consulting firm in the world — not simply because of its size or client roster but because it developed and exported the vocabulary, methodology, and professional norms that have shaped the entire consulting industry. Founded by James O. McKinsey in 1926 as an accounting and management consulting firm, it was transformed under Marvin Bower — who led the firm from 1950 to 1967 — into the model of professional strategy consulting that most major firms still follow today. Bower's most consequential contribution was not a specific framework or methodology but a set of professional values: that consultants should think of themselves as members of a profession, not employees of a company; that they should tell clients what they need to hear, not what they want to hear; and that client impact matters more than client satisfaction.

The McKinsey Model: Structures, Vocabulary, and Methodology

Many of the terms most central to modern consulting vocabulary originated or were popularised at McKinsey. The 'hypothesis-driven' approach — stating an initial answer before conducting analysis and then testing it — is widely associated with McKinsey's problem-solving culture. MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) as a structuring principle was formalised and taught at McKinsey before becoming the industry standard. The 'pyramid principle' of communication — stating the conclusion first, then the supporting arguments, then the evidence — was developed by Barbara Minto while at McKinsey and published in The Minto Pyramid Principle (1987). The firm's emphasis on the 'deck' as the primary communication medium, structured so that each slide makes a single specific point stated in the headline, became the standard for consulting deliverables across the industry.

What McKinsey Teaches About Consulting Norms

Beyond specific methodologies, the McKinsey model established several professional norms that remain central to consulting practice. First, the 'up or out' system: consultants who do not progress to the next level within a defined period leave the firm — creating a culture of continuous development and high performance expectations. Second, the distinction between 'obligation to dissent' and 'obligation to commit': once a team has debated a recommendation and the partner has made a final decision, all team members are expected to commit to that recommendation even if they initially disagreed. Third, the emphasis on 'client impact' rather than 'client satisfaction' — the goal is not to make clients happy but to make them more successful, even when that requires delivering difficult findings. These norms travel with McKinsey alumni — who have become CEOs, ministers, and founders of other consulting firms — propagating the firm's professional culture far beyond its direct client engagements.

Case Study 2: The BCG Matrix and the Power of a Good Framework — How a Simple Tool Transformed Strategic Planning
How Bruce Henderson's two-by-two matrix became one of the most influential analytical frameworks in business history — and what it teaches about the role of frameworks in consulting

Background

The Boston Consulting Group (BCG) was founded by Bruce Henderson in Boston in 1963. BCG's most lasting contribution to management thinking was not its client work but a single analytical framework: the BCG Growth-Share Matrix, developed in 1970. The matrix categorises a company's business units or products into four quadrants based on two dimensions — relative market share and market growth rate — producing the now-famous labels: Stars (high share, high growth), Cash Cows (high share, low growth), Question Marks (low share, high growth), and Dogs (low share, low growth). The framework was initially developed to help large conglomerates allocate capital across their diverse business portfolios — but it became so influential that it reshaped how executives, investors, and analysts think about portfolio strategy.

Why the Matrix Worked

The BCG matrix succeeded not because it was analytically perfect — economists have raised significant objections to its underlying assumptions — but because it provided a structured, visual, and memorable way to organise complex portfolio decisions. Before the matrix, executives had no common language for describing why some business units deserved investment and others deserved harvesting. The matrix gave them that language: 'We need to milk the Cash Cows to fund the Stars and decide which Question Marks to back.' This is precisely what a good consulting framework does: it does not provide the answer but provides the structure within which the answer can be found and communicated. The framework made complex resource allocation decisions discussable — and that is what made it valuable.

What the BCG Matrix Teaches About Frameworks in Consulting

The BCG matrix offers a direct lesson about the role of frameworks in consulting practice. First, a framework is a lens, not an algorithm: it organises information in a useful way but does not replace the judgment required to make good decisions. A business unit that falls in the 'Dog' quadrant is not automatically a candidate for divestiture — context, capability, and strategic fit all matter. Second, the best frameworks are teachable and memorable: the BCG matrix can be explained in two minutes and remembered for a lifetime. Third, frameworks create common language: one of the most valuable things a consulting team does is give a client a shared vocabulary for discussing problems that were previously discussed inconsistently. The BCG matrix gave executives the word 'Cash Cow' — and that word changed how they thought about their portfolios.
