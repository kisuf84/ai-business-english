📚 Key Legal Terms
🌍
ESG
Environmental, Social, and Governance — a framework for analysing the non-financial performance of organisations that has become the dominant vocabulary through which institutional investors, regulators, and corporate leaders assess and communicate the sustainability and ethical dimensions of business. The Environmental dimension covers an organisation's relationship with the natural world: its carbon emissions and climate risk, its use of water and land, its management of waste and pollution, and its biodiversity impact. The Social dimension covers an organisation's relationship with people: its employees (pay, conditions, development, diversity), its supply chains (labour standards, human rights), its customers (data privacy, product safety, access), and its communities (economic inclusion, community investment). The Governance dimension covers how an organisation is led and controlled: board structure and diversity, executive pay, audit and risk management, anti-corruption policies, and transparency. ESG has been criticised for being too broad (encompassing everything), too narrow (excluding some important dimensions), and too susceptible to gaming (organisations report on the metrics that make them look best rather than those most material to their impact). Despite these limitations, it remains the principal shared vocabulary through which organisations communicate their non-financial performance to investors and other stakeholders.
🏢
Stakeholder Capitalism
A model of capitalism in which companies are understood to have responsibilities to all of their stakeholders — employees, customers, suppliers, communities, and the natural environment — rather than solely to their shareholders. Stakeholder capitalism is most associated with Klaus Schwab and the World Economic Forum, whose 'Davos Manifesto' (2020) articulated the principle that 'a company is more than an economic unit generating wealth; it fulfils human and societal aspirations as part of the broader social system.' The concept is the contemporary heir to stakeholder theory as originally developed by R. Edward Freeman in the 1980s. Stakeholder capitalism has been criticised from the right for being a cover for corporate leaders to pursue their own political and social preferences without shareholder accountability; and from the left for being a substitute for state regulation that allows corporations to make voluntary commitments without enforcement mechanisms. Despite these critiques, it has achieved mainstream adoption in the language of institutional investors, regulatory frameworks, and corporate leadership — particularly in the aftermath of the COVID-19 pandemic and the growing evidence of climate risk.
🎯
Corporate Purpose
The fundamental reason an organisation exists — beyond profit generation — that guides its strategic choices, defines the boundaries of its activities, and motivates its people. Corporate purpose has become a central concept in executive communication since the early 2010s, partly as a response to declining public trust in corporations and partly as a strategic recognition that purpose-driven organisations attract better talent, command greater customer loyalty, and are more resilient in times of crisis. A genuine corporate purpose: is specific enough to exclude as well as include (a purpose that is consistent with any activity is not a purpose), is connected to the organisation's actual business (not a philanthropic addendum to it), is capable of guiding real decisions (particularly decisions involving a trade-off between financial returns and purpose commitments), and is understood and owned by the people who implement it rather than being a communications artefact. The most common purpose failure: confusing purpose with mission (what we do) or vision (what we aspire to be), producing a purpose statement that is actually a description of the business's commercial activity dressed in the language of social aspiration.
📊
ESG Reporting
The structured disclosure of an organisation's environmental, social, and governance performance — using recognised frameworks and metrics that enable investors, regulators, and other stakeholders to assess and compare organisations' non-financial performance over time. ESG reporting has evolved from voluntary, discretionary communication to increasingly regulated, standardised disclosure: the European Union's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), effective from 2024, requires large EU companies to report against the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS); the International Financial Reporting Standards Foundation (IFRS) has published IFRS S1 and IFRS S2 sustainability disclosure standards; and the US Securities and Exchange Commission has proposed mandatory climate disclosure rules. The principal frameworks used in ESG reporting include: the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI — the most widely used global framework), the Sustainability Accounting Standards Board (SASB — sector-specific metrics for investor-relevance), the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD — climate risk specifically), and the International Integrated Reporting Council (IIRC — integrated value creation reporting). The most significant challenge in ESG reporting: the absence of a single universal standard has historically enabled significant variation in what is disclosed and how, reducing comparability and enabling selective reporting of favourable metrics.
🔍
Greenwashing
The practice of making misleading or unsubstantiated environmental claims — presenting an organisation, product, or activity as more environmentally friendly or sustainable than the evidence supports. Greenwashing takes many forms: the 'vague claim' (using terms like 'natural,' 'eco-friendly,' or 'sustainable' without evidence), the 'irrelevant claim' (highlighting a minor environmental benefit while obscuring major environmental harm), the 'hidden trade-off' (claiming environmental virtue in one dimension while causing damage in another), the 'false certification' (implying third-party endorsement that does not exist or is not relevant), and the 'distraction' (making a significant public commitment to a long-term goal while continuing damaging short-term practices without a credible transition plan). The term has expanded to cover analogous practices: 'purpose washing' (claiming social purpose that is not reflected in actual business practices), 'impact washing' (overstating the social or environmental impact of commercial activities), and 'social washing' (making human rights or labour practice claims that are not substantiated by supplier due diligence). Regulatory and investor scrutiny of greenwashing has increased significantly since 2020, with several high-profile enforcement actions against misleading sustainability marketing.
💚
Impact Investing
An investment approach that intentionally seeks to generate measurable social and environmental benefit alongside financial returns — distinct from ESG integration (which incorporates ESG factors into investment analysis to manage risk and identify opportunities without requiring a specific positive impact) and from socially responsible investing (which screens out harmful industries). Impact investing requires three components: intentionality (the investor's explicit objective is to generate social or environmental impact), additionality (the investment generates impact that would not have occurred without the investor's capital or engagement), and measurability (the impact is measured against specific, pre-defined indicators). The impact investing market has grown substantially since the Global Impact Investing Network (GIIN) began tracking it in 2010, from an estimated $25 billion to over $1 trillion in assets under management. The principal challenge in impact investing: demonstrating genuine additionality — that the social or environmental impact is attributable to the investment rather than to market forces, regulatory requirements, or other factors that would have produced the same outcome without the investor's involvement.
🌱
Sustainable Development Goals
The 17 Global Goals adopted by all United Nations Member States in 2015 as part of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development — providing a shared framework of objectives for sustainable development that covers poverty, health, education, gender equality, clean energy, climate, biodiversity, economic development, peace, and partnership. The SDGs have been widely adopted by corporations as an organising framework for their sustainability strategies and reporting: companies identify which SDGs their business activities most directly support, set targets aligned with those goals, and report on their progress. The SDG framework has been criticised for: being too broad (with 169 underlying targets, it is difficult to prioritise), enabling SDG-washing (companies cherry-pick the goals that align with their existing activities rather than addressing those where they have the greatest impact or face the greatest risk), and failing to require companies to account for their negative contributions to SDG outcomes alongside their positive ones. The most sophisticated use of the SDG framework in corporate reporting: acknowledging both positive contributions and negative impacts, and identifying the specific goals where the organisation has both the most significant impact and the most material business risk.
📋
Materiality Assessment
The structured process through which an organisation identifies which environmental, social, and governance topics are most significant — from the perspective of their impact on the organisation's financial performance (financial materiality) and from the perspective of the organisation's impact on people and the environment (impact materiality). Materiality assessment is the foundation of credible ESG reporting: it determines which topics the organisation discloses, which metrics it measures, and which management responses it prioritises. Single materiality (used in financial accounting and in some ESG frameworks) considers only what is material to the organisation's financial performance. Double materiality (required by the EU's European Sustainability Reporting Standards) considers both financial materiality and impact materiality — requiring organisations to assess and disclose the significance of their impacts on the world alongside the significance of sustainability risks and opportunities for their own performance. The process typically involves stakeholder engagement (consulting investors, employees, customers, suppliers, communities, and civil society about their priorities), sector analysis (assessing which ESG topics are most significant in the industry), and management review (determining how identified topics connect to strategy, risk, and performance).
🌿
Net Zero
A target state in which an organisation's greenhouse gas emissions are balanced by an equivalent removal of greenhouse gases from the atmosphere — either through the organisation's own activities (direct removals, such as reforestation) or through the purchase of high-quality carbon credits. Net zero is the most common emissions reduction commitment made by corporations, governments, and institutions, with over 2,000 major companies having made net zero commitments as of 2024. Critical distinctions in net zero language: net zero is not the same as carbon neutral (which typically involves offsetting current emissions without necessarily reducing them), not the same as carbon negative (which requires removing more carbon than is emitted), and not the same as 'net zero emissions by [year]' if the claimed reductions rely primarily on carbon offsets rather than actual emissions reductions. The principal criticism of corporate net zero commitments: many rely on low-quality carbon offsets, involve very long timelines (2050 or beyond) without near-term reduction targets, and count reductions in scope 1 and 2 emissions while excluding scope 3 emissions (which typically represent the majority of a company's carbon impact in its supply chain and customer use of products).
⚖️
Social Licence to Operate
The ongoing acceptance or approval by local communities and other stakeholders of an organisation's activities — particularly relevant for industries with significant local environmental and social impacts, such as mining, energy, infrastructure, agriculture, and financial services. The social licence to operate is distinct from the legal licence (the regulatory permissions and permits required to operate) — an organisation can hold all required legal permissions and yet lose the confidence of the communities it affects to a degree that makes continued operation politically or practically impossible. The concept originated in the extractive industries but has broadened to encompass any organisation whose activities significantly affect communities or whose public trust is central to its commercial model. The social licence to operate is won through: genuine engagement with affected communities (not consultation designed to legitimate a decision already made), transparent communication about impacts and trade-offs, equitable distribution of the benefits of the activity, and demonstrated accountability when commitments are not met. In Latin American contexts, the social licence concept is particularly significant in relation to indigenous communities' rights of Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) for activities that affect their territories.
🏦
Responsible Investment
Investment approaches that incorporate environmental, social, and governance considerations into investment decision-making — encompassing a spectrum from negative screening (excluding companies or sectors that do not meet minimum standards) through ESG integration (systematically incorporating ESG data into financial analysis) to thematic investing (focusing on specific sustainability themes such as clean energy or sustainable agriculture) and impact investing (requiring measurable social or environmental benefit alongside financial returns). The responsible investment market has grown from a niche approach to mainstream practice: the UN-supported Principles for Responsible Investment (PRI) now has over 5,000 signatories representing over $100 trillion in assets under management. The most significant debate in responsible investment: whether ESG integration improves or reduces financial returns. Research findings are mixed, but the growing regulatory requirement for ESG disclosure and the increasing evidence of climate-related financial risk has driven institutional investors toward ESG integration as a risk management practice regardless of its return implications.
🤝
Purpose Washing
The practice of articulating a corporate purpose that is not reflected in the organisation's actual strategic choices, resource allocation, or behaviour — using the language of purpose to enhance reputation and attract stakeholders without making the substantive changes that a genuine purpose commitment would require. Purpose washing is the purpose equivalent of greenwashing: it involves the adoption of purpose language as a communications strategy rather than as a genuine strategic framework. Signs of purpose washing: the purpose statement was developed by the communications team rather than through genuine leadership deliberation about what the organisation fundamentally exists to do; the purpose is consistent with every strategic choice the organisation makes, including those that damage the people it claims to serve; the organisation's resource allocation, executive incentives, and governance processes are not aligned with the stated purpose; and the purpose is not used to guide decisions when it conflicts with short-term financial objectives. The test of a genuine purpose: would the organisation turn down profitable business that contradicts its purpose? Are there documented cases where it has done so?
🌐
Supply Chain Responsibility
The governance, standards, and accountability mechanisms through which an organisation ensures that its environmental, social, and governance commitments extend beyond its own operations to the suppliers, sub-contractors, and other entities in its value chain. Supply chain responsibility has become a central dimension of corporate responsibility as research and journalism have increasingly revealed the disconnect between the social and environmental claims of finished-goods companies and the conditions in their supply chains. Effective supply chain responsibility requires: a comprehensive mapping of the supply chain to understand who is producing what and where; the development and communication of supplier codes of conduct that specify minimum standards; supplier assessment and audit programmes to verify compliance with those standards; remediation processes for suppliers who do not meet standards; and the willingness to exit relationships with suppliers who persistently fail standards. The limitations of supply chain responsibility programmes: most companies can effectively monitor only their tier-one suppliers (those with whom they have a direct commercial relationship) and have limited visibility into their tier-two and beyond suppliers where the most significant risks often reside.
📈
Integrated Reporting
A reporting approach that brings together an organisation's financial and non-financial performance in a single, connected narrative — showing how the organisation creates, preserves, or erodes value across multiple capitals (financial, manufactured, human, social and relationship, intellectual, and natural) over the short, medium, and long term. Integrated reporting is based on the International Integrated Reporting Framework developed by the International Integrated Reporting Council (IIRC), which has since merged with the SASB Foundation to form the Value Reporting Foundation, which is itself now part of the IFRS Foundation. Integrated reporting is most widely practised in South Africa, where it has been mandatory for listed companies since 2010 under the King IV Corporate Governance Code. The central insight of integrated reporting: value creation for shareholders is not separable from value creation for other stakeholders — an organisation that damages its human capital, erodes its social licence, or depletes its natural capital is destroying the foundations of its long-term financial performance, even if current financial statements do not capture these costs.
🔄
Circular Economy
An economic model that designs out waste and pollution, keeps products and materials in use at their highest value for as long as possible, and regenerates natural systems — in contrast to the linear 'take-make-dispose' economic model that takes raw materials, makes products, and disposes of them at end of life. The circular economy concept is most associated with the Ellen MacArthur Foundation, which has been the principal advocacy and research organisation for circular economy adoption since 2010. In corporate sustainability language, circular economy principles translate into: product design for durability, repairability, and end-of-life recyclability; business model innovation that shifts from selling products to selling services (thus retaining ownership and responsibility for the product throughout its life); material efficiency programmes that reduce waste in production; and take-back and recycling programmes that keep materials in use beyond the first product lifecycle. The gap between circular economy aspiration and practice: many companies use circular economy language in their communications while making only incremental improvements to the linearity of their business model — a form of purpose washing applied to circular claims.
🌏
Double Materiality
A principle of sustainability reporting that requires organisations to assess and disclose two types of materiality: financial materiality (how sustainability risks and opportunities affect the organisation's own financial performance) and impact materiality (how the organisation's activities affect people, communities, and the natural environment). Double materiality is the principle embedded in the EU's European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) under the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and represents a more demanding standard than single materiality (used in many US-oriented reporting frameworks, which focus primarily on what is material to investors). The distinction matters significantly: a company applying single materiality discloses only the sustainability topics that affect its own financial performance; a company applying double materiality also discloses its impacts on the world, even where those impacts do not currently translate into financial risks or opportunities. Double materiality reflects a theory of corporate responsibility that is broader than investor-focused accountability — it is based on the principle that stakeholders beyond investors have a legitimate interest in understanding how a company affects the world.
🌳
Carbon Footprint
The total amount of greenhouse gas emissions generated by an organisation's activities, measured in tonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e) — typically categorised across three scopes: scope 1 (direct emissions from sources owned or controlled by the organisation, such as fuel burned in company vehicles or manufacturing processes), scope 2 (indirect emissions from purchased electricity, heat, or steam), and scope 3 (all other indirect emissions in the organisation's value chain, including emissions from suppliers, employee commuting, business travel, the use of sold products, and the disposal of sold products at end of life). Scope 3 emissions typically represent between 70% and 90% of a company's total carbon footprint but are the most difficult to measure and the least consistently reported. The limitations of the carbon footprint concept as a corporate sustainability metric: it can create incentives to outsource emission-intensive activities rather than reduce them (shifting scope 1 emissions to scope 3 without reducing total atmospheric carbon), and it may distract from equally important sustainability dimensions such as biodiversity, water, and social impacts.
🏷️
ESG Rating
An assessment of an organisation's environmental, social, and governance performance, typically produced by a specialist ESG rating agency — used by investors to screen, compare, and monitor companies' non-financial performance. The major ESG rating agencies include MSCI ESG Research, Sustainalytics (now owned by Morningstar), S&P Global ESG Scores, and ISS ESG. ESG ratings have been widely criticised for their low inter-rater agreement: studies have found correlations between ESG ratings from different agencies as low as 0.3-0.6, meaning that an organisation rated highly by one agency may be rated poorly by another using different methodologies and weightings. This low agreement reflects genuine methodological differences (different agencies assess different topics, use different data sources, and apply different weightings) rather than simple measurement error. The implication for corporate sustainability directors: a high ESG rating is not necessarily evidence of genuine sustainability performance, and a low rating is not necessarily evidence of poor performance — what matters is understanding the methodology of the specific rating and whether it captures the dimensions most material to the organisation's actual impact.
💰
Social Return on Investment
A framework for measuring and communicating the social, economic, and environmental value created by an organisation's activities — expressing that value as a ratio of the benefit generated to the investment required (e.g., 'for every €1 invested in our financial inclusion programme, we generate €3.50 in social value'). SROI is most commonly used by social enterprises, charities, and impact investors to demonstrate the value of activities that do not generate financial returns but create significant social or environmental benefit. The SROI methodology involves: identifying all stakeholders affected by the activity, quantifying changes in their lives or circumstances resulting from the activity, assigning financial proxies to those changes (using market prices or other proxies where direct financial values do not exist), and calculating the ratio of total social value to the investment required to generate it. The principal criticism of SROI: the assignment of financial proxies to social and environmental outcomes is inherently subjective and can be manipulated to produce a desired result — a high SROI ratio may reflect genuine impact or may reflect the choice of generous financial proxies.
🏛️
Corporate Governance
The systems, processes, and structures through which an organisation is directed, controlled, and held accountable — encompassing the board's role in setting strategy and overseeing risk, the executive's role in managing operations and performance, the audit and risk management frameworks that ensure financial and legal compliance, the executive remuneration policies that align management incentives with organisational objectives, and the transparency and accountability mechanisms that enable shareholders and other stakeholders to assess the organisation's performance. In the ESG context, governance is the 'G' that is often treated as the foundation for credible 'E' and 'S' performance: an organisation whose governance structures are weak, whose board lacks independence, or whose executive incentives are misaligned with stated sustainability commitments will be unlikely to deliver credible environmental and social performance regardless of what its communications state. Key governance dimensions in contemporary ESG analysis: board diversity (gender, ethnicity, skills, and independence), executive remuneration linked to sustainability targets, audit committee oversight of ESG reporting, and the integration of climate and sustainability risk into enterprise risk management.
🌾
Just Transition
The principle that the shift toward a sustainable, low-carbon economy should be managed in a way that is fair to workers, communities, and countries that are currently dependent on carbon-intensive industries — ensuring that the economic costs of the transition are not borne disproportionately by the most vulnerable. The just transition concept originated in the labour movement and has been incorporated into international climate policy through the Paris Agreement and subsequent COP negotiations. In corporate sustainability language, the just transition has specific implications: companies with significant carbon exposure (fossil fuels, steel, cement, aviation, shipping) are expected to develop plans not only for reducing their emissions but for managing the impact on their workers, suppliers, and communities during the transition; and companies in all sectors are expected to consider the social dimensions of their decarbonisation strategies rather than pursuing emissions reductions without regard for their employment and community impacts. In Latin American contexts, the just transition is particularly relevant given the region's dependence on extractive industries and the significant environmental and social justice dimensions of the energy transition.

Text 1: The Language of Corporate Responsibility — How the Words Organisations Use Reveal Their Theory of What a Business Is For

The language through which organisations communicate their social and environmental responsibilities has changed more in the past decade than in the previous five. The vocabulary of corporate responsibility has moved from the peripheral ('corporate philanthropy,' 'community investment,' 'corporate citizenship') to the strategic ('ESG integration,' 'sustainable business model,' 'stakeholder value creation') — a linguistic shift that reflects a genuine change in how the most sophisticated investors, regulators, and business leaders understand the relationship between commercial success and social and environmental impact. This shift has not been uniform: organisations continue to use the full spectrum of corporate responsibility language, from the most anachronistic to the most contemporary, and the language an organisation uses is one of the most reliable signals of where it actually sits on the spectrum from cosmetic compliance to genuine integration.

The most consequential conceptual shift in corporate responsibility language has been the movement from 'risk management' to 'value creation.' The earlier model treated corporate responsibility primarily as a risk management discipline: companies managed their environmental and social impacts to avoid the regulatory, reputational, and legal risks that poor performance in these dimensions would generate. This model produced a compliance-oriented approach to responsibility — do enough to avoid the worst consequences, but do not treat social and environmental performance as a source of competitive advantage or a driver of strategic choice. The contemporary model — reflected in the language of 'shared value,' 'purpose-led growth,' and 'ESG as alpha' — treats social and environmental performance as a source of long-term value creation: the organisation that genuinely serves its employees, customers, communities, and the natural environment builds the trust, the talent, the customer loyalty, and the operational resilience that are the foundations of durable commercial success.

The most dangerous development in corporate responsibility language is not the cynicism of greenwashing — which is at least transparent in its intent — but the proliferation of aspirational language that is sincere but ungrounded. Organisations increasingly communicate genuine commitments to purposes, values, and sustainability goals that their actual strategies, governance structures, and resource allocations do not support. The chief sustainability officer writes about the company's commitment to net zero while the board approves capital allocation to carbon-intensive assets with 30-year payback horizons. The CEO speaks about stakeholder capitalism while the executive incentive structure remains tied exclusively to short-term earnings per share. The communications team publishes a purpose statement while the sales team operates under targets that reward practices the purpose explicitly prohibits. This gap between aspirational language and operational reality is not greenwashing in the traditional sense — the communicators genuinely believe in the aspirations they are articulating. It is something more insidious: the substitution of language for commitment.

Text 2: The Measurement Problem — Why 'What Gets Measured Gets Managed' Is Not Enough for Corporate Social Responsibility

The management aphorism that 'what gets measured gets managed' has become the animating principle of contemporary ESG practice: if organisations can be required to measure and disclose their environmental and social impacts, market forces and regulatory pressure will ensure that the most significant impacts are managed. This logic has driven an enormous expansion in corporate environmental and social measurement and reporting — producing annual sustainability reports, ESG ratings, impact assessments, and stakeholder surveys that document, in increasing detail, what organisations do and with what apparent consequences. The challenge is that the aphorism contains an embedded assumption — that what can be measured is a reliable proxy for what matters — that does not always hold in the domain of social and environmental impact.

The most significant measurement gap in current ESG practice is scope 3 emissions: the greenhouse gas emissions that occur in an organisation's supply chain and in the use and disposal of its products, which typically represent between 70% and 90% of a company's total carbon impact but are the least consistently measured and disclosed. This is not primarily a technical problem — the methodologies for estimating scope 3 emissions exist and are increasingly used. It is a strategic and commercial problem: many companies' scope 3 emissions are so large, so embedded in their business model, and so difficult to reduce without fundamentally changing what they sell or how they source it that genuine measurement and disclosure would require acknowledgement of a carbon exposure that their current business model cannot sustainably address. The gap between disclosed scope 1 and 2 performance and undisclosed scope 3 reality is one of the most significant sources of greenwashing risk in current corporate communications.

The deeper measurement problem is not technical but conceptual: the dominant ESG measurement frameworks measure what organisations do, not what changes as a result of what they do. An organisation can report on the number of hours of employee training delivered, the percentage of suppliers audited, the volume of waste recycled, and the percentage of women in management — all without this data revealing whether employees are more capable, suppliers are more ethical, waste generation has declined, or women have greater organisational power than before. Outputs are easy to measure; outcomes and impact are genuinely difficult. The organisations that are most sophisticated in their approach to social and environmental responsibility are those that have moved beyond output reporting to genuine outcome assessment — asking not 'what did we do?' but 'what changed, and what is our contribution to that change?'

Grammar Point 1: ESG Reporting and Purpose Communication Language
Purpose Language — Specificity as the Test of Genuineness

A corporate purpose is genuine when it is specific enough to exclude as well as include, when it can be used to guide a decision that involves a trade-off, and when it is grounded in the organisation's actual experience of difficult choices. A purpose statement that is consistent with any strategic choice is a description, not a purpose.

Test question for purpose specificity: 'Is there business we would turn down because it contradicts this purpose?' — a purpose that requires no sacrifice is a description of commercial activity dressed in purpose language.
Test question for purpose operationalisation: 'Does every person in this organisation know what this purpose means for their specific role?' — a purpose that is understood only at board level is not guiding the organisation.
Test question for purpose grounding: 'Can we identify a decision in the past twelve months where financial return and purpose pointed in different directions, and we chose purpose?' — a purpose untested by difficult decisions may not have been genuinely adopted.
Genuine purpose framing: 'Our purpose guides us to turn down profitable business that contradicts it — here is a specific example from the past year.' Weak purpose framing: 'Our purpose is central to our strategy and our culture and guides everything we do.'
ESG Reporting Language — Evidence Before Aspiration

Credible ESG reporting presents specific, evidenced performance first — leading with what can be demonstrated — and positions aspirations as the context for that performance rather than as the headline. Aspirational claims without evidence are greenwashing even when the aspirations are genuine.

Weak (aspiration first): 'We are committed to being a leader in financial inclusion and responsible lending — our performance demonstrates this commitment.' — What does 'leader' mean? What evidence demonstrates the commitment?
Strong (evidence first): 'In 2023, 82% of our new customers had no prior access to formal credit. Our average interest rate for this population was 38% — compared with an estimated 120-180% from informal alternatives. Our credit loss rate in this segment was 2.1% — within 0.4 percentage points of our prime customer population.' — The commitment follows from the evidence rather than preceding it.
Scope framing — what is and is not covered: 'Our 2040 net zero target covers scope 1 and 2 emissions, which represent 15% of our total carbon footprint. Our scope 3 emissions — the remaining 85% — are estimated at 680,000 tonnes; we are developing a reduction pathway and will include scope 3 in an updated climate commitment by the end of 2025.'
Gap acknowledgement: 'Our supply chain responsibility programme currently covers tier-one suppliers — approximately 35% of our supply chain by value. Tier-two visibility is our primary supply chain development priority for 2024-2026, and we will report progress annually.'
Greenwashing Risk Language — The Three-Part Test

The three-part test for greenwashing risk: (1) Is the claim specific and verifiable — or vague and unverifiable? (2) Does the claim cover the full scope of the relevant impact — or does it highlight a minor positive while ignoring a major negative? (3) Is the claim supported by a credible plan — or is a long-term aspiration used to distract from current harmful practice?

Vague claim (high risk): 'We are committed to environmental responsibility and sustainable practices.' — What specifically? How measured? What evidence?
Specific, verifiable claim (lower risk): 'We have reduced our scope 1 and 2 emissions by 32% since 2018, verified by [third party], against a science-based target of 46% reduction by 2030.' — Specific, time-bounded, verified, contextualised.
Hidden trade-off (high risk): 'Our logistics operations are carbon neutral.' — Are scope 3 emissions from the products transported included? Is 'carbon neutral' achieved through offsets or actual reductions?
Complete scope framing (lower risk): 'Our direct logistics operations (scope 1 and 2) are carbon neutral, achieved through a combination of fleet electrification (60%) and high-quality offsets (40%). Scope 3 emissions from the goods we transport are not yet included in this commitment; our approach to those emissions is described in section [X].'

Grammar Point 2: ESG Stakeholder Communication Language
Investor Communication on ESG — Distinguishing What You Can Demonstrate From What You Cannot

The most credible ESG investor communication explicitly distinguishes between what the organisation can demonstrate with reliable evidence and what it aspires to but cannot yet measure. This distinction builds investor trust precisely because it demonstrates that the organisation applies the same standards of evidence to its ESG claims that it applies to its financial claims.

Can demonstrate: 'We can show with high confidence that 82% of our new customers had no access to formal credit before Meridian, using data from 1.4 million customer applications and credit bureau cross-referencing.'
Aspires but cannot yet demonstrate: 'We believe our credit provision improves customers' long-term financial resilience, but we do not yet have the longitudinal data or the rigorous counterfactual methodology required to demonstrate this. We are developing both; our first longitudinal customer cohort analysis will be available in 2025.'
Gap acknowledgement with commitment: 'Our scope 3 emissions estimate carries a ±40% uncertainty range. We are disclosing it because the order-of-magnitude matters to your assessment even where precision is not yet available. Our plan to improve precision — including borrower emissions data collection — is attached as an appendix.'
The investor trust principle: an organisation that discloses what it cannot yet demonstrate, alongside what it can, will be trusted more than one that discloses only the positives — because the former demonstrates that its evidence standards apply to its limitations as well as its achievements.
Social Licence Communication — Genuine Engagement Language Versus Consultation Language

The language of genuine community engagement and the language of compliance-oriented community consultation appear similar but have different underlying logics. Genuine engagement language acknowledges that the outcome of the engagement may change the decision; consultation language implies the decision is already made and consultation is required to legitimate it.

Consultation language (compliance-oriented): 'We are conducting community consultation as part of the regulatory approval process for our new facility. We are committed to addressing all concerns raised through this process.' — The facility exists; the consultation manages the approval process.
Genuine engagement language: 'We are in dialogue with the three communities adjacent to the proposed facility site before we submit a planning application — because we want to understand their specific concerns and determine whether the facility design, operational commitments, or community benefit package can address those concerns adequately. If they cannot, we may need to revisit the facility design or location.' — The outcome of the engagement is genuinely open.
FPIC language for indigenous communities: 'Our activities in this region are subject to the Free, Prior, and Informed Consent of the [community name], whose territory the project affects. We have not yet received consent, and we will not proceed without it. The consent process is being conducted on the community's timeline, not ours.'
Social licence accountability: 'In our 2019 project, we conducted community consultation but did not adequately address the groundwater concerns raised by [community name]. The project was withdrawn following organised opposition. In our current proposal, we have designed the water management system in partnership with [community name] from the outset — so that the concerns raised in 2019 are addressed in the design, not in the consultation.'
Supply Chain Communication — What You Know and What You Don't

Credible supply chain responsibility communication explicitly describes the scope of the organisation's visibility into its supply chain and the limits of that visibility — rather than making comprehensive claims about 'our supply chain' that imply uniform standards across a value chain the organisation cannot in fact monitor.

Overstated scope (high risk): 'Our supply chain meets our Ethical Trading Standards, which require compliance with ILO labour standards.' — Does 'our supply chain' include tier-two and beyond? Have all tier-one suppliers been audited? What happened to non-compliant suppliers?
Accurate scope description: 'Our Ethical Trading Standards apply to all 340 tier-one suppliers (those with whom we have direct commercial relationships). In 2023, we audited 80% by value — the 20% not audited are in low-risk categories. Of audited suppliers, 23 received remediation notices; 21 have completed remediation; 2 relationships are under review.'
Acknowledging tier-two limitation: 'We do not currently have reliable visibility into our approximately 2,800 tier-two suppliers. This is the most significant gap in our supply chain responsibility programme, and it is where the most material labour and environmental risks in our sector are concentrated. Our three-year supply chain visibility programme — launching in 2024 — is designed to give us verified data on our 200 highest-risk tier-two suppliers by 2026.'
The visibility gap principle: a company that acknowledges it cannot monitor its tier-two supply chain will be trusted more than one that claims comprehensive responsibility for a supply chain it cannot see — because the former is honest about the limits of its accountability, while the latter is making claims it cannot substantiate.

Grammar Point 3: Advanced ESG Strategy Language
Impact Measurement Language — Outputs, Outcomes, and Attribution

The most sophisticated impact communication distinguishes between outputs (what the organisation does), outcomes (what changes for affected people or the environment), and impact (what change is attributable to the organisation's activities rather than to other factors). Output reporting is the minimum; outcome and impact measurement is what a genuine accountability for social performance requires.

Output reporting (minimum standard): 'We provided credit to 1.4 million customers in 2023, disbursing €850 million in loans.' — Counts what the organisation did, not what changed.
Outcome reporting (intermediate standard): 'Of the 1.4 million customers served, 82% report that access to Meridian credit enabled them to invest in their business or household that would not otherwise have been possible. Average household income in the 24 months following first credit access was 34% higher than in the 24 months before.' — Describes what changed in customers' lives.
Impact reporting (highest standard): 'We estimate that between 40-60% of the income improvement in the 24 months following credit access is attributable to Meridian's credit provision — after accounting for counterfactual income growth, other financial services accessed in the same period, and macroeconomic improvement in the relevant markets. Our estimation methodology is available in our impact annex.' — Attempts to attribute the outcome specifically to the organisation's activities.
Attribution honesty: 'We cannot yet determine what proportion of the observed income improvement is attributable to our credit provision versus other factors. The 34% income improvement is an outcome; our contribution to that outcome is the impact question we are working to answer with our 2024-2026 longitudinal customer research programme.'
Circular Economy Language — Business Model Versus Communications Claim

The circular economy language used in an organisation's communications should accurately reflect the extent to which circular economy principles have been embedded in the organisation's business model — not the extent to which the communications team has found circular economy language applicable to existing activities.

Communications claim without business model change (purpose washing): 'We are committed to the circular economy — our products are designed to be repaired and our packaging is recyclable.' — Is durability designed in from the outset? What percentage of packaging is actually recycled? Does the business model create incentives for customers to extend product life?
Genuine circular model description: 'In 2023, we transitioned our enterprise product line from a sales model to a leasing model — we retain ownership and responsibility for the product throughout its life. Customer contracts include maintenance and end-of-life return. We have reduced the volume of new materials required to service our customer base by 23% since 2021 as a result.' — The business model has changed; the circular claim reflects the change.
Honest transition description: 'Our consumer product line remains a sales model — we do not currently have the take-back infrastructure or business model to support a full transition to a circular model in this segment. Our three-year programme for the consumer segment includes the development of a modular product design that enables component replacement rather than whole-product replacement, and a trial take-back programme in our three largest markets from 2025.'
The circular economy test: 'In a linear model, we benefit when customers buy new products. In a circular model, we benefit when customers retain and maximise value from existing products. Until our revenue model reflects the latter, circular economy is an aspiration rather than a business model description.'
Just Transition Language — Specificity and Accountability

Just transition commitments are credible when they are specific about what will and will not happen to the workers, suppliers, and communities affected by decarbonisation — not when they express a general aspiration for fairness in the energy transition. Specificity enables accountability; generality does not.

Generic just transition language (low accountability): 'We are committed to a just and equitable transition to a low-carbon economy, ensuring that no one is left behind.' — What specifically will not happen? To whom? By when? Verified by whom?
Specific just transition commitment (higher accountability): 'Our decarbonisation plan will not result in involuntary redundancies for any of our 2,300 employees whose roles are directly affected by the transition to renewable energy operations. We will provide retraining and redeployment support to all affected employees, beginning 18 months before the role closure date. We will report annually on the number of affected employees, the retraining programmes accessed, and the redeployment outcomes.'
Supply chain just transition: 'We will not decarbonise our supply chain by switching to lower-carbon suppliers in different geographies. We will work with our existing 23 highest-emission suppliers on emissions reduction, providing technical assistance and access to our preferential finance terms for capital investment in emissions reduction technology. We will report on each supplier's progress annually.'
Community just transition: 'We will not accelerate our exit from the coal supply business in [region] until: (a) we have published a plan for alternative economic activity in the affected communities, developed with community representatives; and (b) we have secured commitments from the relevant government agencies for the infrastructure investment required to support that alternative activity. We expect this process to take 2-3 years; we will not accelerate it for commercial convenience.'

Case Study 1: The Purpose of a Corporation — The Intellectual History of the Debate and Its Implications for How Executives Should Think About Corporate Responsibility
How the debate about what corporations are for — from Friedman's shareholder primacy through Freeman's stakeholder theory to the contemporary stakeholder capitalism movement — provides the intellectual foundation for the language executives use to describe their responsibilities

Background

The dominant intellectual framework for corporate responsibility in the second half of the twentieth century was articulated by Milton Friedman in a 1970 New York Times essay: 'The Social Responsibility of Business is to Increase its Profits.' Friedman's argument was specific: in a free society, business executives are the agents of shareholders and have no mandate to pursue social objectives using shareholders' money — that is, to tax shareholders for social purposes that shareholders have not authorised. Friedman acknowledged that executives might voluntarily act in ways that serve social purposes where doing so also serves shareholders' long-term interests, but he was clear that the social purpose is instrumental (it serves the financial purpose) rather than fundamental (it is the purpose). This framework — shareholder primacy — dominated corporate governance and business ethics teaching for two decades.

The Stakeholder Counter-Revolution

The counter-argument was most influentially articulated by R. Edward Freeman in 'Strategic Management: A Stakeholder Approach' (1984), which proposed that the purpose of a corporation was to create value for all of its stakeholders — not only shareholders but employees, customers, suppliers, communities, and the natural environment. Freeman's stakeholder theory was not primarily an ethical argument — it was a strategic one: organisations that failed to manage their stakeholder relationships effectively would eventually fail, as the trust, capability, and operational licence they depended on eroded. The Business Roundtable's 2019 'Statement on the Purpose of a Corporation' — signed by 181 CEOs of major American corporations, replacing the 1997 statement that had explicitly endorsed shareholder primacy — marked the mainstream adoption of stakeholder language. Whether the statement represented a genuine change in corporate behaviour or primarily a change in corporate language has been the subject of significant subsequent debate and research.

The Contemporary Challenge

The contemporary challenge for executives is neither to choose between Friedman and Freeman — the debate has moved on — nor to simply adopt the language of stakeholder capitalism as a communications strategy. The more demanding task is to develop a genuine theory of how the organisation creates value for multiple stakeholders in a way that is both commercially sustainable and genuinely beneficial. The organisations that are most credible in this space share a specific characteristic: they can identify decisions they have made — and declined to make — on the basis of their stated stakeholder commitments, at a commercial cost. A corporation that claims stakeholder capitalism but has no documented decisions where stakeholder considerations overrode commercial logic is using stakeholder language instrumentally. A corporation that can point to specific choices — declining profitable lines of business, absorbing costs to protect employee welfare, maintaining environmental standards when it would have been cheaper not to — has a basis for claiming that its stakeholder language is genuine.

Case Study 2: Corporate Responsibility in Latin American Business — The Specific Cultural, Regulatory, and Social Dimensions That Shape How Responsibility Is Understood and Practised in the Region
How Latin American business culture, regulatory environments, and social contexts create specific dynamics around corporate responsibility that differ from the Northern European and North American contexts in which most global CSR frameworks are developed

Background

Corporate social responsibility in Latin American business is shaped by a set of contextual factors that differ significantly from the Northern European and North American environments in which the dominant global CSR frameworks — GRI, SASB, TCFD, and the UN SDGs — were developed. Three of these factors are particularly influential. First: the significant inequality in Latin American societies, which creates a specific responsibility context — companies operating in highly unequal societies face different expectations and have different impacts than those operating in more equal environments. Second: the extractive industry heritage, which means that the social licence to operate and the relationship between business and affected communities have a more explicit and sometimes more contentious character in Latin American business than in other regions. Third: the civil society and indigenous rights movements, which have established specific frameworks — particularly Free, Prior, and Informed Consent for activities affecting indigenous communities — that create legal and normative obligations for companies beyond those required in most other jurisdictions.

The Inequality Dimension

The combination of high inequality and large informal economies in Latin American countries creates a specific CSR context for financial services, consumer goods, and infrastructure companies. For financial services companies in particular, the distinction between responsible business practice and responsible business strategy collapses: serving financially excluded populations is simultaneously a business opportunity, a social responsibility, and (in many cases) a regulatory expectation. The question of pricing — whether interest rates, product prices, and service fees charged to lower-income populations are consistent with the organisation's stated purpose and its stakeholder commitments — has a specific sharpness in high-inequality contexts that it does not have in more equal societies. Companies that serve lower-income populations in Latin America face the 'dignity test' more immediately than companies in similar businesses in other markets: are the terms on which we serve this population consistent with the financial dignity we claim to be creating?

The Social Licence and Indigenous Rights Dimension

The social licence to operate has particular salience in Latin America for two reasons. First, the extractive industry has historically had an exploitative relationship with local communities in much of the region, creating a context of justified scepticism about corporate community engagement. Second, the legal and normative frameworks for indigenous rights are relatively strong in Latin American jurisdictions — the ILO Convention 169 (ratified by 15 Latin American countries) and the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples create specific obligations around Free, Prior, and Informed Consent for activities affecting indigenous territories. Companies that navigate these obligations poorly — through consultation designed to legitimate decisions already made rather than genuine engagement — face significant legal and reputational risk. The most effective approach: genuine co-design of the activity with affected communities from the outset, rather than consultation at the regulatory minimum.
