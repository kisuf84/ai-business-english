Module Overview

Welcome to Module 2: Interviewing Techniques and Vocabulary. The interview is the most consequential conversation in the recruitment process — and the one most susceptible to poor design, inconsistent execution, and unconscious bias. For HR professionals operating in English, the interview context requires a precise and specialised vocabulary: the language for designing structured questions, introducing a scoring framework, briefing an interview panel, managing a candidate's experience, redirecting an answer that is going off-track, probing for evidence, and communicating an outcome — both an offer and a rejection — with care and professionalism.

This module covers the complete language of interviewing from the HR practitioner's perspective — from competency frameworks and behavioural question design to the STAR method, interview scoring, bias awareness, and the verbal and written language of candidate communication. You will practise designing interview questions for specific competencies, evaluating candidate responses, and handling the challenging conversational moments that every interviewer eventually faces.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Explain the key principles of structured, competency-based interviewing and describe why structured interviews produce more reliable and legally defensible selection decisions than unstructured ones.
2
Design a set of behavioural and situational interview questions for a specific competency — using the STAR framework as the basis for question structure and response evaluation.
3
Use the specialist vocabulary of interviewing and candidate assessment — including competency framework, behavioural indicator, scoring rubric, panel interview, probing question, and interview debrief — accurately and fluently.
4
Form and use correct question structures in English for professional interviewing — including open questions, probing questions, hypothetical questions, and clarifying questions — while avoiding leading and discriminatory questions.
5
Apply reported speech accurately when writing interview notes, documenting candidate responses, completing scoring records, and summarising panel debrief discussions.
6
Identify and name the most common cognitive biases in interviewing — including halo effect, affinity bias, confirmation bias, and recency bias — and describe specific strategies for mitigating their impact on selection decisions.
7
Write a structured set of interview questions with accompanying scoring criteria for a professional role — and a professionally worded offer letter and rejection communication for candidates.
8
Listen to and understand structured interview conversations, panel debrief discussions, bias awareness training sessions, and candidate feedback calls in authentic HR contexts.

📚 Word Bank

Key Vocabulary

21 essential terms for interviewing, assessment, and candidate selection in professional HR English.

Module Vocabulary
🏗️
Structured interview
An interview format in which all candidates are asked the same predetermined questions in the same order, and their responses are evaluated against a consistent scoring framework. Research consistently shows that structured interviews are significantly more reliable and valid predictors of job performance than unstructured interviews, where the conversation varies by candidate. They are also far more legally defensible if a selection decision is challenged.
🎯
Competency framework
A defined set of behaviours, skills, and attributes that an organisation has identified as critical for effective performance. Competency frameworks form the basis of competency-based recruitment — each interview question is designed to assess one or more competencies, and responses are scored against behavioural indicators that describe what 'good', 'adequate', and 'poor' performance looks like for that competency.
⭐
STAR method
A framework used to structure both interview questions and candidate responses: Situation (describe the specific context), Task (explain what was required), Action (describe what you specifically did), Result (state the outcome). Interviewers use STAR to probe for complete, evidence-based answers. Candidates trained in STAR give more structured, verifiable responses. The most common interviewer error is accepting answers that lack the R (result) element.
💬
Behavioural question
A question that asks a candidate to describe a specific past situation in which they demonstrated a particular competency — based on the principle that past behaviour is the best predictor of future performance. Structure: 'Tell me about a time when you…' or 'Describe a situation in which you had to…' Unlike hypothetical questions, behavioural questions require the candidate to draw on real experience rather than describe what they would do.
🔮
Situational question
A question that presents a candidate with a hypothetical scenario and asks them what they would do. Structure: 'What would you do if…' or 'How would you handle a situation where…' Situational questions are useful for roles where candidates may not have directly comparable experience and for testing judgment. They are less predictive of performance than behavioural questions but remain widely used, particularly in assessment centres.
🔍
Probing question
A follow-up question used by an interviewer to elicit more complete or specific information when a candidate's answer is vague, incomplete, or unclear. Examples: 'Can you tell me a little more about your specific role in that?', 'What was the outcome of that action?', 'What would you say was the most significant challenge you faced?' Probing is an essential interviewing skill — the ability to probe without leading is one of the clearest markers of interviewing competence.
📊
Scoring rubric
A structured scoring guide that defines what constitutes a strong, adequate, or weak response for each interview question or competency. A typical rubric uses a 1–5 scale with behavioural anchors: 1 = no evidence, 2 = limited evidence, 3 = clear evidence, 4 = strong evidence, 5 = exceptional evidence. Rubrics ensure that all panel members are scoring against the same standard rather than their individual impressions.
👥
Panel interview
An interview conducted by two or more interviewers simultaneously. Panels reduce individual interviewer bias, improve reliability by pooling different perspectives, and are more legally defensible than single-interviewer formats. Each panel member typically takes responsibility for one or more competencies. Panels should always include a consistent debrief process in which individual scores are compared and discussed before a decision is reached.
🎭
Halo effect
A cognitive bias in which a positive impression in one area (e.g. strong communication skills, shared educational background, excellent presentation) leads the interviewer to assume positive qualities in all other areas without evidence. The halo effect is one of the most common and damaging biases in interviewing because it tends to reinforce the interviewer's initial impression rather than allowing the evidence to build.
🤝
Affinity bias
The tendency to favour candidates who are similar to oneself — in background, education, communication style, interests, or values. Affinity bias is particularly insidious because it feels like good judgment ('I just felt a strong connection with this candidate') when it is actually a preference for similarity. It consistently produces more homogeneous teams and disadvantages candidates from under-represented groups.
✅
Confirmation bias
The tendency to seek out and interpret evidence that confirms a pre-existing impression of a candidate — formed before or during the interview — while discounting evidence that contradicts it. An interviewer who decides early in the interview that a candidate is strong may unconsciously ask easier follow-up questions and interpret ambiguous answers positively, and vice versa.
🔚
Recency bias
The tendency to give disproportionate weight to the most recent information received — either within a single interview (remembering the end of the conversation more clearly than the beginning) or across a day of interviews (rating the last candidate higher or lower than earlier ones based on memory, not merit). Structured scoring after each response, rather than at the end, is the most effective mitigation.
📋
Interview debrief
A structured meeting between panel members after the interview — and ideally after all interviews for a role are complete — to compare scores, discuss evidence, and reach a consensus decision. A well-run debrief follows a consistent process: each panellist shares their scores and evidence, discrepancies are discussed and resolved, and the decision is based on evidence rather than impression. The debrief should be documented.
📝
Interview notes
Written records made by interviewers during or immediately after a candidate's interview, capturing the evidence provided in response to each question. Notes should record specific, observable behaviours and outcomes rather than impressions or conclusions. In the UK and many other jurisdictions, interview notes may be requested by a candidate under data protection legislation or as evidence in a tribunal case — making accuracy and professionalism essential.
🏆
Behavioural indicator
A specific, observable description of what a competency looks like in practice — used to define what a strong, adequate, or weak response to an interview question looks like. For example, a behavioural indicator for the competency 'Leadership' might be: 'Sets clear expectations for the team, monitors progress proactively, and addresses underperformance promptly and constructively.' Indicators allow multiple interviewers to score consistently.
🎯
Competency-based interview (CBI)
An interview format in which every question is linked to a specific competency in the organisation's framework, and responses are scored against defined behavioural indicators. CBIs are designed to predict job performance by gathering evidence of past behaviour across the most critical competencies for the role. They are the standard approach in most large organisations and public sector bodies.
⚠️
Discriminatory question
A question that refers directly or indirectly to a candidate's protected characteristic — such as age, gender, marital status, religion, disability, nationality, or family plans. These questions are unlawful in most jurisdictions and can form the basis of a discrimination claim. Common examples include: 'Do you have children?', 'Where were you born?', 'Are you planning to get married?' Even apparently innocuous conversation can cross into this territory.
📐
Interview scorecard
A standardised form completed by each interviewer for each candidate, recording a score for each competency assessed and the specific evidence from the interview that supports that score. Scorecards create a documented audit trail of the selection decision and are essential for consistency, legal compliance, and constructive feedback to unsuccessful candidates.
📬
Offer letter
A formal written communication sent to a successful candidate after a verbal offer has been made and accepted, confirming the terms of employment — including role title, salary, start date, working hours, location, and any conditions of appointment (such as satisfactory references or DBS check). The offer letter is usually the first binding document in the employment relationship.
💌
Rejection letter
A written communication sent to unsuccessful candidates following a selection process. A professional rejection letter thanks the candidate for their time, communicates the outcome clearly and respectfully, and — where possible — offers brief, constructive feedback. Generic rejection letters that offer no feedback are increasingly seen as poor practice and can damage an organisation's employer brand.
🔄
Reasonable adjustment
A modification to the standard interview process made to ensure that a disabled candidate is not disadvantaged relative to non-disabled candidates. Examples include: providing interview questions in advance, allowing additional time, conducting the interview remotely, adjusting the physical environment, or accepting alternative formats for assessment tasks. Failure to make reasonable adjustments for a disabled candidate can constitute unlawful discrimination.

Text 1: The Science of Structured Interviewing — Why Consistency Produces Better Decisions

How structured, competency-based interviews outperform traditional approaches — and why most organisations still do not use them

The evidence base for structured interviewing is among the most robust in all of organisational psychology. Meta-analyses covering decades of research consistently show that structured interviews — in which all candidates are asked the same questions in the same order, responses are evaluated against consistent scoring criteria, and decisions are based on documented evidence rather than overall impression — are significantly more valid predictors of job performance than unstructured interviews. The difference is not marginal: structured interviews predict job performance roughly twice as well as unstructured ones, according to research by Schmidt and Hunter that has been replicated and extended across multiple industries and cultures. And yet the majority of interviews conducted globally remain largely unstructured — a fact that reflects the persistent gap between what the evidence recommends and what organisations actually do.

The reasons for this gap are well understood. Unstructured interviews feel more natural: they allow the interviewer to follow their instincts, explore areas of interest, and form a genuine human connection with the candidate. Structured interviews can feel rigid and formulaic to interviewers who are not trained to use them well. Hiring managers often resist the discipline of sticking to predetermined questions, preferring the illusion of insight that comes from an informal, conversation-style interaction. The problem is that this illusion is unreliable: the qualities that make unstructured interviews feel insightful — personal rapport, conversational fluency, presentational confidence — are only weakly correlated with actual job performance in most roles. Structured interviews, by contrast, are designed to gather evidence on the specific behaviours that the organisation has identified as predictive of success.

The practical components of a structured interview are well established. Each question is written to assess a specific competency — a defined, observable behaviour that has been identified as critical for the role. Responses are evaluated against a behavioural anchor scale: a rubric that describes what a strong, adequate, and weak answer looks like for each competency. All interviewers on a panel complete individual scoring before discussing, to prevent the anchoring effect of hearing others' assessments first. And the final decision is based on the documented scores and evidence, not on a summary discussion in which the loudest voice or most senior person dominates. This is not bureaucracy for its own sake; it is the architecture of a fair, reliable, and legally defensible selection process.

Text 2: Bias in the Interview Room — Understanding and Mitigating the Forces That Distort Decisions

The cognitive biases that affect every interviewer — and the practical strategies that reduce their impact

Every interviewer brings bias to the interview room. This is not a character flaw; it is a feature of human cognition. The brain processes vast quantities of information simultaneously by applying heuristics — mental shortcuts that work efficiently in most situations but produce systematic errors in contexts requiring careful, evidence-based judgment. The interview is precisely such a context: a time-pressured, information-dense interaction in which the brain's pattern-matching instincts are fired continuously, producing rapid impressions that feel like insight but often reflect nothing more than familiarity and comfort. Understanding these biases is the essential first step in reducing their impact on selection decisions.

The most impactful biases in interviewing have been documented extensively. The halo effect causes a positive impression in one area — a candidate who is poised, articulate, and professionally dressed, or who shares the interviewer's alma mater or sporting interests — to generate inflated scores in all areas, regardless of the evidence. Affinity bias causes interviewers to favour candidates who remind them of themselves — in communication style, background, or cultural reference points — producing what feels like a 'good fit' judgment but is frequently a good similarity judgment. Confirmation bias causes interviewers who form an early positive impression to seek confirming evidence and discount contradictory information, making an 'gut feeling' self-fulfilling. Recency bias causes the last answer, or the last candidate of the day, to receive disproportionate weight relative to earlier evidence.

The good news is that these biases are not inevitable in their effects: they can be substantially reduced through deliberate process design. Structured interviews with consistent questions and scoring rubrics prevent the halo effect from spreading across competencies. Panel interviews with individual pre-scoring prevent any single interviewer's bias from dominating. Bias awareness training — teaching interviewers to name and recognise the specific biases they are prone to — has a measurable effect on scoring accuracy. Diverse interview panels further reduce affinity bias by ensuring that multiple reference points are represented in the room. And documenting evidence rather than impressions — writing 'the candidate described a situation in which they increased team output by 25% through restructuring daily stand-ups' rather than 'the candidate seemed confident and capable' — forces the interviewer to anchor their score to observable, verifiable behaviour rather than their overall feeling about the person.

Grammar Reference

Detailed reference notes covering all three grammar points in this module.

Grammar Reference 1: Question Formation for Professional Interviewing

Why question formation matters in interviews

The grammatical structure of an interview question determines what kind of answer it invites, how legally safe it is, and how professionally it reflects on the interviewer and the organisation. Professional interviewers must be able to form open, probing, situational, and behavioural questions correctly — and recognise and avoid leading and discriminatory structures.

Open questions — the foundation of the interview

Open questions invite the candidate to give a full, descriptive answer rather than a yes/no response. They typically begin with: What, Tell me, Describe, How, Walk me through.

  • 'Tell me about a time when you had to manage a complex stakeholder relationship.'
  • 'Describe how you approach prioritising when you have multiple competing deadlines.'
  • 'Walk me through your experience managing cross-functional teams.'

Probing questions — following up for evidence

Probing questions follow up on incomplete or vague answers and ask for specific detail. They are not challenges or criticisms — they are invitations to provide evidence.

  • 'You mentioned the project was challenging — could you be more specific about the obstacles you personally encountered?'
  • 'What was the outcome of that action? Can you quantify it?'
  • 'And what role did you specifically play in that decision?'

Avoiding leading and discriminatory questions

Leading questions suggest a preferred answer and should be avoided: 'You'd say you're a strong communicator, wouldn't you?' Discriminatory questions refer to protected characteristics: 'Are you planning to have children?' 'Where are you originally from?' These are unlawful in most jurisdictions and must not appear in any interview guide.

  • ✗ 'You're good with numbers, right?' → ✓ 'Describe your experience working with data and financial information.'
  • ✗ 'Do you have caring responsibilities that might affect your availability?' → ✓ 'The role involves occasional evening and weekend work — are you comfortable with those requirements?'
Grammar Reference 2: Reported Speech in Interview Notes and Debriefs

Why reported speech is essential in HR documentation

After every interview, interviewers must record what the candidate said — in notes, scorecards, and debrief summaries. In English, this requires the accurate use of reported speech, which shifts verb tenses back in time when reporting a past statement. Interview notes that quote candidates directly (without quotation marks) or that use incorrect tense shifts will appear unprofessional and may be inaccurate.

The backshift rule for interview notes

When reporting what a candidate said during a past interview, shift all verb tenses back one step.

  • Present simple → past simple: 'I manage a team of ten' → 'She said she managed a team of ten.'
  • Present continuous → past continuous: 'I am working on a restructure' → 'He mentioned he was working on a restructure.'
  • Past simple → past perfect: 'I led the project' → 'She explained that she had led the project.'
  • Will → would: 'I will bring fresh perspective' → 'He stated that he would bring fresh perspective.'
  • Can → could: 'I can start next month' → 'She confirmed that she could start the following month.'

Reporting verbs for interview notes

Choose reporting verbs that accurately reflect the candidate's intent and tone. Verbs carry meaning beyond mere attribution.

  • said / stated / mentioned → neutral reporting
  • explained / described → the candidate gave more detailed information
  • claimed → use cautiously — suggests possible doubt about the claim
  • acknowledged → the candidate admitted something, possibly a limitation
  • emphasised / highlighted → the candidate deliberately drew attention to this
  • confirmed → the candidate verified something definitively

Time and place shifts in reported speech

Time expressions also shift when using reported speech in written notes.

  • 'this week' → that week | 'today' → that day | 'yesterday' → the previous day
  • 'next month' → the following month | 'now' → then | 'here' → there
Grammar Reference 3: Core Interview Communication Collocations

Why interview collocations define HR professionalism

Interview management in English depends on precise, fixed verb-noun combinations. Using 'do a debrief' instead of 'conduct a debrief', or 'do a reasonable adjustment' instead of 'make a reasonable adjustment', sounds unnatural to native English speakers and signals a lack of fluency in the professional HR register.

Running and managing the interview process

Standard verb-noun combinations for managing interviews:

  • conduct / run / hold → an interview, a debrief, an assessment centre
  • complete / submit / record → a scorecard, notes, scores
  • brief / inform / prepare → the candidate, the panel
  • probe / redirect / follow up on → an answer, a response, a claim

Communicating decisions

Standard collocations for offer and rejection communications:

  • make / extend / confirm → an offer
  • decline / reject / turn down → an offer (from the candidate's perspective)
  • notify / inform / advise → a candidate of the outcome
  • provide / give / offer → feedback to a candidate
  • make → reasonable adjustments

Assessment and evaluation language

Standard collocations for scoring and decision-making:

  • assess / evaluate / test → a competency, a skill, a candidate
  • identify / recognise / name → a bias, a pattern, a strength
  • score / rate / grade → a response, a candidate
  • reach / arrive at / make → a decision, a consensus

D&I Lead: Good morning, everyone. Today we're going to look at the biases that affect us as interviewers — and what we can actually do about them. I want to start by being clear about something: bias in interviewing is not a character flaw. It's a cognitive phenomenon that affects every single one of us, regardless of our experience or our intentions. The brain uses shortcuts — we call them heuristics — to make sense of a lot of information very quickly. In most situations that's helpful. In an interview, where we need to make careful, evidence-based judgments, those shortcuts can lead us seriously astray. Let me walk you through the four biases we see most often in our interview data. First, the halo effect. This is where one strong positive quality — maybe the candidate presents brilliantly, or you discover you went to the same university, or they're immediately likeable — bleeds into all of your other assessments. You end up giving someone a strong score across the board because of one positive signal. The fix is structural: score each competency separately and only after you've heard the answer for that competency — don't form a general impression first. Second, affinity bias. This is where we favour people who are like us. Same communication style, similar background, shared cultural references. It feels like cultural fit. Often it's just similarity. The fix is diverse panels — if everyone in the room shares the same background, affinity bias will go unchallenged. Third, confirmation bias. You form an impression in the first two minutes — which research shows most interviewers do — and then spend the rest of the interview confirming it. If you decided early that someone is strong, you ask easier follow-up questions. If you decided they're weak, you probe harder. The fix is to write down your scores after each answer, not at the end of the interview. And fourth, recency bias — the last candidate, or the last answer, gets disproportionate weight. The fix is the same: score as you go, and review your early notes before the debrief.

Real-World Cases

Case Study 1
Google's Structured Hiring System — How Data Transformed Interviewing at Scale
USA · Technology · 2004–Present · Structured Interviewing at Scale · How Google used data to prove that structured interviews outperform traditional hiring instinct — and redesigned its process accordingly
Background

Google's approach to hiring is among the most widely studied in the world — partly because of the company's scale (hiring tens of thousands of people annually), and partly because Google has been unusually transparent about applying data science to its HR practices. When Laszlo Bock joined as SVP of People Operations in 2006, one of his first actions was to commission an internal study of Google's existing interview process — which at the time included as many as 25 interview rounds for some candidates, brain-teaser questions such as 'How many golf balls fit in a school bus?', and no consistent scoring framework. The study's finding was stark: Google's existing interviews had essentially zero predictive validity for job performance. The length, intensity, and perceived cleverness of the process was producing no better outcomes than chance.

The Redesign

Google's response was to implement a rigorous structured interview programme based on the academic evidence. Brain-teaser questions were eliminated entirely — the data showed they predicted nothing. The number of interview rounds was reduced to a maximum of four, after research showed that the fifth interview added essentially zero incremental information about the candidate. Structured, competency-based questions were introduced, with each question linked to one of Google's core job-related competencies — cognitive ability, role-related knowledge, leadership, and Googleyness (the cultural fit competency). All interviewers were required to complete individual scorecards before participating in debrief discussions. And a dedicated hiring committee — separate from the hiring manager — was established to review all interview evidence and make the final appointment decision, removing the single-manager authority that had been a major source of affinity bias.

Lessons for HR Professionals

Google's experience demonstrates several principles of universal relevance. First, the relationship between interview intensity and predictive validity is not linear: more rounds, harder questions, and longer processes do not produce better selection decisions. After four structured, well-designed interviews, additional rounds add noise rather than signal. Second, removing the hiring manager's sole authority over appointment decisions dramatically reduces affinity bias — because a diverse committee is unlikely to share identical biases. Third, the brain-teaser fallacy illustrates a broader point: questions that feel clever and revealing to the interviewer are frequently useless predictors of job performance. The standard of relevance for any interview question should be: does evidence of performance on this question predict evidence of performance in the role? If the answer is not clearly yes, the question should not be asked.

Case Study 2
The British Civil Service's Success Profiles Framework — Setting a National Standard for Fair Assessment
UK · Public Sector · 2019–Present · Competency-Based Assessment at Scale · How the UK Civil Service redesigned its recruitment and selection framework to produce fairer, more consistent, and more predictive assessments across all government departments
Background

The UK Civil Service is one of the world's largest employers, with approximately 500,000 civil servants across hundreds of departments and agencies. Recruitment at this scale creates significant challenges: the same role across different departments might be assessed using entirely different criteria and methods, producing inconsistent outcomes and exposing the government to equality challenges. In 2019, the Civil Service Commission launched the Success Profiles framework — a replacement for the previous Competency Framework — designed to standardise assessment across all recruitment, ensure that selection decisions were fair, consistent, and legally defensible, and to better predict on-the-job performance by assessing a wider range of candidate qualities than the previous framework had captured.

The Five Elements

Success Profiles assesses candidates across five elements: Behaviours (what people do — the observable actions and approaches that lead to effective performance), Strengths (what people enjoy and do often — qualities that are energising and sustainable), Experience (what people have done — relevant skills and knowledge from their career to date), Technical (role-specific expertise — the specialist knowledge required for the role), and Ability (underlying potential — cognitive capacity, verbal and numerical reasoning). The framework explicitly moved away from the assumption that all roles require the same competencies assessed in the same way — instead requiring HR professionals and hiring managers to design assessments tailored to the specific requirements of each role, selecting the most relevant elements and giving them appropriate weighting.

Lessons for HR Professionals

The Success Profiles framework offers several important lessons for HR professionals designing selection processes. First, a competency framework alone is insufficient — many strong candidates have the right behaviours and knowledge but are not assessed for their underlying potential or their genuine strengths (the things they find energising versus merely competent at). Second, assessment should always be proportionate to the role: a frontline caseworker and a senior director both need to be assessed fairly, but they do not need to be assessed on the same dimensions in the same way. Third, the framework's explicit inclusion of 'Strengths' as an assessment element reflects emerging evidence that assessing what candidates are genuinely motivated to do — not just what they can do — produces better predictions of sustained performance and lower attrition. Finally, standardisation and flexibility can coexist: a common framework applied with role-specific customisation produces both consistency and relevance.
