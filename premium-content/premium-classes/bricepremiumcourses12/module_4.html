Module Overview

Welcome to Module 4: Training and Development Language. Learning and development sits at the intersection of strategy and people — and it is one of the fastest-evolving areas of HR practice. Whether you are commissioning a leadership development programme, facilitating a training needs analysis, writing individual development plans, coaching a manager on giving constructive feedback, or presenting L&D ROI data to the board, you need a precise, confident, and professional command of the specialist English vocabulary of this field.

This module covers the full landscape of training and development language — the vocabulary of learning design, the grammar of writing measurable learning objectives, the communication skills needed to run a development conversation, and the professional writing conventions for L&D reports, TNA summaries, and individual development plans. You will practise all of these contexts with authentic HR scenarios, real-world case studies, and AI-powered writing feedback that analyses your grammar, vocabulary, structure, and length.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Explain the key terms of the learning and development field — including TNA, learning objectives, Kirkpatrick model, blended learning, 70-20-10 framework, and IDP — and use them accurately in spoken and written HR contexts.
2
Write clear, measurable learning objectives using Bloom's taxonomy action verbs — distinguishing between knowledge, skill, and behaviour outcomes — for a range of professional training programmes.
3
Design a structured Training Needs Analysis (TNA) summary and an Individual Development Plan (IDP) using the correct HR vocabulary, professional format, and appropriate future and obligation language.
4
Apply the passive voice correctly in L&D reporting contexts — writing programme summaries, evaluation reports, and board papers that communicate results formally and objectively.
5
Form and use the core L&D collocations — including identify a skills gap, deliver a programme, evaluate learning outcomes, build capability, and commission a training provider — accurately and fluently.
6
Conduct and document a development conversation in English — using open questions, growth mindset language, and forward-looking goal-setting structures that motivate rather than direct.
7
Write a professional Training Needs Analysis summary and an L&D programme evaluation report — both with AI feedback on grammar, vocabulary, structure, and length.
8
Listen to and understand L&D needs analysis meetings, coaching conversations, programme design briefings, and L&D reporting discussions in authentic HR contexts.

📚 Word Bank

Key Vocabulary

21 essential terms for training, development, and L&D in professional HR English.

Module Vocabulary
🔍
Training Needs Analysis (TNA)
A systematic process for identifying the gap between current and required levels of knowledge, skill, or behaviour across an individual, team, or organisation — and determining which gaps can and should be addressed through learning interventions. A TNA typically uses multiple data sources: performance data, manager input, employee self-assessment, strategic objectives, and customer or quality feedback. The output is a prioritised list of learning needs with recommended interventions.
🎯
Learning objective
A precise, measurable statement of what a learner will be able to do, know, or demonstrate as a result of a training intervention. Well-written learning objectives use Bloom's taxonomy action verbs to specify the cognitive level required: 'By the end of this workshop, participants will be able to analyse a performance gap and recommend a suitable learning intervention.' Vague objectives — 'participants will understand leadership' — cannot be assessed and should be rewritten.
📊
Kirkpatrick model
A four-level framework for evaluating training effectiveness: Level 1 — Reaction (did participants find the training engaging and relevant?); Level 2 — Learning (did participants acquire the intended knowledge or skills?); Level 3 — Behaviour (are participants applying what they learned back in the workplace?); Level 4 — Results (what measurable business outcomes resulted from the change in behaviour?). Most organisations measure Level 1 easily but struggle to demonstrate Level 3 and 4 impact.
📐
70-20-10 framework
A model of workplace learning that proposes that most professional development occurs through: 70% on-the-job experience (stretch assignments, projects, problem-solving), 20% social learning (feedback, coaching, mentoring, peer learning), and 10% formal learning (courses, workshops, e-learning). The framework does not prescribe a rigid formula but rather challenges L&D teams to design development that extends well beyond formal training.
🌱
Bloom's taxonomy
A hierarchical classification of cognitive learning objectives, developed by Benjamin Bloom in 1956 and revised in 2001. The six levels, from lower to higher order: Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyse, Evaluate, Create. Each level is associated with specific action verbs used to write learning objectives: Remember — 'list', 'define'; Understand — 'explain', 'describe'; Apply — 'demonstrate', 'use'; Analyse — 'compare', 'examine'; Evaluate — 'assess', 'justify'; Create — 'design', 'develop'.
📋
Individual Development Plan (IDP)
A structured document — co-created by an employee and their manager — that identifies the employee's development goals, the skills or behaviours they need to develop, the specific actions they will take, and the timeline and support required. An effective IDP is linked to the individual's role requirements and career aspirations, references specific learning activities, and includes defined milestones for review. It is a living document, not a form completed once and filed.
🧩
Blended learning
A learning design approach that combines multiple delivery modalities — typically face-to-face workshops, e-learning, on-the-job practice, coaching, and social learning — into a coherent programme. Blended learning is now the dominant model in corporate L&D because it leverages the strengths of different modalities: digital content for knowledge acquisition at scale, live sessions for skill practice and discussion, and workplace application for behavioural transfer.
🖥️
Learning Management System (LMS)
Software that enables organisations to create, deliver, manage, and track learning content and participation. An LMS typically hosts e-learning modules, manages enrolments, tracks completion, records assessment scores, and produces reports on learning activity across the organisation. Modern LMS platforms also support social learning features, certifications, and integration with HR information systems. Common examples include Cornerstone, Workday Learning, and SAP SuccessFactors.
📉
Skills gap
The difference between the skills an organisation currently has in its workforce and the skills it needs to achieve its strategic objectives — either now or in the future. Skills gaps can be identified at individual, team, or organisational level. They may result from changes in strategy, technology, market conditions, or workforce composition. Addressing skills gaps through L&D is one of the primary justifications for investment in learning programmes.
💬
Coaching
A development relationship in which a trained coach uses structured questions, active listening, and reflective techniques to help the individual gain insight, clarify goals, identify options, and commit to action. Coaching differs from mentoring (which involves a more experienced person sharing advice and guidance) and from training (which transfers specific knowledge or skill). In workplace contexts, coaching is used for leadership development, performance improvement, career transitions, and behavioural change.
👥
Mentoring
A development relationship in which a more experienced individual — the mentor — shares knowledge, advice, networks, and perspective with a less experienced person — the mentee. Unlike coaching, mentoring typically involves the mentor drawing on their own experience and offering guidance. Formal mentoring programmes match mentors and mentees systematically and provide structure to the relationship. Reverse mentoring — in which a junior employee mentors a senior one on a specific topic such as technology or diverse perspectives — has become increasingly common.
🏆
Leadership development
A deliberate programme of activities designed to build the leadership capability of current and future leaders — covering areas such as strategic thinking, stakeholder influence, change leadership, coaching skills, and commercial acumen. Leadership development programmes typically combine formal learning, action learning sets, executive coaching, stretch assignments, and 360-degree feedback. They represent the largest category of L&D investment in most large organisations.
📈
Return on investment (ROI) of training
A metric that attempts to quantify the financial return generated by a training investment — calculated by comparing the measurable business outcomes attributable to the training against the cost of delivering it. Calculating L&D ROI is methodologically challenging because isolating the effect of training from other variables is difficult. The Phillips ROI Methodology is the most widely used framework for doing so. ROI is most easily demonstrated for training that directly improves measurable outputs — such as sales skills training or error-reduction programmes.
🔄
Action learning
A development approach in which small groups of learners — called action learning sets — work on real, complex organisational problems, taking action and reflecting on results over time. The learning comes from both the problem-solving process and the structured reflection facilitated by a set facilitator. Action learning is particularly effective for senior leaders, as it develops judgment, collaborative problem-solving, and systemic thinking through direct experience rather than classroom instruction.
🌐
E-learning
Learning that is delivered digitally — through computer-based modules, video content, interactive simulations, virtual classrooms, or mobile learning platforms. E-learning enables learning to scale across large, geographically distributed workforces at low marginal cost per learner. Its limitations include low completion rates, limited effectiveness for complex skill development, and the absence of social learning. It is most effective when used as part of a blended programme rather than as a standalone solution.
🔢
Capability framework
A structured description of the knowledge, skills, behaviours, and values required for effective performance across different roles and levels within an organisation. A capability framework is broader than a competency framework — it typically encompasses technical, functional, and leadership capabilities. It is used to inform recruitment, performance management, succession planning, and L&D design. When L&D programmes are explicitly mapped to capability framework gaps, their strategic relevance is clearer and their impact is easier to measure.
📅
Learning and development plan
An organisational document — typically produced annually by the L&D or HR team — that sets out the learning priorities, planned interventions, budget allocation, and expected outcomes for the coming year. The L&D plan is derived from the TNA process and aligned to the organisation's strategic objectives. It is the primary planning document that governs what learning activity will be commissioned, delivered, and evaluated during the period.
🎓
Continuous Professional Development (CPD)
The ongoing process by which professionals maintain and enhance their knowledge, skills, and competence throughout their careers. CPD may be formal (completing a qualification, attending a conference) or informal (reading, peer discussion, reflective practice). Many professional bodies — including CIPD for HR professionals — require members to evidence a minimum number of CPD hours or activities each year. Organisations that support and fund CPD signal a commitment to long-term employee development.
📊
Learning evaluation
The systematic process of assessing the effectiveness of a learning intervention — typically using the Kirkpatrick model or a similar framework. Evaluation data is collected through learner reaction surveys, pre- and post-assessments, line manager observation, performance data, and business outcome metrics. Effective evaluation distinguishes between measuring the activity (how many people attended) and measuring the impact (what changed as a result). Most organisations over-invest in activity measurement and under-invest in impact measurement.
🔁
Succession planning
The process of identifying and developing internal talent to fill key leadership and specialist roles in the future — ensuring organisational continuity and reducing the risk of critical role vacancies. L&D plays a central role in succession planning by designing and delivering the development programmes that prepare identified successors. A succession plan without a supporting development plan is a wish list; the development plan is what makes succession possible.
🧠
Growth mindset
A concept developed by psychologist Carol Dweck describing the belief that intelligence, talent, and ability can be developed through effort, effective strategies, and constructive feedback — as opposed to a fixed mindset, which holds that these qualities are innate and unchangeable. In an L&D context, fostering a growth mindset is a prerequisite for a learning culture. Managers who use growth mindset language in development conversations — 'not yet', 'let's identify what you need to build this capability' — create significantly more developmental environments than those who evaluate rather than develop.

Text 1: Designing Effective Workplace Learning — From Training Needs to Measurable Outcomes

Why most corporate training fails to change behaviour — and what L&D professionals can do differently

The gap between what organisations spend on learning and development and the measurable impact that investment produces is one of the most persistent challenges in HR. Global corporate training spend exceeds $370 billion annually, yet research consistently shows that the majority of learning does not transfer to the workplace. Studies by the Association for Talent Development suggest that only 10–15% of training content is actually applied on the job after completion. This is not primarily a content problem — most training content is adequate. It is a design problem, a context problem, and a follow-up problem: the conditions required for learning to transfer to behaviour are rarely in place.

The design of effective workplace learning begins not with content but with a rigorous Training Needs Analysis. A TNA that is conducted thoroughly — using performance data, manager input, strategic objectives, and direct conversations with the learners themselves — produces a precise picture of what is needed: not just what skills are absent, but why, in what contexts, and with what urgency. This precision is what separates a learning programme that addresses a genuine performance gap from a programme that addresses a request. Learning objectives — the statements of what learners will be able to do, know, or demonstrate as a result of the intervention — should be derived directly from the TNA findings and written at the appropriate level of Bloom's taxonomy. A programme designed to 'improve leadership skills' is not a programme; it is an aspiration. A programme designed so that 'participants will be able to apply coaching techniques in weekly one-to-one conversations with direct reports' is a programme that can be designed, delivered, and evaluated.

Evaluation is the final and most frequently neglected element of the learning design cycle. The Kirkpatrick model's four levels provide a framework for moving beyond simple satisfaction surveys to genuine impact measurement. Level 3 behaviour evaluation — assessing whether learners are applying what they have learned in the workplace — requires commitment from line managers, who must observe, support, and reinforce application. Level 4 results evaluation — demonstrating measurable business outcomes — requires pre-agreed success metrics that are tracked before and after the intervention. L&D teams that build evaluation into the design of a programme from the start, rather than adding it as an afterthought, produce dramatically better evidence of impact and make a significantly stronger business case for continued investment.

Text 2: The Language of Development Conversations — How HR Professionals Talk About Growth

Why the words used in development conversations determine whether they produce insight or compliance

A development conversation is not a performance review. The distinction matters enormously — and not just conceptually. The language, tone, and structure of a development conversation must be fundamentally different from those of an appraisal or a one-to-one focused on operational delivery. Where performance conversations are primarily backward-looking and evaluative — 'how did you do against your targets?' — development conversations are forward-looking and exploratory: 'what do you want to develop, what's getting in the way, and how can I help?' The manager who does not make this shift — who turns a development conversation into a performance debrief — produces a discussion in which the employee gives compliant rather than candid answers, because the stakes feel evaluative.

The vocabulary and question types of an effective development conversation are grounded in coaching principles. Open questions that invite reflection — 'What has been the most stretching part of your role in the last six months?' — produce better insight than closed questions seeking confirmation: 'Are you finding the role challenging?' Growth mindset language — language that frames ability as developable rather than fixed — is essential: 'What capability would you like to build?' is more productive than 'What are you not good at?' Similarly, forward-looking language — 'What would make you more effective?' — produces better dialogue than deficit language — 'Where are you falling short?' The manager who uses the vocabulary of development fluently signals safety, investment, and belief in the employee's potential.

The written output of a development conversation — the Individual Development Plan — must reflect the quality and intention of the conversation itself. An IDP that lists 'complete leadership training course' as the sole development action is a compliance document, not a development plan. An effective IDP names the specific capability being developed, the evidence of current performance that justifies the priority, the range of development activities planned (drawing on the 70-20-10 model), and the observable indicators of progress that will confirm the development is working. Writing an IDP in clear, professional HR English — using precise vocabulary, appropriate future forms, and specific measurable language — is a direct reflection of the L&D professional's own development capability.

🔍 Grammar Review

Grammar Reference

Detailed reference notes covering all three grammar points in this module.

Grammar Reference 1: Writing Measurable Learning Objectives Using Bloom's Taxonomy

Why precision matters in learning objectives

A learning objective is not a description of training content — it is a statement of observable outcome. The difference between 'participants will understand leadership' and 'participants will be able to apply coaching techniques in a live conversation' is the difference between an aspiration and an evaluable commitment. All professional learning objectives should be SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound.

Bloom's taxonomy levels and their action verbs

Use the correct Bloom's level and associated verb for the type of learning you are targeting.

  • Remember (knowledge recall): list, define, name, recall, state — 'Participants will be able to list the four Kirkpatrick levels.'
  • Understand (comprehension): explain, describe, summarise, classify — 'Participants will be able to explain the 70-20-10 framework to their team.'
  • Apply (using in new situations): demonstrate, use, conduct, apply, implement — 'Participants will be able to conduct a structured coaching conversation using the GROW model.'
  • Analyse (breaking down, examining): analyse, compare, differentiate, examine — 'Participants will be able to analyse a performance gap and identify appropriate L&D interventions.'
  • Evaluate (making judgments): assess, justify, evaluate, judge, critique — 'Participants will be able to assess an L&D programme against the Kirkpatrick framework.'
  • Create (producing something new): design, develop, construct, produce — 'Participants will be able to design a 12-month individual development plan for a direct report.'

The standard structure of a professional learning objective

Use this formula as the foundation for all learning objectives:

  • By the end of [this programme / workshop / module], participants will be able to [Bloom's action verb] + [specific content or skill] + [context or standard] + [optional: to / at a level that / using].
  • Example: 'By the end of this workshop, participants will be able to design a structured coaching conversation plan using the GROW model, and deliver a 20-minute coaching session to a peer with evidence of all four model stages.'
Grammar Reference 2: Passive Voice in L&D Reports and Programme Summaries

Why the passive voice is standard in L&D reporting

L&D reports, programme evaluations, and board papers use the passive voice to communicate results objectively and formally — focusing on what was done, measured, or achieved rather than on who did it. The passive signals institutional register and depersonalises findings, which is appropriate when reporting to senior stakeholders.

Forming the passive — key structures

The passive is formed using the correct form of 'to be' + the past participle of the main verb.

  • Simple past passive: was/were + past participle — 'The programme was delivered to 42 managers.'
  • Present perfect passive: has/have been + past participle — 'The evaluation report has been submitted to the Board.'
  • Future passive: will be + past participle — 'The findings will be presented at the Q4 session.'
  • Modal passive: should be / must be / was to be + past participle — 'All learning objectives should be reviewed annually.'

Common L&D reporting passive structures

These passive constructions appear frequently in L&D reports:

  • 'The programme was delivered to / attended by / completed by [number] participants...'
  • 'Data was collected / gathered / analysed using [method]...'
  • 'Learning objectives were mapped / aligned to / derived from the capability framework...'
  • 'A total of [X] learning hours were logged / recorded across [scope]...'
  • 'The evaluation was conducted / carried out in accordance with the Kirkpatrick model...'
  • 'Significant improvements were identified / noted / recommended as a result of the pilot...'
Grammar Reference 3: Core L&D and Development Conversation Collocations

Why L&D collocations define professional credibility

L&D in English has a specific vocabulary of fixed verb-noun combinations. Using 'do a skills gap' instead of 'identify a skills gap', or 'make a development conversation' instead of 'hold a development conversation', sounds unnatural to native English-speaking HR professionals and signals limited fluency in the professional register.

Planning and designing L&D

Standard verb-noun combinations for L&D planning:

  • identify / close / address → a skills gap
  • commission / design / deliver → a programme
  • conduct / carry out / complete → a TNA
  • build / develop / strengthen → capability
  • align / map → learning objectives to the framework

Delivering and evaluating learning

Standard collocations for L&D delivery and evaluation:

  • deliver / facilitate / run → a workshop, a training session, a programme
  • evaluate / measure / assess → learning outcomes, impact, ROI
  • collect / gather / analyse → participant feedback, evaluation data
  • demonstrate / evidence / prove → impact, return on investment

Development conversations

Standard collocations for development conversation and IDP language:

  • hold / conduct / structure → a development conversation
  • agree on / set / review → development goals, IDP actions
  • identify / articulate / own → development priorities
  • apply / embed / practise → new behaviours, learning, skills
  • use / ask / deploy → open questions, coaching techniques, reflective prompts

Case Study 1
Deloitte's 'Learning Organisation' Transformation — Building Development Into the Flow of Work
USA / Global · Professional Services · 2016–Present · Strategic L&D Transformation · How Deloitte redesigned its L&D operating model to shift from event-based training to continuous, embedded learning
Background

Deloitte, one of the Big Four professional services firms with over 450,000 employees globally, faced a significant L&D challenge in the mid-2010s: its workforce needed to continuously develop new capabilities in technology, analytics, and cross-disciplinary skills as the consulting landscape shifted — but the traditional model of sending professionals on courses was too slow, too expensive, and too disconnected from the pace of project-based work. A landmark insight from Deloitte's own research — published in its Global Human Capital Trends report — found that 'the half-life of a learned skill is now less than five years', and that the gap between the skills organisations have and the skills they need was widening faster than traditional L&D models could close. The implication was stark: the future of L&D lay not in better training courses but in building a genuine learning organisation — one in which learning was embedded in the daily flow of work.

The Transformation

Deloitte's L&D transformation had several defining features. First, it moved the majority of its learning investment away from classroom-based programmes towards what it called 'learning in the flow of work' — micro-learning content, expert networks, on-demand resources, and AI-curated learning recommendations that employees could access when they needed them, in the context of the work they were doing. Second, Deloitte invested heavily in building a coaching and feedback culture — recognising that the 20% social learning component of the 70-20-10 model was the most scalable and impactful lever available. Third, the L&D function was restructured: rather than being a centralised training department, it was distributed into the business — with L&D business partners embedded in each practice area, responsible for linking learning strategy to business priorities. Fourth, evaluation was redesigned: Deloitte moved from measuring completion rates and satisfaction scores towards measuring capability progression and business impact.

Lessons for HR Professionals

Deloitte's transformation offers three fundamental lessons for L&D professionals in any sector. First, the learning organisation is a business strategy, not an HR initiative: unless senior leaders model continuous learning, fund it appropriately, and hold managers accountable for developing their teams, no amount of L&D programme design will produce a learning culture. Second, the most effective L&D investment is often in the conditions for learning — coaching capability, psychological safety, feedback culture, and managerial skill in development conversations — rather than in formal content delivery. Third, measurement must evolve: L&D functions that report only completion data are reporting activity, not impact, and will struggle to demonstrate their strategic value. The shift to capability-level and business-outcome measurement is not just good practice; it is the basis on which investment in learning will be justified or cut.

Case Study 2
Unilever's '5-Hour Rule' — Building a Learning Culture at the Top of the Organisation
UK / Global · Consumer Goods · 2018–Present · Leadership Learning Culture · How Unilever used senior leadership commitment to build a learning culture from the top down — and why it accelerated capability development across the organisation
Background

Unilever, the global consumer goods company, faced a challenge familiar to many large organisations: despite significant investment in L&D programmes, the learning culture within the organisation was uneven — strong in some functions, virtually absent in others. Research into what distinguished the high-development functions from the low-development ones revealed a consistent pattern: in high-development functions, senior leaders visibly invested time in their own learning and talked openly about it. In low-development functions, learning was treated as something that happened to junior employees — not something that senior leaders did themselves. The implication was that the most powerful driver of a learning culture was not the quality of the L&D programmes available but the visible learning behaviour of senior leaders.

The 5-Hour Rule

Inspired by research showing that many of history's most productive thinkers — including Benjamin Franklin, Bill Gates, and Elon Musk — deliberately set aside at least one hour per day for learning, reflection, and deliberate practice, Unilever introduced what it called the '5-Hour Rule' for its senior leadership community: each senior leader was expected to invest a minimum of five hours per week in deliberate learning — reading, attending learning sessions, engaging in reflective practice, or participating in peer learning discussions. This was not a mandatory training requirement; it was a cultural expectation, modelled by the CEO and reinforced through visible storytelling. Leaders were encouraged to share what they were learning — through internal blogs, team briefings, and leadership forums — creating a social learning dynamic that cascaded the culture down through the organisation.

Lessons for HR Professionals

Unilever's experience teaches a principle of universal applicability: you cannot install a learning culture from below. The most sophisticated L&D infrastructure in the world — advanced LMS, curated content libraries, executive coaching programmes — will not produce a genuine learning culture if senior leaders are not visibly and genuinely invested in their own development. HR and L&D professionals who want to build a learning organisation must first focus on the top: supporting, enabling, and making visible the learning behaviour of senior leaders. The second lesson is about the power of social learning: when leaders share what they are learning, they normalise learning as a senior activity, reduce the implicit stigma of not knowing, and create permission structures that allow others at all levels to invest in their own development without fear of being seen as inadequate.
