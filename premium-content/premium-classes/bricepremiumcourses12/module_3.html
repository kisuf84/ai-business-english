Module Overview

Welcome to Module 3: Onboarding and Orientation Language. The first days and weeks in a new role have a disproportionate impact on an employee's long-term engagement, productivity, and likelihood of staying. Research by the Society for Human Resource Management (SHRM) consistently shows that employees who experience a structured, welcoming onboarding process are significantly more likely to still be with the organisation at the 12-month mark — and significantly more likely to feel confident and competent in their role. The language HR professionals use during onboarding is central to that experience.

This module focuses on the full range of English communication skills needed to deliver effective onboarding — from pre-arrival welcome communications and first-day orientation scripts to buddy programme conversations, 30-60-90 day check-ins, and the written documentation that guides new employees through their first months. You will build the vocabulary, grammar, and interpersonal communication skills to make new employees feel genuinely welcomed, clearly informed, and well supported in an English-speaking or international workplace.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Explain what onboarding is, distinguish it from orientation, and describe the key phases of a structured onboarding programme from pre-arrival through to the 90-day review.
2
Write and deliver a professional pre-arrival welcome communication that sets clear expectations, provides practical information, and makes the new employee feel genuinely welcomed before their first day.
3
Use appropriate welcoming and introductory language to conduct a first-day orientation — introducing the workplace, the team, the culture, and the essential policies in a warm and accessible way.
4
Use future forms — will, going to, and the present continuous for scheduled events — correctly to describe onboarding plans, upcoming activities, and first-week schedules.
5
Use imperative and instructional language — including softened imperatives, modal suggestions, and sequenced instructions — correctly to explain procedures, policies, and tasks to new employees.
6
Form and use the core HR onboarding collocations — including complete the induction, settle into the role, meet the team, and review progress — accurately and naturally in written and spoken contexts.
7
Write a structured welcome email to a new employee and a 30-day check-in summary that documents progress, highlights support needs, and confirms the integration plan going forward.
8
Listen to and understand first-day welcome conversations, buddy programme introductions, HR check-in meetings, and onboarding programme design discussions across different workplace contexts.

Key Vocabulary

21 essential terms for onboarding and orientation in professional HR English.

Module Vocabulary
🎓
Onboarding
The structured process of integrating a new employee into the organisation — covering their role, the team, the culture, the systems, and the policies. Goes beyond administrative induction to focus on engagement, belonging, and early performance.
🗺️
Orientation
The initial, often one-day or one-week programme that introduces a new employee to the organisation's basic structures, facilities, policies, and key people. Orientation is typically the first phase of a broader onboarding programme.
📬
Pre-boarding
The activities and communications that take place between the employee signing the contract and their first day of work — including welcome emails, IT setup, access provisioning, and sending useful information about the role and organisation.
📋
Induction
A formal process during which new employees receive essential information about the organisation, their role, health and safety requirements, and HR policies. Used interchangeably with orientation in British English, though induction tends to be more procedural.
🤝
Buddy programme
A structured arrangement in which a new employee is paired with an experienced colleague — the 'buddy' — who provides informal guidance, answers practical questions, and helps them navigate the social and cultural aspects of the workplace.
📅
30-60-90 day plan
A structured onboarding roadmap that sets out specific learning objectives, relationship-building goals, and performance milestones for the new employee's first 30, 60, and 90 days in the role.
🏠
Cultural onboarding
The intentional process of helping a new employee understand the organisation's values, norms, communication style, unwritten expectations, and ways of working — the 'how we do things here' that is rarely written down but critically important.
📊
New employee survey
A structured questionnaire sent to new employees — typically at 30 and 90 days — to gather feedback on their onboarding experience, identify gaps in support, and measure early engagement and satisfaction.
👥
Line manager
The employee's direct manager, who plays a central role in day-to-day onboarding — setting expectations, providing role-specific guidance, giving feedback, and conducting the 30-60-90 day check-in conversations.
🔑
Access provisioning
The process of setting up a new employee's IT accounts, system access, email, badge, building access, and any other tools they need to do their job before or on their first day.
📝
Onboarding checklist
A structured list of tasks, documents, and activities that must be completed during the onboarding process — used by HR, the line manager, and the new employee to track progress and ensure nothing is missed.
🗓️
First-week schedule
A day-by-day plan for a new employee's first week, including meetings, introductions, training sessions, and administrative tasks — designed to reduce anxiety and give the new starter a clear sense of what to expect.
💬
Check-in meeting
A brief, structured meeting between the new employee and their line manager or HR business partner at key points during the onboarding period — typically at 30, 60, and 90 days — to review progress, address concerns, and confirm the integration plan.
🌱
Early engagement
The degree to which a new employee feels motivated, committed, and connected to the organisation in their first weeks and months. Research shows that early engagement is a strong predictor of long-term retention.
📖
Employee handbook
A comprehensive written guide to the organisation's policies, procedures, values, benefits, and expectations — typically provided to new employees as part of their onboarding documentation.
🔄
Integration
The process by which a new employee becomes a fully effective and socially connected member of the team and organisation — developing the relationships, knowledge, and confidence needed to perform at their best.
🏅
Time to productivity
The period from a new employee's start date to the point at which they are fully contributing at the expected level for their role. A key metric for evaluating onboarding effectiveness — shorter is generally better.
📌
Stakeholder introductions
A planned series of meetings or conversations between a new employee and the key people — internal and external — that they will work with regularly. Especially important for senior hires or client-facing roles.
🌍
Remote onboarding
The process of onboarding an employee who works primarily or entirely remotely, requiring additional intentional effort to replicate the connection, culture, and informal learning that physical proximity naturally provides.
🔁
Feedback loop
A systematic mechanism for collecting and acting on feedback from new employees about their onboarding experience — ensuring that the programme improves continuously based on real input from those who have gone through it.
⚖️
Compliance onboarding
The mandatory element of onboarding that ensures new employees complete all legally required documentation, training, and acknowledgements — including right-to-work verification, data protection training, health and safety briefings, and contract signing.

Text 1: Why Onboarding Matters — The Evidence for Structured New Employee Integration

What the research tells us about the link between onboarding quality and employee retention

The gap between organisations that invest seriously in onboarding and those that treat it as a one-day administrative exercise is large and measurable. Research by Gallup consistently finds that only 12% of employees feel their organisation does a great job of onboarding. Yet the consequences of poor onboarding are significant: the Society for Human Resource Management (SHRM) has found that organisations with a strong onboarding process improve new hire retention by 82% and productivity by over 70%. Conversely, a poor onboarding experience — or no structured experience at all — is one of the strongest predictors of early attrition. Employees who leave within their first year often cite confusion about their role, feeling unwelcome or disconnected, or failing to build relationships with colleagues as the primary reasons for departure.

Understanding why structured onboarding produces these outcomes requires understanding what new employees actually need in their first weeks. Researchers Talya Bauer and Berrin Erdogan, whose work on onboarding is widely cited in the HR literature, identify four dimensions of what they call 'onboarding success': (1) role clarity — understanding exactly what is expected of them and how their work connects to the broader organisation; (2) self-efficacy — developing confidence in their ability to perform the role; (3) social integration — building relationships with colleagues and feeling they belong; and (4) knowledge of culture and values — understanding the 'how we do things here' that is never fully documented. A structured onboarding programme specifically and deliberately addresses all four of these dimensions — something that a purely administrative induction process cannot do.

The financial cost of poor onboarding is also compelling. Industry estimates suggest that replacing an employee costs between 50% and 200% of their annual salary, depending on the role and seniority level. For an organisation that loses 20% of new hires within their first 45 days — which is not unusual in sectors with historically poor onboarding practices — the cumulative cost is enormous. The strategic case for investing in onboarding is therefore not a 'soft' HR argument about employee wellbeing — it is a hard commercial argument about revenue, productivity, and the efficiency of the talent pipeline.

Text 2: Designing an Effective Onboarding Programme — Principles and Practice

From pre-arrival preparation to the 90-day review — what good onboarding looks like in practice

Effective onboarding does not begin on the employee's first day — it begins the moment the offer letter is signed. The pre-boarding period, which spans from contract signature to the first day of work, is one of the most underutilised opportunities in the entire employee lifecycle. A well-designed pre-boarding experience sends a welcome pack, establishes IT access, introduces the buddy, provides a first-week schedule, and creates a genuine sense of anticipation rather than anxiety. Research shows that new employees who receive structured pre-boarding communications feel more confident and prepared on day one — and the first day itself is critically important as a predictor of long-term engagement.

Once the employee arrives, the focus of onboarding shifts through three broad phases. The first phase — typically covering the first two to four weeks — is about orientation and compliance: helping the employee understand the physical and social environment, meeting their immediate team and key stakeholders, completing the mandatory compliance requirements, and establishing the basic routines of the role. The second phase — from approximately weeks three to eight — shifts the focus towards performance and contribution: the employee begins to take on real work, receives early feedback, develops their understanding of how the team operates, and starts to build their professional identity within the organisation. The third phase — from week eight to the end of the first 90 days — is about consolidation and ownership: the employee should be developing genuine independence, contributing meaningfully to team goals, and beginning to think about their medium-term development within the organisation.

The 30-60-90 day check-in structure provides a natural framework for monitoring progress through these phases. Each check-in should cover three areas: what has gone well, what has been challenging, and what support or resources are needed going forward. The 30-day check-in is often the most critical — it is the moment at which a struggling employee is most likely to be feeling overwhelmed and least likely to ask for help unprompted. HR professionals who are skilled in onboarding language are able to create the psychological safety needed for a new employee to be honest about their experience — and then to respond in a way that supports and encourages rather than judges.

Grammar Reference 1: Future Forms in Onboarding Language

Why future forms matter in onboarding communications

Onboarding involves a great deal of forward-looking communication — describing plans, schedules, upcoming activities, and what the new employee can expect. Using the correct future form signals whether something is fixed, planned, or spontaneously decided — and choosing the wrong form can confuse or mislead new employees.

Will — for decisions made at the moment of speaking and promises

Use will for commitments made as you are writing or speaking, for promises, and for information being shared in real time.

  • 'I will send you the first-week schedule by tomorrow morning.'
  • 'Your HR contact will be James Chen — he will be in touch before your start date.'
  • 'If you have any questions, I will be happy to help.'

Going to — for plans and intentions already decided

Use going to when a plan has been made in advance and the decision was made before the moment of communication.

  • 'We are going to hold a team lunch at the end of your first week.'
  • 'You are going to have introductory meetings with all department heads.'
  • 'The HR team is going to send you a pre-reading pack before you start.'

Present continuous — for scheduled and arranged future events

Use present continuous when an event is specifically arranged — with time, people, and place confirmed. Common in first-week schedules and diary entries.

  • 'On Tuesday morning you are having your IT systems training.'
  • 'Your buddy is meeting you at reception at 9am on Monday.'
  • 'You are attending the company values workshop on Thursday afternoon.'
Grammar Reference 2: Imperative and Instructional Language

Why instructional language needs care in onboarding

Onboarding documents and communications often need to give instructions — but the tone matters enormously. Bare imperatives ('Do this', 'Complete that') can feel abrupt or even rude to new employees who are already feeling uncertain. Professional instructional language balances clarity with warmth.

Softened imperatives — adding please and context

Adding please and a brief reason transforms a command into a professional request.

  • ABRUPT: 'Return this form to HR.'
  • PROFESSIONAL: 'Please return this form to HR before the end of your first week.'
  • ABRUPT: 'Read the handbook.'
  • PROFESSIONAL: 'Please take some time to read through the employee handbook at your own pace.'

Modal suggestions — using should, could, might

These structures give the employee agency while still communicating what is expected or recommended.

  • 'You should read through the employee handbook before your first team meeting.'
  • 'It might be worth scheduling a brief call with your IT contact before you start.'
  • 'You could ask your buddy about the team's preferred communication style — they'll be well placed to advise.'

Sequencing language — first, then, next, finally

Sequencing words make multi-step instructions easy to follow and reduce the risk of mistakes or missed steps.

  • 'First, open the HR portal and click on New Employee Setup.'
  • 'Next, enter your employee ID number, which you will find in your welcome email.'
  • 'Then follow the prompts to create your password and security questions.'
  • 'Finally, click Confirm to complete your account setup.'
Grammar Reference 3: Core HR Onboarding Collocations

Why collocations define HR credibility

HR professionals who write and speak with fluent, natural English use specific verb-noun and adjective-noun combinations that are standard in the field. Wrong combinations sound unnatural and reduce professional credibility — especially when communicating with new employees who may be forming their first impressions of the HR function.

Complete, conduct, and deliver — for programme activities

These verbs describe the formal activities of onboarding:

  • complete → an induction, mandatory training, the onboarding checklist, required documentation
  • conduct → a check-in meeting, a review, an orientation session
  • deliver → an onboarding programme, a training session, a welcome briefing

Settle, integrate, and build — for employee experience

These verbs describe the employee's journey through the onboarding period:

  • settle into → the role, the team, the new working environment
  • integrate → into the team, the culture, the organisation
  • build → relationships, confidence, an understanding of the role

Set, establish, and provide — for HR and manager actions

These verbs describe what HR and line managers actively do during onboarding:

  • set → expectations, objectives, a 90-day plan, clear goals
  • establish → a routine, a relationship, access to systems
  • provide → support, guidance, feedback, information, an employee handbook

Case Study 1
Google's Onboarding Research — The Manager's Critical Role in New Employee Success
USA · Technology · 2007–Present · Data-Driven Onboarding Excellence · How Google's People Analytics team discovered that one manager behaviour on the Sunday before a new employee's start date had a measurable impact on productivity nine months later
Background

Google has long been one of the most data-driven organisations in the world when it comes to people management — and their approach to onboarding is no exception. In the mid-2000s, Google's People Analytics team began studying the factors that determined how quickly new employees (whom Google calls 'Nooglers') reached full productivity. The assumption most managers held was that productivity ramp-up time was primarily a function of the employee's skill level and intelligence. The data told a different story.

The Sunday Email Experiment

Google's analysis found that the single highest-impact variable in a new employee's time to productivity was not their qualifications, not their previous experience, and not the quality of their technical training — it was the behaviour of their direct manager in the first week. Specifically, the research identified that managers who proactively checked in with their new hire before the first day — even with something as simple as a Sunday evening email or message welcoming the person, confirming the start time, expressing genuine excitement about having them join, and asking if they had any questions — produced employees who reached full productivity measurably faster than those whose managers made no such contact. The effect was not trivial: in follow-up studies, the productivity difference was still measurable nine months after the start date. Google began building this finding directly into their manager guidance, and the 'pre-start contact' became a standard element of their onboarding checklist.

Lessons for HR Professionals

Google's onboarding research produces several important lessons for HR professionals. First, the quality of a new employee's onboarding experience is heavily influenced by their direct manager — and HR's role includes equipping and encouraging managers to play that role well, not just designing administrative processes. Second, small, human gestures — a personal welcome message, a handwritten note, a genuine expression of excitement — can have an outsized impact on how connected and motivated a new employee feels. Third, data and measurement are essential for understanding what actually drives onboarding outcomes, as opposed to what we assume drives them. The most impressive aspect of Google's approach is not the cleverness of the Sunday email itself — it is the culture of measurement and experimentation that revealed its importance in the first place.

Case Study 2
Zappos: Onboarding as a Cultural Immersion — Paying New Hires to Leave
USA · E-commerce · 1999–Present · Cultural Onboarding Innovation · How Zappos used a radical onboarding approach to ensure only genuinely committed employees joined their famously strong culture
The Zappos Culture Philosophy

Zappos, the online shoe and clothing retailer known for its exceptional customer service and distinctive company culture, built its entire business model on the belief that happy, engaged employees produce happy customers. CEO Tony Hsieh was explicit: culture was not a nice-to-have at Zappos — it was the company's most important competitive advantage, and protecting it required careful, intentional attention from the first moment a new employee joined. This conviction shaped Zappos's onboarding programme in some highly unconventional ways.

The Offer to Leave — and Why It Worked

Perhaps the most famous element of Zappos's onboarding is what became known as 'The Offer'. After completing a four-week paid onboarding and training programme — during which new employees learned about the company's culture, values, customer service philosophy, and internal systems — Zappos offered every new starter a cash payment to quit. The original offer was $2,000 (later raised to $4,000). The message was simple: if, after four weeks of full immersion in the Zappos culture, you do not feel that you genuinely fit here — if you are here only for the pay cheque — we will pay you to leave. The logic was as clear as it was counterintuitive: a small financial incentive to leave now was far less expensive than the cost of carrying an uncommitted, disengaged employee for months or years. In practice, fewer than 3% of new hires took the offer — but those who stayed did so as a positive, active choice, having been fully informed about the culture they were choosing to join.

What HR Can Learn

The Zappos approach demonstrates several important principles for onboarding design. First, onboarding is not just about providing information — it is about authentic cultural immersion, giving employees the genuine experience of what it feels like to work at the organisation before they are fully committed. Second, self-selection is powerful: employees who choose to stay after being genuinely invited to leave become more committed advocates than those who simply drifted into the role without reflection. Third, the most effective onboarding investments are not always the most obvious ones — sometimes the best way to build a committed workforce is to make it genuinely easy for the uncommitted to leave early.
