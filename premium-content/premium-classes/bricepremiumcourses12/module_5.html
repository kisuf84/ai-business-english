Module Overview

Welcome to Module 5: Performance Reviews and Feedback. The performance review is one of the most consequential conversations in any organisation. When conducted well, it motivates, develops, and retains employees. When handled poorly, it demoralises, confuses, and creates legal risk. For HR professionals and managers working in English, the language used in appraisals — both spoken and written — must be precise, balanced, empathetic, and evidence-based.

This module covers the complete language of performance management: from feedback frameworks such as SBI (Situation-Behaviour-Impact) and SMART objective language, through hedging structures for balanced appraisal writing, to the conditional grammar of development conversations and the collocations that define professional performance English. The Writing section features AI-powered feedback on your grammar, vocabulary, structure, and suggestions for improvement.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Describe the purpose and key stages of a formal performance review cycle and explain the principles of effective feedback: specific, timely, evidence-based, and development-focused.
2
Apply the SBI (Situation-Behaviour-Impact) framework to structure both positive and developmental feedback in a way that is clear, specific, and non-personal.
3
Write and use SMART objective language correctly when setting, reviewing, and documenting performance goals.
4
Use hedging language — tends to, has shown a pattern of, there are occasions when, could benefit from — accurately to write balanced and legally defensible appraisal observations.
5
Apply conditional structures — particularly second and third conditional — to discuss hypothetical scenarios and performance alternatives in feedback and development conversations.
6
Form and use the core performance review collocations accurately: exceed expectations, identify development areas, demonstrate consistent performance, and address a performance gap.
7
Write a balanced annual performance appraisal summary and a PIP introduction email that meet professional HR standards for clarity, evidence, balance, and legal defensibility, and receive AI-generated feedback on grammar, vocabulary, length, structure, and improvements.
8
Listen to and understand a full performance review conversation, a feedback coaching session, an underperformance discussion, and a 360-degree feedback briefing.

Key Vocabulary

21 essential terms for performance reviews and feedback in professional HR English.

Module Vocabulary
📊
Performance review
A formal, structured conversation between a manager and employee to assess performance against agreed objectives, discuss strengths and development areas, and set goals for the next period. Also called an appraisal or performance appraisal.
🎯
SMART objectives
A framework for setting clear goals: Specific (clearly defined), Measurable (quantifiable), Achievable (realistic), Relevant (aligned to role and organisational goals), and Time-bound (with a clear deadline). The standard for all professional performance objectives.
🔍
SBI framework
Situation-Behaviour-Impact: a structured feedback model. Describe the Situation where behaviour occurred, the observable Behaviour itself, and the Impact it had. SBI keeps feedback specific, objective, and non-personal, avoiding generalisations about character.
📈
Performance rating
A formal score or category assigned to an employee's overall performance — typically: Exceeds Expectations, Meets Expectations, Partially Meets Expectations, or Does Not Meet Expectations. Ratings must be evidence-based and consistently applied across the team.
🔄
360-degree feedback
A feedback process in which structured input comes from multiple sources including the manager, peers, direct reports, and sometimes external stakeholders. Provides a more rounded view of performance and behaviour than manager-only assessment.
💬
Constructive feedback
Feedback that identifies a specific area for improvement and provides guidance on how to develop, rather than simply criticising. Effective constructive feedback is evidence-based, specific, forward-looking, and delivered with genuine intention to support the employee.
📋
Performance Improvement Plan (PIP)
A formal documented plan used when an employee is not meeting minimum performance expectations. Sets out specific, measurable improvement targets with a defined review period of typically 30 to 90 days, and clear consequences if targets are not met.
🌟
Exceeds expectations
A performance rating indicating that the employee has consistently performed above what was required — demonstrating exceptional results, initiative, or contribution beyond their core responsibilities. Must be supported by specific evidence.
📉
Performance gap
The difference between the level of performance currently demonstrated and the level required by the role or organisation's standards. Identifying and addressing performance gaps is a core HR and management responsibility.
🏆
Calibration
A process in which managers discuss and align their performance ratings in a group meeting to ensure consistency and fairness across the organisation and to reduce the impact of individual manager bias.
🧠
Recency bias
A cognitive bias in which recent events are given disproportionate weight in performance assessment, at the expense of earlier performance. Managed by keeping ongoing performance notes throughout the year.
✨
Halo effect
A cognitive bias in which a positive impression of one aspect of performance leads to an overall positive rating, even when other areas are weaker. The reverse — the horn effect — distorts ratings in the other direction. Both undermine fair assessment.
📝
Development plan
A documented plan identifying skills, knowledge, and behaviours to develop, the actions required, and the timeline and support needed. Focuses on growth rather than remediation — appropriate for all performance levels.
🔗
Performance cycle
The complete annual (or periodic) sequence of performance management activities: from objective-setting, through mid-year reviews and ongoing check-ins, to the year-end appraisal and rating.
🗣️
Formative feedback
Ongoing, informal feedback given during the year to support continuous improvement — in contrast to summative feedback delivered at the end of the review period. Most effective when frequent, specific, and timely.
📌
Development area
A specific skill, behaviour, or knowledge gap identified as a priority for professional growth. Should be framed constructively and supported with concrete actions, resources, and a realistic timeline.
⚖️
Appraisal documentation
The written record of a performance review, including assessment of objectives, ratings, evidence, development plans, and agreed next steps. Must be accurate, evidence-based, balanced, and stored securely in line with data protection law.
🔁
Continuous performance management
An approach that replaces or supplements the annual appraisal with frequent check-ins, real-time feedback, and dynamic goal-setting. Aims to make performance conversations more timely, relevant, and developmental.
🧩
Behavioural competency
A defined, observable behaviour or set of behaviours that the organisation values and assesses as part of performance evaluation — such as communication, collaboration, problem-solving, or leadership.
🚦
Performance improvement conversation
A formal or semi-formal discussion with an employee whose performance is below expectations, aimed at identifying barriers, agreeing an improvement plan, and setting clear expectations and consequences. Requires care, consistency, and documentation.
📏
Appraisal language
The specific vocabulary and grammatical structures used in written performance appraisals. Effective appraisal language is specific, evidence-based, balanced, professional, and free from bias, vagueness, or legally problematic absolute claims.

Text 1: The Art and Science of Performance Feedback

Why feedback is the most powerful — and most misused — tool in performance management

Feedback is the cornerstone of effective performance management — and yet one of the most consistently mishandled activities in professional life. Research by Gallup found that employees who receive meaningful feedback on a weekly basis are nearly four times more likely to be engaged at work than those who receive little or none. Yet a landmark study by Kluger and DeNisi in 1996, analysing the results of 607 feedback experiments, found that more than a third of all feedback interventions actually decreased the performance of the person receiving them. The difference between feedback that energises and feedback that demoralises is not simply a matter of content — it is a matter of language, framing, timing, and the relationship in which it is delivered.

The most common language failure in feedback is vagueness. Statements like 'you need to communicate better' or 'your work has been inconsistent' convey almost nothing actionable to the recipient. The SBI framework — Situation, Behaviour, Impact — was developed precisely to address this failure. By describing the specific situation in which a behaviour occurred, the observable behaviour itself, and the measurable or observable impact of that behaviour, the feedback giver provides a clear, objective, and actionable account. SBI also keeps feedback non-personal: it describes what someone did, not who they are, avoiding the defensive reactions that identity-level feedback typically triggers.

Written appraisal language presents distinct challenges. Unlike spoken feedback, appraisal documentation becomes a permanent record — potentially subject to legal scrutiny, formal grievance procedures, and employment tribunal challenge. This creates a dual requirement: the language must be honest and accurate enough to support performance management decisions — including decisions about pay, promotion, and dismissal — and it must also be defensible, evidence-based, and free from discriminatory language. Phrases such as 'he is not a team player' or 'she lacks confidence' are not only vague but can constitute discriminatory language if they reflect unconscious bias or are applied inconsistently across genders, ages, or protected characteristics. The professional HR standard requires that every appraisal observation is linked to a specific, observable behaviour or outcome.

Text 2: Writing Balanced Appraisals — Language That Develops and Defends

How to write performance reviews that motivate, document, and protect the organisation

Writing a balanced performance appraisal combines HR expertise, professional writing ability, and a solid understanding of employment law principles. The word 'balanced' does not mean artificially equal amounts of positive and negative comment, nor softening genuine concerns with hollow praise. A truly balanced appraisal accurately reflects the full picture of an employee's performance — acknowledging genuine achievements with specific evidence, identifying development areas with clear examples, and framing both in language that is constructive, professional, and actionable. The guiding principle: the employee should read the appraisal and understand exactly where they stood, why they received the rating they did, and what they need to do to maintain or improve performance going forward.

Hedging language plays a critical role in professional appraisal writing. When commenting on patterns rather than specific incidents, good appraisal writing uses softening structures: 'there are occasions when David tends to...' rather than 'David always...'; 'Sarah has shown a pattern of...' rather than 'Sarah consistently fails to...'; 'James could benefit from developing his...' rather than 'James is weak at...'. Each hedging structure describes observed behaviour accurately while signalling that the observation is based on a pattern rather than an absolute, leaving room for the employee to disagree with evidence and reducing the risk of a legally challengeable claim.

SMART language is equally important in the forward-looking elements of an appraisal. Development goals that read 'improve presentation skills' are essentially meaningless in a performance management context — they provide no basis for assessing achievement, no timeline, and no clarity about what level of improvement is required. A SMART reformulation — 'Deliver three internal presentations to the senior leadership team by Q3, receiving an average feedback score of 3.5 out of 5 or above' — provides a specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound target that both parties can track and assess. Well-written SMART objectives protect the employee's right to know what is expected and the organisation's ability to manage performance defensibly.

Grammar Reference

Detailed reference notes for all three grammar points in this module.

Grammar Reference 1: Hedging Language in Professional Appraisal Writing

Why hedging is essential in appraisal documentation

Absolute statements in written appraisals create two risks: they can be legally challenged if they cannot be fully evidenced, and they can feel unfair to the employee. Hedging describes patterns accurately while leaving room for the employee to respond with evidence.

Frequency hedges — patterns rather than absolutes

Replace 'always' and 'never' with accurate, defensible frequency patterns:

  • AVOID: 'Sarah always delivers late.' USE: 'There are occasions when Sarah's deliverables have not met the agreed deadline.'
  • AVOID: 'Tom never communicates clearly.' USE: 'Tom's written communications could, on occasion, benefit from greater clarity.'
  • AVOID: 'Emma constantly struggles.' USE: 'Emma has, at times, found this area challenging.'

Pattern and tendency hedges

Use these structures to describe observed patterns without universal claims:

  • 'has shown a pattern of...' — signals a recurring observation
  • 'tends to...' — describes general behaviour without claiming universality
  • 'there appears to be a tendency to...' — careful and qualified
  • 'there have been instances where...' — links to specific evidence

Development hedges — growth rather than criticism

Frame development areas as opportunities:

  • 'could benefit from developing...' — constructive and forward-looking
  • 'would strengthen further by...' — assumes capability, invites growth
  • 'an area for continued focus is...' — ongoing journey
  • 'with continued attention to..., the following progress is expected...'
Grammar Reference 2: Conditional Structures in Performance Conversations

Why conditionals are essential in performance language

Performance conversations involve discussing what could have been done differently, what will happen if improvements are made, and hypothetical scenarios. Each requires a different conditional structure.

First conditional — real, likely future outcomes

If + present simple, will + infinitive. For realistic conditions and expected outcomes — setting forward-looking expectations.

  • 'If you continue to develop your leadership skills, you will be well-positioned for promotion next year.'
  • 'If you meet these objectives, we will be able to discuss an expanded scope.'
  • 'If the gap is not addressed, we will need to initiate a formal support plan.'

Second conditional — hypothetical present or future

If + past simple, would + infinitive. For hypothetical or advisory scenarios.

  • 'If you improved your time management, the quality of your work would reach a higher standard.'
  • 'If I were in your position, I would focus on building stakeholder relationships before the technical gaps.'
  • 'What would you do differently if you could approach that project again?'

Third conditional — past hypotheticals and missed opportunities

If + past perfect, would have + past participle. For reflecting on past events that did not happen.

  • 'If you had sought feedback from the team first, you would have produced a stronger report.'
  • 'If the issue had been escalated earlier, we would have been able to prevent the client complaint.'
  • 'If you had been given more support in Q1, the mid-year results would have looked quite different.'
Grammar Reference 3: Core Performance Review Collocations

Why collocations matter in appraisal English

Performance management has its own fixed verb-noun combinations. Using them correctly signals professional credibility. Incorrect combinations sound unnatural and undermine the authority of written appraisals.

Exceed, meet, and fall short of — performance against targets

The three core verbs describing performance relative to objectives:

  • exceed → expectations, targets, objectives, the required standard
  • meet → expectations, objectives, the agreed targets, the standard
  • fall short of / fail to meet → expectations, targets, the required standard
  • partially meet → objectives (for mixed performance)

Identify, address, and demonstrate — core appraisal verbs

Most frequently used verbs in professional performance documentation:

  • identify → development areas, strengths, a performance gap, opportunities
  • address → a performance gap, a development need, a concern
  • demonstrate → consistent performance, strong skills, improvement, commitment to development
  • deliver → results, objectives, high-quality work, on time and to standard

Set, review, and achieve — objective management language

Standard expressions for the objective management cycle:

  • set → SMART objectives, clear expectations, a development plan, performance targets
  • review → progress, objectives, the development plan, performance against targets
  • achieve → objectives, targets, a significant milestone, the agreed standard

Case Study 1
Adobe's Abolition of the Annual Performance Review — Check-In Culture
USA · Technology · 2012–Present · Continuous Performance Management · How Adobe replaced the annual appraisal with a continuous feedback system and reduced voluntary attrition by 30%
The Problem with the Annual Appraisal

In 2012, Adobe estimated its annual performance review process consumed approximately 80,000 hours of manager time per year — equivalent to 40 full-time employees working for an entire year. Yet research suggested the process produced poor outcomes: employees found it anxiety-inducing and backward-looking, managers found it bureaucratic and divorced from day-to-day realities, and the forced distribution rating system — ranking employees on a curve — was actively damaging team cohesion and collaboration. Adobe's CHRO, Donna Morris, made the decision to abolish the annual review entirely and replace it with regular, ongoing check-in conversations between managers and their direct reports.

The Check-In System

Adobe's check-in system required managers to have regular conversations covering three areas: current expectations (what the employee is working on and whether priorities have shifted), feedback and development (specific observations about recent performance and growth opportunities), and inspiration (the employee's motivation, career aspirations, and how the manager can support longer-term goals). These conversations were deliberately unstructured and undocumented in a formal sense — there was no standardised form or mandatory frequency, though the expectation was that managers would check in meaningfully at least quarterly, with many doing so monthly. Critically, the check-in system was completely decoupled from compensation decisions, which were handled separately through market-benchmarking and manager discretion.

Results and Lessons

Adobe reported a 30% reduction in voluntary attrition in the two years following the introduction of check-ins — a significant outcome given the cost of replacing technology talent. Managers reported spending less time on administrative performance management and more time on coaching and development. The most important lesson from Adobe's experience is not that annual reviews are always wrong, but that the purpose of performance management must be genuinely developmental rather than bureaucratic compliance. Organisations that replicated Adobe's success share one characteristic: their managers were trained and supported to have high-quality feedback conversations — not simply freed from paperwork. Removing the annual review without investing in manager capability tends to produce a vacuum, not improvement.

Case Study 2
Microsoft's Cultural Transformation — From Stack Ranking to Growth Mindset
USA · Technology · 2013–Present · Performance Culture Transformation · How Microsoft replaced a destructive performance ranking system with a growth mindset culture and transformed employee engagement
The Damage of Stack Ranking

From the 1990s until 2013, Microsoft used a 'stack ranking' performance management system — a forced distribution model in which, regardless of actual performance, a fixed percentage of employees in every team were required to be rated as top, average, and low performers at risk of termination. The system had several devastating consequences. It incentivised political behaviour over genuine collaboration, since helping a colleague could improve their ranking at the expense of your own. Teams were reluctant to hire talented people because a new high performer could threaten existing team members' rankings. And the system created a culture of fear fundamentally at odds with the risk-taking and innovation Microsoft needed to compete in a rapidly changing technology landscape.

The Shift to Growth Mindset

In 2013, new CEO Satya Nadella and the HR leadership team abolished stack ranking and replaced it with a performance philosophy rooted in Carol Dweck's growth mindset theory — the idea that talents and abilities are developed through effort and learning, not fixed at birth. The new system evaluated employees not just on what they achieved, but on how: whether they modelled learning and growth, enabled the success of others, and contributed to innovation. Feedback language shifted dramatically: instead of 'where does this person rank relative to peers', managers were asked 'what has this person learned and how have they contributed to others' learning?'

Outcomes and Lessons

The cultural transformation under Nadella is widely credited as a significant factor in Microsoft's remarkable recovery: from approximately $290 billion market capitalisation when Nadella joined in 2014 to over $2 trillion by 2023. Employee engagement improved consistently, collaboration increased, and Microsoft's employer brand strengthened significantly. The HR lesson is that the language and metrics of performance management are never neutral — they shape behaviour at scale. A system rewarding relative performance at the expense of peers creates a competitive, self-protective culture. A system rewarding learning, contribution, and collaboration creates the opposite. The most important performance management decision is not the format of the appraisal form, but the values and behaviours the organisation chooses to measure and reward.
