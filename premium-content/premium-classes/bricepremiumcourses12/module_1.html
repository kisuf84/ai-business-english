Module Overview

Welcome to Module 1: Recruitment and Job Ad Language. The recruitment process begins with words. A job advertisement is simultaneously a legal document, a marketing communication, and a reflection of an organisation's culture and values. Written well, it attracts the right candidates efficiently and signals a professional, inclusive employer. Written poorly, it excludes qualified candidates through biased language, attracts mismatched applicants through vague requirements, or exposes the organisation to legal risk through discriminatory wording. For HR professionals working in English, the ability to write and evaluate job advertisements with precision is a fundamental skill.

This module covers the complete language of recruitment — from the structure and vocabulary of job advertisements and person specifications to the principles of inclusive language, the legal framework surrounding recruitment advertising, and the employer branding strategies that define how organisations present themselves to talent. You will study real-world examples, practise writing job ads for different roles and sectors, and develop the critical language skills to evaluate and improve existing recruitment copy.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Identify and explain the key sections of a professional job advertisement — job title, role summary, key responsibilities, requirements, and company overview — and describe the purpose and language conventions of each section.
2
Distinguish between essential and desirable criteria in a person specification and write accurate, legally sound requirements using the correct HR vocabulary and grammatical structures.
3
Identify and correct examples of biased, exclusionary, or legally problematic language in job advertisements — including gendered language, age references, and unnecessary requirements — and replace them with inclusive, legally compliant alternatives.
4
Apply the standard grammar conventions of professional job advertisements — including the use of third-person present simple, action verbs, and parallel structure in bullet-point lists — accurately and fluently.
5
Use the specialist HR vocabulary of recruitment and talent acquisition — including candidate pipeline, employer branding, applicant tracking system, talent pool, and EVP — correctly and naturally in spoken and written HR contexts.
6
Write a compelling, complete, and legally compliant job advertisement for a professional role — incorporating an engaging role summary, clear responsibilities, accurate requirements, and a strong employer brand section.
7
Write a structured person specification that clearly separates essential from desirable criteria, uses measurable language, and avoids discriminatory requirements.
8
Listen to and understand HR team discussions, job brief meetings, recruitment advertising briefings, and inclusive hiring workshops — extracting key information about role requirements and recruitment strategy.

📚 Word Bank

Key Vocabulary

21 essential terms for recruitment, job advertising, and HR talent acquisition in professional English.

Module Vocabulary
📋
Job description (JD)
A formal document that outlines the duties, responsibilities, purpose, and scope of a role — as well as its position within the organisational structure. The job description is the internal HR document from which a job advertisement is created. It typically includes the job title, department, reporting line, key responsibilities, and performance standards. It is distinct from the person specification, which describes the ideal candidate.
👤
Person specification
A document that describes the ideal candidate for a role — typically divided into Essential and Desirable criteria across categories such as qualifications, experience, skills, knowledge, and personal attributes. Person specifications are used for shortlisting and must be based on genuine job requirements. Any criterion that cannot be justified by the demands of the role may constitute indirect discrimination.
📢
Job advertisement (job ad)
An external communication designed to attract suitable candidates to apply for a vacancy. A job ad is derived from the job description and person specification but is written in a more engaging, candidate-facing style. It must be accurate, compelling, inclusive, and legally compliant. The quality of the job ad directly affects the quality and diversity of the candidate pool.
🎯
Essential criteria
The minimum qualifications, skills, experience, or attributes that a candidate must possess to be considered for a role. Candidates who do not meet essential criteria are excluded from the selection process at shortlisting. Essential criteria must be genuinely required by the role — not merely preferable — and must not inadvertently discriminate against protected groups.
✨
Desirable criteria
Additional qualifications, skills, or experience that would strengthen a candidate's application but are not required for appointment. Desirable criteria are used to differentiate between candidates who all meet the essential requirements. They should be realistic and genuinely relevant to the role rather than an extended wish list that discourages strong candidates from applying.
🏷️
Employer brand
The reputation and identity of an organisation as a place to work — as perceived by current employees, potential candidates, and the wider labour market. Employer brand is communicated through job advertisements, careers pages, social media, employee reviews, and the behaviour of recruitment teams. A strong employer brand reduces time-to-hire, improves application quality, and reduces recruitment costs.
💎
Employee Value Proposition (EVP)
The combination of rewards, benefits, culture, development opportunities, and working conditions that an organisation offers in exchange for an employee's skills and effort. The EVP answers the question: 'Why would a talented person choose to work here rather than anywhere else?' It should be reflected clearly in every job advertisement and wider employer brand communication.
🔍
Applicant Tracking System (ATS)
Software used by organisations to manage the recruitment process — from receiving applications to tracking candidate progress through screening, interview, and offer stages. ATS systems often scan CV and cover letter text for keywords matched against the job advertisement. This means job ads must be written with relevant, searchable terminology to attract candidates who optimise their applications accordingly.
🌊
Talent pool
A database or network of potential candidates — either those who have previously applied, expressed interest, or been identified proactively — that an HR team can draw on when a vacancy arises. Maintaining a talent pool reduces time-to-hire and cost-per-hire. Organisations with strong employer brands naturally attract candidates to their talent pool between active recruitment campaigns.
📊
Candidate pipeline
The total flow of candidates moving through the stages of the recruitment process for a specific role or across multiple roles — from initial applications to shortlisting, interviewing, offering, and hiring. A healthy pipeline has a sufficient number of qualified candidates at each stage. 'Pipeline management' refers to the HR team's active management of candidates through these stages.
⚖️
Equal opportunities statement
A formal declaration — typically included at the end of a job advertisement — confirming that the organisation welcomes applications from all qualified candidates regardless of protected characteristics such as age, gender, ethnicity, disability, religion, or sexual orientation. While the statement itself does not guarantee inclusive practice, its absence is increasingly noted negatively by candidates.
🚫
Indirect discrimination
When a requirement, criterion, or practice that appears neutral on its face puts people with a particular protected characteristic at a disadvantage, without justification. In recruitment, indirect discrimination often occurs through seemingly neutral requirements — such as specifying a particular qualification route or requiring full-time availability — that disproportionately disadvantage women, older workers, or people with disabilities.
🔤
Inclusive language
Language that is neutral, welcoming, and free from assumptions about the identity, background, or characteristics of the reader. In job advertisements, inclusive language avoids gendered pronouns and adjectives, age-related references, cultural idioms, and unnecessarily coded language that signals a preference for a particular type of candidate. Research consistently shows that inclusive job ads attract a more diverse candidate pool.
💼
Talent acquisition
The strategic HR function responsible for identifying, attracting, and hiring talent — as distinct from simple recruitment. Talent acquisition takes a long-term view, building pipelines, developing employer brand, and aligning hiring strategy with business objectives. A talent acquisition team does not merely fill vacancies; it proactively shapes the future workforce.
⏱️
Time-to-hire
The number of days from when a vacancy is approved to when an offer is accepted. Time-to-hire is a key recruitment efficiency metric. A long time-to-hire can indicate poor job ad quality (attracting too few or wrong-fit candidates), slow interview processes, or a competitive talent market. Reducing time-to-hire without compromising quality is a central goal of effective talent acquisition.
💰
Cost-per-hire
The total investment required to fill a single vacancy — including advertising costs, recruiter time, agency fees, and onboarding costs — divided by the number of hires. Cost-per-hire is used to assess recruitment efficiency. Strong employer branding and a healthy talent pool reduce cost-per-hire by reducing reliance on paid advertising and recruitment agencies.
🧲
Sourcing
The proactive process of identifying and approaching potential candidates who may not be actively looking for a new role — through LinkedIn, professional networks, referrals, talent pools, and direct outreach. Sourcing is a core skill of talent acquisition teams and is particularly important for senior, specialist, or hard-to-fill roles where active candidates are rare.
📝
Job brief
An internal meeting or document in which a hiring manager communicates the requirements, context, and priorities of a role to the HR or recruitment team. The job brief is the starting point for creating the job description and job advertisement. A thorough job brief prevents misalignment between what the hiring manager expects and what the job ad communicates to candidates.
🎯
Requirement inflation
The tendency to add unnecessary qualifications, experience levels, or skills to a job advertisement beyond what the role genuinely requires — often resulting in fewer applications, a less diverse candidate pool, and longer time-to-hire. Research has shown that women are less likely than men to apply for a role when they do not meet every listed requirement, making requirement inflation a particular D&I risk.
📍
Vacancy
An open position within an organisation that needs to be filled. A vacancy may arise through a new role being created, an existing employee leaving, a secondment, a promotion, or business growth. Authorising a vacancy — getting approval to hire — is typically the first step in the recruitment process. The vacancy brief then informs the job description and job advertisement.
🌐
Employer value proposition (EVP) pillars
The specific categories of value that make up an organisation's EVP — typically including compensation and benefits, career development, culture and values, work environment, and purpose. Each pillar should be addressed in a job advertisement and wider employer brand communication. The most compelling EVPs are authentic, differentiated, and specific — not generic claims like 'great place to work'.

Text 1: The Art and Science of Writing a Compelling Job Advertisement

Why most job ads fail to attract the right candidates — and how to write one that works

A job advertisement has a more demanding communication task than almost any other professional document. In the space of four to eight paragraphs, it must simultaneously attract the attention of the right candidates, accurately describe the role and its requirements, communicate the culture and values of the organisation, and motivate a qualified person to invest time in an application — all while complying with equality legislation and avoiding the linguistic traps that cause talented candidates from underrepresented groups to self-exclude. Most job advertisements fail at most of these tasks simultaneously. They are too long, written from the employer's perspective rather than the candidate's, filled with requirement inflation that discourages strong applicants, and padded with generic statements about company values that no candidate finds convincing.

The structure of an effective job advertisement follows a consistent and logical sequence. The job title must be both accurate and recognisable — using standard market terminology rather than internal jargon that candidates will not search for. A well-crafted role summary of two to four sentences should answer the three questions every candidate asks when they first read an ad: What will I actually be doing? Who will I be doing it with? And why does this role matter? The responsibilities section should be a concise list of the most important duties — typically five to eight bullet points — written using strong action verbs in the present tense and ordered by importance rather than alphabetically. The requirements section is where legal precision becomes critical: essential criteria must be genuinely necessary for the role, and each criterion should be expressed as an observable, assessable capability rather than a vague character trait.

The employer brand section — often called 'About Us' or 'Why Join Us' — is the section most consistently written poorly and read most carefully by candidates. Generic statements such as 'we are a dynamic, fast-growing company with a passion for excellence' are entirely unconvincing to experienced professionals and actively signal a lack of self-awareness. The most effective employer brand sections are specific, honest, and candidate-facing: they address what the organisation genuinely offers the candidate in terms of development, culture, flexibility, and impact — not what the organisation wishes to be perceived as. The advertisement should close with a clear, low-friction call to action — how to apply, by when, and what to include — and an equal opportunities statement that signals a genuine commitment to inclusive hiring.

Text 2: Inclusive Language in Job Advertisements — The Evidence, the Law, and the Practice

How the words in a job ad determine who applies — and who doesn't

The relationship between the language used in a job advertisement and the diversity of the resulting candidate pool is one of the most thoroughly evidenced phenomena in organisational psychology. Studies by researchers at Waterloo and Duke universities found that job advertisements using stereotypically masculine language — words such as 'dominant', 'competitive', 'aggressive', 'ambitious', and 'driven', as well as phrasing like 'hit targets', 'crush the competition', and 'rockstar performer' — were perceived as less appealing by women and by candidates from certain cultural backgrounds, even when the role itself was equally suited to all genders. The effect was not that these candidates judged themselves as less capable; they judged the culture as one in which they would not thrive, which is a rational and accurate response to signal-laden language.

The legal dimension of job advertisement language is equally significant. Under equality legislation in the UK, USA, EU member states, and most other jurisdictions, it is unlawful to express a preference for, or to discourage applications from, candidates with protected characteristics — including age, gender, race, disability, religion, and sexual orientation. Seemingly neutral requirements can constitute indirect discrimination if they disproportionately disadvantage a protected group. Requiring 'recent graduates only' may disadvantage older workers. Requiring 'native English speaker' may discriminate on the basis of national origin unless there is a genuine operational requirement. Requiring 'full-time availability only' may disproportionately disadvantage women, who still bear a disproportionate share of caregiving responsibilities in most societies.

The practical guidance that follows from both the evidence and the law is relatively straightforward: job advertisements should use gender-neutral language throughout, replace coded masculine or feminine adjectives with skill-based descriptions of what the role actually requires, limit requirements to those that are genuinely necessary, avoid age-related references, and include a clear equal opportunities statement. Technology tools such as Textio and Gender Decoder can identify potentially exclusionary language in job ad drafts. However, the most effective approach is not algorithmic but cultural: organisations that genuinely value diversity train their HR teams and hiring managers to understand why these language choices matter, and build inclusive language review into the standard job ad approval process.

🔍 Grammar Review

Grammar Reference

Detailed reference notes covering all three grammar points in this module.

Grammar Reference 1: Verb Forms in Professional Job Advertisements

Why verb form consistency matters in job ads

Job advertisements follow specific grammatical conventions that signal professionalism and clarity. Inconsistent verb forms — mixing infinitives, gerunds, and passive constructions in the same section — look unprofessional and can confuse candidates about what is required versus what is preferred. Each section of a job ad has its own standard grammatical pattern.

Responsibilities section — base infinitive (imperative form)

Bullet points in the responsibilities section use the base infinitive without 'to' — as if giving an instruction. This is the most concise, direct, and professional form. All bullets in the same section should use the same form.

  • Manage a portfolio of enterprise client accounts.
  • Develop and implement quarterly performance review processes.
  • Collaborate with cross-functional teams to ensure successful delivery.
  • Report on team performance metrics to senior leadership monthly.

Requirements section — noun phrases and modal structures

Requirements are most commonly expressed as noun phrases (without a verb) for clean bullet lists, or using 'should have / must have / will have' for sentence form. Avoid mixing forms within the same section.

  • Noun phrase form: 'Minimum three years' experience in a relevant HR role.'
  • Modal form: 'Candidates should have demonstrable project management experience.'
  • Avoid: 'Having three years' experience is required.' (gerund — awkward)
  • Avoid: 'You will be having experience.' (wrong tense)

Role summary and company overview — third-person present simple

The role summary and company overview sections use standard declarative sentences in the third person present simple — describing what the role involves and what the company is.

  • This is an exciting opportunity for an experienced HR Business Partner to join our growing People team.
  • The successful candidate will partner with senior leaders to develop and deliver the people strategy.
  • CloudPath is a SaaS company serving 3,000+ enterprise clients across 40 countries.
Grammar Reference 2: Noun Phrases for Professional Job Requirements

Why noun phrases are preferred in person specifications

Professional requirements in person specifications and job advertisements are most commonly expressed as noun phrases rather than full sentences. This produces a clean, parallel, scannable structure. Using full sentences for some requirements and noun phrases for others breaks the parallelism and looks inconsistent.

Structure of a professional requirement noun phrase

The standard pattern is: Adjective(s) + noun (head) + optional qualifier. The noun should be concrete and assessable — not a vague character trait.

  • Excellent [written and verbal] communication skills [at all organisational levels]
  • Minimum [three years'] experience [in a generalist HR or HRBP role]
  • Proven track record [of leading and developing high-performing teams]
  • Strong analytical skills [with proficiency in Excel or equivalent data tools]

Avoiding vague trait language

Person specifications should describe skills and experiences that can be assessed at interview — not character traits that cannot be observed or evidenced. Replace trait language with skill or behaviour language.

  • ✗ 'A team player' → ✓ 'Demonstrated ability to collaborate effectively across cross-functional teams'
  • ✗ 'Passionate about HR' → ✓ 'Strong commitment to best-practice HR and continuous professional development'
  • ✗ 'Good communicator' → ✓ 'Excellent written and verbal communication skills, including the ability to present complex data clearly to non-specialist audiences'
  • ✗ 'Leadership skills' → ✓ 'Proven experience leading and motivating teams of five or more in a high-pressure environment'

Essential vs desirable — phrasing signals

The language used in the requirement signals whether it is essential or desirable.

  • Essential: 'Degree-level qualification or equivalent professional experience' / 'minimum x years' / 'proven' / 'demonstrable'
  • Desirable: 'Experience of…would be an advantage' / 'Familiarity with…is desirable' / 'CIPD qualification (preferable)'
Grammar Reference 3: Inclusive Language — Rewrites and Standards

Why word choice determines who applies

Specific vocabulary choices in job advertisements send signals about the type of person the organisation is looking for — signals that candidates from different backgrounds decode differently. Inclusive language replaces coded terminology with neutral, skill-focused alternatives without losing precision or professional tone.

Gendered language — common problems and fixes

Many job ads contain language that research associates with either masculine or feminine stereotypes. Neither is inclusive when the role is open to all.

  • ✗ 'Rockstar / ninja / guru' → ✓ 'Expert / specialist / highly experienced'
  • ✗ 'Dominant / aggressive / competitive' → ✓ 'Confident / results-driven / commercially focused'
  • ✗ 'Supportive / nurturing / collaborative' (if all three, may code feminine) → ✓ Balance with outcome-focused language
  • ✗ 'He/she will be responsible for...' → ✓ 'The successful candidate will be responsible for...'
  • ✗ 'Manpower' → ✓ 'Workforce / headcount / team capacity'

Age-related language — avoid or remove

Any reference that implies a preferred age range is potentially discriminatory in most jurisdictions.

  • ✗ 'Young professional' / 'recent graduate' / 'energetic' (as an age proxy) → ✓ Remove or replace with skill-based language
  • ✗ 'Digital native' → ✓ 'Confident and proficient in digital tools and platforms'
  • ✗ 'New blood' → ✓ 'Fresh perspectives / innovative thinking'

Availability and lifestyle requirements

Requirements that assume a particular lifestyle or personal situation can constitute indirect discrimination.

  • ✗ 'Must be available to work unsociable hours' (without justification) → ✓ Specify the actual schedule requirement
  • ✗ 'No family commitments' / 'free to travel at short notice' → ✓ 'Some international travel required; scheduling will be agreed in advance'
  • ✗ 'Full-time only — no flexibility' → ✓ 'Full-time role; flexible working arrangements may be considered'

Case Study 1
Unilever's Inclusive Job Ad Transformation — How One FMCG Giant Rewrote Its Recruitment Language
UK / Global · Consumer Goods · 2018–2022 · Inclusive Recruitment · How Unilever used language analytics to identify bias in job ads and measurably increase diversity in its candidate pools
Background

Unilever, one of the world's largest consumer goods companies with over 150,000 employees in 190 countries, launched a systematic review of its job advertisement language in 2018 as part of its broader commitment to inclusion and equity. The organisation had set ambitious targets for gender parity and representation across its leadership pipeline, but analysis revealed that its recruitment advertising was working against these goals: job ads for technical, commercial, and leadership roles were using disproportionately masculine-coded language that was depressing application rates from women, while ads for administrative and support roles were using feminine-coded language that was suppressing male and non-binary applications. The problem was systemic — it had been inherited over decades of role-specific hiring norms — and it was invisible to the hiring managers who had written the ads.

The Language Analytics Approach

Unilever partnered with Textio, a language analytics platform that analyses job advertisement text against a database of recruitment outcomes from hundreds of millions of job ads globally. The platform identifies words and phrases that are associated with particular demographic application patterns and suggests neutral, high-performing alternatives. The analysis revealed that Unilever's engineering and supply chain job ads contained 2.4 times more masculine-coded language than its marketing and HR ads, and that this correlated directly with the gender split of the resulting applicant pools. By systematically replacing exclusionary language with neutral alternatives — 'exceeded targets' instead of 'crushed the competition', 'confident stakeholder management' instead of 'dominant presence' — without changing the role requirements themselves, Unilever achieved a 16% increase in female applicants for technical roles within 18 months.

Lessons for HR Professionals

Unilever's experience demonstrates several principles that apply to any organisation seeking to improve the inclusivity of its recruitment process. First, language bias in job advertisements is typically invisible to those writing them — it requires external analysis or structured review to surface. Second, inclusive language does not mean weaker language: replacing masculine-coded terms with neutral, skill-focused alternatives does not reduce the clarity or attractiveness of the ad to any candidate; it expands the pool. Third, systematic change requires process change, not just awareness: training hiring managers to recognise exclusionary language is insufficient without building a review step into the job ad approval workflow. Finally, the business case is quantifiable: a 16% increase in qualified female applicants for technical roles represents a material expansion of the talent pool from which Unilever could hire — directly improving the quality of its long-term workforce.

Case Study 2
The BBC's Gender-Neutral Job Ad Initiative — Rewriting Recruitment for the 21st Century
UK · Broadcasting & Media · 2017–Present · Inclusive Recruitment Language · How the BBC systematically rewrote its job advertisement templates to remove gendered and exclusionary language across all divisions
Background

The BBC, the United Kingdom's public broadcaster, faced significant public scrutiny from 2017 onwards following revelations about gender pay gaps within its senior workforce. One outcome of this scrutiny was a deep review of the organisation's recruitment practices — examining whether its hiring processes were systematically advantaging certain groups from the very first point of contact: the job advertisement. The BBC's internal analysis, conducted in partnership with its D&I team and external language consultants, found that a significant proportion of its job advertisements contained exclusionary language, gender-coded terminology, and requirements that were either inflated or ambiguous. The findings were particularly pronounced in technical and editorial roles, where job ads had historically been written by departmental managers with limited HR oversight and no standard language guidance.

The Reform Programme

The BBC's response was structured and organisation-wide. It developed a set of standardised job advertisement templates for each role category, incorporating mandatory inclusive language standards developed collaboratively between HR, D&I specialists, and legal teams. All requirements were reviewed against the following test: can this criterion be directly linked to a specific, observable requirement of the role? Any criterion that failed this test was removed or rewritten. The language guidelines specifically prohibited age-related references, gendered adjectives, and availability assumptions that could not be justified by genuine operational requirements. Hiring managers were required to attend a two-hour inclusive recruitment briefing before posting any advertisement. The BBC also adopted salary transparency for all roles below senior executive level — a decision that research showed increased application rates, particularly from candidates from lower socioeconomic backgrounds who were more likely to self-exclude without salary information.

Lessons and Outcomes

The BBC's reform programme produced measurable results: applications from women for technical roles increased by 22% in the first full year of the new system, and applications from candidates from ethnic minority backgrounds increased by 18%. The programme also produced an unexpected secondary benefit: the standardised templates and mandatory review process significantly reduced the average time spent by hiring managers on writing job ads, because they had a clear framework to work within rather than starting from scratch. The broader lesson for HR professionals is that inclusive recruitment language is not merely a compliance or ethics issue — it is a talent strategy issue. Organisations that systematically exclude qualified candidates from applying through biased language are narrowing their talent pool, increasing their time-to-hire, and ultimately reducing the quality of their workforce relative to those who have solved the language problem.
