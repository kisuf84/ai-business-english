Module Overview

Welcome to Module 11: Exit Interviews and Termination. Every employment relationship ends — through resignation, redundancy, dismissal, or the natural end of a fixed-term contract. How an organisation manages these endings has profound consequences: for the departing employee's dignity and legal rights, for the organisation's legal exposure and employer brand, and for the employees who remain. The language HR professionals use in these moments — in conversation, in letters, in consultation meetings — must be simultaneously accurate, legally sound, and humanly decent.

This module covers the complete language of employment exit: from conducting effective exit interviews and managing offboarding, to writing dismissal and redundancy letters in the formal register expected of legally precise HR documentation. The grammar focus is on passive voice and nominalisation — the two most important linguistic tools for professional termination writing. The Writing section features Auto-Correct & Get Feedback — instant feedback on your grammar, vocabulary, length, and structure.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Explain the key stages and purposes of a formal offboarding process — including resignation acceptance, handover planning, garden leave, final pay calculation, and reference provision — using accurate HR terminology throughout.
2
Conduct, document, and analyse an exit interview using professional English, asking open questions that uncover genuine departure drivers and recording responses without bias or leading language.
3
Communicate a dismissal, redundancy, or contract non-renewal professionally in written and spoken English, using language that is legally appropriate, factually accurate, and appropriately empathetic.
4
Use the passive voice correctly and consistently in termination documentation — including dismissal letters, redundancy notices, and reference letters — to describe decisions and procedures without assigning unnecessary personal blame.
5
Use abstract nouns and nominalisation correctly to write formal, professional HR documents about termination and departure, transforming verbs and adjectives into the noun-heavy register expected in legal correspondence.
6
Form and use the core exit and termination collocations accurately: serve notice, accept a resignation, conduct an exit interview, issue a P45, provide a reference, and settle a final payment.
7
Write a professionally structured resignation acceptance letter and a redundancy notification letter that are legally appropriate, clear, and appropriately professional in tone.
8
Listen to and understand an exit interview, a redundancy consultation meeting, a dismissal outcome communication, and a leaver's offboarding briefing.

📚 Word Bank

Key Vocabulary

21 essential terms for exit interviews and termination in professional HR English.

Module Vocabulary
🚪
Resignation
The voluntary act by which an employee formally notifies their employer that they intend to end their employment. A valid resignation must be clear, unequivocal, and given in writing when required by the employment contract. The employee must serve their notice period unless a shorter period is agreed or PILON is offered.
⚖️
Dismissal
The termination of an employee's contract by the employer. A dismissal must be for a potentially fair reason — capability, conduct, redundancy, statutory illegality, or some other substantial reason — and must follow a fair procedure. Unfair dismissal occurs when the reason is not fair or the procedure is flawed.
📉
Redundancy
A form of dismissal that occurs when an employer reduces its workforce because a role is no longer required — due to business closure, a reduction in the need for work of a particular kind, or a site closure. Redundancy selection must be fair, and employees with two or more years of service are entitled to statutory redundancy pay.
🌿
Garden leave
A period during which an employee is required to remain away from the workplace during their notice period, while remaining employed and continuing to receive full pay and benefits. Garden leave is typically used for senior employees or those in sensitive roles, to protect confidential information and client relationships.
💼
Offboarding
The structured process of managing an employee's departure from the organisation — including resignation acceptance, handover planning, return of company property, IT access revocation, final pay settlement, exit interview, and leaver documentation. Effective offboarding protects the organisation legally and maintains a positive relationship with the departing employee.
📋
P45
A statutory document that an employer must provide to an employee when their employment ends, showing their total earnings and tax paid in the current tax year. The employee uses the P45 to ensure they are placed on the correct tax code by their new employer and to claim any tax refund owed.
🤝
Settlement agreement
A legally binding agreement between an employer and an employee in which the employee agrees to waive their right to bring specified employment claims in return for a financial payment and other agreed terms. The employee must receive independent legal advice before signing. Often used to resolve disputes or manage sensitive departures.
💳
PILON (Payment in Lieu of Notice)
A payment made by the employer to the departing employee in lieu of requiring them to work their notice period. The employee leaves immediately and receives a lump sum equivalent to the pay they would have received during the notice period. PILON is taxable as earnings.
📝
Reference
A formal statement provided by a former employer to a prospective employer about the departing employee's employment history, role, and performance. Employers have no general legal duty to provide a reference but must not provide a misleading or inaccurate one. Many organisations have a policy to confirm only dates of employment and job title.
🔍
Exit interview
A structured conversation or survey conducted with an employee who is leaving the organisation, aimed at understanding their reasons for departure and gathering feedback that can inform retention and engagement strategy. Exit interviews are limited by social desirability bias — departing employees may not disclose their real reasons for leaving.
⚠️
Constructive dismissal
A situation in which an employee resigns in response to a fundamental breach of contract by the employer — such as a significant reduction in pay, a unilateral change in working conditions, or persistent bullying or harassment. If the breach is serious enough, the resignation is treated as a dismissal in law, and the employee may bring an unfair dismissal claim.
🏛️
Redundancy consultation
A legally required process in which an employer must meet with employees at risk of redundancy to explain the reasons, discuss alternatives, and invite the employee to suggest ways to avoid or reduce redundancies. For 20 or more redundancies, collective consultation with employee representatives is required and must last at least 30 days.
📊
Redundancy selection criteria
The factors used by an employer to determine which employees within a pool are selected for redundancy when not all employees in that pool will be made redundant. Selection criteria must be objective, measurable, and consistently applied — and must not discriminate based on protected characteristics.
🌱
Outplacement support
Assistance provided by an employer to employees who have been made redundant, to help them find new employment. Typically includes CV writing support, interview coaching, career counselling, and access to job search resources. Not a legal requirement but increasingly offered as a demonstration of good faith and corporate responsibility.
😔
Survivor syndrome
The psychological impact experienced by employees who remain with an organisation after a redundancy programme. Common symptoms include guilt, anxiety, reduced morale, increased workload concerns, and reduced trust in leadership. Managing survivor syndrome through honest communication is a critical post-redundancy HR responsibility.
📎
Leaver documentation
The complete set of documents that must be provided to an employee on departure — including the P45, a reference (if the organisation's policy permits), confirmation of final pay calculations, pension transfer information, and any agreed confidentiality obligations. Prompt and complete leaver documentation is a legal and professional obligation.
🔒
Restrictive covenant
A contractual clause that restricts what a former employee can do after leaving — for example, prohibiting them from working for a direct competitor, soliciting former clients, or poaching former colleagues, for a defined period. Restrictive covenants must be reasonable in scope and duration to be enforceable.
🔄
Handover
The process by which a departing employee transfers their knowledge, responsibilities, ongoing projects, and relationships to a colleague or successor before their last day. A well-managed handover is essential for business continuity and is typically planned by the line manager in collaboration with the departing employee.
📣
Constructive feedback at exit
Feedback provided by a departing employee about their experience of the organisation, management, culture, or processes during the exit interview. When collected and analysed systematically, exit feedback provides qualitative insight that can drive meaningful improvements in retention, engagement, and management quality.
🗂️
Statutory redundancy pay
The minimum redundancy payment an employer must make to an eligible employee, calculated based on the employee's age, weekly pay (capped at a statutory maximum), and length of service. Employees must have at least two years of continuous service to qualify. The formula gives 1.5 weeks' pay for each year of service over 41, 1 week's pay for each year between 22 and 40, and half a week's pay for each year under 22.
🎗️
Non-disclosure agreement (NDA)
A legally binding contract restricting one or both parties from disclosing specified confidential information. In employment exits, NDAs are used to protect trade secrets, client information, or — controversially — to prevent employees from disclosing workplace misconduct. The use of NDAs to silence victims of harassment or discrimination has been widely criticised and is subject to increasing legal restriction.

Text 1: The Exit Interview — Data, Bias, and the Art of the Honest Conversation

Why exit interviews underdeliver — and how to design one that actually works

The exit interview is one of the most consistently underperforming HR tools in organisational practice. In theory, it represents a unique opportunity: the departing employee has nothing to lose by being honest, is no longer subject to the political constraints of ongoing employment, and possesses first-hand knowledge of the real reasons they are choosing to leave. In practice, the data collected is frequently sanitised, partial, and structurally biased in ways that make it unreliable as a basis for organisational improvement. Understanding why exit interview data fails — and how to design processes that collect more honest and actionable information — is a core competency for HR professionals.

The primary source of bias in exit interviews is social desirability bias. Most people, even when leaving an organisation they are unhappy with, do not want to cause conflict, damage relationships, or create a hostile reference situation on their way out of the door. As a result, they give acceptable rather than honest answers. 'I am leaving for a better opportunity' is consistently the most cited reason in exit interviews — and is also consistently the least likely to be the real reason. The real reasons — a poor relationship with a specific manager, a sense of being passed over, a culture of overwork with no recognition, feeling undervalued or invisible — are rarely disclosed in formal exit interviews, particularly when the interview is conducted by an HR professional the employee does not know or trust, or when there is any ambiguity about how the information will be used or who will see it.

Organisations that collect exit interview data effectively tend to share several design features. First, the interview is conducted by someone other than the employee's line manager — typically an HR Business Partner or, for senior leavers, an external consultant. Second, confidentiality is explicit, credible, and limited: the employee is told clearly that individual responses will not be attributed to them personally, and that only aggregated themes will be shared with leadership — but they are not told that nothing will be shared, which would undermine the entire point of the exercise. Third, the questions are specific and behavioural rather than abstract — not 'Were you happy here?' but 'Can you describe a specific moment when you decided to start looking for another role?' Fourth, the data is actioned: leadership receives thematic reports, specific patterns are investigated, and employees who are still present can see that something has changed as a result. Exit interviews where the data disappears into a database and is never acted upon actively damage the organisation's credibility with remaining employees.

Text 2: Managing Termination with Dignity — The Language of Endings

How HR professionals communicate dismissal, redundancy, and departure in ways that are legally sound and humanly decent

The language of termination is one of the most challenging areas of professional HR communication. At the moment of dismissal or redundancy, an employee is receiving news that will fundamentally alter their financial security, professional identity, and daily life. The HR professional in that room — or on that video call — must simultaneously fulfil multiple obligations that pull in different directions: to communicate the decision accurately and without ambiguity; to explain the legal basis and process clearly; to ensure the employee understands their rights, including the right of appeal; and to do all of this in a way that preserves the employee's dignity and does not expose the organisation to unnecessary legal risk. The language that achieves all of this is not a set of scripts — it is a set of principles that HR professionals must internalise and apply with judgement.

The first principle is factual accuracy without punitive language. A dismissal letter that describes the employee as 'dishonest' or 'untrustworthy' is both legally risky and humanly unnecessary when 'the investigation found that company funds were misappropriated in three instances' communicates the same facts more precisely and defensibly. Active voice in termination language can feel accusatory — 'you failed to...' — while passive voice — 'the investigation concluded that...' — describes the same situation from a more neutral, institutional perspective that is both more professional and more legally defensible. The legal test for a fair dismissal does not require the employer to use damaging personal characterisations of the employee — it requires the employer to demonstrate that a fair process was followed and that the decision fell within the range of reasonable responses.

The second principle is clarity without false comfort. One of the most common HR communication errors in termination settings is the use of softening language that leaves the employee genuinely uncertain about whether a decision has actually been made. 'We have been thinking about the direction of the team' and 'things may need to change' are not an adequate communication of a redundancy. Similarly, during a formal redundancy consultation, language that suggests the outcome is already decided — when consultation is legally required to be genuine — is both legally dangerous and corrosive of trust. The HR professional must find the narrow space between ambiguity (which creates false hope and causes additional distress when the outcome is confirmed) and predetermination (which makes a mockery of the consultation process and exposes the organisation to legal challenge). That space is: 'We are at risk of redundancy, the process requires us to consider alternatives genuinely, and no final decision has been made — but I want to be honest with you about the context.'

Grammar Reference

Detailed reference notes for all three grammar points in this module.

Grammar Reference 1: Passive Voice in Termination Documentation

Why passive voice dominates termination documents

Termination documents — dismissal letters, redundancy notices, settlement agreements — use the passive voice for two critical reasons: (1) it describes decisions and findings institutionally rather than personally, reducing the accusatory tone; and (2) it is more legally defensible — describing what the investigation found, not what the person did.

Decision passives — confirming what has been decided

Use will be + past participle or has been + past participle for formal decisions:

  • 'Your employment will be terminated with effect from 31st March 2025.'
  • 'It has been decided that a final written warning should be issued.'
  • 'Your role has been identified as redundant following the restructure of the Operations division.'

Finding passives — reporting investigation outcomes

Use was + past participle or has been + past participle for investigation findings:

  • 'The investigation concluded that the conduct amounted to gross misconduct.'
  • 'It was found that company property had been removed without authorisation.'
  • 'The allegation was upheld following a thorough review of the evidence.'

Process passives — describing what will happen

Use will be + past participle for future process steps in leaver communications:

  • 'You will be informed of the outcome of your appeal within five working days.'
  • 'A final payment will be made to you within 14 days of your leaving date.'
  • 'Your P45 will be provided on your last day of employment.'
  • 'All employees at risk of redundancy will be consulted individually.'
Grammar Reference 2: Nominalisation and Abstract Nouns in Formal HR Documents

What is nominalisation and why does it matter in HR writing?

Nominalisation is the process of converting verbs or adjectives into noun forms. Formal HR documents — especially legal correspondence — use nominalised language to create an authoritative, impersonal, professional register. Over-reliance on verbs sounds conversational; nominalised language sounds institutional.

Common verb-to-noun transformations in HR termination writing

Learn these patterns — they appear constantly in formal HR correspondence:

  • decide → decision: 'We write to inform you of our DECISION to terminate your employment.'
  • conclude → conclusion: 'The CONCLUSION of the investigation was that misconduct had occurred.'
  • accept → acceptance: 'We write to confirm our ACCEPTANCE of your resignation.'
  • consult → consultation: 'A 30-day CONSULTATION period will begin immediately.'
  • apply → application: 'The fair APPLICATION of selection criteria was essential.'
  • contribute → contribution: 'We value your CONTRIBUTION over your years of service.'

Adjective-to-noun transformations

Adjectives are also nominalised in formal HR documents:

  • redundant → redundancy: 'Your role has been identified for REDUNDANCY.'
  • confidential → confidentiality: 'All parties are bound by a duty of CONFIDENTIALITY.'
  • disruptive → disruption: 'We acknowledge the DISRUPTION this decision may cause.'

Why nominalisation makes writing more formal

Compare these two versions of the same information:

  • INFORMAL (verb-heavy): 'We decided to terminate you because you acted dishonestly.'
  • FORMAL (nominalised): 'The decision to terminate your employment was reached following a finding of gross misconduct.'
  • The nominalised version is more impersonal, more defensible, and more appropriate for legal correspondence.
Grammar Reference 3: Core Exit and Termination Collocations

Why collocations signal professionalism in termination language

Exit and termination language has its own precise vocabulary of verb-noun combinations. Using the correct pairings is a marker of HR professional expertise. Using the wrong verb — however close in meaning — signals unfamiliarity with the professional register.

Serve, accept, and conduct — core process verbs

The most frequently used verbs for describing the departure process:

  • serve → notice, a notice period, their notice, garden leave
  • accept → a resignation, an appeal, a settlement, an offer of outplacement
  • conduct → an exit interview, a redundancy consultation, a disciplinary hearing

Issue, provide, and settle — documentation and payment verbs

These expressions describe the administrative and financial elements of departure:

  • issue → a P45, a formal warning, a redundancy notice, a settlement offer
  • provide → a reference, outplacement support, written confirmation, leaver documentation
  • settle → a final payment, outstanding holiday pay, a dispute, a claim

Appeal, exercise, and waive — rights and legal language

These broader expressions describe the legal dimension of termination:

  • appeal → a decision, a dismissal, a redundancy selection
  • exercise → the right of appeal, the right to be accompanied, the right to a reference
  • waive → the right to bring a claim, the right to a hearing, a contractual entitlement

Case Study 1
The P&O Ferries Mass Dismissal — A Case Study in How Not to Handle Redundancy
UK · Maritime Transport · March 2022 · Mass Dismissal and Employment Law · How P&O Ferries' decision to dismiss 800 workers without consultation became one of the most studied cases of employment law violation in recent UK history
What Happened

On 17th March 2022, P&O Ferries dismissed approximately 800 seafarers without any advance warning, without any consultation, and without following the statutory redundancy process required under UK law. Workers were informed via a pre-recorded video message while physically on board ships. Security contractors were simultaneously deployed to ports to remove employees from vessels. The employees were replaced immediately by agency workers paid below the UK National Living Wage. P&O's CEO, Peter Hebblethwaite, subsequently appeared before a UK Parliamentary Select Committee and confirmed that he had known the dismissals were unlawful before they occurred but had made a commercial decision to proceed anyway, calculating that the cost of settlements and fines would be less than the benefit of the cost savings achieved. The statement attracted widespread condemnation from politicians, trade unions, employment lawyers, and the public.

The Legal Failures

The P&O dismissals violated multiple legal requirements simultaneously. Under the Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992, collective consultation with employee representatives is required at least 45 days before dismissing 100 or more employees. P&O dismissed 800 employees with zero notice and zero consultation. Under the Employment Rights Act 1996, employees with two or more years of service have the right not to be unfairly dismissed — requiring a fair reason and a fair process. P&O followed neither. Settlement payments were offered to affected workers, conditional on signing NDAs — a use of NDAs to silence workers' legal claims that attracted significant criticism. The organisation also failed to notify the Secretary of State in advance, as required when making 100 or more redundancies — a criminal offence. Despite the CEO's public admission that the process was unlawful, no criminal prosecution followed.

HR Lessons

The P&O case is a stark illustration of what happens when employment law obligations are treated as optional variables in a commercial cost-benefit calculation. For HR professionals, the case contains several critical lessons. First, collective consultation is not a formality — it is a substantive legal right that cannot be waived by a commercial decision, however financially convenient. Second, the use of NDAs to silence workers' legitimate employment claims is ethically indefensible and increasingly subject to legal restriction. Third, mass redundancies conducted without proper process do not simply create legal liability — they destroy employer brand, damage the organisation's ability to recruit in the future, and can cause lasting reputational harm that far exceeds any short-term cost saving. Finally, the CEO's public admission of knowing illegality was an extraordinary illustration of a leadership team that had disconnected its decision-making entirely from both legal obligation and basic human decency.

The Business Case for Excellent Offboarding

Research by Glassdoor and LinkedIn consistently shows that the experience an employee has on the way out of an organisation has a disproportionate effect on employer brand relative to its cost. A departing employee who is treated with professionalism, dignity, and genuine appreciation will almost always leave a positive Glassdoor review, speak positively about the organisation to their new colleagues and networks, and potentially return as a rehire in the future. Research by Workplace Intelligence found that 76% of employees who had a positive offboarding experience indicated they would consider returning to the organisation in the future — compared to only 19% who had a poor experience. The term for employees who return after leaving is 'boomerang employees', and they are increasingly valued by HR professionals because they arrive with lower onboarding costs, faster time to productivity, and a genuine commitment that is informed by experience of the external market.

What Excellent Offboarding Looks Like

Organisations recognised for excellent offboarding share several characteristics. First, the process is structured but personalised — there is a consistent framework (offboarding checklist, exit interview, reference policy, leaver documentation) but the communication is adapted to the individual and their reasons for leaving. Second, the line manager plays a central role — not just the HR team. A personal conversation from the line manager acknowledging the employee's contribution, wishing them well, and maintaining the relationship is often cited by departing employees as the most memorable and valued element of the offboarding experience. Third, the organisation stays in touch — through alumni networks, LinkedIn connections, and — for senior leavers — a structured alumni engagement programme. McKinsey's alumni network is perhaps the most well-known example: former McKinsey employees report a strong sense of ongoing identity and affiliation with the firm, which benefits both parties professionally.

The Cost of Poor Offboarding

Poor offboarding is not merely a missed opportunity — it is an active source of reputational and commercial harm. An employee who is dismissed without explanation, made redundant without dignity, or processed through an impersonal checklist and escorted from the building without acknowledgement will almost invariably share that experience — on Glassdoor, on LinkedIn, in conversations with colleagues and industry contacts. In a talent market where employer reputation has a measurable effect on the quality and cost of future recruitment, the financial value of treating every departure with professionalism is significant. HR professionals who champion excellent offboarding are not simply being humane — they are protecting and building one of their organisation's most important strategic assets: its reputation as an employer.
