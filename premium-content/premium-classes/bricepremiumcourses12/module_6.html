Module Overview

Welcome to Module 6: HR Policies and Compliance Terms. HR professionals operate within a complex framework of legal obligations, organisational policies, and regulatory requirements. The ability to understand, draft, and communicate policies clearly in professional English is not merely a language skill — it is a legal and organisational necessity. Poorly written policies are unenforceable. Ambiguous compliance language creates risk. Unclear communication of rights and obligations undermines trust and exposes organisations to challenge.

This module covers the complete language of HR policy and compliance: from core employment law terminology and equality legislation vocabulary, through data protection and GDPR language, to the grammar of obligation and prohibition used in policy documents, and the writing skills needed to produce legally sound, professionally clear policy communications. The Writing section features Auto-Correct & Get Feedback — instant, detailed feedback on your grammar, vocabulary, length, and structure.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Explain the key areas of employment law that HR professionals must understand — including the equality duty, unfair dismissal, data protection obligations, and the contract of employment — and use the correct legal terminology accurately.
2
Read and interpret HR policy documents written in formal English, identifying the purpose, scope, obligations, and consequences sections, and explaining their meaning in plain language.
3
Use modal verbs of obligation, prohibition, and permission — must, shall, may, must not, are required to, are entitled to — correctly and consistently in the context of HR policy writing.
4
Apply passive voice and impersonal structures correctly to write legally appropriate policy language that describes obligations and procedures without assigning unnecessary personal blame.
5
Form and use the core HR policy and compliance collocations accurately: raise a grievance, breach a policy, comply with legislation, invoke a procedure, and uphold a complaint.
6
Write a professionally structured HR policy brief and a formal disciplinary outcome letter using the correct register, structure, and compliance language appropriate to each document type.
7
Distinguish between formal, technical, and plain English registers in HR communication and explain when each is appropriate for different audiences — from policy documents to employee communications.
8
Listen to and understand a disciplinary hearing briefing, a GDPR compliance briefing, a grievance procedure explanation, and an equality and diversity training session.

Module Vocabulary
⚖️
Equality Act 2010
UK legislation protecting individuals from discrimination based on nine protected characteristics: age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, and sexual orientation. A cornerstone of UK employment law.
🔒
GDPR
General Data Protection Regulation — a comprehensive EU and UK data protection framework governing how personal data including employee data must be collected, stored, processed, and deleted. Breaches carry significant fines and reputational consequences.
📋
Disciplinary procedure
A formal process followed when an employee's conduct or performance falls below the required standard. Must comply with the ACAS Code of Practice and includes investigation, notification, hearing, decision, and right of appeal.
📣
Grievance procedure
A formal process that allows an employee to raise a complaint about working conditions, treatment by colleagues or management, or any other workplace concern. Employees must be given a reasonable opportunity to raise and resolve grievances.
🚨
Gross misconduct
Serious workplace behaviour that fundamentally breaches the employment contract — such as theft, fraud, violence, or serious health and safety violations — and that may justify summary dismissal without notice or a prior warning.
🛡️
Protected characteristic
One of nine categories under the Equality Act 2010 that form the basis of unlawful discrimination claims: age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, and sexual orientation.
📝
Contract of employment
A legally binding agreement between employer and employee setting out terms and conditions of the employment relationship, including pay, hours, duties, notice periods, and entitlements. Must be provided in writing within two months of starting.
🔊
Whistleblowing
The act of an employee reporting suspected wrongdoing, illegal activity, or public interest concerns. Protected by the Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998, which prohibits detriment or dismissal for making a protected disclosure.
♿
Reasonable adjustment
A change an employer must make to remove or reduce a disadvantage experienced by an employee or job applicant with a disability. Employers are legally required to make reasonable adjustments unless doing so would be an unreasonable burden.
💼
Vicarious liability
The legal principle by which an employer can be held responsible for the unlawful acts of its employees carried out during the course of employment — for example, harassment or discrimination by one employee against another.
📊
Data subject
Any identified or identifiable living person whose personal data is being processed. In HR, this includes employees, job applicants, and contractors. Data subjects have specific GDPR rights including access, rectification, and erasure.
🤝
Settlement agreement
A legally binding agreement where an employee waives the right to bring employment claims in exchange for a financial payment. The employee must receive independent legal advice before signing.
🔕
Non-disclosure agreement (NDA)
A legally binding contract restricting one or both parties from disclosing specified confidential information. Used in employment to protect trade secrets or, controversially, to prevent disclosure of workplace misconduct.
📌
ACAS Code of Practice
Guidance from the Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service setting out minimum standards for disciplinary and grievance procedures. Employment tribunals take ACAS Code compliance into account when determining awards.
🧑‍⚖️
Unfair dismissal
A claim an employee can bring at an Employment Tribunal if dismissed without a fair reason or without a fair procedure. Employees generally require two years of service to qualify for unfair dismissal rights.
🌍
Direct discrimination
Treating someone less favourably than another person because of a protected characteristic. For example, refusing to promote a candidate because of their age. Direct discrimination can almost never be justified.
🌐
Indirect discrimination
Applying a provision, criterion, or practice that appears neutral but puts people with a protected characteristic at a particular disadvantage, and which cannot be justified as a proportionate means of achieving a legitimate aim.
📢
Zero-tolerance policy
An organisational policy stating that certain behaviours — typically harassment, bullying, violence, or substance misuse — will be treated as gross misconduct and result in immediate disciplinary action up to and including dismissal.
🗂️
Personal data
Any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person, including name, email address, identification number, or any factor specific to the physical, physiological, economic, or social identity of that person.
📐
Statutory right
A right guaranteed by legislation that cannot be removed or reduced by an employment contract — such as the National Minimum Wage, statutory sick pay, and 5.6 weeks of annual leave. Contractual rights may exceed statutory minimums but not fall below them.
🧠
Unconscious bias
Implicit attitudes or stereotypes that affect decisions and behaviours without conscious awareness. In HR contexts, unconscious bias can influence recruitment, performance ratings, promotions, and disciplinary outcomes, leading to systemic unfairness.

Text 1: Writing HR Policies That Work — Clarity, Compliance, and Communication

Why the language of HR policy matters as much as its legal content

An HR policy is only as effective as the clarity with which it is written and communicated. A policy that is legally impeccable but written in dense, inaccessible legal jargon fails in its fundamental purpose — to provide employees and managers with clear guidance on what is expected, what is permitted, and what the consequences of non-compliance will be. Research by the Plain English Campaign and numerous employment law authorities consistently finds that employees are significantly more likely to comply with policies they can understand, and that organisations face greater legal challenge when policies are ambiguous, internally inconsistent, or poorly communicated.

Effective HR policy writing requires the author to serve two audiences simultaneously. The first audience is the legal and compliance audience — the Employment Tribunal, the regulator, the auditor — who will assess whether the policy meets the required legal standard, whether the procedures it describes are fair, and whether the language is defensible. For this audience, precision is paramount: the policy must use the correct legal terminology, reference the appropriate legislation, and set out obligations and consequences clearly and without ambiguity. The second audience is the employee — who may have no legal training, may be reading the policy in a stressful situation such as during a disciplinary or grievance process, and who needs to understand their rights, the process they are in, and what will happen next. For this audience, clarity and accessibility are paramount.

Three structural elements define a well-written HR policy. First, a clear statement of purpose and scope — who the policy applies to, what it covers, and why it exists. Second, a plain-language explanation of the key obligations and rights — what employees must do, what they are entitled to, and what is prohibited. Third, a description of the procedure that will be followed if the policy is invoked — step by step, with specific timelines, named roles, and the rights of appeal clearly stated. Policies that omit any of these elements create gaps that are invariably exploited when things go wrong.

Text 2: Data Protection in the Workplace — What Every HR Professional Must Know

Understanding GDPR obligations in the employment relationship

The General Data Protection Regulation — GDPR — came into force across the European Union in May 2018 and was retained in UK law as UK GDPR following Brexit. For HR professionals, GDPR is not a theoretical compliance obligation — it governs virtually every activity the HR function performs. Recruiting, onboarding, managing performance, handling grievances and disciplinaries, processing payroll, and managing leavers all involve the collection, storage, and processing of personal data, often including sensitive categories of data such as health information, disciplinary records, and trade union membership. Every HR professional must understand the six lawful bases for processing personal data, the rights of data subjects, and their organisation's obligations around data retention and deletion.

The most important concept for HR professionals to internalise is that personal data may only be collected and processed for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes — and may not be processed in a way that is incompatible with those purposes. This is the principle of purpose limitation. In practice, it means that data collected during a recruitment process cannot subsequently be used for a purpose the candidate was not informed of. Health information disclosed by an employee during a sickness absence process cannot be shared more widely than is necessary for managing that absence. Performance appraisal data cannot be retained indefinitely — it must be deleted or anonymised once the legitimate purpose for which it was collected has expired.

When an employee's data is breached — accessed, disclosed, or lost without authorisation — the organisation must notify the Information Commissioner's Office within 72 hours of becoming aware of the breach if the breach is likely to result in a risk to the rights and freedoms of individuals. Notifying affected individuals may also be required. The consequences of a serious data breach can be severe: the ICO has the power to issue fines of up to £17.5 million or 4% of annual global turnover under UK GDPR. HR professionals who understand and apply data protection principles correctly protect their organisation from regulatory risk — and protect the trust and dignity of every individual whose data they handle.

🔍 Grammar Review

Grammar Reference

Detailed reference notes for all three grammar points in this module.

Grammar Reference 1: Modal Verbs of Obligation, Prohibition, and Permission in Policy Language

Why modals define the legal force of policy language

The choice of modal verb directly determines the legal weight of a statement in HR policy. Using the wrong modal can make a mandatory requirement appear optional, or a prohibition appear merely advisory.

Must and shall — absolute obligation

Use must for non-negotiable legal requirements and internal obligations. Shall is the traditional legal drafting term — still used in formal policy documents.

  • 'All employees must complete data protection training within 30 days.' (non-negotiable)
  • 'The investigating officer shall conduct an impartial investigation.' (formal legal drafting)
  • 'Personal data must be retained for no longer than seven years.' (GDPR obligation)

Must not and shall not — absolute prohibition

Use must not or shall not for absolute prohibitions.

  • 'Employees must not share confidential disciplinary information outside the process.'
  • 'Managers shall not contact a whistleblower's colleagues during an investigation.'
  • 'Personal data must not be transferred outside the UK without appropriate safeguards.'

May and are entitled to — permission and rights

Use may to grant permission and are entitled to for legal rights belonging to the employee.

  • 'Employees may request flexible working arrangements after 26 weeks.'
  • 'Employees are entitled to be accompanied at any formal disciplinary hearing.'
  • 'The employer may extend the investigation period in exceptional circumstances.'
Grammar Reference 2: Passive Voice in Policy and Compliance Writing

Why passive voice dominates policy documents

Policy language uses the passive voice to describe procedures, obligations, and standards impersonally — making policies apply universally and sound authoritative rather than personal.

Describing procedures — future passive

Use will be + past participle for what will happen in a procedure:

  • 'The employee will be informed of the outcome in writing within five working days.'
  • 'All complaints will be investigated thoroughly and impartially.'
  • 'A copy of the meeting record will be provided to the employee within 10 days.'

Describing obligations — must be + past participle

Use must be + past participle for mandatory actions:

  • 'All personal data must be stored securely and accessed only by authorised personnel.'
  • 'The right of appeal must be clearly explained to the employee.'
  • 'Reasonable adjustments must be made where required by disability legislation.'

Describing standards — is/are + past participle

Use is/are + past participle for permanent standards:

  • 'All employees are expected to comply with the organisation's code of conduct.'
  • 'Job applicants are assessed solely on their skills and experience.'
  • 'The outcome of the appeal is considered final unless new evidence emerges.'
Grammar Reference 3: Core HR Policy and Compliance Collocations

Why policy collocations signal professional credibility

HR policy language has its own fixed vocabulary of verb-noun combinations. Using correct pairings signals professional competence and legal literacy.

Raise, breach, and comply — the three core process verbs

The most frequently used verb-noun combinations:

  • raise → a grievance, a concern, a complaint, an issue
  • breach → a policy, the code of conduct, confidentiality, data protection obligations
  • comply with → legislation, policy, the Code of Practice, obligations

Uphold, dismiss, and invoke — decision and procedure language

Expressions for HR decisions and activating formal procedures:

  • uphold → a complaint, a grievance, an appeal
  • dismiss → a complaint, a grievance, an appeal (as not well-founded)
  • invoke → a procedure, the disciplinary process, the right of appeal

Maintain, protect, and foster — organisational culture language

Broader expressions for ongoing obligations:

  • maintain → confidentiality, a safe working environment, records, standards
  • protect → personal data, the identity of a whistleblower, employees from detriment
  • foster → a culture of compliance, an inclusive environment, respect and dignity

Case Study 1
Mercer v. Alternative Future Group — Trade Union Rights and Employer Detriment
UK · Employment Law · 2024 Supreme Court · Industrial Action and Worker Protections · How a landmark Supreme Court ruling reshaped employer obligations during industrial action — and what it means for HR policy compliance
Background

Ms. Mercer was a support worker employed by Alternative Future Group (AFG), a care provider. She was also a trade union representative and was involved in organising strike action. The case centred on whether placing her on a restricted duties rotation — preventing her from attending union meetings — constituted an unlawful detriment under the Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992. The case exposed a gap in UK law: workers could be protected from dismissal for taking strike action, but could be subjected to other forms of detriment immediately. The case reached the Supreme Court.

The Supreme Court Ruling

The Supreme Court's ruling in 2024 confirmed that this gap was incompatible with Article 11 of the European Convention on Human Rights — the right to freedom of association, which includes the right to take collective action. The ruling established that workers who participate in official industrial action have the right not to be subjected to detriment short of dismissal. For HR professionals, this ruling changed what compliance means in the context of industrial action: any management action taken against an employee for participating in official strike action — including roster changes, exclusion from meetings, or changes to working arrangements — now requires very careful legal scrutiny.

HR and Compliance Lessons

This case carries several important compliance lessons. First, employment law evolves through case law — policies must be regularly reviewed against new judgments. Second, trade union representatives have specific legal protections that must be observed. Third, management decisions during periods of industrial action must be documented and scrutinised to ensure they are not motivated by union activities. Fourth, a policy compliant two years ago may not be compliant today. For policy writers, the Mercer case is a reminder to build in review mechanisms, track key case law, and never assume that a policy written some years ago continues to reflect the current legal position.

Case Study 2
Eweida v. British Airways — Religion and Belief in the Workplace
UK · Equality Law · European Court of Human Rights · Religion and Belief Discrimination · How a high-profile religious discrimination case reshaped employer obligations around religious expression at work
What Happened

Nadia Eweida was a check-in worker employed by British Airways who was told she could not visibly wear a small silver cross necklace because it contravened the company's uniform policy prohibiting visible jewellery or accessories. Ms. Eweida was a committed Christian who believed she had a duty to manifest her faith by wearing the cross visibly. British Airways initially refused to change its position and offered Ms. Eweida the option of working in a non-customer-facing role — which she declined. She was sent home without pay. The case attracted significant media attention. British Airways subsequently changed its policy to allow religious symbols, but Ms. Eweida pursued her legal claim, which reached the European Court of Human Rights in 2013.

The Legal Outcome

The European Court of Human Rights found that the UK had violated Ms. Eweida's Article 9 rights — the right to manifest religious belief — because the domestic courts had not given sufficient weight to her right to manifest her religion through wearing the cross. The Court found that British Airways' interest in projecting a corporate image was a legitimate aim, but not sufficient to override the fundamental right in this case. The case established that employers cannot automatically prohibit the visible wearing of religious symbols, particularly discreet ones that do not interfere with the employee's ability to perform their duties. Employers must instead engage in a balancing exercise, weighing the business need against the right to manifest religious belief.

Compliance Lessons

The Eweida case carries several important lessons. First, a facially neutral uniform or dress code policy can constitute indirect religious discrimination if it has a disproportionate impact on employees of a particular religion and cannot be justified as proportionate to a legitimate aim. Second, the concept of a balancing exercise is now central to how employers must approach requests related to religious expression — blanket prohibitions are very difficult to defend. Third, the case illustrates the reputational risk of rigid policy enforcement without proper consideration of individual circumstances: British Airways suffered significant public criticism and ultimately changed its policy without legal compulsion. Equality policies must be applied with judgment and proportionality, not mechanical rule-following.
