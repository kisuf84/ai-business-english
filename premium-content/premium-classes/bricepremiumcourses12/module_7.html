Module Overview

Welcome to Module 7: Conflict Resolution and Workplace Issues. Conflict is an inevitable feature of any organisation. When handled well, it surfaces important information, drives necessary change, and strengthens working relationships. When handled poorly, it escalates into formal processes, damages culture, and creates significant legal and reputational risk. For HR professionals working in English, this domain demands a very precise vocabulary and a highly calibrated register — one that is firm without being aggressive, empathetic without being partial, and legally precise without being cold.

This module covers the full language of conflict resolution in professional HR contexts: the vocabulary of grievance, discipline, mediation, investigation, and employment law; the grammar of formal written notices, investigation reports, and outcome letters; and the conversational structures used in investigatory interviews, mediation sessions, and difficult conversations with employees. The Writing section features AI-powered feedback on your grammar, vocabulary, structure, and suggestions for improvement.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Explain the key stages of a formal grievance procedure and a disciplinary procedure, distinguish between the two processes, and describe the HR professional's role at each stage.
2
Apply the correct vocabulary of workplace conflict — including grievance, disciplinary, mediation, investigation, misconduct, and gross misconduct — accurately and fluently in spoken and written HR contexts.
3
Write formal HR correspondence including grievance acknowledgement letters, disciplinary invitation letters, and investigation outcome letters using precise, legally compliant language and an appropriate professional register.
4
Use hedging language correctly in investigation reports — distinguishing between what has been established as fact, what is alleged, and what is a matter of interpretation — to produce legally defensible documentation.
5
Apply the language of natural justice principles — including the right to be accompanied, the right to respond to allegations, and the duty to investigate before deciding — accurately in HR processes and documents.
6
Conduct and document an investigatory interview in English, using non-leading, open questions, appropriate probing techniques, and professional note-taking language.
7
Write a formal investigation report summary and a disciplinary outcome letter with AI-powered feedback on grammar, vocabulary, structure, and specific suggestions for improvement.
8
Listen to and understand grievance meetings, disciplinary hearings, mediation conversations, and HR investigation briefings in authentic professional HR contexts.

📚 Word Bank

Key Vocabulary

21 essential terms for conflict resolution and workplace issues in professional HR English.

Module Vocabulary
⚖
Grievance
A formal complaint raised by an employee about a workplace matter — such as the behaviour of a colleague or manager, a breach of contract, or unfair treatment. A grievance triggers a formal procedure in which the complaint must be properly investigated, a hearing held, and a written outcome provided. The employee has a statutory right to appeal any grievance outcome. Grievance and disciplinary processes must be kept entirely separate to preserve fairness.
⚖
Disciplinary procedure
A formal process through which an employer addresses an employee's conduct or performance that falls below the required standard. A fair disciplinary procedure follows the ACAS Code of Practice: the employee is informed of the allegation in writing, a thorough investigation is conducted, a hearing is held at which the employee can present their case, a decision is made and communicated in writing, and the right of appeal is offered. Failure to follow a fair procedure can render a dismissal unfair at tribunal.
🔎
Investigation
A fact-finding process conducted by a neutral HR professional or manager before any disciplinary or grievance decision is made. The investigation gathers evidence, interviews witnesses, and produces a written report setting out what has been established, what remains unclear, and what the investigator recommends. The investigator does not decide the outcome — that is the role of the hearing manager. Investigations must be thorough, timely, and impartial.
📜
Misconduct
Behaviour that falls below the standards expected of an employee but does not — in isolation — justify dismissal. Examples include persistent unauthorised absence, minor dishonesty, rudeness to a colleague, or repeated failure to follow a reasonable instruction after warnings. Misconduct typically results in a verbal or written warning, with the expectation of improvement within a defined review period.
⚠
Gross misconduct
Behaviour so serious that it fundamentally destroys the trust and confidence between employer and employee, justifying summary dismissal without notice. Examples include theft, physical violence, serious harassment, fraud, or deliberate breach of health and safety rules. Before dismissing for gross misconduct, the employer must still conduct a fair investigation and hearing — the seriousness of the allegation does not remove procedural obligations.
⚖
Natural justice
The fundamental principles of fairness that must be applied in any disciplinary or grievance process: (1) the employee must be clearly informed of the allegation against them; (2) the employee must have a genuine opportunity to respond before a decision is made; (3) the decision must be made by an impartial person who has not been involved in the investigation. Breach of natural justice principles is a significant risk factor in employment tribunal claims.
💬
Mediation
A voluntary, confidential process in which a trained, neutral third party — the mediator — facilitates a structured conversation between two or more parties in conflict, with the aim of reaching a mutually agreed resolution. Mediation is not arbitration: the mediator does not decide the outcome. It is most effective for interpersonal conflicts, communication breakdowns, and disputes that have not yet reached a formal stage. Early mediation significantly reduces escalation rates and tribunal claims.
📝
Without prejudice
A legal term used to describe communications made in a genuine attempt to settle a dispute, which cannot later be used as evidence in employment tribunal proceedings. HR professionals use without-prejudice conversations to explore settlement options with an employee without those discussions being admissible if the matter proceeds to litigation. The protection applies only if there is an existing or contemplated dispute and the communication is a genuine offer to settle.
📄
Settlement agreement
A legally binding contract between employer and employee that settles all potential employment claims in exchange for agreed compensation and other terms — typically including a confidentiality clause and a reference agreement. The employee must receive independent legal advice before signing. Settlement agreements are used to bring employment relationships to an agreed end without tribunal risk, and are often offered alongside a without-prejudice conversation.
⚖
ACAS Code of Practice
The UK's Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service publishes a Statutory Code of Practice on Disciplinary and Grievance Procedures. While the Code is not legally binding in itself, employment tribunals are required to take it into account when assessing whether a process was fair. Employers who fail to follow the Code without good reason may face an uplift of up to 25% in any compensation awarded. The Code sets the minimum procedural standard for all formal HR processes.
👥
Right to be accompanied
A statutory right under the Employment Relations Act 1999 that entitles an employee to be accompanied by a trade union representative or a work colleague at a formal disciplinary or grievance hearing. The companion may address the hearing, ask questions on the employee's behalf, and confer with the employee — but may not answer questions directed to the employee or prevent the employer from explaining its case.
📄
Allegation
A formal claim that a specific behaviour or incident occurred. In disciplinary and grievance processes, allegations must be stated precisely and specifically — identifying what is alleged to have happened, when, and how. Vague allegations ('the employee has a bad attitude') are not appropriate in formal documentation. Each allegation must be investigated on its own merits and the employee must have the opportunity to respond to each one specifically.
📊
Balance of probabilities
The standard of proof used in employment tribunal proceedings and in most disciplinary processes: it is more likely than not that the alleged behaviour occurred — i.e. the evidence supports the conclusion to a level above 50%. This is a lower standard than the criminal 'beyond reasonable doubt'. HR professionals should document disciplinary decisions in terms of whether it is reasonable to conclude, on the balance of probabilities, that the conduct occurred.
💬
Early conciliation
A mandatory step in the UK employment tribunal process, administered by ACAS, in which an ACAS conciliator attempts to help the parties reach a settlement before a tribunal claim is formally registered. Early conciliation is free to both parties and is often effective in resolving disputes before they escalate to full tribunal proceedings. It is a legal prerequisite for most types of employment tribunal claim.
⚖
Harassment
Unwanted conduct related to a protected characteristic (age, disability, gender reassignment, race, religion, sex, or sexual orientation) that has the purpose or effect of violating the recipient's dignity or creating an intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating, or offensive environment. Harassment is a specific legal definition under the Equality Act 2010. HR professionals must use this term with precision — not every form of unpleasant behaviour constitutes harassment in the legal sense.
🔐
Victimisation
Treating an employee less favourably because they have made a complaint about discrimination or harassment, or because they have supported someone else who has done so. Victimisation is unlawful under the Equality Act 2010. HR professionals must be vigilant about the risk of victimisation following a formal complaint — for example, excluding a grievant from team activities, withholding a pay review, or giving them an unfavourable performance rating following a complaint.
📄
Investigatory interview
A structured, non-disciplinary conversation conducted as part of a workplace investigation — either with the subject of the allegation or with a witness. The purpose is to gather factual information rather than to establish guilt. Investigatory interviews must use open, non-leading questions; the interviewee must be informed in advance of the purpose of the meeting; and a full note should be taken and provided to the interviewee for approval. An investigatory interview is not a disciplinary hearing.
📜
Outcome letter
A formal written communication sent after a disciplinary or grievance hearing, setting out the decision reached, the reasons for that decision, any sanction imposed (in disciplinary cases), what improvement is required and over what timescale, and the right of appeal. Outcome letters must be accurate, evidence-based, and written in clear professional English. They form part of the permanent HR record and may be disclosed in tribunal proceedings.
💬
Informal resolution
An attempt to address a workplace issue or conflict without invoking a formal procedure — typically through a managed conversation facilitated by HR, a coaching intervention, or a team-based discussion. Informal resolution is appropriate for minor interpersonal tensions, communication difficulties, and one-off incidents where the parties are willing to engage. It should always be documented. Persistent issues, or those involving serious allegations, require formal processes regardless of the preferences of the parties.
⚖
Suspension
Placing an employee on paid leave during a disciplinary investigation, where their continued presence in the workplace would risk the integrity of the investigation, the safety of others, or the operation of the business. Suspension is a neutral act — not a punishment or an assumption of guilt — and must be communicated in writing. It should be as short as possible and regularly reviewed. Automatic or routine suspension without proper justification can itself become grounds for a constructive dismissal claim.
📄
Constructive dismissal
A legal concept in which an employee resigns in response to a fundamental breach of their employment contract by the employer — and claims that the resignation constitutes a dismissal for legal purposes. Common triggers include a sudden demotion, a serious breach of the implied duty of mutual trust and confidence, persistent bullying, or failure to address a substantiated grievance. HR professionals must be aware that how they handle workplace conflicts and grievances directly affects the risk of constructive dismissal claims.

Text 1: The Language of Formal HR Processes — Precision, Fairness, and Legal Defensibility

Why the words HR professionals choose in formal processes carry legal weight — and how to choose them correctly

Every letter, report, and record produced in the course of a disciplinary or grievance process is a potential exhibit in an employment tribunal. This single fact determines the standard to which all formal HR documentation must be written: it must be accurate, evidence-based, legally precise, and written in language that is professional without being aggressive, firm without being dismissive, and clear without being simplistic. HR professionals who treat formal documentation as an administrative inconvenience — producing vague, informal, or emotionally charged letters — expose their organisations to significant legal and reputational risk. Those who treat it as a professional craft produce documents that protect the organisation, support the employee, and withstand the scrutiny of an external tribunal.

The language of formal HR processes has a number of distinctive features. First, allegations must be stated specifically and factually: not 'the employee was rude to customers' but 'on 14 March 2024, the employee raised their voice at a customer and used language the customer described as threatening, as witnessed by two colleagues.' Second, findings must be clearly distinguished from allegations: the investigation report must use language that signals what has been established (‘the evidence confirms that...’), what remains alleged (‘it is alleged that...’), and what is contested or unclear (‘the accounts of X and Y differ in relation to...’). Third, outcome letters must link every finding to the evidence on which it is based, explain the reasoning behind the sanction selected, and set out improvement expectations in specific, measurable terms.

The register of formal HR documents requires particular attention. Letters to employees in formal processes should be professional and clear — avoiding legal jargon that may confuse a non-specialist reader — but should also reflect the gravity of the process. Opening a disciplinary outcome letter with 'Hi James' or closing it with 'Thanks so much' signals an inappropriate informality for a document that may determine whether someone loses their job. Equally, documents that are cold, clinical, or dehumanising fail to reflect the HR profession's commitment to treating people with dignity. The goal is a register that is authoritative without being intimidating, precise without being impersonal, and clear without oversimplifying the seriousness of the process.

Text 2: Mediation and Early Resolution — The Case for Preventing Escalation

Why early intervention in workplace conflict produces better outcomes than formal processes — and the language skills required to make it work

The formal grievance and disciplinary processes that HR professionals are trained to administer are, in an important sense, admissions of failure. They are the infrastructure that exists for when conflict has already escalated beyond the point at which two people — or a person and their employer — can resolve their differences through direct conversation. The costs of formal processes are well documented: the average employment tribunal claim costs an employer between £8,500 and £30,000 to defend, regardless of outcome; the management time consumed by a contested disciplinary case often runs to thirty or forty hours; and the psychological and reputational damage sustained by all parties in a formal conflict process is rarely recovered quickly. Research by the CIPD consistently finds that the most effective conflict resolution interventions are those that happen earliest, before positions have hardened and before identities have become attached to outcomes.

Mediation is the most evidence-backed of these early resolution tools. A trained workplace mediator facilitates a structured conversation in which both parties are given equal time to explain their experience, to be heard without interruption, and to identify what they need in order to move forward. The mediator does not arbitrate, adjudicate, or recommend outcomes: their role is to create the conditions in which the parties can reach their own agreement. The language of mediation is consequently quite different from the language of formal HR processes: instead of allegations and findings, mediators work with needs, interests, and options. Instead of 'what happened and who was responsible', the conversation focuses on 'what both parties need to work effectively together going forward'. This shift in framing — from the past to the future, from blame to problem-solving — is what makes mediation effective in cases where formal processes typically leave lasting damage.

The HR professional's role in creating a conflict-resilient organisation extends well beyond individual cases. Building a culture in which managers have the skills and confidence to have early, direct, non-escalating conversations about conflict — before issues reach formal stage — is one of the most valuable investments an HR function can make. This requires training managers in the language of early intervention: how to name a tension without aggravating it, how to ask about someone's experience without implying fault, how to propose a resolution without imposing one. The vocabulary of conflict resolution — de-escalation, common ground, mutual understanding, forward-focused conversation — is not merely descriptive; it is operational. Choosing the right words in a conflict conversation is not a soft skill; it is the skill.

🔍 Grammar Review

Grammar Reference

Detailed reference notes covering all three grammar points in this module.

Grammar Reference 1: Formal Notice Language in HR Correspondence

Why register matters in formal HR letters

Formal HR letters in disciplinary and grievance processes are legal documents. Their register must be professional, precise, and appropriately serious. Informal language, contractions, and colloquialisms are entirely inappropriate. At the same time, unnecessarily aggressive or dehumanising language can itself become grounds for complaint. The goal is clarity, authority, and professionalism.

Opening letters formally

Always open formal HR letters with a clear statement of purpose. Avoid casual greetings.

  • 'I am writing to invite you to a disciplinary hearing...'
  • 'I am writing to acknowledge receipt of your formal grievance...'
  • 'I am writing to confirm the outcome of the disciplinary hearing held on...'
  • 'I am writing to inform you that you are suspended on full pay with immediate effect...'

Stating allegations and findings

Allegations must be factual and specific. Findings must be linked to evidence and framed in terms of the standard of proof.

  • 'The allegation is that on [date] you [specific behaviour] as witnessed by [name]...'
  • 'Having considered the evidence, the panel has found the allegation proven/not proven on the balance of probabilities...'
  • 'The panel is satisfied that the conduct, on the balance of probabilities, occurred as alleged...'

Setting out rights and process steps

Use formal verb structures to set out rights and next steps. Avoid ambiguity.

  • 'You have the right to be accompanied at this hearing by a trade union representative or a work colleague.'
  • 'You have the right to appeal this decision within ten working days of receipt of this letter.'
  • 'You are required to attend / You are invited to attend / You are requested to provide...'
Grammar Reference 2: Hedging Language in Investigation Reports

Why hedging is essential in investigation reports

An investigation report is a fact-finding document, not a judicial verdict. It must carefully distinguish between what is established, what is alleged, what is contested, and what the investigator cannot determine. Using over-confident language ('the employee clearly lied') creates legal risk and oversteps the investigator's role. Using appropriately hedged language produces defensible, fair documentation.

Signalling established findings

Use these phrases when evidence is sufficient to support a conclusion on the balance of probabilities.

  • 'The evidence supports the conclusion that...'
  • 'On the balance of probabilities, it is reasonable to conclude that...'
  • 'Witness accounts consistently corroborate the allegation that...'
  • 'Based on the documentary evidence and witness testimony, the investigator concludes that...'

Signalling alleged or contested matters

Use these phrases for what is claimed but not yet established, or where accounts conflict.

  • 'It is alleged that...'
  • 'The complainant states that... however, this account is not corroborated by...'
  • 'The accounts of A and B differ in relation to...'
  • 'It has not been possible to establish with certainty whether...'

Signalling limitations and uncertainty

Use these phrases honestly to acknowledge the limits of the evidence.

  • 'No contemporaneous documentary evidence was available to...'
  • 'In the absence of independent corroboration, the investigator is unable to...'
  • 'Having been unable to substantiate this allegation on the balance of probabilities...'
  • 'This finding does not mean the allegation was false; rather, the available evidence was insufficient...'
Grammar Reference 3: Core Conflict Resolution and Disciplinary Collocations

Why collocations define HR professional credibility

Conflict resolution and disciplinary processes in English have a specific vocabulary of fixed verb-noun combinations. Using 'do a grievance' instead of 'raise a grievance', or 'make a suspension' instead of 'impose a suspension', sounds unprofessional and signals limited fluency in formal HR English.

Grievance and investigation language

Standard verb-noun pairings for grievance and investigation processes:

  • raise / submit / lodge → a grievance, a complaint, an appeal
  • conduct / carry out / initiate → an investigation, an interview, a hearing
  • substantiate / uphold / dismiss → an allegation, a grievance, a complaint
  • gather / consider / weigh → evidence, witness accounts, documentation

Disciplinary and outcome language

Standard collocations for disciplinary processes and outcomes:

  • issue / impose / serve → a warning, a sanction, a dismissal letter
  • reach / make / arrive at → a decision, a finding, a conclusion
  • chair / conduct / facilitate → a hearing, a meeting, a process
  • set out / confirm / communicate → the outcome, the right of appeal, the improvement required

Mediation and resolution language

Standard collocations for mediation and informal resolution:

  • facilitate / mediate / chair → a conversation, a session, a discussion
  • identify / establish / find → common ground, shared interests, a way forward
  • reach / agree on / commit to → a resolution, an agreement, next steps
  • maintain / observe / respect → confidentiality, neutrality, impartiality

Case Study 1
ACAS and the Culture of Early Resolution — How the UK Transformed Workplace Dispute Management
UK · Public Policy · 2000–Present · Workplace Conflict Resolution · How ACAS reshaped UK employment relations by shifting the emphasis from tribunal adjudication to early, workplace-based resolution
Background

The Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service — ACAS — is the UK government's statutory body for workplace relations, funded by the Department for Business and Trade. Its mandate is to improve employment relations and facilitate the early resolution of workplace disputes before they reach the employment tribunal system. ACAS estimates that each employment tribunal claim costs the responding employer an average of £8,500 in direct legal and HR costs, with contested multi-day hearings frequently exceeding £30,000. Beyond financial cost, disputes that reach tribunal typically represent the complete breakdown of the working relationship and cause significant psychological harm to all parties. The ACAS approach is grounded in a single core insight: that most workplace disputes — including those involving allegations of discrimination, unfair treatment, and breach of contract — are capable of resolution through structured, facilitated conversation, provided that intervention happens early enough.

The Early Conciliation System

Since 2014, all potential employment tribunal claimants in the UK have been legally required to contact ACAS and attempt early conciliation before submitting a tribunal claim. An ACAS conciliator contacts both parties — the prospective claimant and the employer — and, with their agreement, facilitates a structured dialogue aimed at reaching a settlement. The conciliator is not a judge and does not assess the merits of the claim; their role is to explore whether there is a negotiated outcome both parties can accept. In 2023, ACAS handled over 100,000 early conciliation notifications, of which approximately one-third resulted in a settlement before a tribunal claim was formally registered. This represents a significant reduction in tribunal caseload and a corresponding saving of public and private resource. Importantly, the early conciliation process is entirely free to both parties.

Lessons for HR Professionals

The ACAS model carries several lessons directly applicable to in-house HR practice. First, early intervention is consistently more effective than late-stage resolution: ACAS data shows that disputes resolved at the early conciliation stage typically involve lower settlement costs and higher levels of both-party satisfaction than those resolved at or after the tribunal hearing stage. Second, the role of a neutral facilitator is distinct from the role of an advocate or decision-maker — HR professionals conducting internal mediations must understand and maintain this distinction. Third, the availability of an external, independent dispute resolution mechanism — known by both employers and employees — changes the dynamic of internal conflict management: parties are more willing to engage with informal resolution when they know that a fair external option exists if they cannot agree. HR functions that communicate clearly about the ACAS process, and that embed early resolution skills in their managers, are consistently better positioned to resolve disputes before they escalate.

Case Study 2
Google’s Internal Conflict Resolution System — Building a Culture of Psychological Safety
USA · Technology · 2012–Present · Organisational Conflict Management · How Google used structured research to understand what makes it safe to raise concerns — and built conflict resolution principles accordingly
Background

In 2012, Google launched Project Aristotle — an internal research programme examining what made some teams at Google significantly more effective than others. The research team analysed data from 180 teams across the organisation, measuring performance outcomes, team composition, leadership styles, and dozens of other variables. The finding that emerged most strongly was not about technical expertise, seniority, or personality composition: it was about psychological safety — the shared belief within a team that it is safe to take interpersonal risks, to raise concerns, to admit mistakes, and to challenge the status quo without fear of punishment or humiliation. Teams with high psychological safety consistently outperformed teams with lower psychological safety, regardless of individual talent levels. The practical implication was that an organisation's ability to prevent and resolve conflict was directly linked to whether its culture permitted people to raise concerns early, before they escalated.

Translating Research into HR Practice

Google's response to Project Aristotle was to invest in the conversational infrastructure that supports psychological safety. This included training managers in the specific language of creating safety: how to respond to a raised concern without dismissing or defending, how to acknowledge a mistake without defensiveness, how to invite challenge without creating risk for the challenger. It also included redesigning feedback mechanisms to ensure that employees at all levels had structured, low-stakes channels for raising concerns — including anonymous surveys, skip-level conversations with senior leaders, and direct access to HR without the requirement to escalate through line management first. The organisation explicitly recognised that conflict resolution begins long before the formal HR process — it begins in the moment when someone decides whether or not it is safe enough to speak.

Lessons for HR Professionals

The Google experience translates into a set of principles with broad applicability for HR functions seeking to build conflict-resilient organisations. First, the most effective investment in conflict resolution is not in the formal process infrastructure — the grievance procedure, the investigation framework, the disciplinary policy — but in the cultural conditions that determine whether concerns are raised early or suppressed until they become unmanageable. Second, manager behaviour is the primary determinant of team psychological safety: a manager who dismisses, deflects, or penalises raised concerns will generate suppressed conflict regardless of how well-written the organisation's policies are. Third, HR professionals can have a significant impact on organisational conflict culture by training managers in the specific language of psychological safety: validating concerns without prejudging outcomes, responding to challenges with curiosity rather than defensiveness, and treating early conflict signals as valuable data rather than inconvenient problems.
