Texts & Comprehension

Read two professional texts: why organisations fail in crisis — how the natural instincts of information minimisation, qualified accountability, and incremental disclosure produce the pattern of secondary crises that compounds rather than resolves the original damage, how transparency produces better outcomes through narrative control (communicate first, set the frame), genuine accountability versus reluctant compliance, and decisive action signalling genuine concern; and the apology question — how the genre of performative apology has inoculated audiences against genuine ones (creating the paradox that organisations most need to be believed when most sceptical), the six components of effective apologies (expression of regret, explanation, acknowledgement of responsibility, declaration of repentance, offer of repair, promise of reform), and the structural difference between a genuine apology (specific acknowledgement, direct accountability, specific repair) and a performative one (general regret, qualified responsibility, generic commitment).

📋   Grammar Review

Reference Guide

Three reference boxes: crisis communication language — the holding statement three-element test (is the crisis specifically named? is there a specific confirmable commitment? is there a specific time for more information?), accountability language (active not passive, specific not general, unqualified not hedged), and recovery communication (evidence-led not aspiration-led, honest about what has not yet recovered, specific timelines); crisis leadership language — overriding legal advice (what the legal advice says, the specific trade-off in each case, named personal accountability), communicating under uncertainty (the uncertainty language — 'we currently estimate,' 'we believe,' 'we can confirm' — and the update commitment), and the apology (the proportionality test, the repair specificity test — 'we are committed to making this right' versus 'full refunds plus 20% compensation within 14 days'); and long-term reputation management — evidence-led recovery (the evidence test for every recovery claim), reputational resilience (behaviour not communication — 'communication cannot create reputation, only reveal it'), and post-crisis review learning (specific changes with named owners and delivery dates, independent verification of implementation).

🏛️   Case Studies

Real-World Cases

Explore crisis communication benchmarks — the Johnson & Johnson Tylenol recall (1982) establishing that acting genuinely in the interests of those at risk, at significant cost ($100M) without legal obligation, creates authenticity that defensive responses cannot replicate; the Exxon Valdez (1989) as the template for how delayed, defensive, financially minimising responses compound an initially manageable crisis; the Volkswagen emissions scandal (2015) illustrating how active, sustained concealment creates a crisis far more damaging than the original failure; and the common structure of damaging responses (initial denial/minimisation, qualified accountability, incremental disclosure) which organisations following typically believe is managing the crisis when it is extending and compounding it; and the specific dynamics of corporate crisis management in Latin American contexts — high social media penetration creating a 2-4 hour (not 12-24 hour) initial communication window, civil society mobilisation capacity, the dignity test applied to crisis responses affecting economically vulnerable populations, and the specific recovery challenge where quick transparent response with inadequate remediation still fails the regional standard.
