Texts & Comprehension

Read two professional texts: the preparation paradox — why the negotiators who do the most preparation are also those who appear most flexible (four underinvested tasks: rigorous other-party BATNA assessment, identification of interests beneath positions, inventory of potential trades, identification of objective criteria; the paradox: preparation focused on the other party's interests enables the most flexible and integrative behaviour at the table); and the language of negotiation — how what you say and how you say it reveals whether you are seeking agreement or victory (two simultaneous conversations: substantive and relationship; language patterns predicting successful outcomes: curiosity-oriented not position-assertive, interest-focused not position-focused, conditional language exploring possibilities without creating false commitments, acknowledging before asserting; the most revealing single indicator: what the negotiator does with information about the other party's constraints — exploit it or use it for creative problem-solving).

📋   Grammar Review

Reference Guide

Three reference boxes: principled negotiation language — curiosity before position (the curiosity test: has the negotiator asked at least two genuine questions before the first position statement?), conditional language (the conditional test: does the statement create a possibility without committing to it or signalling unconditional availability?), and objective criteria (the criteria test: if the other party asked 'what independent standard supports this?', would the negotiator have a specific credible answer?); persuasion and influence language — conclusion-first with a specific time-bounded ask (the ask test: has the presentation opened with a specific, time-bounded, low-stakes framing of what the audience is being asked to do?), influence without authority serving their interest not just yours (the influence test: has the speaker identified the specific concern of the specific person and designed their communication to address that specific concern?), and cross-cultural face-saving language (the face-saving test: does the disagreement expression acknowledge the other party's dignity while communicating the 'no' clearly enough to move the conversation forward?); and advanced negotiation and impasse language — impasse diagnosis naming the real issue (the impasse test: has the intervention identified the specific cause and proposed a specific change in the conversation's structure?), concession sequencing conditional-decreasing-costly (the concession test: conditional on reciprocal movement? explicitly costly? smaller than the previous concession?), and silence after a proposal with the ten-second rule and how to read the response.

🏛️   Case Studies

Real-World Cases

Explore Fisher and Ury's Getting to Yes (1981) — principled negotiation as the dominant framework for four decades; the four core elements (separate people from problem, focus on interests not positions, invent options for mutual gain, insist on objective criteria); why the framework's insights have been substantially confirmed by research; where it has been supplemented (psychological and cultural dimensions underweighted in the original; more applicable to long-term relationship negotiations than one-shot commercial transactions); and the insight that has most transformed executive negotiation practice (most stated positions are not the party's bottom line — they are the strongest version of their interests they feel comfortable advocating for publicly, and understanding the interests beneath the position almost always reveals more flexibility and often reveals agreement opportunities that positional bargaining would never produce); and negotiation in Latin American business contexts — relationship as prerequisite for commercial discussion not by-product (in contexts shaped by high interpersonal trust as a substitute for weak institutional trust, executives who treat relationship-building as a delay to be minimised systematically underperform); indirect communication and face-saving ('that would be difficult for us' means 'no' as clearly as 'no' does but preserves the other party's face); and the relationship between personal closeness and communication directness (the closer the personal relationship, the more direct the communication is likely to be — relationship investment increases the negotiator's confidence in interpreting what is communicated indirectly).
