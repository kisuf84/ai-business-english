📚 Key Legal Terms

Strategic Framing
The deliberate choice of context, language, and emphasis that shapes how a message is interpreted — determining whether information is received as a threat or an opportunity, a problem or a challenge, a loss or a necessary investment. Strategic framing is not spin: spin involves misrepresenting facts to serve a desired interpretation; framing involves choosing which accurate facts to emphasise, in which context, and with which language, to create the most accurate and useful understanding of a complex situation. Every communication involves framing choices — there is no neutral frame. The question is not whether to frame but how deliberately and how responsibly. Effective strategic framing in executive communication: establishes the context before presenting the content (the audience interprets new information through the lens of what they already understand), connects the message to what the audience cares about (framing in terms of the audience's interests rather than the speaker's), and closes the interpretive gap (reducing the likelihood that different audience members will draw fundamentally different conclusions from the same information).
🏗️
Message Architecture
The deliberate structure imposed on a complex communication — organising information into a hierarchy of claims, supporting evidence, and conclusions that enables the audience to follow the argument, evaluate the evidence, and arrive at the intended conclusion. Message architecture is the application of the pyramid principle (developed by Barbara Minto at McKinsey) to executive communication: lead with the key message or recommendation (what do you need the audience to know or decide?), follow with the supporting arguments (why is this the right conclusion?), and substantiate each argument with evidence (what data, examples, or analysis supports each argument?). The pyramid structure is counterintuitive for many communicators, who are trained to build to a conclusion rather than to lead with it — but at the executive level, audiences rarely have the patience for a lengthy build-up, and leading with the conclusion signals confidence and respects the audience's time.
📣
Executive Messaging
The distilled, deliberate communication from senior leadership — typically a small set of clear, consistent, and repeatable messages that the organisation can align around and that leaders can deliver consistently across multiple audiences and occasions. Effective executive messaging has three characteristics: it is specific enough to be meaningful (not 'we are committed to excellence' but 'we will deliver 97% on-time to our top 20 customers by Q3'), it is memorable enough to be repeated (senior leaders cannot deliver different messages every time they speak without creating strategic confusion), and it is authentic enough to be believed (executive messages that are inconsistent with observable organisational behaviour destroy credibility faster than silence would have). Message development at the executive level involves: identifying the two or three things the organisation most needs stakeholders to understand, testing whether the current communication is producing that understanding, and developing language that is consistent enough to be repeated and flexible enough to be adapted for different audiences.
🔍
Audience Analysis
The systematic process of understanding who will receive a communication, what they already know and believe, what they care about, what concerns they are likely to have, and what they need from the communication to make it useful. Effective audience analysis at the executive level goes beyond demographics: it asks what the audience's mental model of the issue is (how do they currently understand the situation?), what they stand to gain or lose from the proposed decision (where are their interests?), what objections or questions they are likely to bring (what must the communication address?), and what the communication needs to achieve for each audience segment (inform, persuade, energise, reassure, or align?). Senior communicators who skip audience analysis — who communicate the same content in the same way to all audiences — consistently underperform those who invest in understanding their audience before designing the communication, because the most accurate and well-argued message will fail if it is framed in terms that do not connect with what the audience cares about.
⚡
Crisis Communication
The strategic management of an organisation's communication during a significant adverse event — a product failure, a regulatory investigation, a leadership failure, a safety incident, or any situation in which the organisation's reputation, relationships, or licence to operate is at risk. Effective crisis communication follows several principles: speed (the first communication sets the frame; organisations that communicate late cede the frame to others — typically their critics), accuracy (in a crisis, speculation and incomplete information are more dangerous than honest acknowledgement of uncertainty), empathy (demonstrating genuine concern for those affected before defending the organisation's position or explaining what happened), accountability (taking responsibility for what the organisation is responsible for, clearly and without qualification), and action (communicating what is being done to address the situation, not only what happened). The most common crisis communication failures: saying too little too late (the silence frame is interpreted as guilt or incompetence), over-lawyering the response (legal caution produces language that sounds defensive rather than genuine), and failing to distinguish between factual accuracy and human acknowledgement (getting the facts right is necessary but not sufficient — people need to feel heard).
📊
Investor Relations Language
The specialised communication register used in communications with financial investors — including earnings calls, annual reports, investor presentations, and market guidance — that balances transparency with strategic discretion, and combines financial precision with narrative context. Investor relations language has specific characteristics: it uses precise financial vocabulary (EBITDA, free cash flow, guidance, run rate, underlying performance), it is subject to strict regulatory requirements about material non-public information and selective disclosure, it distinguishes between reported performance (what the accounts show) and underlying performance (adjusting for one-off items to show the trajectory of the business), and it balances the requirement for candour about risks and performance with the strategic need to maintain investor confidence. The most important IR communication skill is managing expectations: investors are less sensitive to bad news than to surprises. An organisation that consistently meets the expectations it has set — even when those expectations are modest — maintains investor trust better than one that consistently promises more than it delivers.
🏛️
Board Communication
The structured communication between executive leadership and the board of directors — including board papers, presentations, verbal briefings, and the interactive dialogue that occurs in board meetings. Effective board communication has a distinctive character: board members are intelligent, experienced, and appropriately sceptical — they are not audiences to be persuaded but governors to be informed and engaged. Board papers should be: concise (the board cannot read long documents; a 3-page paper with a clear recommendation is almost always more effective than a 30-page paper), structured on the pyramid principle (lead with the recommendation, follow with the rationale, substantiate with the analysis), honest about uncertainty and risk (the board cannot govern effectively if the executive is presenting only the optimistic scenario), and explicit about what the board is being asked to do (note, approve, decide, or advise). The most common executive failure in board communication is presenting an analysis rather than a recommendation — leaving the board to draw its own conclusions from the data rather than engaging with a clear, argued position.
🔺
The Pyramid Principle
A communication structure developed by Barbara Minto at McKinsey in the 1970s that organises information hierarchically — with the key message or recommendation at the top, the main supporting arguments below it, and the evidence supporting each argument below that. The pyramid principle inverts the traditional academic or analytical communication pattern (evidence → analysis → conclusion) in favour of an executive communication pattern (conclusion → arguments → evidence). The principle is based on the observation that executive audiences want to know 'what do you recommend and why?' before they want to hear the detailed analysis that supports the recommendation. The structure also enables communication to be adapted for different time constraints: with 2 minutes, the executive can deliver the top of the pyramid (the recommendation and the three supporting arguments); with 20 minutes, they can go one level deeper; with 2 hours, they can present the full analysis. The pyramid principle applies to written communications (board papers, strategic memos, executive emails) as well as verbal presentations.
🌊
Narrative Transportation
The psychological mechanism by which a listener or reader becomes emotionally and cognitively absorbed in a story — suspending critical evaluation and experiencing the story's world as real. Research by Melanie Green and Timothy Brock demonstrated that narrative transportation is one of the most powerful mechanisms for attitude change: people who are transported into a story update their beliefs and attitudes in ways that closely parallel a narrative's message, even when they might reject the same message if presented as an argument. For strategic communicators, narrative transportation has a specific implication: abstract strategic concepts (market disruption, organisational transformation, competitive threat) are much more effectively communicated through specific, vivid stories than through data and analysis alone. The story makes the concept real in a way that statistics cannot. Effective use of narrative transportation in executive communication: use specific characters (not 'customers' but 'a specific customer with a name and a situation'), locate the story in a specific context, and connect the story's resolution to the strategic point being made.
📋
Spokesperson Credibility
The degree to which an audience trusts and believes a communicator — a function of perceived expertise (do they know what they're talking about?), perceived trustworthiness (are they telling the truth?), and perceived goodwill (do they have the audience's interests at heart?). Spokesperson credibility is the single most important determinant of communication effectiveness: the same message delivered by a highly credible spokesperson will produce significantly more attitude change than the same message delivered by a low-credibility spokesperson. Credibility is built over time through consistent, honest communication — and destroyed rapidly by a single significant credibility failure (a statement found to be misleading, a promise not kept, or a message that is clearly inconsistent with observable facts). Leaders who have built high credibility through sustained honesty have significantly more communication capital to draw on in a crisis; leaders who have sacrificed credibility for short-term message management find that their crisis communication is doubted even when it is accurate.
🔄
Reframing
The communication act of presenting information within a new context, concept, or framework — changing the way the audience understands the information without changing the information itself. Reframing is a legitimate and powerful communication tool when used to provide a more accurate, complete, or useful understanding of a situation: presenting a cost increase as an investment in capability, presenting a market share decline as evidence of strategic focus on higher-value segments, or presenting a partnership as a risk reduction rather than a capability gap. Reframing becomes dishonest when it is used to mislead — when the new frame omits or misrepresents material information in a way that would change the audience's understanding if they were aware of it. The test of legitimate reframing: would an audience that knew all the facts consider the framing accurate and fair? If yes, the reframe is a communication skill; if no, it is spin.
🎤
Message Discipline
The organisational practice of ensuring that all spokespersons — from the CEO to frontline managers — communicate the same key messages consistently across all audiences and occasions. Message discipline is essential in complex organisations where different leaders communicate independently to different stakeholder groups: if the CEO tells investors the priority is profitability while the CHRO tells employees the priority is growth and hiring, the contradiction creates confusion, destroys credibility, and exposes the organisation to reputational and governance risk. Message discipline requires: a small, agreed set of key messages that are specific, true, and defensible; regular communication briefings that ensure all leaders are aligned on what to say and what not to say; and a culture that values honesty about organisational constraints rather than promising different things to different audiences. Message discipline is not the same as groupthink or suppression of legitimate debate — it is the practice of communicating consistent positions externally while maintaining genuine debate internally.
🧭
Stakeholder Mapping
The systematic identification and analysis of all individuals and groups who have an interest in or influence over a decision or initiative — enabling the communicator to tailor their communication strategy to each stakeholder's specific interests, concerns, and influence level. Effective stakeholder mapping for strategic communication involves: identifying who the stakeholders are (those who are affected by the decision and those who have power to affect it), assessing the level of each stakeholder's interest and influence (creating a matrix of high/low interest against high/low influence), understanding what each stakeholder needs from the communication (information, reassurance, consultation, or approval), and designing a communication sequence that addresses the most influential stakeholders first. Stakeholder mapping at the executive level requires particular attention to 'silent stakeholders' — those who have significant influence but who are not obvious participants in the decision (regulators, media, community groups, and influential customers who are not formally in the room but whose reactions will determine the outcome).
📝
The Headline Test
A communication discipline in which the communicator asks: 'If a journalist summarised this communication in a single headline, what would it say?' — and then checks whether that headline is what they intended to communicate. The headline test is a powerful diagnostic tool for executive communication because it forces the communicator to identify the single most important element of their message and to assess whether the structure of their communication makes that element unambiguously clear. A communication that fails the headline test — where the journalist's likely headline is different from the communicator's intended message — has a structural problem: the most important information is buried, qualified, or otherwise positioned in a way that invites misinterpretation. The headline test is particularly valuable for crisis communications (where the gap between intended and likely interpretation is most dangerous), board papers (where the recommendation must be unambiguous), and investor communications (where a single misleading headline can move markets).
🌐
Cross-Cultural Communication
The adjustment of communication style, register, content emphasis, and non-verbal behaviour to be effective across different cultural contexts — recognising that communication norms (what constitutes directness, respect, appropriate formality, and acceptable disagreement) vary significantly across cultures and that communication strategies effective in one cultural context may be ineffective or counterproductive in another. For Latin American executive communication, specific considerations include: the greater emphasis on relationship context before business content (communication that begins with the business topic without establishing personal connection may be perceived as cold or disrespectful), the preference for indirect communication of disagreement in some cultural contexts (a polite 'we will consider this' may mean 'no'), the importance of seniority and hierarchy in determining who speaks and in what order, and the value placed on rhetorical elegance and narrative quality in communication. Cross-cultural communication competence is not about stereotyping — it is about developing the sensitivity to observe and adapt to cultural differences rather than defaulting to the communication style that feels natural in one's own cultural context.
💡
Data Storytelling
The practice of presenting quantitative data within a narrative framework that makes the data meaningful, memorable, and actionable for a non-technical audience. Raw data — tables, charts, and statistical analyses — rarely persuades at the executive level; data embedded in a compelling narrative about what it means and what it implies for action does. Effective data storytelling in executive communication: selects the data that most directly evidences the key message (not all available data, but the specific data that answers the question the audience is asking), translates raw numbers into human terms (not '2.3 million users' but '2.3 million families who now have access to...'), uses visual representation to reveal patterns that are obscured in tables, and connects the data to a decision or action (the data is presented not as an end in itself but as evidence for a conclusion). The most common data storytelling failure: presenting data that is accurate but irrelevant — overwhelming the audience with numbers that do not answer the question the communication is designed to address.
🔊
All-Hands Communication
A large-group organisational communication event — typically a town hall meeting or equivalent — in which senior leadership addresses the entire organisation or a significant subset of it, creating a shared experience of hearing the same message at the same time. All-hands meetings are powerful for: announcing significant organisational changes (ensuring everyone hears the same message simultaneously, preventing the distortion of the message through cascade), demonstrating leadership accessibility (making senior leaders visible and available to the organisation), acknowledging organisational achievements (recognising and celebrating performance in a shared context), and creating cultural alignment (reinforcing the organisation's values and direction in a format that the whole organisation experiences together). All-hands meetings are frequently poorly executed because leaders underestimate the preparation required: the message must be crafted with more precision than in smaller settings (every word reaches everyone), the Q&A must be genuinely open (rehearsed questions destroy credibility), and the emotional register of the communication must match the emotional state of the audience.
⚖️
Communicating Under Uncertainty
The leadership communication skill of being honest about what is not known — acknowledging uncertainty, incomplete information, and evolving understanding — without undermining confidence in the organisation's leadership or direction. Communicating under uncertainty is one of the most difficult and most important executive communication skills: leaders who overclaim certainty they do not have destroy credibility when the reality diverges from the claim; leaders who are paralysed by uncertainty communicate helplessness that is equally damaging. The discipline of effective uncertainty communication: distinguish clearly between what is known (specific and certain), what is likely (based on current evidence), and what is genuinely unknown (requiring further information or time); make specific commitments about when and how more information will be provided; and demonstrate that the organisation is taking action on what it can control rather than waiting passively for uncertainty to resolve. A leader who says 'I don't know yet, but here is what I can tell you and here is what I'm doing about the rest' is more credible than one who claims certainty they don't have.
🎭
Rhetorical Structure
The deliberate organisation of a speech or presentation into a pattern that maximises the audience's engagement, comprehension, and persuasion — using the accumulated wisdom of rhetoric (the classical art of persuasion developed by Aristotle, Cicero, and others) to structure communication for maximum effect. Effective rhetorical structures used in executive communication include: the rule of three (presenting arguments, examples, or commitments in groups of three — the minimum for a pattern, the maximum for easy memorability); the problem-solution-benefit structure (establishing the problem, proposing the solution, and articulating the benefits of the solution); the past-present-future structure (establishing how we got here, where we are, and where we are going); and the ethos-pathos-logos structure (establishing credibility, creating emotional engagement, and presenting the logical argument — the classical Aristotelian framework). The choice of rhetorical structure is itself a framing decision: a communication that begins with 'what we've achieved' frames the context very differently from one that begins with 'what the challenge is.'
📧
Strategic Memo
A written communication from a senior leader — typically to peers, superiors, or a board — that presents a strategic recommendation, decision, or analysis in a concise, structured format designed for a time-pressed executive audience. The strategic memo is among the most important written communication formats at the senior leadership level: it is the format in which major decisions are documented, recommendations are advanced, and strategic analysis is preserved for governance purposes. An effective strategic memo is: concise (2-4 pages for most strategic decisions; longer only when the complexity of the decision requires it), structured on the pyramid principle (recommendation first, then rationale, then analysis), honest about risks and uncertainties (not just the case for the recommendation but the strongest case against it), explicit about what the reader is being asked to do, and written in active, direct language (passive constructions and academic hedging are communication failures in this format). The executive summary of a strategic memo should be complete enough that a reader who reads only the first page understands the recommendation, the rationale, and the decision required.
🌱
Empathy in Communication
The deliberate demonstration of genuine understanding of another person's perspective, feelings, or situation — in executive communication, the capacity to acknowledge what stakeholders are experiencing before communicating the organisation's position or decision. Empathy in strategic communication is not sentimentality: it is the recognition that people who do not feel understood are less likely to engage with the substance of what is being communicated. A leader who announces a difficult decision without first demonstrating that they understand its human impact will lose the audience emotionally before the audience has heard the argument. Practical empathy language in executive communication: naming the specific difficulty (not 'I know this is challenging' but 'I know that for many of you, this means uncertainty about your role — and I want to address that directly'); acknowledging the legitimacy of emotional responses ('It is entirely reasonable to be concerned about this'); and distinguishing between empathising with the difficulty and changing the decision (empathy does not require apologising for a decision that is right, but it does require acknowledging that the decision has a human cost).

Text 1: The Structure of Persuasion — Why What You Say Last Is Not What Your Audience Remembers First

Most professionals learn to communicate analytically: gather data, analyse it, draw conclusions, present the conclusions. This sequence is logical, rigorous, and almost perfectly wrong for executive communication. At the senior leadership level, the audience has already heard far more analysis than they can absorb, they are under significant time pressure, and they are making decisions that require clarity about what to do rather than understanding of how the conclusion was reached. A communication that begins with the data and builds to the recommendation will lose the executive audience before it reaches the point. The conclusion is buried at the end — where the exhausted audience least wants to be, and where the presentation is most likely to be interrupted, curtailed, or deferred. The solution is counter-intuitive for analytically trained professionals: lead with the conclusion. State the recommendation first, then the reasons for it, then the evidence supporting those reasons. This is the pyramid principle.

Leading with the conclusion does not mean abandoning rigour. The pyramid structure demands more analytical discipline than the build-up structure, not less: the communicator must be clear enough about their conclusion to state it first, confident enough in their argument to invite challenge immediately, and organised enough to present their reasoning in a logical hierarchy. The most common objection to the pyramid principle is that it seems presumptuous — that presenting the conclusion before the analysis assumes the audience will accept it. This objection misunderstands the principle. Leading with the conclusion invites the audience to engage critically with the reasoning from the beginning, rather than withholding their judgment until the analysis is presented. A board that knows the recommendation from the first sentence can ask better questions, challenge more specifically, and govern more effectively than a board that is trying to understand where a 40-page analysis is heading while simultaneously evaluating each individual finding.

The pyramid principle also serves a practical communication resilience function: it ensures that if a communication is cut short — a meeting runs over, a call drops, an executive reads only the first page — the most important information has been conveyed. An analysis that builds to a conclusion will be useless if the audience never reaches the conclusion. A pyramid communication will have conveyed the essential message even if the supporting analysis is never reached. This resilience function is particularly important in writing: executive readers almost never read a document in full. They read the executive summary, the introduction, and the conclusion — and they may read the sections they find most relevant or most questionable. A document structured on the pyramid principle will communicate its key message to the executive reader who reads only the first two paragraphs; a document structured as a build-up will communicate nothing to the same reader.

Text 2: Crisis Communication — The Principles That Hold When Everything Else Is Falling Apart

Crisis communication is where the quality of an organisation's normal communication practice is most severely tested. In a crisis, the pressures that push against good communication are at their peak: legal counsel advises minimal disclosure, the facts are incomplete and evolving, the emotional temperature is high, and the instinct for self-protection runs directly counter to the transparency that effective crisis communication requires. The organisations that communicate well in a crisis are almost always those that have developed the communication disciplines — speed, accuracy, empathy, accountability, and action — as normal practice. The organisations that communicate badly are often those that discover, in the worst possible circumstances, that they have no communication infrastructure, no clear decision rights about who can say what, and no culture of honest disclosure to fall back on.

The research on crisis communication — compiled by academics including Timothy Coombs, whose Situational Crisis Communication Theory has become the dominant framework in the field — consistently produces the same finding: the single most important factor in the reputational outcome of a crisis is not what the crisis was but how the organisation communicated about it. Organisations that communicate early, acknowledge responsibility where it exists, demonstrate genuine concern for those affected, and commit to specific corrective actions routinely recover from crises that appear catastrophic in the moment. Organisations that delay, deny, deflect, or minimise — even when the underlying facts would support a sympathetic interpretation — consistently produce worse reputational outcomes than those that communicate directly from the start.

The first hours of a crisis are the most critical, and they are the hours in which the temptation to wait for complete information before communicating is strongest. The paradox of crisis communication: the incomplete information available in the first hours is exactly when communication is most needed, because it is when the frame of the crisis is being established — by journalists, social media users, affected parties, and the organisation's critics, if not by the organisation itself. An organisation that says nothing while waiting for complete information cedes the framing of the crisis to whoever is speaking. An organisation that communicates promptly — acknowledging what it knows, acknowledging what it does not know, demonstrating concern for those affected, and committing to provide more information as it becomes available — establishes itself as the authoritative, credible source from which subsequent reporting and understanding will flow.

Grammar Point 1: Structuring Executive Communications
The Pyramid Principle in Practice — Conclusion First

Executive communications must lead with the recommendation or conclusion — then the reasons — then the evidence. The audience needs to know 'what are you recommending and why?' before they can usefully evaluate the analysis. Leading with the conclusion also enables the communication to survive being cut short: if the meeting ends after five minutes, the audience has still heard the key message.

Board paper opening (pyramid): 'Recommendation: we should exit the Consumer Insurance segment by Q2. Three reasons: six of seven years below cost of capital, €80M capital available for redeployment, declining competitive position. Supporting analysis follows.'
Investor communication opening (pyramid): 'Q3 revenue was €78.4M against guidance of €82-85M. The primary driver — 80% of the gap — was a single large contract that closed in the first week of Q4. The remaining gap reflects slower SME conversion in Mexico. Full-year guidance €310-315M — I'll explain the basis for this range.'
Strategic memo opening (pyramid): 'Recommendation: enter Brazil via partnership with Grupo Meridian rather than wholly-owned subsidiary. This reduces our capital requirement by €60M in the first two years and generates first revenue within 6 months versus 18 months for a wholly-owned entry. The memo below sets out the three strategic arguments, the two principal risks, and the decision requested.'
What the pyramid prevents: 'We have conducted an extensive analysis of the Brazilian market entry options... [23 pages later] ...therefore our recommendation is to enter via partnership.' This structure gives the audience no way to evaluate the analysis without first understanding where it leads.
Crisis Communication Language — Speed, Accuracy, Empathy, Accountability, Action

Effective crisis communication follows a specific sequence: what happened (accurate, specific, not speculative), who is affected and how (empathy first, before defence), what is being done about it (specific actions, not general commitments), and what you will communicate next and when (maintaining control of the information flow).

Speed: 'I want to communicate directly with you about an issue that we identified this morning' — not: 'We are aware of reports and are investigating.'
Accuracy: 'The information accessed included your name, email address, and account number. It did not include your password, payment card details, or financial transaction data' — specific and factual, not 'some data may have been affected.'
Empathy before defence: 'The 180 positions affected are not abstractions — they are people who have contributed to what this organisation has become' — naming the human impact before the strategic rationale.
Accountability: 'We identified a disclosure issue in 2022-23. We found it ourselves, we fixed it, and we paid the people who were affected' — direct acknowledgement without qualification.
Action and timeline: 'I will provide a second update at 4pm today, including the results of the initial root cause investigation' — specific, not 'we will communicate as soon as we have more information.'
Message Discipline — The Three-Message Rule

At the executive level, a communication rarely needs more than three main messages. More than three messages are rarely remembered; fewer than three may feel thin. The three-message structure also enables adaptation: the same three messages can be delivered in 2 minutes (headlines only), 20 minutes (with one layer of support), or an hour (with full supporting evidence).

Three-message structure for an all-hands: 'I want to address you today with three things: what is true, what is uncertain, and what I am asking of you' — then each of the three, developed appropriately for the audience.
Adapting the same three messages: to the board — state messages, provide supporting analysis; to employees — state messages, provide human context and implications; to investors — state messages, provide financial evidence and guidance implications.
Message discipline in a media interview: 'I want to respond to the specific claims in the report. Some are accurate and some are not — and I want to address each specifically' — controlling the structure of the response rather than responding to the journalist's frame.
When asked a question outside the three messages: 'That's an important question — let me connect it to the point I think is most relevant here' — and bridge back to one of the three messages.

Grammar Point 2: Persuasion and Framing Language
Strategic Framing — Choosing the Accurate and Useful Interpretation

Every communication involves a framing choice. The discipline is to choose the framing that is both accurate and most useful — not the framing that is most comfortable for the communicator or the most flattering to the organisation. The test: would an audience that knew all the facts consider this framing accurate and fair?

From product recall to responsible response: 'We identified a manufacturing defect in Batch 2024-0847 and immediately placed a voluntary hold — this is the quality management process working as it should' — reframing from 'failure' to 'responsible detection.'
From profit warning to managed transparency: 'We have made a deliberate decision to front-load our credit loss provisioning — choosing accuracy over optics' — reframing from 'bad news' to 'demonstration of financial integrity.'
From contract loss to strategic intelligence: 'The information value of what we learned in Brazil may exceed the commercial cost of the contract we didn't win' — legitimate reframe when followed by specific evidence of the learning.
The spin test: 'We have discontinued a profitable product because it was not consistent with our purpose' is a legitimate frame — it accurately describes both the commercial sacrifice and the values motivation. 'Our financial performance was strong despite market headwinds' without specifying what the market headwinds were is evasive — it uses a real concept to obscure a real performance gap.
Empathy Language — Specific, Not Generic

Generic empathy language ('I know this is difficult', 'I appreciate the concern') has less impact than specific empathy — naming the specific experience of the audience and acknowledging its legitimacy directly.

Generic (insufficient): 'I know this is a difficult time for our employees.'
Specific (effective): 'Many of you joined this organisation because of what it stood for — the commitment to building careers here, the promise of stability in exchange for the quality of your work. The decision we're announcing today affects 87 people whose professional choices were made partly in reliance on that commitment.'
Specific empathy in crisis: 'The 4,200 customers who were affected by this breach trusted us with information that is important to their financial lives. That trust was violated through an attack that we did not anticipate and did not prevent. I am deeply sorry for that.'
The distinction between empathy and concession: 'I am not going to tell you that this is the right decision for everyone in this room. It is not. It is the right decision for the organisation's long-term sustainability.' — acknowledging the difficulty of the decision for those affected, without withdrawing the decision.
Rhetorical Structure — The Rule of Three and Other Patterns

The rule of three is the most persistently effective rhetorical structure because it creates the minimum of a pattern and the maximum of memorability. Three points feel complete; two feel incomplete; four feel like too many. Other effective rhetorical structures include past-present-future (how we got here, where we are, where we are going) and the problem-solution-benefit pattern (the challenge, the response, the outcome).

Rule of three in crisis: 'Here is what you should do. Here is what we are doing. Here is our commitment.' — three clear action-oriented statements.
Rule of three in investor communication: 'The acquisition eliminates our largest competitive gap. It represents a 12% discount to comparable transactions. Completing before Q4 means we capture the 2025 planning cycle with the combined team.'
Past-present-future structure: 'We began this organisation as a response to a problem in the financial system. We have built, over twelve years, the data and the relationships to solve that problem at scale. And we are now choosing between two very different versions of the next chapter.'

Grammar Point 3: Advanced Communication Disciplines
Communicating Under Uncertainty — The Three Buckets

When facing genuine uncertainty, organise communication into three clearly distinguished categories: what you know with certainty (specific and committed), what you believe with reasonable confidence (clearly flagged as an estimate), and what you genuinely cannot yet say (with a specific commitment about when you will know). This structure is more credible than either false certainty or vague acknowledgement of uncertainty.

Structure: 'I want to be specific about three things: what I know, what I'm uncertain about, and what I genuinely cannot tell you yet.'
What you know: 'The board has approved the strategic pivot. The headcount impact will be no more than 8% of the current workforce. The transition timeline is nine months. These are committed facts.'
What you believe: 'I expect a 12-15% reduction in Q3 revenue as we redirect commercial resources — but this is an estimate, not a commitment. The range reflects the uncertainty in our transition timeline.'
What you don't know yet: 'I cannot yet tell you which specific roles will be affected — that determination requires the organisational design work that starts next week and concludes in four weeks. I will update you on that specific question by [date].'
The commitment: 'When I know something moves from uncertain to certain, you will be the first people I tell' — and then keeping that commitment.
Board Communication — The Four Questions

Every board paper should answer four questions: What is the recommendation or decision required? What are the three strongest reasons for it? What are the two or three most significant risks — and how are they being managed? What specifically is the board being asked to do — approve, note, advise, or decide? If a board paper cannot answer these four questions in its first page, it needs to be restructured.

The recommendation question: 'My recommendation is that the board authorise us to proceed to exclusivity on the acquisition of Andean Commerce Corp at a consideration of $240 million.'
The reasons: 'Three reasons: eliminates our largest competitive gap; at $240M represents a 12% discount to comparable transactions; completing before Q4 means we capture the 2025 planning cycle with the combined team.'
The risks: 'Two principal risks. First: integration risk — we have an integration plan, but integration is the most common failure mode in acquisitions. Second: regulatory risk — competition approval in two markets. Our legal assessment is that approval is probable but not certain, and we have a plan if it is not granted.'
The ask: 'I am asking the board to approve proceeding to exclusivity and instructing legal counsel to negotiate the definitive agreement.' — specific, not 'I would welcome the board's view on this opportunity.'
Cross-Cultural Communication — Building the Relationship Before the Business

In Latin American executive communication, the relationship context typically precedes the business content — and attempts to skip the relationship building and go directly to the commercial discussion are often counterproductive. The relationship is not a preamble to the real conversation; it is the foundation on which the real conversation can be held.

Building the relationship first: 'Before we get to the proposal itself, I want to understand your situation — not as context for the proposal, but because I think any partnership worth building starts from real understanding of what each party is working with.'
Genuine curiosity: 'What is the thing you're most proud of in what you've created? And what is the thing you worry about most as you think about the next chapter?' — not generic small talk but substantive questions that show genuine interest in the counterpart's situation.
Indirect acknowledgement of concern: 'I suspect that from your perspective, a partnership with an organisation of our scale may raise questions about how the relationship would actually work in practice — and I'd like to address those directly.' — naming the concern they may have without requiring them to raise it directly.
Hierarchy and speaking order: in formal meetings, allowing the most senior person to speak first — and not interrupting or completing the sentences of more senior counterparts — is a significant communication signal in contexts where hierarchy is more explicit.

Case Study 1: The Pyramid Principle — How Barbara Minto's Communication Framework Transformed Executive Writing and Continues to Shape Senior Leadership Communication
How a McKinsey consultant's observation about how busy executives read documents produced one of the most influential executive communication frameworks ever developed — and what it means for senior leaders who must communicate complex ideas to time-pressed audiences

Background

Barbara Minto joined McKinsey & Company in 1963 as one of the firm's first female associates and was assigned to work on communication quality within the consulting practice. Her observation was simple but radical: management consultants were trained to think analytically — building from evidence to analysis to conclusions — but the executives who read their reports were time-pressed, highly intelligent, and primarily interested in the conclusion and its rationale, not the analytical process that produced it. A 40-page consulting report that built carefully to its recommendation in the final chapter was a communication failure — the executive who needed to make the decision would read the executive summary, scan the first section, flip to the conclusion, and return the report to the shelf. Minto developed a structured approach to executive communication that she called the Pyramid Principle: organise information hierarchically, with the key message at the top, supporting arguments below it, and evidence below that. The pyramid could be read from top to bottom for the complete communication, or from the top alone for the essential message.

The Three Rules of the Pyramid

Minto articulated three structural rules that define the pyramid communication. First, ideas at any level of the pyramid must always be summaries of the ideas grouped below them — a claim at the argument level must genuinely summarise the evidence below it, not simply precede it. This rule prevents the common failure of placing an assertion at the top and then providing evidence that supports a slightly different assertion. Second, ideas in each grouping must always be of the same kind — supporting arguments at the same level must be logically parallel (all risks, all reasons, all benefits) rather than mixed. Third, ideas in each grouping must always be in a logical order — either deductive (the argument follows from a premise and a statement about the premise), inductive (multiple arguments of the same type, listed in a logical sequence), or sequential (steps in a process). These three rules — the SCQA framework for openings (Situation, Complication, Question, Answer) and the pyramid structure for the body — produce communications that are not just shorter but structurally clearer.

The Legacy and the Limits

The Pyramid Principle has had a remarkable reach: it is taught in most major management consulting firms, in top business schools, and by communications professionals worldwide. Its primary contribution is in eliminating a specific class of communication failure — the long build-up that loses the executive reader before reaching the point. Its primary limitation is that it is a structural framework, not a complete communication framework: it addresses organisation but not audience analysis, tone, emotional resonance, or relationship. A perfectly pyramidal communication to the wrong audience, in the wrong tone, without empathy for the audience's situation, will fail even if it is structurally impeccable. Senior communicators who understand the pyramid principle use it as the structural foundation of their communication — and then build the human elements on top of it. Those who treat it as a complete system risk producing technically well-structured communications that are analytically clear but emotionally disconnected from their audience.

Case Study 2: Crisis Communication in Latin America — How the Region's Trust Environment Shapes the Stakes of Organisational Communication Failures
How the specific features of the Latin American trust environment — fragile institutional trust, high media amplification, and community-level accountability — create both higher stakes for crisis communication failures and specific opportunities for organisations that communicate with genuine accountability

Background

Crisis communication operates within a trust environment — and the trust environment in Latin America has specific characteristics that shape both the stakes of communication failures and the opportunities for organisations that respond well. Institutional trust in Latin America has been shaped by decades of economic volatility, political instability, and corporate failures that have left citizens with a calibrated scepticism toward large organisations and a finely tuned ability to detect the gap between what institutions claim and what they deliver. This environment has two implications for crisis communication. First, the cost of a credibility failure is higher: an organisation caught misrepresenting its situation in a crisis loses not just the crisis story but the residual trust that would enable it to be believed in any subsequent communication. Second, the benefit of genuine accountability is larger: in an environment where institutional deception is expected, an organisation that demonstrates genuine honesty and accountability differentiates itself in a way that is difficult to achieve in higher-trust environments.

The Specific Communication Challenges

Latin American crisis communication faces specific challenges that shape effective communication strategy. Media amplification is rapid and community-level: regional social media ecosystems are highly interconnected, and a story that reaches a regional influencer or community WhatsApp group can achieve significant penetration within hours. The speed of reputational spread is faster than in many other markets. Legal frameworks governing crisis communication vary significantly by country and sector: what is required to be disclosed, in what form, and to whom, varies across Colombia, Mexico, Peru, and other major markets, requiring organisations to navigate both their communication obligations and their legal constraints simultaneously. The cultural preference for personal accountability — expressed by a named individual who takes responsibility and demonstrates genuine remorse — is stronger in Latin American contexts than in Northern European or North American ones: the organisation that communicates through a spokesperson reading a legal statement is perceived as institutionally evasive in a context where personal accountability is the expected norm.

The Opportunity

The high-trust-penalty environment of Latin American crisis communication also creates a distinctive opportunity. Research on consumer response to crisis communication in the region consistently finds that organisations that communicate quickly, personally, and with genuine accountability not only recover trust faster than those that do not — they often emerge from a crisis with stronger customer loyalty than they had before. The mechanism: in an environment where deception is expected, genuine honesty is surprising, emotionally resonant, and memorable in a way that it might not be in higher-trust environments. Customers who experience an organisation acknowledging a failure, making them whole, and demonstrating that the failure was taken seriously — rather than minimised, deflected, or managed through legal language — tend to form stronger bonds with that organisation than they would have without the crisis. This is the paradox of crisis communication in Latin America: done well, a crisis can be an organisation's most powerful demonstration of what it actually stands for.
