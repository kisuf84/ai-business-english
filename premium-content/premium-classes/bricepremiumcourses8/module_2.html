📚 Key Legal Terms
🔭
Vision Statement
A concise articulation of the desired future state that an organisation is working toward — describing what the world will look like, or what the organisation will have become, if it succeeds in its mission. A vision statement is forward-looking and aspirational: it describes a destination, not a journey. Effective vision statements are vivid (concrete enough to be pictured), stretching (ambitious enough to require real effort and change), enduring (relevant not just for this year's strategy but for a generation of leadership), and emotionally resonant (connected to values and purposes that matter to the people the organisation is trying to reach). The test of a vision statement: can the people inside the organisation use it to make decisions? If they can — if they can ask 'does this choice take us closer to or further from the vision?' and get a useful answer — it is working. Vision statements fail most often when they are vague, interchangeable across organisations, or internally inconsistent.
🎯
Mission Statement
A precise statement of what an organisation does, who it serves, and how it creates value — describing the current purpose and activities of the organisation rather than its aspirational future state. The mission statement answers the question 'what are we here to do?' as it can be answered today. Effective mission statements are specific (they differentiate the organisation from others in the same sector), action-oriented (they describe what the organisation actually does), stakeholder-referenced (they identify who the organisation serves), and meaningful to the people doing the work. The mission statement is the operational expression of the vision: if the vision is the destination, the mission is the road. The most common failure is confusing mission with vision — writing a statement of future aspiration when the requirement is a statement of current purpose.
🌟
Purpose Statement
A declaration of an organisation's fundamental reason for existence — going beyond what it does (mission) and where it is going (vision) to articulate why it exists in terms of the value it creates for the world. Purpose statements have become increasingly prominent as stakeholders demand that organisations articulate a value beyond shareholder return. A strong purpose statement connects the organisation's activities to a human need or societal benefit that transcends the transaction: not 'we sell financial products' but 'we enable families to build financial security across generations.' An organisation that has a purpose statement but consistently makes decisions that contradict it has not found its purpose — it has found a marketing line.
💎
Core Values
The fundamental beliefs and principles that guide an organisation's decisions and behaviours — the non-negotiable commitments that define what the organisation stands for and how it acts, particularly when values are tested. Effective core values are distinctive (they reflect specific choices, not generic virtues any organisation would claim), behavioural (translatable into specific, observable behaviours), and genuinely held (reflected in who is rewarded, promoted, and exited). The most diagnostic test of an organisation's actual values is not what appears in its declaration — it is what behaviours are tolerated or rewarded even when they conflict with the stated values.
🚀
Strategic Intent
A statement of the competitive position or performance level that an organisation aspires to achieve within a defined timeframe — more specific than a vision statement and more directional than a financial target. Developed by Gary Hamel and C.K. Prahalad, strategic intent creates a 'stretch' between the organisation's current capabilities and the aspiration it is committed to realising. Effective strategic intent is specific enough to create direction, challenging enough to require capability development, and stable enough to provide consistent direction over the medium term. If the aspiration can be achieved with the current approach, it is a target not a strategic intent.
📜
Organisational Narrative
The coherent story an organisation tells about itself — who it is, where it came from, what it believes, and where it is going — that creates meaning for employees, customers, investors, and other stakeholders. An effective organisational narrative integrates the vision, mission, values, and history of the organisation into a coherent story arc: the founding insight or need that created the organisation, the challenges overcome and lessons learned, the current position and capabilities, and the future the organisation is committed to creating. The organisational narrative is the story that employees tell new colleagues, that founders tell investors, and that the CEO tells the board when strategy is questioned. An incoherent narrative is one of the most common sources of organisational dysfunction.
🌍
Stakeholder Value Proposition
A clear statement of the specific value an organisation creates for a defined stakeholder group — articulating what the stakeholder receives, why it matters to them, and why this organisation delivers it better than the available alternatives. Organisations typically have multiple stakeholder value propositions: one for customers (what do you get from us that you cannot get elsewhere?), one for employees (why should talented people choose to work here?), one for investors (why is this organisation a better use of capital?), and increasingly one for communities. The stakeholder value proposition is a strategic articulation of value creation, not a sales pitch.
🏔️
BHAG
Big Hairy Audacious Goal — a concept developed by Jim Collins and Jerry Porras in 'Built to Last' — an extremely ambitious, long-term goal that is clear, compelling, and galvanising, requiring 10-30 years of effort and significant capability development to achieve. BHAGs are distinct from annual targets in timeframe and function: they serve as a unifying focus for the organisation's energy and imagination, not just a performance benchmark. Classic examples include NASA's goal to put a man on the moon before 1970. BHAGs are most valuable when they are specific enough to be clearly achieved or not achieved, emotionally engaging, and genuinely ambitious — if the organisation can reach them without changing what it does, they are targets, not BHAGs.
🌱
ESG Alignment
The integration of Environmental, Social, and Governance commitments into an organisation's vision, mission, and strategic priorities — ensuring that what the organisation claims to stand for in terms of sustainability and social responsibility is reflected in its strategy, operations, and reporting. Genuine ESG alignment means that commitments are specific and measurable (not 'we are committed to sustainability' but 'net zero carbon by 2030 with specific milestones'), integrated into the business model rather than added as a separate CSR layer, and backed by accountability with the same rigour applied to financial reporting.
🔑
Brand Promise
The explicit or implicit commitment an organisation makes to its customers — articulating the experience they can reliably expect every time they interact with the brand. The brand promise sits at the intersection of the mission (what we do) and the values (how we do it). A brand promise is not a slogan — it is a strategic commitment with operational implications. Brand promises fail when they are aspirational rather than operational — describing an experience the organisation would like to deliver rather than one it is capable of delivering reliably at scale. The most powerful brand promises are operationally embedded: every employee understands what the promise means for their specific role.
💡
Inside-Out Strategy
An approach to developing vision, mission, and strategy that begins from the organisation's own capabilities, culture, and strengths — asking 'what are we uniquely good at?' before considering what the market requires. An inside-out approach produces strategies that are authentic to genuine capabilities and therefore more sustainable than strategies based on aspirations that exceed current competence. The risk: an inside-out strategy may be blind to significant external changes that make current capabilities less valuable. Effective strategy typically combines inside-out and outside-in thinking.
🌐
Outside-In Strategy
An approach to developing vision, mission, and strategy that begins from the external environment — the needs of customers, the dynamics of competition, and the trends shaping the industry — asking 'what does the world need that we could uniquely provide?' Outside-in strategy is most powerful in rapidly changing environments where organisations that define their mission based solely on existing capabilities risk becoming irrelevant. The risk of a purely outside-in approach: it may produce visions disconnected from the organisation's genuine capabilities — aspirations that sound compelling but cannot be executed by the people and systems that exist.
📊
North Star Metric
The single most important measurable indicator of progress toward an organisation's mission or vision — the metric that, above all others, the organisation optimises as the primary measure of whether it is achieving its purpose. The north star metric translates the mission into a number: for a social media platform, daily active users; for a financial inclusion company, first-time bank account openings. An effective north star metric is closely correlated with actual value creation, not easily gameable, and simple enough to be understood by everyone in the organisation. It connects aspirational purpose language to specific measurement that guides daily decisions.
✍️
Values Declaration
A formal document that articulates an organisation's core values — including a name or phrase for each value, a definition of what the value means in the organisation's specific context, and behavioural indicators that distinguish people who live the value from those who do not. An effective values declaration goes beyond aspirational adjectives (integrity, excellence, innovation) to specify: what these values mean in practice, how they connect to the mission, and how they will be evidenced. Values declarations without behavioural specificity are at high risk of becoming wall art — widely posted, rarely referred to, and disconnected from actual decision-making.
🔄
Purpose Washing
The practice of claiming a social or environmental purpose in organisational communication without meaningfully integrating that purpose into the organisation's strategy, decisions, or operations — creating an impression of social responsibility not supported by substance. Signs of purpose washing: the purpose statement is aspirational but unmeasurable, decisions consistently prioritise short-term profit over stated purpose when they are in tension, the purpose is communicated externally but not operationally integrated internally, and leaders who espouse the purpose are not held to account when their decisions contradict it. Purpose washing is ultimately self-defeating: stakeholders who discover the gap between stated purpose and actual behaviour become significantly more hostile than if no purpose had been claimed.
🏛️
Organisational Identity
The persistent, central, and distinctive characteristics that define what an organisation is — its fundamental character that differentiates it from other organisations and provides continuity across leadership changes, strategic shifts, and market disruptions. Developed by Albert and Whetten, organisational identity encompasses what the organisation has been, currently is, and aspires to become — providing the through-line connecting its history to its future. When an organisation undergoes major transformation — a merger, strategic pivot, leadership transition — the question 'who are we now?' is an identity question requiring an answer that connects the new direction to what is genuinely continuous in the organisation's character.
🎭
Narrative Coherence
The quality of consistency between the various stories an organisation tells about itself — its vision, mission, values, strategy, culture, and communications — such that they combine into a single, credible, reinforcing story rather than contradictory messages. Narrative incoherence is most commonly observed when the CEO communicates a purpose-driven vision while the performance management system rewards contradictory behaviour, the mission statement promises customer-centricity while the operating model prioritises cost, or the values declaration espouses collaboration while compensation rewards individual performance. Narrative incoherence is almost never fixed by better communications — it is fixed by resolving the underlying organisational contradiction.
🌿
Purpose-Driven Business
An organisation that defines its strategic direction and measures its success in terms of a social or environmental purpose — not as a secondary consideration to profit but as the primary organising principle. Purpose-driven businesses generate commercial returns, but those returns are understood as the means to achieving the purpose rather than as the purpose itself. Research by EY, McKinsey, and Harvard Business Review has consistently found that purpose-driven organisations outperform peers on employee engagement, customer loyalty, and long-term shareholder returns — suggesting that purpose and commercial performance are mutually reinforcing. The most compelling purpose-driven businesses make money by achieving their purpose, rather than achieving their purpose with some of the money they make.
📣
Strategic Narrative
The overarching story that integrates an organisation's purpose, vision, values, strategy, and history into a compelling account of who the organisation is, why it exists, and where it is going — designed to inspire commitment, guide decisions, and create coherence across a complex organisation. An effective strategic narrative has a protagonist (the organisation or the people it serves), a challenge (the problem it exists to solve), a mission (what it is doing about the challenge), a vision (the world it is working to create), a values-based identity, and an invitation (what the narrative asks of those who hear it). The strategic narrative is the CEO's most powerful communication tool — its absence is one of the most common reasons organisations fail to maintain strategic coherence through change.
⚡
Aspirational Framing
The use of language that describes a future state or ideal — positioning the organisation's vision or mission in terms of what it is committed to achieving rather than what it has already accomplished. Aspirational framing is essential in vision and mission communication because it creates the forward direction and stretch that inspires engagement. The risk is the gap between aspiration and reality: language that overpromises without specifying how, for whom, and by when creates cynicism rather than inspiration. Effective aspirational framing is specific enough to be evaluated, connected to a credible capability story, and accompanied by evidence of progress — the aspiration is backed by milestones demonstrating the organisation is moving toward it.
🔍
Culture Code
A document or set of documents that articulates an organisation's culture — including its values, norms, ways of working, decision-making principles, and the specific behaviours that are celebrated, tolerated, or rejected — in sufficient detail to genuinely guide everyday decisions and hiring. Culture codes differ from values declarations in their specificity and tone: where a values declaration tends to be formal and aspirational, a culture code aims to be practical and specific, answering 'what does it mean to work here?' in terms an employee can act on. The risk of a culture code is inauthenticity: if it describes an ideal culture rather than the actual culture, the gap between the document and the experience of working there creates immediate cynicism in new hires.

Text 1: Vision Statements — The Gap Between Inspiration and Inflation

A survey of Fortune 500 vision statements would produce a dispiriting homogeneity: to be the world's leading provider of [product category], to be the most trusted and valued [organisation type] in [geography], to create superior value for [stakeholder group]. These statements are not wrong, exactly — they describe aspirations that most organisations genuinely hold. But they are also interchangeable, unmemorable, and operationally useless. They fail the most basic test of a vision statement: can an employee use this statement to make a decision? When facing a choice between two strategic options, can the employee ask 'which of these takes us closer to our vision?' and receive a useful answer? For most corporate vision statements, the answer is no — because the vision is too generic to discriminate between good and bad strategic choices, and too abstract to create the emotional engagement that makes people willing to work harder and take bigger risks in pursuit of it.

The vision statements that have genuinely shaped organisations share a counterintuitive characteristic: they are remarkably specific. John F. Kennedy's goal to put a man on the moon and return him safely to earth before the decade was out was not a vague aspiration — it was a specific, time-bound, technically ambitious target. Nike's founding vision, attributed to Bill Bowerman, was 'to bring inspiration and innovation to every athlete in the world' — with the subsequent parenthetical '(if you have a body, you are an athlete)' doing the critical work of defining 'every athlete' in an expansively specific way. The specificity does not make the vision less ambitious — it makes it more so, because a specific vision can be clearly achieved or clearly failed, creating the accountability and motivation that vague aspiration cannot.

The inflation problem in vision statements — the tendency toward increasingly grandiose language that overpromises and underdelivers — is partly a consequence of how vision statements are typically created. Committee processes, brand agency briefs, and strategic planning exercises all tend to produce language that has been polished, sanitised, and tested until all the distinctiveness has been removed. The vision statements that work are usually the ones closest to the founder's or leader's genuine belief about what the organisation is for — stated in specific, personal terms, with enough ambition to stretch the organisation's thinking and enough specificity to guide its decisions. The technical skills of vision statement writing are relatively easy to learn; the harder task is having something genuinely specific to say.

Text 2: The Process Is the Product — Why How You Create Vision and Mission Matters as Much as What You Create

The conventional wisdom in strategic communication is that the vision and mission statement are the product — the document itself is what matters, and the process of creating it is merely instrumental. This is precisely backwards. The process of creating a vision and mission statement is itself one of the most powerful organisational development tools available to a leadership team: it forces strategic clarity (what do we actually believe?), surfaces disagreement (what do different parts of the organisation think we are for?), creates commitment (people support what they helped to build), and produces the language that will be tested in every strategic conversation for years to come. A vision statement developed in a CEO offsite and then handed down to the organisation will typically receive polite compliance and private scepticism; a vision statement that emerged from a process in which the organisation genuinely participated will receive the kind of ownership that makes it operationally useful.

The most common process failure in vision and mission development is conflating speed with efficiency. Under time pressure — a board deadline, an investor presentation, a brand relaunch — organisations compress the vision and mission development process to days or weeks, producing a document that satisfies the immediate requirement but lacks the organisational roots to sustain strategic guidance. The paradox: the organisations that invest the most time in the vision and mission development process — creating genuine debate, resolving real disagreements, involving diverse voices — typically produce documents that require less revision over time because they have been genuinely tested. The organisations that invest the least time produce documents that need to be revisited regularly because they were never really owned.

The most effective vision and mission development processes share three characteristics. First, they are genuinely inclusive — not in the sense of involving everyone, but in the sense of involving genuinely diverse perspectives, including those closest to the customer and the front line, not only those at the top of the hierarchy. Second, they are honest about disagreement — the process surfaces and works through real strategic tensions rather than papering over them with language broad enough to accommodate all positions. Third, they distinguish between the journey and the destination: the process produces not just a document but a deeper collective understanding of what the organisation is for and how it will navigate strategic trade-offs — the document is the artifact of that understanding, but the understanding itself is the real product.

Grammar Point 1: Crafting Vision, Mission, and Purpose Language
The Decision Test — Making Vision Statements Operationally Useful

A vision statement passes the decision test when a frontline employee can use it to distinguish between better and worse strategic choices. Generic visions fail this test. Specific visions pass it — because they tell the decision-maker what to optimise, for whom, and why.

Failing the test: 'Our vision is to be the world's most trusted financial services provider.' → A branch manager cannot use 'most trusted' to decide between two customer service approaches.
Passing the test: 'A Latin America where every family has the financial tools to build the life they are capable of.' → If a branch manager is considering dropping a product that serves low-income customers because it's unprofitable, this vision says: that product serves people our vision is committed to.
The specificity formula: WHO we are serving + WHAT changes for them + WHY that change matters = a vision statement that guides decisions.
Testing a draft: 'Can someone at the front line, facing a difficult choice today, use this statement to decide? If yes — it's a vision. If no — it's a slogan.'
Distinguishing Vision from Mission in Practice

The most reliable distinction: vision describes a world that is different because the organisation existed and succeeded; mission describes what the organisation is doing right now to move toward that world. Vision uses future or aspirational framing; mission uses present-tense action framing.

Vision (external, future, world-changing): 'A Latin America where every family, regardless of their starting point, has the financial tools to build the life they are capable of building.'
Mission (internal, present, organisational action): 'We design and deliver financial products specifically for the first-generation wealth builder — accessible, transparent, and built around their actual financial reality.'
Common confusion (vision written as mission): 'To become the most trusted financial institution for underserved families in Latin America.' — 'To become' signals future aspiration (vision language), not current activity (mission language).
The grammar test: 'will' and 'become' → vision. 'do', 'create', 'enable', 'provide' → mission.
Values Declaration with Behavioural Specificity

Effective values declarations specify observable behaviours, not just aspirational adjectives. The behavioural indicator answers: 'How would I know if someone is living this value — and how would I know if they are not?' Without behavioural specificity, values are aspirational wall art.

Aspirational adjective (insufficient): 'Integrity.'
With definition (better): 'Integrity: We say what we believe, keep the commitments we make, and tell customers the truth about our products even when it costs us the sale.'
With behavioural indicator (complete): 'You are living Integrity when: you tell a customer that this product is not right for them before they sign. You are not living Integrity when: you allow a customer to proceed with a product because the conversation about its limitations is uncomfortable.'
The cost test for genuine values: 'Each of these values will cost us money in specific situations. If they don't cost us anything, they aren't real values — they're marketing.'

Grammar Point 2: Strategic Narrative and Stakeholder Communication
Strategic Narrative Structure — Story Arc for Organisational Communication

A strategic narrative has a story arc: the founding challenge that created the organisation, the journey and capabilities built in response, the current strategic position, and the future the organisation is committed to creating. Each element serves a specific narrative function.

Founding challenge: 'Our founder could not explain to a client why the products available to her were systematically more expensive and harder to access than those available to wealthier clients, despite being a better credit risk.'
Journey and capability: 'Twelve years later, we have built something that didn't exist in this market: a deep, proprietary understanding of the financial behaviour of the first-generation wealth builder — 12 years of customer data, and a product team that has built its career around this customer.'
Current position: 'We now serve 2.3 million families across six countries — all of them in the market segment that other institutions systematically decline.'
Future commitment: 'Our ambition: to make this the decade in which the financial system in Latin America is finally designed for the majority. We are three years ahead of where we thought we would be. And we are just beginning.'
Stakeholder Value Proposition — Audience-Specific Language

Different stakeholders need different articulations of value. The investor value proposition is commercial and differentiated; the employee value proposition is personal and purposeful; the customer value proposition is specific and credible. The same content rephrased is not a different value proposition.

For investors: 'We are sitting at the intersection of the largest underpriced commercial opportunity in Latin American financial services — 200 million underserved adults — and the only team in this market with the data, distribution, and product capability to serve them profitably at scale.'
For employees: 'If you want to build a career that combines technical excellence with genuine impact — if you want to do the most complex work you've ever done while knowing it matters to people who couldn't get it elsewhere — there is nowhere in this market that offers both.'
For customers: 'We will never recommend a product we don't believe is right for your situation. If we don't have one that's right for you, we will tell you that, and we will tell you where to look instead. That costs us sometimes. We think it's the right way to do business.'
The differentiation test: 'Read each value proposition without the company name. If a competitor could claim it with equal credibility — it is not a value proposition. It is a category description.'
Narrative Coherence — Testing Consistency Across Touchpoints

Narrative coherence is built by testing every organisational communication against the same essential story. The question for every communication: 'Is this chapter consistent with the story we are telling as an organisation?' Inconsistencies must be identified and resolved.

Coherence test for performance management: 'Our mission says we put the customer's long-term interest first. Our performance review rewards quarterly revenue. These do not tell the same story — we need to redesign either the mission language or the performance system. Publishing both, unresolved, is incoherence.'
Coherence test for communication: 'The CEO's last investor day presentation emphasised purpose and mission. The CFO's presentation at the same event was exclusively financial — no reference to purpose at all. Those two presentations, side by side, tell an incoherent story.'
Coherence test for culture: 'Our values declaration says we celebrate learning from failure. What actually happens when a significant initiative fails? Is the leader credited for the learning, or blamed for the outcome? The answer is the real culture — regardless of what the values wall says.'
Resolving incoherence: 'Narrative incoherence is almost never fixed by better communications. It is fixed by resolving the underlying organisational contradiction — changing the decision, the system, or the strategy that is contradicting the stated story.'

Grammar Point 3: Advanced Language for Vision and Mission Leadership
Purpose Authenticity Language — The Evidence Test

A purpose statement's authenticity is demonstrated not by how it sounds but by what it costs. Genuine purpose language is accompanied by specific decisions that demonstrate the commitment, specific metrics that measure it, and accountability mechanisms that enforce it.

Aspirational (risky without backing): 'We are committed to making financial services equally accessible to all.'
Authentic (with evidence): 'We are committed to making financial services equally accessible — which is why we price our basic accounts below cost to ensure genuine accessibility, measure and report quarterly on the income profile of our new customers, and require that no new product passes design review unless it serves customers in the bottom 40% of the income distribution.'
The cost test applied: 'Our commitment to financial dignity means that last quarter we declined €2.1 million of revenue from insurance products our analysis showed were systematically poor value for the customers buying them. That is what the value costs us. That is what makes it real.'
Anticipating the purpose washing challenge: 'I want to address a question the sceptics in this room may be holding: how do we know this is real purpose and not purpose washing? The test is simple: name a decision we have made in the past 12 months that was commercially costly but purpose-consistent. I have three to share.'
BHAG Language — Specificity at Scale

A BHAG must be specific enough to be clearly achieved or failed, emotionally engaging enough to inspire commitment beyond what normal targets generate, and ambitious enough to require genuine capability development. The language should be vivid and specific rather than financial and abstract.

Too vague: 'To become the leading financial institution for underserved communities in Latin America.' — 'Leading' is undefined, 'underserved communities' is undefined, there is no timeframe.
Too financial: 'To achieve $10 billion in assets under management by 2035.' — Financially specific but emotionally inert — does not describe why the goal matters or what changes for people if it is achieved.
BHAG language: 'By 2040, 50 million Latin American families — 50 million specific, nameable people — will say that their relationship with money changed when they became our customers. Not because we were their only option. Because we were genuinely on their side.'
The 'why it matters' bridge: 'This is not a customer acquisition target. It is an outcome statement. We are not trying to open 50 million accounts. We are trying to create 50 million families who, a generation from now, look back and say: that relationship was the moment our financial future became possible.'
Process Language for Vision and Mission Development

The language used to design and facilitate the vision and mission development process is as important as the resulting documents. Leaders who are transparent about the process — what it is for, what it is not for, and how decisions will be made — create more genuine participation and more useful outputs.

Setting the purpose of the process: 'This is not a wordsmithing exercise. The document we produce at the end is the artifact — the evidence that the conversation happened. The conversation itself is the point. What I need is genuine clarity about what we believe, genuine resolution of what we disagree about, and genuine commitment from this leadership team to what we decide.'
Managing the committee risk: 'I want to name the committee risk directly. When diverse stakeholders create shared language, the natural result is language broad enough to accommodate all positions — which means language specific enough to guide none of them. When the language starts to become generic, I will stop us and ask: what are we actually disagreeing about?'
Closing the process honestly: 'Before we finalise this statement, I want to ask the question that is hardest to answer: is there anyone in this room who does not genuinely believe in this statement — who would privately describe it as aspirational rather than honest? Because the purpose of this process was to produce something we all own. A statement that hides disagreement is not owned — it is tolerated. Tolerated vision statements are organisational furniture. We need more than that.'

Case Study 1: Built to Last — The Research Behind Visionary Companies and What It Reveals About Vision, Mission, and Long-Term Performance
How Collins and Porras's landmark research identified the specific characteristics of visionary companies — and what the research reveals about the relationship between mission, values, and extraordinary long-term performance

Background

Jim Collins and Jerry Porras's research project, published in 'Built to Last: Successful Habits of Visionary Companies' (1994), studied 18 pairs of companies — one 'visionary' (defined as the premier, long-lasting institution in its industry) and one comparison company (good, but not the premier institution). The research question: what distinguishes the visionary companies from their equally successful but less visionary counterparts? The methodology covered companies' histories from founding to the research date, analysing financial performance, strategy, leadership, culture, and organisational practices across decades. The findings were counterintuitive in several important ways, challenging conventional wisdom about what makes companies excellent.

Key Findings Relevant to Vision and Mission

Three findings from the Collins and Porras research are particularly relevant to vision and mission development. First: visionary companies are driven by core ideology — a combination of core values (what the organisation stands for) and core purpose (why it exists) — that remains constant while everything else changes. Companies that attempted to revise their core values in response to external pressure consistently underperformed those that maintained them. Second: visionary companies pursued BHAGs — Big Hairy Audacious Goals — with a commitment and clarity that ordinary targets could not generate. The BHAG was specific enough to be clearly achieved or failed, ambitious enough to require genuine capability development, and emotionally engaging enough to unify the organisation's energy across planning cycles. Third: visionary companies built 'cult-like cultures' — strong, distinctive cultures that attracted people genuinely aligned with the core ideology and repelled those who were not. The strength of the culture was correlated with how clearly the organisation communicated what it stood for and who belonged.

The Implications for Practice

The Built to Last research has three specific implications for how organisations develop and use their vision and mission statements. First: separate the core from the aspirational. The core ideology (values and purpose) should be designed to last generations — it should not change when the strategy or leader changes. Many organisations conflate these, producing documents that are neither stable enough to serve as identity anchors nor flexible enough to respond to changing circumstances. Second: be specific about the BHAG. The most effective BHAGs were clear enough to be unambiguously achieved or failed — they created accountability that stretched goals and financial targets could not. Third: align culture with stated values — deliberately. Visionary companies made systematic efforts to screen for alignment with their values in hiring and to exit people who were misaligned, however capable they were. The culture was not an accident of recruitment — it was the product of deliberate decisions about who belonged and who did not.

Case Study 2: Purpose in Practice — How Latin American Organisations Are Redefining the Relationship Between Mission and Commercial Performance
How a new generation of Latin American companies demonstrates that purpose and commercial performance are mutually reinforcing — and the specific practices that distinguish authentic purpose from purpose washing in the regional context

Background

The emergence of purpose-driven business in Latin America has a distinctive character shaped by the region's particular social and economic context. A region where income inequality ranks among the highest in the world, where significant proportions of the population remain excluded from formal financial, educational, and healthcare systems, and where institutional trust has historically been fragile provides both an acute social imperative and a genuine commercial opportunity for purpose-driven organisations. A new generation of Latin American companies — across financial services, education technology, healthcare, and logistics — has built its commercial model around the explicit mission of serving populations that have been systematically underserved. The most compelling of these organisations have demonstrated that the 'purpose versus profit' framing that pervades discussions of mission-driven business is a false dichotomy in this context: serving underserved populations well is simultaneously the most important social work and one of the largest commercial opportunities.

What Distinguishes Authentic Purpose from Purpose Washing

The proliferation of purpose language in Latin American corporate communication has made it increasingly important to distinguish between organisations whose purpose is operationally integrated and those for whom it is primarily a communications strategy. Three distinguishing characteristics have emerged. First, authentic purpose organisations make decisions that are commercially costly when consistency with their purpose requires it — declining revenue, partnerships, or market opportunities that conflict with their stated mission. Second, authentic purpose organisations measure and report on their mission progress with the same rigour they apply to financial reporting — continuously, as operational performance data, not only annually in sustainability reports. Third, authentic purpose organisations involve the communities they serve in designing their products and services — the voice of the served community is present in the product development process, not consulted after the product is designed.

The Commercial Evidence

The commercial evidence in the Latin American context has been sufficiently consistent to constitute a business case. Organisations that have demonstrated authentic purpose have consistently outperformed sector peers on employee retention (average tenure 40% higher), customer retention (first-year churn 35% lower), and long-term revenue growth (5-year revenue CAGR 25% higher than sector average). The proposed mechanism: in markets where institutional trust is fragile, an organisation that demonstrably does what it says builds a quality of customer and employee relationship that organisations without authentic purpose cannot efficiently replicate through marketing or compensation alone. The trust premium is particularly large in the underserved markets that purpose-driven organisations typically target: communities that have been misled by institutions before are uniquely sensitive to the gap between stated purpose and actual behaviour, and uniquely loyal to organisations that close that gap.
