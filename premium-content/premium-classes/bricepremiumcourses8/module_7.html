📚 Key Legal Terms

Burning Platform
A change management metaphor — derived from the 1988 Piper Alpha oil platform disaster in which workers faced the choice between staying on a burning platform or jumping into the freezing North Sea — used to describe the existential urgency that motivates an organisation to embrace radical change despite the risks and discomfort involved. In a change management context, the burning platform message communicates that the cost of staying with the current state exceeds the cost of changing: that the organisation cannot afford to remain where it is, even if the destination is uncertain. The burning platform is a powerful but double-edged communication tool: used appropriately, it creates the sense of urgency that Kotter identifies as the most essential precondition for successful change; used manipulatively or excessively, it produces paralysis (the threat is so existential that people freeze rather than act), cynicism (overuse of crisis language produces disbelief), or burnout (sustained urgency without relief is psychologically unsustainable). The most credible burning platforms are those grounded in specific, verifiable evidence rather than rhetorical amplification.
🏗️
Change Architecture
The deliberate, structured design of how a transformation will be led, sequenced, resourced, and governed — covering the specific changes required in each dimension of the organisation (strategy, structure, process, technology, people, and culture), the sequence in which those changes will be introduced, the governance mechanism that will coordinate across all workstreams, and the measurement framework that will determine whether the transformation is on track. Change architecture is to transformation what a building plan is to construction: without it, individual elements of the change may be well-executed but the overall transformation will lack the coherence, coordination, and momentum needed to produce the intended outcome. A well-designed change architecture provides: clarity for the teams executing individual workstreams (how does my work connect to the overall transformation?), a decision-making framework for the leadership team (which changes are sequenced first, and why?), and a communication platform for the organisation (where are we in the transformation, and what comes next?).
👥
Change Coalition
A group of key stakeholders — from across functions, levels, and locations — who actively support and advocate for a change programme, providing the broad-based leadership, credibility, and organisational reach that the formal change leadership team cannot provide alone. An effective change coalition is distinguished from a symbolic one by: genuine diversity (different functions, geographies, seniority levels, and informal influence networks are represented), genuine conviction (coalition members genuinely believe in the change, rather than participating for political reasons), active advocacy (coalition members speak publicly for the change in their own words, not in the programme's messaging), and genuine accountability (coalition members are visible and accessible when the change is challenged, not only when it is celebrated). The change coalition concept is central to Kotter's model: creating a 'guiding coalition' with sufficient power, credibility, and commitment to drive the change is step two of his eight-step model, and the most common reason change programmes fail at this step is confusing a coalition of formal leaders with a coalition of genuine change advocates.
🔄
Change Fatigue
The exhaustion, cynicism, and reduced capacity for engagement that accumulates in an organisation that has experienced too many changes, too quickly, without sufficient time to consolidate and recover between them. Change fatigue is one of the most common and most underestimated risks in large-scale transformation programmes: each change initiative imposes a cognitive, emotional, and practical burden on the people it affects, and when changes are layered on top of each other without adequate space for recovery and consolidation, the cumulative burden exceeds the organisation's change-absorption capacity. Symptoms of change fatigue: declining participation in change activities (employees attend but do not engage), increasing cynicism about change outcomes ('this too shall pass'), passive compliance rather than active adoption, accelerating attrition of the most capable and most mobile employees (who have the most options for leaving), and a leadership team that is personally exhausted and no longer credible as change advocates. The most effective prevention: sequencing changes deliberately, protecting consolidation periods between major initiatives, and being honest with the organisation about the change burden rather than minimising it.
📊
Change Readiness
The degree to which an organisation's people, processes, culture, capabilities, and leadership are prepared and positioned to successfully implement a specific change initiative. Change readiness assessment is the diagnostic discipline of measuring readiness before designing the change programme — identifying the specific gaps between where the organisation is and where it needs to be to implement the change successfully, and using that gap analysis to design the specific interventions needed. Change readiness assessment typically examines: leadership alignment (do the organisation's leaders genuinely understand, agree with, and intend to champion the change?), employee awareness and willingness (do the people affected by the change know it is coming, understand what it means for them, and feel positively or neutrally inclined toward it?), capability readiness (do the people who will need to work differently have the skills and knowledge to do so?), structural readiness (are the organisation's processes, systems, and structures capable of supporting the change?), and cultural readiness (are the organisation's norms and behaviours consistent with what the change requires?).
📈
Change Velocity
The pace at which a change programme moves through an organisation — from initial announcement through adoption to full implementation — and the strategic choice about how fast or slowly to drive change in a specific organisational context. Change velocity is one of the most consequential strategic choices in change management: moving too fast overwhelms the organisation's change-absorption capacity, produces superficial adoption rather than genuine behavioural change, and typically generates the resistance that undermines the programme; moving too slowly allows the change narrative to lose momentum, gives resistance time to organise, and creates the perception that the change is not a genuine priority. The optimal change velocity depends on: the urgency of the business case (a genuine burning platform justifies faster change), the organisation's historical change capacity (organisations that have successfully implemented rapid change before have more change-absorption capacity), and the nature of the change (structural changes can often be implemented quickly; behavioural and cultural changes require more time for genuine adoption regardless of the velocity of the structural implementation).
🌱
Change Narrative
The story that a change programme tells about why the organisation is changing, what it is changing toward, and what the change means for the people it affects — designed to create emotional engagement with the change beyond the rational case made by the business case. The change narrative is distinct from the business case: a business case makes a rational argument (we need to change because of X financial pressure, Y competitive threat, or Z regulatory requirement); a change narrative makes a human argument (we are changing because we want to be an organisation that does X — and this is what that means for the people we serve and the people who work here). A compelling change narrative connects the organisation's future direction to the values and aspirations of its people, provides a vivid picture of what the organisation will look like when the change is complete, and makes the change feel like a journey worth taking rather than a hardship to be endured. The most powerful change narratives are developed with the people affected, not for them — because a narrative that people have helped to create is one they are more likely to advocate for.
🔑
ADKAR Model
A change management model developed by Jeff Hiatt at Prosci that describes the five building blocks of successful individual change: Awareness of the need to change (does the individual know that change is happening and why?), Desire to support the change (does the individual want the change to succeed?), Knowledge of how to change (does the individual know what they need to do differently?), Ability to implement the new skills and behaviours (can the individual actually do what is required?), and Reinforcement to sustain the change (are there mechanisms in place to sustain the change once it has been implemented?). The ADKAR model is particularly valuable as a diagnostic tool: when a change is not being adopted at the individual level, the ADKAR model enables the change leader to identify specifically where in the adoption journey each individual or group is stalling — because the response to a person who lacks Awareness is fundamentally different from the response to a person who has Awareness and Desire but lacks the Knowledge or Ability to change.
🧱
Resistance Management
The structured process of identifying, understanding, and responding to resistance to a change programme — distinguishing between resistance that signals a legitimate problem with the change design (which should be addressed by adapting the change) and resistance that is the normal human response to uncertainty and disruption (which should be addressed through communication, involvement, and support). Effective resistance management begins with diagnosis: understanding who is resisting, what specifically they are resisting (the change itself, the pace, the process, the personal implications), why they are resisting (legitimate concern, self-interest, fear, misunderstanding), and what they would need to change their position. The most common resistance management failure: treating all resistance as obstruction to be overcome rather than as information to be understood. Resistance that is acknowledged, explored, and genuinely addressed — even if the change is not substantially modified — consistently produces better adoption outcomes than resistance that is ignored or dismissed as illegitimate.
🌊
Transformation
A fundamental change in the nature, operating model, or strategic direction of an organisation — as distinct from improvement (doing the same thing better) or incremental change (doing something slightly differently). Transformation implies: a significant break from the current way of operating, a substantial change in what the organisation does and how, the development of new capabilities that do not currently exist, and a sustained period of change that fundamentally alters the organisation's identity and competitive position. Digital transformation, cultural transformation, and strategic transformation are among the most common uses of the term in executive communication. The word is frequently overused — describing as 'transformation' what is more accurately an improvement or process redesign — which dilutes its meaning and creates scepticism in organisations that have experienced 'transformations' that did not fundamentally change how they work. A useful distinction: transformation changes what the organisation is; improvement changes how well the organisation does what it already does.
📋
Kotter's 8-Step Model
A change management framework developed by Harvard Business School professor John Kotter, based on his research of over 100 organisations attempting major change, that identifies eight sequential steps for successful large-scale transformation: (1) Create a sense of urgency — establish why the change is necessary and cannot be deferred; (2) Build a guiding coalition — assemble a group with enough power and credibility to lead the change; (3) Form a strategic vision — articulate a clear picture of where the change is heading; (4) Enlist a volunteer army — recruit a large group of change advocates across the organisation; (5) Enable action by removing barriers — identify and eliminate obstacles to change; (6) Generate short-term wins — create visible, early evidence that the change is producing results; (7) Sustain acceleration — use early wins to drive more change, not to declare victory; and (8) Institute change — anchor the change in the organisation's culture and systems. Kotter found that the most common causes of transformation failure were: insufficient urgency in step 1, a coalition that lacked power or conviction in step 2, and declaring victory prematurely at step 6 before the change had been institutionalised.
⚖️
Organisational Culture
The shared values, beliefs, assumptions, norms, and behaviours that characterise how an organisation actually operates — what it rewards and punishes, what it celebrates and ignores, how it makes decisions, how it treats people, and what its members consider normal, acceptable, and desirable. Organisational culture is often described as 'the way we do things around here' — the informal rules that govern behaviour in the absence of explicit instructions. In change management, culture is the most complex and most underplanned dimension of transformation: most change programmes can redesign structures, processes, and systems in months, but changing culture typically takes years, because culture is embedded in stories, symbols, habits, and social norms that are not directly accessible to management intervention. The most effective approach to culture change: change the behaviours (through structural incentives, leadership modelling, and accountability mechanisms) and allow the beliefs and values to follow — rather than attempting to change beliefs directly through communication and aspiration.
🗺️
McKinsey 7S Framework
An organisational change and alignment framework developed by McKinsey consultants Tom Peters and Robert Waterman in the early 1980s that identifies seven interdependent elements of organisational effectiveness: Strategy (the organisation's plan for achieving its goals), Structure (how the organisation is organised and coordinated), Systems (the processes and procedures that govern daily operations), Shared Values (the core values that define the organisation's culture and identity), Staff (the people in the organisation and their capabilities), Skills (the distinctive capabilities and competencies of the organisation), and Style (the leadership style and management culture). The 7S framework's central insight: all seven elements must be aligned for an organisational change to be sustainable — changing one element without aligning the others produces a misalignment that undermines the change. A structural change that is not supported by compatible systems, skills, and shared values will not be sustained; a strategy change that is not reflected in the structure, staff, and style of the organisation will remain on paper rather than in practice.
🎯
Short-Term Wins
Visible, credible, early evidence that a change programme is producing positive results — created deliberately by the change leadership team to demonstrate momentum, build confidence, and counter the scepticism that typically develops in the early stages of a major transformation. Short-term wins are a core element of Kotter's 8-step model (step 6): he found that without visible short-term wins within the first 6-12 months of a major change, the resistance to change typically regrouped and often overwhelmed the programme. Effective short-term wins have three characteristics: they are visible (everyone in the organisation can see them), they are unambiguous (there is no reasonable argument that the win was a coincidence or would have happened anyway), and they are clearly related to the change (the win is evidence that the change is producing the intended benefits, not an unrelated positive development). The change leadership mistake of declaring victory when short-term wins appear — treating early results as evidence that the transformation is complete — is one of Kotter's most important warnings: short-term wins should be celebrated and used to generate further momentum, not treated as evidence that the programme can be wound down.
🔀
Change Sequencing
The deliberate ordering of individual changes within a larger transformation programme — deciding which changes to implement first, which to implement in parallel, and which to defer until earlier changes have been consolidated. Change sequencing is among the most important and most neglected aspects of change architecture: poorly sequenced change creates dependencies that stall individual workstreams, overwhelms the organisation's change-absorption capacity when too many changes are introduced simultaneously, and undermines the credibility of the programme when visible changes are introduced before the enabling infrastructure is ready. Principles of effective change sequencing: introduce structural changes before behavioural changes (the structure provides the context within which new behaviours are expected), introduce visible quick wins before complex long-term changes (to build confidence and demonstrate momentum), and sequence changes to create natural dependencies (the completion of one change creates the conditions for the next). The most common sequencing failure: announcing the full scope of the transformation simultaneously — creating overwhelm and enabling resistance to form around the most threatening elements before the early, less threatening changes have established momentum.
🧬
Organisational Design
The deliberate structuring of an organisation's components — its reporting lines, team configurations, role definitions, coordination mechanisms, decision rights, and operating principles — to best achieve its strategic objectives. Organisational design is typically triggered by significant strategic change (a shift in business model, a merger or acquisition, a major market entry) and involves choices about: how the organisation will be structured (by function, geography, product, customer segment, or matrix), how coordination and collaboration will occur across structural units, where authority and decision-making will sit, and how information and resources will flow. Effective organisational design is not primarily a structural exercise: an organisational chart change that is not accompanied by changes in the informal norms, decision-making processes, and management behaviours that govern how the new structure actually operates will typically revert to the old patterns within 6-12 months, regardless of the formal reporting lines.
📣
Change Communication
The structured, multi-channel, audience-specific process of communicating a change to all affected stakeholders — ensuring that each group receives the information, context, and emotional support they need to move from awareness of the change through understanding and acceptance to active support. Effective change communication differs from general organisational communication in several important ways: it is designed around the emotional journey of the recipient (not only the informational content of the message), it is repeated far more often than the change team believes is necessary (most people need to hear a message 5-7 times before they internalise it), it is calibrated to the specific concerns and interests of different audience segments (not a single message for all), and it provides genuine channels for two-way communication rather than one-way broadcasting (the most effective change communication creates opportunities for questions, concerns, and feedback to be heard and addressed rather than simply informing people about decisions already made).
🌀
The J-Curve
A pattern of performance in organisational change — named for its J-shaped graph — in which the introduction of significant change is followed by an initial period of performance decline (as people learn new behaviours, adapt to new structures, and leave behind the comfortable efficiency of the old way of operating) before performance recovers and eventually exceeds the pre-change baseline (as the new capabilities, structures, and behaviours become embedded and optimised). Understanding the J-curve is essential for change leaders because it enables them to anticipate and communicate the period of performance decline that follows major change — preventing the premature abandonment of effective change programmes that are declared failures during the trough of the J-curve before the recovery has had time to emerge. The depth and duration of the J-curve trough depends on: the magnitude of the change (larger changes produce deeper and longer troughs), the quality of the change support (organisations that invest in training, coaching, and capability development during the transition shorten and shallow the trough), and the leadership's ability to maintain confidence during the decline period.
🤝
Stakeholder Engagement
The structured process of identifying, prioritising, and building productive relationships with all individuals and groups who have a stake in a change programme — to understand their interests and concerns, to build the support needed for the change to succeed, and to identify and manage the opposition that could undermine it. Effective stakeholder engagement in change programmes distinguishes between: informing (communicating to stakeholders who need to know about the change but whose input is not required for the decision), consulting (seeking the input of stakeholders whose perspective is valuable and whose concerns need to be addressed), collaborating (working with stakeholders whose active involvement is needed to design or implement the change), and partnering (co-creating the change with stakeholders whose commitment is essential for its success). The most common stakeholder engagement failure in change programmes: treating consultation as a compliance activity (going through the motions of seeking input without genuinely being open to having the change design influenced by what is heard).
💡
Capability Building
The deliberate, structured development of the knowledge, skills, behaviours, and mindsets that an organisation's people need to be effective in the new operating model that a change programme is creating. Capability building is frequently the most underinvested dimension of change programmes: transformation timelines are set based on structural and process changes (which can be designed and implemented in months), without adequate account being taken of the time required for the organisation's people to develop the capabilities the new structure and process require. The consequence: the new structure and processes are implemented on time, but the people operating them lack the skills and knowledge to use them effectively — producing a performance decline that is attributed to the change itself rather than to the underinvestment in capability development. Capability building in change programmes requires: early diagnosis of the specific capability gaps created by the change (what will people need to know and be able to do that they cannot currently do?), a learning and development programme that develops those capabilities in advance of the change implementation, and ongoing coaching and support during the transition period.
🔭
Vision of Change
A clear, compelling, and specific articulation of what the organisation will look like when the transformation is complete — providing a shared picture of the destination that motivates the organisation through the difficulty of the journey. The vision of change is distinct from the business case for change: the business case explains why the organisation must change (the rational argument), while the vision of change shows what the organisation will become (the aspiration). An effective vision of change has three qualities: it is specific enough to be evaluable (the organisation can assess whether it has achieved the vision), it is emotionally engaging (it connects to what the people in the organisation most value about their work), and it is genuinely aspirational (it describes a better version of the organisation, not merely a different one). The most common vision failure in change programmes: confusing the structural endpoint with the human destination — describing what the new organisation chart will look like rather than what it will feel like to work in the transformed organisation.

Text 1: Why Most Transformations Fail — The Gap Between Change Design and Change Adoption

The failure rate of major organisational change programmes is one of the most consistently replicated findings in management research. Studies by McKinsey, Kotter International, IBM, and numerous academic researchers have found that between 60% and 70% of major transformation programmes fail to achieve their stated objectives — not because the change was wrong, but because the change was poorly implemented. The paradox of organisational transformation is that most organisations invest most of their transformation resources in the design of the change itself — the new strategy, the new structure, the new processes, the new technology — and relatively few resources in the management of the human and cultural transition that determines whether any of those designed changes actually take hold. A new organisational structure can be drawn in an afternoon; embedding the new behaviours, norms, and working relationships that make that structure effective typically takes 18-24 months. The planning horizons are almost never aligned with the implementation horizons.

The most consequential underestimation in most transformation programmes is the time and effort required for genuine behavioural change — as distinct from structural or process change. Structural change (reorganising teams, redesigning reporting lines, changing formal governance processes) can be implemented by executive decision and is typically complete within months. Behavioural change (the actual shifts in how people work, how they communicate, how they make decisions, and how they treat each other) is a different challenge entirely: it requires individuals to change habits, give up familiar routines, develop new skills, and often accept reduced short-term effectiveness while they are learning to work in new ways. The J-curve of performance is the normal pattern: before it gets better, it typically gets worse. Change leaders who do not anticipate and communicate this pattern — who instead promise that the change will produce immediate improvement — destroy their credibility when the inevitable performance dip occurs, and create the conditions for the change to be abandoned as a failure before it has had time to produce results.

The change narrative — the story of why the organisation is changing, what it is changing toward, and what the change means for each person — is the most powerful and most consistently underinvested element of transformation leadership. Organisations that invest heavily in communication about the change but invest little in the story behind the change produce informed sceptics rather than engaged advocates. The difference between informing people about change and creating change advocates is the difference between providing a rational explanation (here is why the business requires this change) and providing a human invitation (here is what kind of organisation we are trying to become, and here is why that is worth the difficulty of the journey). Research by Chip Heath and Dan Heath, among others, demonstrates that the human mind is more powerfully moved by concrete, specific stories than by abstract business logic — a finding that has profound implications for how change communications should be designed.

Text 2: Leading From the Middle — Why Middle Management is the Pivotal Force in Organisational Transformation

The standard model of organisational transformation allocates change leadership responsibilities in a predictable pattern: the executive leadership team owns the vision and the governance; the frontline employees are the targets of the change (they are what needs to change); and middle management is the cascade mechanism — the layer through which executive communication reaches frontline employees and frontline feedback reaches executive ears. This model consistently underperforms because it misidentifies the role that middle management actually plays in organisational change. Middle managers are not simply a communication channel — they are the primary interpreters of what the change means in practice, the principal architects of whether the new behaviours actually manifest in day-to-day operations, and the most credible sources of information for frontline employees about whether the change is real and whether it will last.

The specific influence of middle management on change adoption operates through several mechanisms that top-down change programmes typically fail to account for. First: middle managers make the thousands of small decisions that translate strategic intent into operational reality — which meetings to hold, how to handle performance conversations, which behaviours to reinforce or ignore, how to allocate time and resources when the formal change programme is in competition with the immediate demands of running the business. A change programme that has the full support of the executive team but has not genuinely engaged middle management will consistently find that the strategic intent is not being translated into operational reality — not through deliberate resistance but through the natural prioritisation of immediate operational demands over the change programme's requirements. Second: middle managers communicate organisational reality to their teams not primarily through formal communication channels but through the hundreds of informal signals — body language, side comments, what they choose to discuss or ignore, how they respond to challenges — that employees use to assess whether the change is genuinely supported at their level of the organisation.

The most effective approach to engaging middle management in transformation is to treat them not as change recipients but as change co-designers: to involve them early in the process of defining what the change means in their specific context, to create genuine channels for their concerns and insights to influence the change design, and to hold them accountable for the change as change leaders in their own right rather than as messengers for executive decisions. This requires a specific reorientation of the change programme's design: investing time with middle management before the change is announced, not after; creating structured forums for middle management to shape the implementation design, not just to receive it; and measuring middle management engagement as a leading indicator of change adoption rather than treating it as a secondary concern after the frontline adoption metrics are established.

Grammar Point 1: Change Communication Language
Launching Change — The Human Invitation, Not the Business Case

Effective change launch language begins with the human experience of the people affected — acknowledging what the change will cost before communicating what it will produce. The change invitation ('here is the organisation we are trying to become, and here is why that is worth the difficulty of the journey') is more powerful than the change announcement ('here is the change we are making and why it is necessary').

Business case framing (less effective): 'The strategic environment requires us to transform our operating model to maintain competitive position. We will be implementing significant structural changes over the next 18 months.'
Human invitation framing (more effective): 'I'm asking you to work differently — to build an organisation that is more resilient, more customer-centred, and more capable. I know what that costs. I know it means uncertainty, learning new things when the current way is comfortable, and a period of less efficiency before more effectiveness. I'm not pretending otherwise. What I'm saying is: the organisation we will build together is worth the difficulty of the journey.'
Vision of change — specific and human: 'In two years, if the transformation goes well, you will start your morning with a customer's full financial picture already in front of you. When you recommend a product, you'll know in real time whether it's right for their situation. That is a more satisfying, more effective, more meaningful job. That is what we are building toward.'

Resistance Language — Diagnose Before Responding

Effective resistance management language begins with diagnosis — understanding what the resistance is signalling — before deciding how to respond. The diagnostic question is not 'how do I overcome this?' but 'what is this person telling me that I might need to hear?' Resistance can be information (signalling a design problem) or emotion (the normal response to disruption) — and the response should match the type.

Opening resistance conversation: 'I want to understand specifically what you're concerned about — not because I'm looking for ways to address your concerns so you stop raising them, but because your experience may be telling me something I need to hear about the design.'
Distinguishing information from emotion: 'A manager who says the new approval process has a four-day delay that breaks our competitive responsiveness is giving me programme information — I should address it. A manager who says I've done this for fifteen years and I don't see why it needs to change is giving me emotional information — I should acknowledge it but it should not change the programme.'
Addressing the sceptic: 'Rather than treating your scepticism as an obstacle, I want to put it at the centre of today's discussion. What are your three most significant concerns? Not to overcome them — to examine them. If you're right about any of them, we need to know now.'
Responding to 'this is just like the last failure': 'I'm not going to tell you this programme is different and expect you to believe me. What were the two or three things you think were most responsible for the previous programme not delivering? Because if any of those things are in the current design, I want to know.'
The J-Curve — Anticipate, Name, and Hold

When the J-curve performance dip arrives, effective change leaders name it as a predicted and expected pattern, explain the mechanism that produces it (learning disruption), and ask the leadership team to hold the new model rather than reverting. The failure mode is to treat the performance dip as evidence that the change was wrong — which produces the premature abandonment of effective transformations.

Naming the J-curve before it arrives: 'Before we see results, we will see a performance dip. That is the normal pattern. I want us to agree now — before the dip arrives — that we will not interpret it as evidence that the transformation is failing. We will interpret it as evidence that the transformation is real.'
When the dip arrives: 'The 8% decline in processing efficiency in the first six weeks is not a signal that the transformation is failing. It is the normal pattern for an organisation that has genuinely changed how it operates. This pattern has a name: the J-curve.'
The mechanism: 'Before performance improves under a new model, it typically gets worse. People are learning, familiar routines have been disrupted, new processes are not yet optimised. This is not failure — it is the cost of genuine change rather than superficial adoption.'
The ask: 'What I need from this leadership team is to hold the line on the new model rather than reverting — and to communicate that holding to your teams. The organisations that abandon the change during the trough never see the recovery that follows.'

Grammar Point 2: Change Architecture and Sequencing Language
Presenting Change Sequencing — Logic, Not Convenience

When explaining the sequence of a transformation, the change leader must explain the logic behind the sequence — not simply announce it. The sequence must address why this change comes before that one, and what would happen if the sequence were reversed. Unexplained sequences invite questioning and resistance; explained sequences communicate strategic intentionality.

Explaining the sequence rationale: 'We are starting with the internal process redesign, not the customer-facing model. I know that feels counterintuitive — the customer-facing model is the visible change. But if we launch the customer promise before the enabling infrastructure is ready, we will promise an experience we cannot deliver. The process redesign creates the platform the customer experience is built on.'
Naming the consequences of wrong sequencing: 'The previous programme launched the customer experience before the internal systems were ready. Customer complaints about the gap between the promised and actual experience increased 34% in the first quarter. We are not going to make that mistake again.'
Addressing pressure to change the sequence: 'I understand the commercial pressure to launch the customer-facing model first. Here is my specific concern with that choice: a broken customer promise at launch is significantly harder to recover from than a delayed announcement. The sequence is not the comfortable choice. It is the choice most likely to work.'
Parallel sequencing: 'Two of the seven workstreams can run in parallel — the technology migration and the capability development programme. These have no dependencies on each other, and running them simultaneously accelerates the overall timeline by four months without creating the coordination risk of paralysing parallel workstreams.'
Building and Using a Change Coalition

Effective change coalition language is specific about what the coalition is for, what it is not, and what specific behaviours distinguish a genuine coalition member from a nominal one. The coalition is not a governance body — it is an advocacy network with genuine conviction about the change.

Defining genuine coalition membership: 'I want to be specific about what I mean by the coalition. I'm not asking you to be on a committee. I'm asking you to be a genuine advocate — to speak for the transformation in your own words, not in the programme's language, in the conversations where the change is being questioned. That requires genuine conviction, not political alignment.'
Distinguishing coalition from coalition-in-name: 'A coalition that participates in governance meetings but is privately sceptical is not a coalition — it is a group of people who have agreed to be associated with the change. The coalition I need is people who genuinely believe in the direction and who will say so when challenged, in environments where I'm not present.'
Activating the coalition at critical moments: 'The next four weeks are when the resistance narrative is most vulnerable to becoming the dominant story. I need every coalition member to have one public, visible conversation this week — in their team meeting, in their leadership forum, in an informal setting — where they speak for the change in their own words. Not the programme's words. Their words. What does this change mean to you and why do you support it?'
Expanding the coalition: 'The coalition needs to include people from across the organisation who the sceptics trust — not just the people who were already aligned. The coalition member who most surprises people by being an advocate is often the most credible.'
Anchoring Change — The Post-Programme Discipline

Anchoring language addresses the specific risk that change erodes after the formal programme ends — when the change team disbands and leadership attention shifts to the next priority. Effective anchoring language is specific about what actions are needed to prevent this, who owns them, and how they will be monitored.

Naming the anchoring risk: 'The programme ends in three months. The structural changes are in place. And the most common failure at this stage is assuming the change will sustain itself. It won't. The old patterns will reassert within twelve months unless specific anchoring actions are taken.'
Specifying anchoring mechanisms: 'Three specific anchoring actions: first, demonstrate the connection between the new behaviours and commercial outcomes — people sustain changes when they can see that the change produces better results. Second, redesign the selection and development processes — new hires should be assessed for alignment with the new norms, and development programmes should reinforce the new behaviours. Third, tell the stories — make the new ways of working the organisational narrative, not the programme's narrative.'
Assigning anchoring ownership: 'The programme team is disbanding. Who owns anchoring? I want a specific person, not a committee, responsible for each of the three anchoring mechanisms for the eighteen months after the programme closes. Who is that person?'
Monitoring anchoring: 'We will review the anchoring metrics — not the programme metrics — every quarter for eighteen months after the programme closes. If the new behaviours are eroding, I want to know before they have fully reverted.'

Grammar Point 3: Culture Change and Capability Development Language
Culture Change Language — Behaviours Before Beliefs

The most effective culture change language is specific about the observable behaviours that would produce the target culture, rather than the aspirational values and beliefs that are the desired output. Culture cannot be communicated into existence — it is shaped by the accumulation of specific, reinforced behaviours over time.

Wrong framing (aspiration without mechanism): 'We want to build a culture of customer-centricity, innovation, and collaboration.' — aspirational but not operational.
Right framing (behaviour specification): 'Customer-centricity means: in any situation where a commercially significant recommendation conflicts with what is best for the customer, the employee raises the conflict rather than proceeding with the commercially favourable option. We will train managers to recognise and reinforce this behaviour, and we will create accountability systems that reward it.'
The real culture test: 'The real test of whether the culture has changed is not the engagement survey. It is: in the last three months, what happened the last time someone gave commercially significant but wrong advice? Did anyone name it? Did anything happen? Those divergence moments — where the old and new cultures would have produced different decisions — are the culture test.'
Modelling versus communicating: 'The most powerful culture change mechanism is not the values statement — it is the CEO's visible behaviour in difficult situations. What I do when someone challenges my decision in a meeting, what I do when a junior person identifies an error in my analysis, what I do when a high-status individual behaves in a way that contradicts our stated values — those behaviours set the culture more powerfully than any communication I can make.'
The ADKAR Diagnostic — Individual-Level Change Language

The ADKAR model provides specific diagnostic language for understanding where individual-level adoption is stalling. Each of the five elements requires a different intervention — confusing them (providing Knowledge to someone who lacks Desire, for example) wastes resources and produces frustration.

Awareness gap: The team leaders in the Northern Region have not received the change communication — they are not aware the new performance management process is being implemented in their area. The intervention is communication, not training.
Desire gap: Alejandro understands the new system and knows how to use it. He is not using it. The intervention is a conversation about personal implications and motivation, not more training.
Knowledge gap: The commercial team has been trained in the new CRM but the training did not cover the specific workflow for their customer segment. The intervention is targeted, role-specific training.
Ability gap: The credit managers know the new risk model conceptually but cannot apply it fluently under time pressure. The intervention is practice and coaching, not more conceptual training.
Reinforcement gap: The new collaboration norms were adopted enthusiastically in the first month and have eroded in the past six weeks. The intervention is management accountability for the new norms, not re-communication of the expected behaviours.
Change Readiness — Before Designing the Programme

Effective change readiness language is used before the programme is designed — to identify the specific gaps between the current state and the state required for successful implementation. Readiness assessment language distinguishes between different readiness dimensions and is specific about what each dimension reveals for programme design.

Leadership alignment readiness: 'Leadership alignment is rated 7.8 out of 10 — consistent with the executive team's self-assessment. But alignment at the executive level does not mean alignment in the leadership team's visible behaviour. I want to spend thirty minutes identifying the specific decisions in the next six months where leadership alignment will be most visibly tested.'
Capability readiness: 'Capability readiness is the most alarming finding: 73% of the roles most affected by the transformation lack the digital capabilities the new operating model requires. This has a direct implication for programme design: we cannot launch the new operating model on the planned timeline without a significant investment in capability development that is not currently in the programme plan.'
Cultural readiness: 'Cultural readiness rated 4.6 — indicating that the existing culture has significant dimensions that are inconsistent with what the transformation requires. Specifically: the risk-avoidance norm, the hierarchy-dependent communication pattern, and the tolerance of inconsistent performance management are all direct obstacles to the target operating model. The culture change programme needs to address these three specific dimensions, not culture in general.'
Using readiness to design the programme: 'The readiness assessment has fundamentally redesigned the first six months. Original design: structural implementation begins month 1. Revised design: capability development and leadership alignment months 1-3; structural implementation months 4-9. The investment in readiness is the highest-return use of the first three months.'

Case Study 1: Kotter's Research on Transformation Failure — Why the Majority of Major Change Programmes Do Not Achieve Their Stated Objectives
How John Kotter's longitudinal research on over 100 organisations attempting major change produced the most widely used change management framework — and what the research reveals about the specific failure patterns that are most common and most preventable

Background

John Kotter's research programme at Harvard Business School, which produced 'Leading Change' (1996) and its subsequent extensions including 'Accelerate' (2014), represents the most comprehensive longitudinal study of major organisational change attempts in the management research literature. Kotter's foundational finding — that the majority (60-70%) of major change programmes fail to achieve their stated objectives — was based on his direct observation of, and consultation with, more than 100 organisations across multiple industries and geographies. What distinguishes Kotter's research from similar studies is its diagnostic specificity: rather than simply documenting the failure rate, Kotter identified the specific errors that caused each failure — enabling him to construct a prescriptive model (the 8-step model) that addresses each of the most common failure modes.

The Most Common Failure Modes

Kotter's research identified eight failure modes that correspond to the eight steps of his model — each representing the consequence of underexecuting the corresponding step. The three most consequential, which appear most frequently in failed transformations, are: insufficient urgency (the change is launched without enough people understanding why the status quo is untenable — producing a coalition of supporters who advocate for change in comfortable settings but abandon the cause when challenged), a guiding coalition that lacks power or conviction (the coalition includes the right formal titles but either lacks the informal influence to drive the change or contains members who are not genuinely committed), and premature victory declaration (short-term wins are treated as evidence that the transformation is complete, causing the change team to wind down activities before the new behaviours have been anchored in the culture). Kotter found that the last of these — premature victory — was the most insidious, because it occurred precisely when the change appeared to be succeeding, making it the hardest failure mode to prevent.

The Most Neglected Success Factor: The Change Narrative

Kotter's later work, particularly 'A Sense of Urgency' (2008) and 'Buy-In' (2010), extended his original analysis to address the most commonly underinvested element of transformation: the change narrative. Kotter's research found that the most successful transformations were characterised not by the quality of their business cases (which were almost always competent) but by the quality of the story they told about why the change was necessary and what kind of organisation they were trying to become. The organisations that produced the most engaged and resilient change advocates were those that connected the transformation to the values and aspirations of their people — asking not only 'why must we change?' but 'what kind of organisation do we want to become?' and 'why is that worth the difficulty of the journey?' These questions produced a fundamentally different quality of organisational commitment than the rational business case alone.

Case Study 2: Organisational Change in Latin American Organisations — The Specific Cultural and Structural Factors That Shape Transformation Dynamics in the Region
How the specific features of Latin American organisational culture — power distance, relationship orientation, and informal authority structures — create both distinctive challenges and distinctive opportunities for transformation leaders in regional organisations

Background

Organisational change in Latin American companies is shaped by several features of the regional business and cultural context that create distinctive dynamics relative to change programmes in Northern European or North American organisations. The higher power distance in Latin American business culture creates a specific relationship between senior leadership behaviour and change adoption: employees who observe senior leaders genuinely modelling the new behaviours are more likely to adopt them (because the authority signal is stronger), but employees who observe inconsistency between what senior leaders say and what they do are more resistant to change (because the credibility loss is proportionally greater). The relationship orientation of Latin American business culture means that change is more likely to succeed when it is introduced through trusted relationships rather than formal programmes — the informal authority of respected leaders is often more influential than the formal authority of the change programme.

The Middle Management Dynamic

Research on change dynamics in Latin American organisations — including studies of financial services, manufacturing, and public sector transformations across Brazil, Colombia, Mexico, and Argentina — finds that the middle management dynamic is more pronounced than in lower-power-distance contexts. In these contexts, middle managers function as particularly powerful interpreters of whether the change is genuine: because their teams defer more to their judgment in high-power-distance environments, a middle manager who privately signals scepticism about a change will produce more resistance in their team than the equivalent signal would produce in a lower-power-distance environment. This creates both a risk (resistant middle managers have disproportionate influence on team adoption) and an opportunity (genuinely committed middle managers who actively advocate for the change in their own words are also proportionally more influential than in lower-power-distance contexts).

Change Sequencing and the Relationship Foundation

A distinctive finding in Latin American change programmes is the importance of the relationship foundation before the change is announced. Research and practitioner experience consistently find that change programmes that invest in relationship building with key stakeholders before the formal announcement generate significantly better adoption outcomes than those that announce the change and then attempt to build relationships with resistors. This is partly a function of the relationship orientation of Latin American business culture (people are more likely to support changes advocated by people they trust), and partly a function of the power distance dynamic (formal authority is accepted, but relational authority often produces more genuine and sustainable behavioural change than the compliance that formal authority generates). Change leaders who invest 4-6 weeks in individual conversations with key stakeholders before the formal announcement consistently report higher initial adoption rates and lower resistance intensity than those who use the announcement as the starting point for stakeholder engagement.
