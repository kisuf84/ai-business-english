📖   Reading

Texts & Comprehension

Read two professional texts: why most stakeholder engagement programmes fail — the three structural conditions that prevent genuine exchange (engagement after decisions are made, abstraction preventing specific accountability, no follow-through on commitments) and how treating engagement as governance rather than communications is the distinguishing characteristic of the most effective organisations; and the art of engaging difficult stakeholders — why hostile stakeholders' expertise (community hydrology knowledge, product performance in practice, systemic risk awareness) is the most valuable external intelligence available, how dismissing opposition as politically motivated misses the specific information it contains, and how separating positions from interests reveals addressable concerns far more specific and manageable than the public opposition suggested.

📋   Grammar Review

Reference Guide

Three reference boxes: stakeholder mapping and engagement planning — from category to individual (the individual map test: could a new team member know specifically who to call, what to say, and what the relationship history is?), engagement objectives as activity versus outcome (the outcome objective test: could an independent observer assess achievement?), and commitment language as specific-owned-time-bound (the commitment test: could the stakeholder determine six months from now whether it was kept?); trust communication and difficult engagement — position versus interest (the test: 'if we addressed that specific concern, would your opposition change?'), trust rebuilding after breach (specific acknowledgement first, honest explanation second, one modest commitment third), and proactive communication (the proactive test: would the stakeholder feel we deliberately withheld it if they found out elsewhere?); and coalition building and multi-stakeholder facilitation — shared interest not shared allegiance, facts-before-positions facilitation, and capturing common ground and real differences as more useful than false consensus.

🏛️   Case Studies

Real-World Cases

Explore Mitchell, Agle, and Wood's stakeholder salience theory (1997) — power, legitimacy, urgency; dormant/expectant/definitive stakeholders; dynamic salience (legitimacy present before power and urgency, triggered by a single event combining with political access or media attention); and the Latin American application where community and indigenous organisations have high legitimacy and urgency but forms of power that conventional frameworks underestimate (community mobilisation, international civil society and investor attention) — organisations applying formal institutional power definitions systematically underestimate their salience until a crisis makes it visible; and community engagement practice in Latin American contexts — FPIC (ILO 169, UNDRIP: prior, free, informed, consent not just consultation), the most common FPIC failure (treating it as consultation rather than consent), and four effective engagement practices (own language and culturally appropriate formats, genuinely representative counterparts, long-term sustained commitment, specific evidence that engagement influenced decisions communicated to adjacent communities).
