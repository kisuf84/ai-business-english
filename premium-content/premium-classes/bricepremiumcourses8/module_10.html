Stakeholder Map
A structured representation of all individuals, groups, organisations, and systems that have an interest in, are affected by, or have the ability to affect an organisation's activities — typically organised along two or more analytical dimensions to reveal the relative priority of different relationships. The most commonly used dimensions in stakeholder mapping are power (the ability to influence outcomes relevant to the organisation) and interest (the degree to which the stakeholder is affected by or concerned with the organisation's activities). More sophisticated frameworks add legitimacy (whether the stakeholder's claim on the organisation is considered valid by other stakeholders) and urgency (whether the stakeholder requires immediate attention). An effective stakeholder map is not a static document — it changes as the organisation's activities evolve, as stakeholder interests shift, and as relationships develop. The most common stakeholder mapping failure: treating the map as a one-time analytical exercise rather than as a living tool for ongoing relationship management, and populating it with categories rather than individuals.
⚖️
Stakeholder Salience
The degree of priority that organisational managers give to competing stakeholder claims — determined by a combination of three attributes: power (the ability to impose one's will), legitimacy (the social acceptance that the stakeholder's claim is appropriate and proper), and urgency (the degree to which the stakeholder's claim demands immediate attention). Stakeholders who possess all three attributes simultaneously are defined as 'definitive stakeholders' and require immediate and primary attention. Stakeholders who possess only one attribute are 'latent stakeholders' who require monitoring but not necessarily active engagement. Stakeholders who possess two attributes are 'expectant stakeholders' who warrant engagement because their salience can increase rapidly if the third attribute is acquired. The salience framework provides a dynamic rather than static basis for stakeholder prioritisation.
📊
Engagement Spectrum
A framework for characterising the depth and nature of engagement between an organisation and its stakeholders — ranging from inform (one-way communication of information, with no expectation of input) through consult (seeking stakeholder views on decisions not yet made, with genuine openness to being influenced) through collaborate (working with stakeholders to develop proposals or recommendations) to co-create (giving stakeholders a genuine role in designing and deciding outcomes, sharing control). The appropriate position for any specific stakeholder relationship depends on: the legitimacy of the stakeholder's claim, the value of their knowledge for the quality of the outcome, the degree of trust, and the organisation's genuine willingness to be influenced. The most common failure: consulting stakeholders on decisions already made — using the language of consultation while the outcome is predetermined, which irreparably damages the credibility of future engagement.
🤝
Trust Building
The sustained, deliberate process of developing the confidence of stakeholders in an organisation's intentions, competence, and reliability — recognising that trust is built slowly through consistent behaviour and destroyed rapidly through a single significant breach. Trust has three interconnected components: competence trust (the belief that the organisation can do what it says it will do), integrity trust (the belief that the organisation will do what it says it will do, even when costly), and benevolence trust (the belief that the organisation genuinely considers the stakeholder's interests alongside its own). Most organisations focus on demonstrating competence while underinvesting in integrity and benevolence trust. The behaviours most strongly associated with trust building: consistent follow-through on commitments, transparent communication about problems (not only successes), genuine acknowledgement of errors, and visible behaviour that demonstrates consideration of the stakeholder's interests even at some cost.
🔴
Hostile Stakeholder
A stakeholder who has actively opposed or publicly criticised the organisation's position, activities, or decisions — and whose engagement requires specific techniques that differ from those applicable to neutral or supportive stakeholders. Hostile stakeholders are typically articulate advocates for a conflicting position, have invested significant social capital in their opposition, and have developed an understanding of the organisation's vulnerabilities. The most common responses — ignoring them, attempting to discredit them, or waiting for the opposition to dissipate — consistently produce worse outcomes than genuine engagement. The principles of effective hostile stakeholder engagement: engage early (before positions have hardened), engage directly (with the person who holds the position), find the legitimate concern underlying the opposition (typically more specific and more addressable than the public position suggests), and demonstrate genuine responsiveness to that concern.
🏛️
Regulatory Engagement
The structured management of an organisation's relationship with the regulatory bodies that govern its activities — including developing the regulator's understanding of the business model, communicating the organisation's perspective on proposed regulatory changes, and building a relationship of mutual respect and productive dialogue. Effective regulatory engagement is distinguished from regulatory lobbying by its purpose: lobbying seeks to change regulatory outcomes in the organisation's favour, while engagement seeks genuine dialogue that serves both the regulator's mandate and the organisation's legitimate interests. The most effective regulatory engagement: is proactive (engaging before issues arise), is transparent (providing accurate information even when unflattering), treats the regulator as a genuine partner in managing risk (not as an adversary to be managed), and is conducted at multiple levels.
🌐
Government Relations
The strategic management of an organisation's relationship with government — including elected officials, civil servants, ministers, and government agencies — to ensure government understands the organisation's activities and perspective, and that the policy environment is informed by accurate information. Government relations is distinguished from lobbying by its breadth: lobbying involves advocacy on specific policy positions, while government relations encompasses the broader relationship-building that provides the foundation for effective advocacy when specific issues arise. The ethical boundaries: providing accurate information and legitimate advocacy are appropriate; providing financial benefits to officials, misrepresenting facts, and exerting undue pressure are not. In Latin American contexts, the function must navigate both formal institutional processes and the informal relationship networks that often determine how policy is actually made.
💬
Multi-Stakeholder Dialogue
A structured conversation involving stakeholders with different — and often competing — interests in a specific issue or decision, facilitated to enable genuine exchange of perspectives, build shared understanding, and in some cases produce shared recommendations. Multi-stakeholder dialogue explicitly brings together parties who may not naturally sit in the same room — community groups, business representatives, government officials, scientists, and civil society advocates — structuring the conversation to create learning across positions rather than advocacy for positions. The facilitation challenges are significant: power dynamics between participants (some have resources, expertise, and social confidence that others lack), different communication styles, and the risk of capture by the best-resourced participants dominating the outcome. The most productive dialogues begin by establishing shared facts and shared interests before engaging with areas of disagreement.
📋
Stakeholder Engagement Plan
A strategic document that identifies the organisation's key stakeholders, characterises their interests and concerns, specifies the engagement approach for each, defines the objectives of each engagement relationship, and provides a calendar of planned engagement activities. An effective plan is distinguished from a communications plan by its focus on relationship outcomes rather than communication outputs: its measure of success is not the number of meetings held but the quality of the relationships developed and the extent to which engagement has genuinely influenced organisational decision-making. Essential components: a current stakeholder map (with power, interest, and attitude assessments), an engagement objective for each priority stakeholder (what specific understanding or change does this aim to achieve?), an engagement approach (what level of the spectrum?), a commitment register (what has been committed to and by when?), and a feedback mechanism.
🌱
Community Engagement
The sustained process of building productive relationships with the communities in which an organisation operates — understanding community interests and concerns, informing communities about the organisation's activities and their impacts, seeking community input on decisions that affect wellbeing, and demonstrating genuine responsiveness to community feedback. Community engagement differs from community consultation in its continuity and genuine openness to being influenced: consultation is conducted at specific decision points to satisfy regulatory requirements; genuine engagement is ongoing, relationship-centred, and involves communities as partners rather than consultation subjects. The most common failure: conducting genuine engagement when community support is needed (project planning, licence applications) and then abandoning the relationship once regulatory approval has been secured. In Latin American contexts, engagement must be in the communities' preferred language and formats, acknowledge the historical context, and involve individuals who are genuinely representative.
📣
Investor Relations
The strategic management of communication between an organisation and its current and potential investors — providing accurate and complete information about performance and prospects, building investor confidence in strategy and management, and managing the relationship through both strong and weak performance periods. Investor relations is distinguished from marketing by its regulatory context (listed companies have specific legal disclosure obligations) and by the analytical sophistication of the investor audience (institutional investors employ analysts who subject corporate communications to rigorous financial scrutiny). Investors are typically high-power and high-interest stakeholders requiring consistent, accurate, and structured communication — but the engagement is constrained by regulatory requirements limiting selective information sharing. The most significant challenge: engaging meaningfully with activist investors who have specific operational or strategic demands without creating information asymmetry with other investors.
🤲
Civil Society Engagement
The management of an organisation's relationship with non-governmental organisations, advocacy groups, think tanks, academic institutions, and other civil society actors. Civil society engagement is strategically valuable because: civil society organisations often have deep expertise in the social and environmental issues most relevant to the organisation's licence to operate; their endorsement provides legitimacy that cannot be obtained through self-reporting; and their opposition, if it develops, can be among the most damaging to reputation. The risks: civil society organisations may use information gained through engagement to campaign against the organisation's positions; the relationship may create expectations of influence the organisation is unwilling to fulfil; and the organisation may be accused of capturing or co-opting independent voices. The most productive partnerships are those in which both parties are explicit about the limits of the relationship.
🔑
Engagement Commitment
A specific, documented undertaking made to a stakeholder in the context of engagement — that the organisation will take a particular action, provide specific information, or achieve a defined outcome by a specified date. Engagement commitments are the primary accountability mechanism in stakeholder relationship management: they convert the goodwill of engagement conversations into specific obligations that both parties can track. The most destructive element of stakeholder engagement programmes: making commitments that are not followed through — either because they were made without authority to deliver, as a way of managing the conversation rather than as a genuine undertaking, or because circumstances changed and the commitment was quietly abandoned. A commitment register — a documented list of all commitments made, with the stakeholder, the specific commitment, the responsible person, and the delivery date — is the minimum governance infrastructure required. The test: could the stakeholder take the commitment and hold the organisation publicly accountable for delivering it?
🔀
Coalition Building
The process of assembling a group of stakeholders who share sufficient common interests to work together toward a shared objective — amplifying the influence of each individual through collective action. Organisations that cannot achieve objectives through bilateral engagement often find that a coalition of aligned stakeholders — speaking with a collective voice representing multiple legitimate interests — can create conditions for change that no individual actor could produce alone. Effective coalition building requires: identifying shared interests that are genuine and substantial (not superficial alignments), designing governance that gives all coalition members a genuine voice, and maintaining the coalition through the setbacks and compromises that complex advocacy involves. The risks: coalitions can be captured by their most powerful members; they can become ends in themselves; and they can create obligations to partners that limit the organisation's flexibility to adapt its position.
🔭
Stakeholder Salience Analysis
The systematic assessment of the power, legitimacy, and urgency of each stakeholder's claim on the organisation — used to prioritise engagement investment across a complex stakeholder ecosystem. Salience analysis goes beyond the two-dimensional power-interest matrix by adding legitimacy as a third dimension — recognising that a stakeholder may have significant power without having a legitimate claim, and vice versa. In practice it involves: assessing each stakeholder's power (financial, political, social, informational, or reputational), assessing the legitimacy of their claim (is it based on genuine interest, legal right, or moral principle?), assessing the urgency (does it require immediate attention?), and combining these assessments to determine the salience priority — which stakeholders require proactive, intensive engagement, which require monitoring, and which can be addressed through general communications.
🌊
Conflict Resolution
The structured process of addressing disagreements, disputes, and opposing positions between an organisation and its stakeholders — with the objective of reaching outcomes that acknowledge the legitimate interests of all parties rather than simply imposing the most powerful party's preferred solution. Conflict resolution requires: a genuine willingness to listen without immediately defending the organisation's position, the ability to distinguish between the stakeholder's stated position (what they say they want) and their underlying interests (what they actually need), the willingness to explore solutions that serve the underlying interests of both parties even if they do not match either party's stated position, and the credibility to make and keep commitments as part of the resolution. The most common failure: engaging in apparent conflict resolution while maintaining the original position — going through the motions of listening without genuine openness to changing the outcome.
📱
Digital Stakeholder Engagement
The use of digital platforms — social media, online consultation tools, digital surveys, virtual meetings, and online community forums — to extend the reach, accessibility, and continuity of stakeholder engagement beyond what in-person interactions can achieve. Digital engagement has transformed the stakeholder environment: it has enabled organised opposition to develop and coordinate much faster (a social media campaign can mobilise significant public opposition within hours); it has made it easier to reach geographically dispersed stakeholders; and it has created an expectation of real-time responsiveness that the communication protocols of most large organisations are not designed to meet. The specific risks: online platforms amplify extreme positions more effectively than moderate ones, the anonymity of online environments reduces accountability, and the public nature of digital engagement means conversations that might be managed constructively in private become adversarial when conducted in public.
🎯
Engagement Objective
The specific, defined outcome that an organisation seeks from a particular stakeholder engagement interaction or programme — answering the question 'what would success look like for this engagement relationship?' An engagement objective is more specific than a relationship goal — it identifies a concrete change in understanding, attitude, commitment, or behaviour that the engagement aims to achieve (for example: 'the regulator understands and is supportive of our approach to alternative credit data by the end of Q3'). Having a clear objective disciplines the engagement strategy — the choice of channel, the content, the frequency, and the measures of success all flow from the objective. The most common failure: defining objectives in terms of the organisation's activities ('conduct quarterly meetings') rather than in terms of stakeholder outcomes ('the community understands and accepts the noise management approach by the end of the consultation period').
💡
Listening to Stakeholders
The disciplined practice of genuinely attending to what stakeholders say — as distinct from the performance of listening that characterises much formal stakeholder consultation. Genuine listening requires: creating conditions in which stakeholders feel safe to communicate their genuine concerns, actively seeking out perspectives that challenge the organisation's assumptions (rather than validating the preferred narrative), demonstrating that what has been heard has genuinely influenced the organisation's thinking or decisions, and feeding back to stakeholders what was heard and what, specifically, will change as a result. The test of genuine listening: does the engagement produce information that the organisation did not already know and that is genuinely useful for improving its decisions? If the engagement primarily confirms what the organisation already believed, it is a performance of listening rather than genuine engagement.
🌐
Cross-Sector Partnership
A collaborative relationship between organisations from different sectors — typically business, government, and civil society — to address a problem or opportunity that no single sector can effectively address alone. Effective cross-sector partnerships require: a genuinely shared objective (not a collaboration in which each party has a different primary objective), clear governance that gives all partners genuine voice and accountability, an explicit understanding of what each partner brings and expects in return, and a process for managing the inevitable tensions between organisations with different cultures, time horizons, and accountability structures. The most significant risk: the more powerful partners — typically the business or the government agency — inadvertently or deliberately shaping the partnership's agenda in ways that reflect their interests rather than the shared objective.
📞
Proactive Stakeholder Communication
The discipline of communicating with stakeholders before they seek information from the organisation — sharing relevant information, providing advance notice of decisions, and updating stakeholders on commitments — rather than only responding to stakeholder requests. Proactive communication is the opposite of the defensive posture that characterises many large organisations: in defensive posture, the organisation releases as little information as possible and responds to enquiries reactively. Proactive communication recognises that in an era of digital media and active civil society, information not provided will typically be sought elsewhere — from less accurate sources, in a less favourable context, and without the organisation's interpretation — making proactive disclosure consistently more effective than information management. The key principle: tell your stakeholders something important before they find out from someone else.

Text 1: Beyond Consultation — Why Most Stakeholder Engagement Programmes Fail to Produce the Relationships They Claim to Seek

The language of stakeholder engagement has become more sophisticated than the practice it describes. Most large organisations now speak fluently about 'genuine partnership,' 'co-creation,' and 'listening deeply to the communities we serve.' Their stakeholder engagement strategies reference the International Association for Public Participation engagement spectrum and commit to moving beyond information provision to genuine collaboration. And yet the research on community and stakeholder perceptions of corporate engagement programmes — from mining communities in Latin America and Australia to financial services customers in Europe and Southeast Asia — consistently finds that the primary experience of stakeholders is being managed rather than genuinely heard. The gap is not primarily one of intention: most executives who commission engagement programmes genuinely believe that their organisations are listening. The gap is primarily one of structure: the structures through which engagement is conducted — consultations with predetermined agendas, feedback processes with no genuine decision-making connection, community meetings designed to legitimate decisions rather than inform them — systematically prevent the genuine exchange of perspectives that the language of engagement promises.

The structural conditions that prevent genuine stakeholder engagement are identifiable and largely consistent across sectors and geographies. First: the engagement occurs after the decision has effectively been made. The organisation has reached an internal conclusion about what it will do, and the engagement process is designed to explain and justify that conclusion — not to genuinely explore alternatives. Stakeholders who raise concerns are given information and reassurance rather than genuine alternatives; their feedback is recorded and documented but does not change the outcome. Second: the engagement is conducted at a level of abstraction that prevents specific accountability. The organisation consults on 'our approach to water management' rather than on the specific water management practice that will change the groundwater level of a specific community's wells. The abstraction prevents stakeholders from raising the specific concerns that are most material to them, and prevents the organisation from making the specific commitments that would be most valuable. Third: the follow-through does not exist. Commitments made in engagement conversations are not documented, tracked, or reported — and the stakeholders who raised specific concerns receive no feedback on whether those concerns influenced any decision, ever.

The organisations that are most effective at stakeholder relationship management share a characteristic that is operationally simple and politically difficult: they treat stakeholder engagement as a governance mechanism — a source of information, challenge, and accountability — rather than as a communications function. The implication is that genuine engagement findings must be capable of changing decisions, which means that engagement must occur before decisions are made, must involve stakeholders in the analysis of the problem rather than only in the reaction to the proposed solution, and must be connected to decision-making processes through formal mechanisms that ensure the information generated by engagement reaches the people with authority to act on it. Organisations that treat engagement as a governance mechanism accept a constraint that most prefer to avoid: genuine stakeholder input can change the organisation's plans.

Text 2: The Art of Engaging Difficult Stakeholders — Why the Stakeholders Most Worth Engaging Are Often the Ones Organisations Most Avoid

There is a consistent pattern in the stakeholder engagement programmes of most large organisations: engagement resources are concentrated in relationships with stakeholders who are sympathetic or neutral, while the stakeholders who most actively oppose the organisation's activities — the most vocal critics, the organised opposers, the persistent challengers — receive either avoidance (the organisation refuses to engage) or managed contact (the organisation engages in a way designed to manage the opposition without genuinely addressing its concerns). This pattern is understandable: engaging with sympathetic stakeholders is comfortable, productive in the short term, and generates the testimonials and endorsements that demonstrate the value of the engagement programme. Engaging with hostile stakeholders is uncomfortable, slow, and frequently fails to produce the visible results that justify the investment. But the research on long-term stakeholder outcomes — across industries from mining to financial services, from infrastructure to technology — consistently finds that the most durable stakeholder problems are those that were not addressed through genuine engagement with the opposing stakeholders when the opportunity existed.

The most valuable thing about a hostile stakeholder is typically not their hostility — it is their expertise. Organised opposition to an organisation's activities is almost always grounded in a specific, substantive concern that the organisation's internal analysis has not adequately addressed: a community's knowledge of the local hydrology that the organisation's environmental model did not capture, a consumer advocate's understanding of how a financial product performs in practice rather than in the scenarios modelled by the organisation's product designers, a regulator's awareness of systemic risks that the organisation's risk management framework treats as low-probability events. The hostile stakeholder's specific, substantive concerns — if they can be accessed — represent the most valuable external intelligence available to the organisation. The organisation that dismisses opposition as politically motivated misses the specific information that the opposition contains. The organisation that engages genuinely with the substantive concerns beneath the public opposition acquires exactly the intelligence needed to address the issue before it becomes a crisis.

The specific technique of engaging genuinely with hostile stakeholders requires what skilled negotiators call 'separating positions from interests.' The hostile stakeholder's public position — 'we oppose this development entirely' — is typically not a negotiating position or even a stable preference. It is the hardest version of a set of concerns that the stakeholder has adopted because they do not trust the organisation to respond to a more nuanced expression of their interests. The experienced engagement practitioner does not engage with the position — doing so leads to positional argument, which produces winners and losers rather than solutions. Instead, they engage with the question: what would need to be true for this stakeholder to be satisfied? What are the specific concerns beneath the public opposition, and which of those concerns is the organisation actually able to address? The answers — which almost always require patient, direct, individual conversation rather than formal consultation — typically reveal a set of addressable concerns that are significantly more specific and more manageable than the public position suggested.

Grammar Point 1: Stakeholder Mapping and Engagement Planning Language
Stakeholder Map — From Category to Individual

An effective stakeholder map operates at the level of specific individuals with specific interests and concerns — not at the level of categories like 'regulators' or 'community groups.' The difference is the difference between knowing who your stakeholders are and knowing what they actually think.

Category map (insufficient): 'Banking regulators — high power, high interest.' — Who specifically? What specifically do they care about?
Individual map (effective): 'Director of Supervision, [name] — high power, high interest. Primary concern: model transparency. Secondary concern: consumer default experience. Currently: neutral/curious. Priority: pre-entry briefing before regulatory submission.'
Dynamic mapping — tracking attitude change: 'National Association of Informal Traders — moved from opposed to cautious following direct engagement in March. Specific concern acknowledged and partially addressed: predatory pricing. Remaining concern: absence of default support mechanism. Engagement objective: demonstrate redesigned credit product with coaching support element.'
The individual map test: could a new team member read this map and know specifically who to call, what to say, and what the relationship history is? If not, the map is still at the category level.
Engagement Objectives — Activity Versus Outcome

An engagement objective specifies a change in understanding, attitude, commitment, or behaviour that the engagement aims to achieve — not the engagement activity itself. An activity objective can be achieved regardless of whether the engagement produces any value; an outcome objective is achieved only when the engagement has produced genuine change.

Activity objective (insufficient): 'Conduct quarterly regulatory engagement meetings.' — This can be achieved whether or not the meetings produce any useful outcome.
Outcome objective (effective): 'By end of Q3, the banking regulator's policy team has sufficient understanding of our alternative credit model to accurately represent it in their position paper on alternative data regulation.'
Commitment objective: 'By the first board meeting after the community meeting, the community liaison committee has received our specific response to each of the five concerns raised in July, with a clear statement of which concerns we are addressing and which we are not, and why.'
The outcome objective test: could an independent observer assess whether this objective has been achieved? If yes, it is an outcome objective. If it can be achieved by completing activities regardless of their effect, it is an activity objective.
Commitment Language — Specific, Owned, and Time-Bound

An engagement commitment follows the same discipline as an action item in a meeting: specific deliverable, named owner, specific date. The most destructive engagement commitment is one made sincerely but not specific enough to be held accountable for — a promise that felt genuine at the time but that the stakeholder and the organisation interpret differently.

Vague commitment (high risk): 'We will keep you informed as the project develops.' — What information? How frequently? By whom? What constitutes 'keeping informed'?
Specific commitment (lower risk): 'By the last Friday of each month, [name] will send the community liaison committee a written update on the construction progress, including any changes to the noise management schedule and any incidents that occurred during the month.'
Commitment with an explicit constraint: 'I can commit to providing you with the water quality data by April 28th. I cannot commit to what that data will show — but I can commit that you will receive it before we make any operational decisions based on it, and that I will call you personally to discuss it.'
The commitment test: could the stakeholder read this commitment and determine, six months from now, whether it has been kept? If yes, it is a commitment. If not, it is a general undertaking that will produce different interpretations on both sides.

Grammar Point 2: Trust Communication and Difficult Engagement Language
Engaging With Hostility — Position Versus Interest

The discipline of separating the stakeholder's stated position (what they say they want) from their underlying interests (what they actually need) is the most important technique in difficult stakeholder engagement. Engaging with positions produces argument; engaging with interests produces solutions.

Engaging with position (typical and counterproductive): 'We understand you oppose our development. We would like to explain why our development meets the relevant standards...' — This begins an argument about positions that will harden rather than resolve the conflict.
Engaging with interest (effective): 'Before I explain our position on anything, I want to understand specifically what concerns you. Not the statement you've published — I've read that. What specifically would need to be different about what we are planning for you to have a different view? What is the specific outcome you're most concerned about?'
Validating the concern beneath the position: 'I hear that you're concerned about groundwater contamination. I want to understand the specific contamination pathway you're most concerned about — because the way we address a surface runoff risk is different from the way we address a deep aquifer risk, and I want to address the actual concern rather than a generic version of it.'
The position-interest test: 'If we addressed that specific concern completely, would your opposition change?' — If yes, you are engaging with an interest. If the answer is 'there's still the issue of X,' you have identified a new interest to engage with.
Trust Rebuilding After Breach — Sequence and Specificity

Rebuilding trust after a commitment failure requires three elements in sequence: a specific acknowledgement of what was committed and not delivered (not a general apology), an honest explanation of why (not an excuse), and one specific, modest new commitment — not a comprehensive programme, but one thing that can be delivered and verified.

Wrong sequence (general apology first): 'We apologise for any disappointment caused by our failure to deliver the information on time. We are committed to improving our communication processes...' — The generality signals that the organisation is managing the incident rather than genuinely acknowledging the failure.
Right sequence (specific acknowledgement first): 'We committed to providing you with the water quality data by March 31st. We did not provide it. Here is specifically why: [honest explanation]. Here is the one specific thing we will do differently: [one specific commitment with date and named owner].'
The explanation without excuse: 'The reason we did not deliver is that we made an internal decision to wait for reliable data before sharing anything. That was the wrong decision because we made it without telling you — effectively withdrawing from a commitment without communicating that we were doing so.'
The modest first commitment: 'I am not going to make a comprehensive list of changes. I am going to make one commitment: by April 28th, [name] will send you the water quality data with a personal call to discuss it. When that commitment is kept, I will ask you what the next most important commitment should be.'
Proactive Communication — Before, Not After

The discipline of proactive stakeholder communication requires the organisation to communicate difficult information before the stakeholder hears it from another source. The sequence — tell first, explain fully, acknowledge the implications — is reversed in defensive communication, which explains what happened after the stakeholder already knows.

Reactive (defensive) sequence: 'You will have seen the report in today's newspaper about [incident]. We want to provide you with the accurate picture of what happened...' — The stakeholder knows; the organisation is now explaining.
Proactive sequence: '[Call before the newspaper story breaks.] I want to tell you something difficult before you read it elsewhere. [Incident description]. Here is what we know, here is what we don't know yet, here is what we are doing, and here is when I will next update you.' — The stakeholder hears it from the organisation first.
Proactive regulatory communication: 'I am requesting this meeting because we have identified an issue in our credit model that I believe you should hear about from us immediately rather than through our formal reporting cycle. I do not have complete information yet — but I believe you should know what we know now rather than what we know after our internal review is complete.'
The proactive test: 'If our stakeholder found out about this from someone else, would they feel we had deliberately withheld it from them?' If yes — communicate proactively, immediately, even if the information is incomplete.

Grammar Point 3: Coalition Building and Multi-Stakeholder Facilitation Language
Coalition Proposal Language — Shared Interest, Not Shared Allegiance

An effective coalition proposal identifies the specific shared interest that justifies collaboration — rather than asking for a general alignment or endorsement. The most productive coalition partnerships are the most narrow: organisations with a single shared interest who are transparent about what they agree on and what they do not.

Broad coalition request (often rejected): 'We share the same values around financial inclusion and we believe we could achieve more by working together...' — This asks for general alignment that may not exist.
Specific coalition proposal (more effective): 'I am proposing that our two organisations jointly advocate for one specific regulatory change: the recognition of informal business income in credit assessment. We disagree on many other things — and I'm not asking you to change those positions. I'm asking whether, on this one specific issue where our interests are identical, we can work together.'
Coalition boundary setting: 'I want to be clear about what this coalition would and would not mean. It would mean joint advocacy to the regulator on informal income recognition. It would not mean endorsement of Meridian's lending practices generally. We can collaborate on the specific issue without either organisation implying broader agreement.'
Unexpected coalition building: 'I know this proposal will surprise you given our previous public disagreements. I am not pretending those disagreements don't exist. But on this specific issue, we have genuinely identical interests — and our combined credibility with the communities we both serve and with the regulator is greater than either of us separately.'
Multi-Stakeholder Facilitation Language — Facts Before Positions

The most productive multi-stakeholder dialogues establish shared factual understanding before engaging with areas of disagreement. The facilitation language that achieves this separates 'what do we agree is true?' from 'what do we disagree about what to do?' — preventing positions from hardening before a shared factual foundation has been established.

Premature positional engagement: 'Today we will hear each organisation's position on the regulatory framework for alternative credit data.' — This immediately puts organisations in advocacy mode, preventing genuine dialogue.
Facts-first facilitation: 'Before we discuss positions on the regulatory framework, I want us to spend the morning establishing what we can agree is factually true about the current state of financial access in Chile. I will present three data sources. My request: if any organisation believes the data is inaccurate, tell me specifically what you believe the accurate data to be and why. We will not move to the policy discussion until we have established the factual foundation.'
Shared interest articulation: 'We have identified from each organisation's submission that you all share a stated interest in improving financial access for informal sector workers. Before we discuss how, I want to test the shared interest more specifically: are there conditions under which this shared interest would not be a genuine priority for your organisation? I want to understand where the shared interest is real and where it is nominal.'
Capturing common ground and real differences: 'At the end of today, I want to produce one document: an accurate picture of where there is genuine common ground among all parties and where there are genuine differences. Not a consensus document — an honest map of the landscape. That map is more useful than a false consensus.'

Case Study 1: Stakeholder Salience Theory — The Most Influential Framework for Understanding Why Organisations Prioritise Some Stakeholders Over Others
How Mitchell, Agle, and Wood's 1997 theory of stakeholder identification and salience transformed the practice of stakeholder management — and what the theory reveals about why apparently powerful organisations sometimes lose to apparently powerless ones

Background

The most influential academic framework for understanding stakeholder prioritisation was published by Ronald Mitchell, Bradley Agle, and Donna Wood in the Academy of Management Review in 1997. Their paper, 'Toward a Theory of Stakeholder Identification and Salience: Defining the Principle of Who and What Really Counts,' proposed that managers give priority to stakeholder claims based on the combination of three attributes: power (the ability to impose one's will), legitimacy (the social acceptance that the claim is appropriate and proper), and urgency (the degree to which the claim requires immediate attention). The theory generates eight types of stakeholders based on which combination of these attributes they possess — from 'dormant stakeholders' (power only, no legitimacy or urgency) through 'definitive stakeholders' (power, legitimacy, and urgency) to 'non-stakeholders' (none of the three attributes). The most important practical insight of the theory: salience is not fixed. A stakeholder who initially lacks one of the key attributes can rapidly acquire it — a community group with high legitimacy and high urgency but low power that acquires a powerful political ally becomes a high-salience stakeholder almost overnight, and organisations that did not engage with it when its salience was low find themselves with a crisis that early engagement might have prevented.

The Dynamic Nature of Salience

The practical value of the Mitchell, Agle, and Wood framework is not primarily in its classification of current stakeholder salience but in its prediction of salience change. The theory suggests that organisations should monitor not only which stakeholders are currently high-salience but which low-salience stakeholders have the potential to rapidly acquire the attributes that would make them high-salience. In particular: legitimacy is often present before power and urgency — a community group that has a legitimate claim on an organisation often lacks the power to enforce it and the urgency to prioritise it. When a single triggering event (an environmental incident, a product failure, a wage dispute) combines with available political access or media attention, the legitimacy that was always there becomes the foundation for rapidly acquired urgency and power. The organisations most effective at stakeholder management are those that engage with high-legitimacy stakeholders before those stakeholders have acquired power and urgency — when the engagement can be genuine and constructive rather than reactive and defensive.

Application to Latin American Contexts

The salience framework is particularly useful in Latin American business contexts because of the specific power dynamics of civil society and community stakeholders in the region. Community groups and indigenous organisations in Latin America often have high legitimacy and high urgency but relatively low formal power — as defined by the Mitchell, Agle, and Wood framework, which measures power primarily in terms of financial, political, and informational resources. However, these organisations frequently have access to forms of power that the framework underestimates: the ability to mobilise community-level action that makes operations practically impossible even without formal political or legal power, and the ability to attract international civil society and investor attention to local issues in ways that translate community legitimacy into significant reputational and financial consequences for the organisations involved. The practical implication: Latin American businesses and international investors entering Latin American markets that apply a power definition derived primarily from formal institutional resources will systematically underestimate the salience of community and civil society stakeholders until a crisis makes that underestimation visible.

Case Study 2: Community Engagement in Latin American Business — The Specific Cultural, Legal, and Social Dynamics That Shape What Effective Community Engagement Requires in Regional Contexts
How Latin American community engagement contexts differ from other regions — and the specific practices, legal obligations, and relationship disciplines that distinguish effective from counterproductive community engagement in the region

Background

Community engagement in Latin American business contexts operates within a distinctive set of legal, cultural, and historical factors that significantly shape what genuine engagement requires. The legal dimension includes the specific obligations created by Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) frameworks — derived from ILO Convention 169 and the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples — which apply in many Latin American jurisdictions and create specific obligations for engagement with indigenous communities that go beyond the requirements of most global stakeholder engagement frameworks. The cultural dimension includes the specific historical context of corporate-community relationships in the region — characterised in many industries by a history of extraction that benefited external investors at the expense of local communities — which creates a context of justified scepticism that effective community engagement must acknowledge and address. The social dimension includes the specific dynamics of informal economy communities, where the formal institutional representation structures that community engagement programmes often rely on may not exist or may not be representative of the communities they nominally represent.

The FPIC Framework

Free, Prior, and Informed Consent is a principle that establishes the right of indigenous peoples to be consulted about and to consent to activities that affect their territories, resources, and ways of life — before those activities commence (prior), without coercion or artificial time pressure (free), with adequate information about the nature and scope of the activities and their potential impacts (informed), and through legitimate processes that are recognised by the communities themselves (consent, not merely consultation). FPIC has been incorporated into the legal frameworks of many Latin American countries and has been adopted as a standard by the IFC Performance Standards and the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, making it a relevant obligation for any organisation seeking international finance or operating to international governance standards. The most common FPIC implementation failure: treating FPIC as a consultation requirement rather than a consent requirement — conducting engagement designed to satisfy a procedural obligation without genuine openness to the community withholding consent.

The Practice of Effective Community Engagement

Research and practitioner experience in Latin American community engagement consistently identifies several practices that distinguish effective from counterproductive engagement. First: engagement must occur in the communities' own language and in formats that are culturally appropriate — engagement conducted primarily through written submissions and formal meetings will systematically exclude community members who are most affected but least comfortable with formal processes. Second: the community members who participate in engagement must be genuinely representative of the communities they speak for — in many Latin American communities, engagement with formal community leaders may exclude women, young people, and community members from specific social groups who have distinct interests in the organisation's activities. Third: engagement must be sustained over the long term — organisations that build community relationships when they need licences and abandon them when they have them will find that the relationship cannot be restored when it is next needed. Fourth: the organisation must demonstrate, through specific evidence, that community engagement has influenced its decisions — communities that see their input recorded but not acted upon will disengage and will communicate that disengagement to adjacent communities, reducing the effectiveness of future engagement across the region.
