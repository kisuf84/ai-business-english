📚 Key Legal Terms
🏛️
Corporate Governance
The system of rules, practices, and processes by which a company is directed and controlled. It balances the interests of shareholders, management, employees, and other stakeholders, and defines how objectives are set, attained, and monitored.
👔
Board of Directors
The governing body of a company, elected by shareholders to oversee management and protect the interests of the company and its stakeholders. Responsible for strategy, risk oversight, and executive accountability.
⚖️
Fiduciary Duty
A legal obligation of the highest trust owed by one party to another — specifically, the duty a director owes to act in the best interests of the company. Directors must act in good faith, avoid conflicts, and not profit at the company's expense.
📊
Shareholder
An individual or institution that owns shares in a company, giving them voting rights and a claim on profits (dividends) and assets. Shareholders are the legal owners of a company and can hold the board accountable.
🌐
Stakeholder
Any party with an interest in a company's activities — including shareholders, employees, customers, suppliers, regulators, and the wider community. Good governance balances the interests of all stakeholders, not shareholders alone.
📅
Annual General Meeting
The yearly meeting at which shareholders exercise their rights — including voting on resolutions, approving accounts, electing directors, and approving remuneration policies. Listed companies are legally required to hold an AGM.
📋
Articles of Association
The constitutional document of a company, setting out the rules governing internal management — including powers of directors, shareholder voting rights, and share transfer procedures. Binding on the company and its members.
🔑
Company Secretary
A senior officer responsible for ensuring the company complies with statutory and regulatory requirements. Maintains corporate records, organises board meetings, and advises the board on governance matters. Listed companies must have one.
🔍
Non-Executive Director
A board member who is not part of the executive management team. Their role is to provide independent oversight, challenge management, and protect the interests of all stakeholders. The UK Code requires a majority of independent NEDs on listed company boards.
📑
Audit Committee
A sub-committee of the board responsible for overseeing financial reporting, internal controls, and the relationship with external auditors. Must comprise at least three independent non-executive directors, one of whom must have recent relevant financial experience.
💷
Remuneration
The total package of pay and benefits received by directors and senior management. Executive remuneration must be disclosed and approved by shareholders. The remuneration committee — comprising independent NEDs — determines executive pay.
🔎
Due Diligence
A thorough investigation of a company's financial, legal, operational, and governance position before a transaction or significant decision. A governance obligation requiring boards to make informed decisions based on adequate information.
📣
Whistleblowing
The act of reporting suspected wrongdoing — illegal activity, regulatory breaches, or serious risks — through internal or external channels. Companies must have a protected disclosure policy. Protection is provided by the Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998.
⚠️
Conflict of Interest
A situation where a director's personal interests could improperly influence their decisions on behalf of the company. Directors must declare conflicts to the board, and typically must not participate in decisions where a conflict exists.
🔓
Transparency
The principle that companies must disclose accurate, timely, and relevant information about their activities, performance, and governance to shareholders and other stakeholders. A cornerstone of investor confidence and market integrity.
📢
Accountability
The obligation of directors and management to explain and justify their decisions and performance to shareholders and other stakeholders. Enforced through board oversight, external audit, regulatory supervision, and legal liability.
📜
Compliance
Adherence to all applicable laws, regulations, codes, and internal policies. In a governance context, compliance is the responsibility of the board, which must establish a culture of compliance throughout the organisation.
🏗️
Regulatory Framework
The body of laws, regulations, codes, and guidance that govern a sector or area of activity. The UK corporate governance framework includes company law, the UK Corporate Governance Code, FCA listing rules, and sector-specific regulations.
🌱
Corporate Social Responsibility
A company's voluntary commitment to operating in an ethical, socially responsible, and environmentally sustainable way. Increasingly embedded in governance frameworks as stakeholder expectations and ESG requirements grow.
🚨
Insider Trading
The buying or selling of securities using material non-public information, in breach of a duty of confidentiality. A criminal offence under the Criminal Justice Act 1993 and a serious regulatory offence under the Market Abuse Regulation.
🛑
Anti-Bribery Compliance
Systems and controls designed to prevent, detect, and respond to bribery. The UK Bribery Act 2010 creates a strict liability offence for commercial organisations that fail to prevent bribery — the only defence is having adequate procedures.

Text 1: The UK Corporate Governance Code and the Duties of Directors

The UK Corporate Governance Code — published by the Financial Reporting Council and most recently updated in 2024 — sets out principles and provisions for the governance of companies with a premium listing on the London Stock Exchange. It applies on a 'comply or explain' basis: listed companies must either comply with its provisions or explain clearly in their annual report why they have not done so. This approach reflects a philosophy that good governance cannot be reduced to a simple checklist — it requires judgement, context, and transparency. The Code is organised around five themes: Board Leadership and Company Purpose; Division of Responsibilities; Composition, Succession and Evaluation; Audit, Risk and Internal Control; and Remuneration.

At the heart of the Code is the board of directors — collectively responsible for the long-term success of the company and accountable to shareholders. The Code requires that a majority of board members should be independent non-executive directors (NEDs), whose role is to provide constructive challenge to executive management while bringing relevant skills and experience. The Code emphasises that the board must have a clear understanding of company culture and ensure that it is aligned with the company's values and strategy. NEDs are expected to scrutinise the performance of management, contribute to strategy development, and satisfy themselves that financial information is accurate and that financial controls and systems of risk management are robust.

Directors owe fiduciary duties to the company under the Companies Act 2006 — codified in Sections 171 to 177. These include: the duty to act within powers; the duty to promote the success of the company; the duty to exercise independent judgment; the duty to exercise reasonable care, skill, and diligence; the duty to avoid conflicts of interest; the duty not to accept benefits from third parties; and the duty to declare any interest in a proposed transaction. These duties are owed to the company — not to individual shareholders — and breach of a fiduciary duty may result in civil liability, disqualification as a director, and in some cases criminal prosecution.

Text 2: Regulatory Frameworks — Anti-Bribery, Money Laundering, and Market Abuse

Corporate compliance in the United Kingdom rests on three particularly demanding regulatory frameworks. The first is the Bribery Act 2010, which created the most stringent anti-bribery regime in the world. The Act creates four offences: bribing another person; being bribed; bribing a foreign public official; and — most significantly for organisations — the corporate offence of failing to prevent bribery. This last offence is one of strict liability: a commercial organisation is guilty if a person associated with it bribes another person to obtain or retain business, unless the organisation can demonstrate that it had 'adequate procedures' in place to prevent bribery. The six principles for adequate procedures — proportionate procedures, top-level commitment, risk assessment, due diligence, communication, and monitoring — are set out in government guidance and form the basis of any robust anti-bribery compliance programme.

The second pillar is the anti-money laundering (AML) framework, governed principally by the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 and the Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds Regulations 2017. Money laundering is the process by which criminally obtained funds are made to appear legitimate. The AML regime places obligations on regulated businesses — including banks, law firms, accountants, and estate agents — to carry out customer due diligence, monitor transactions for suspicious activity, and report suspicions to the National Crime Agency. Failure to report a known or suspected instance of money laundering — a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) — is itself a criminal offence. The consequences of AML failures can be severe: regulatory fines, criminal prosecution of individuals, and reputational damage that can threaten the survival of the business.

The third framework is the Market Abuse Regulation (MAR), which applies to all companies with securities traded on regulated markets in the UK. MAR prohibits insider dealing — trading in securities using material non-public information — and market manipulation. It imposes positive obligations on companies: maintaining insider lists, implementing market soundings procedures, and making prompt disclosure of inside information to the market under the Disclosure and Transparency Rules. For compliance officers, MAR is a daily operational challenge — particularly around managing earnings announcements, M&A transactions, and the treatment of analysts. Breach of MAR can result in substantial FCA fines, public censure, and criminal prosecution for the individuals involved.

Grammar Point 1: Complex Sentences in Governance Writing
Concession Clauses — 'Although', 'Despite', 'Notwithstanding'

Concession clauses acknowledge a complicating factor while maintaining a primary point. In governance writing, they are essential for presenting balanced, nuanced analysis — particularly in 'comply or explain' statements.

'Although the company did not comply with Provision 9, the board considers that its existing structure is appropriate given the company's size and ownership profile.'
'Despite receiving legal advice that the transaction was permissible, the board decided not to proceed in order to avoid any perception of a conflict of interest.'
'Notwithstanding the challenging regulatory environment, the company maintained full compliance with all applicable provisions of the Code.'
Purpose Clauses — 'In Order To', 'So As To', 'So That'

Purpose clauses explain why an action is taken. They are used extensively in governance reports, board minutes, and compliance documentation to link actions to their intended objectives.

'The board conducted an annual evaluation in order to assess its effectiveness and identify areas for improvement.'
'The audit committee met with the external auditor without management present so as to ensure a frank and independent exchange.'
'The company maintains an insider list so that it can demonstrate to the FCA which individuals had access to inside information at any given time.'
Result Clauses — 'As a Result', 'Consequently', 'Therefore'

Result clauses link a cause to its consequence. In governance and compliance writing, they are used to explain regulatory consequences, board decisions, and remedial actions.

'The board identified a material weakness in internal controls; as a result, the audit committee commissioned an immediate independent review.'
'The company failed to file its Suspicious Activity Report within the required timeframe; consequently, it faced regulatory action from the FCA.'
'The director failed to disclose his conflict of interest. The board therefore determined that his participation in the relevant vote was invalid.'
📋
Grammar Point 2: Formal Reporting Language in Governance Documents
Board Minutes — Reporting Decisions and Actions

Board minutes are formal records of decisions, actions, and discussions. They use consistent, precise language to record what was confirmed, instructed, approved, and noted — avoiding informal language entirely.

'The board confirmed that...' / 'The chair confirmed that...' (recording a confirmation)
'The board approved / resolved / agreed that...' (recording a decision)
'The audit committee instructed management to...' (recording an instruction)
'The board noted that...' / 'It was noted that...' (recording information received without a decision)
Governance Reports — Formal Verbs and Structures

Annual governance reports use a specific register that is formal, objective, and achievement-oriented. The passive voice is common; where the active voice is used, the subject is usually the board, committee, or company — not named individuals.

'The board conducted a formal evaluation of its own performance during the year.'
'The company complied with all applicable provisions of the UK Corporate Governance Code.'
'The remuneration committee considered and approved the executive pay policy in accordance with the approved remuneration policy.'
'A review of the company's risk management and internal control systems was undertaken by the audit committee.'
Regulatory Communications — Precision and Non-Admission

Communications with regulators must be precise, complete, and carefully qualified. Avoid inadvertent admissions; use 'it appears', 'on the information available', and 'without admission' where appropriate.

'On the basis of our current understanding of the facts, it appears that...'
'The company does not accept that the conduct described in your letter constitutes a breach of the applicable regulations.'
'This response is provided without prejudice to the company's position on any related matter.'
'We are cooperating fully with your investigation and will provide the requested information by [date].'
📋
Grammar Point 3: Noun Clauses in Board Minutes and Reports
'That' Clauses — Reporting Facts and Decisions

'That' clauses are the standard structure for reporting what was said, decided, confirmed, or noted in governance documents. The conjunction 'that' is usually retained in formal writing (unlike informal speech where it is often omitted).

'The board confirmed that the company had complied with all material obligations under the Code.'
'The audit committee noted that the external auditor had raised no significant concerns.'
'The compliance officer reported that the SAR had been filed on the required date.'
'The company secretary reminded directors that declarations of interest must be made at each meeting.'
Wh- Clauses — Reporting Questions and Explanations

'How', 'what', 'which', and 'why' clauses report what was asked, explained, or discussed. They are used in minutes to record the substance of discussions without quoting individual speakers directly.

'The NED asked how the company intended to address the shortfall in the compliance budget.'
'Management explained what steps had been taken to remediate the control weakness identified in the audit.'
'The board discussed why the project had exceeded its budget and what corrective action was proposed.'
'The chair inquired what engagement had taken place with major shareholders ahead of the AGM.'
Embedded Questions — Indirect Reporting of Inquiries

When questions are reported in minutes or governance reports, they become embedded noun clauses using the normal sentence word order (subject + verb) — not the inverted question word order.

Direct question: 'How did management respond to the auditor's findings?'
Reported (correct): 'The NED asked how management had responded to the auditor's findings.'
Direct question: 'What is the basis for the provision?'
Reported (correct): 'The audit committee asked what the basis for the provision was.'

Introduction to Corporate Governance

Welcome to Module Six of Legal and Compliance English. In this module, we explore the language of corporate governance and regulation — the frameworks, duties, and processes through which companies are directed, controlled, and held accountable. Corporate governance matters because companies — particularly large, publicly listed ones — wield enormous economic and social power. When governance fails, the consequences can be catastrophic: the collapse of Enron, the failure of Lehman Brothers, and more recently the governance failures exposed during various corporate scandals have all demonstrated the cost of poor oversight, misaligned incentives, and inadequate accountability. Good corporate governance, by contrast, builds investor confidence, reduces the cost of capital, attracts talented directors and management, and creates the conditions for sustainable long-term performance. In this module, you will learn the vocabulary of the boardroom: the duties of directors, the role of non-executive directors, the structure of board committees, and the obligations imposed by the UK Corporate Governance Code. You will also study three critical regulatory frameworks — anti-bribery, anti-money laundering, and market abuse — that every compliance professional in the UK must understand. The language skills you develop will equip you to draft board minutes, compliance memos, and governance reports with confidence and precision.

Anti-Bribery Compliance: The Bribery Act 2010
Regulatory Briefing · B2 Level · approx. 106 sec
0:00

The UK Bribery Act 2010 is one of the most demanding pieces of anti-corruption legislation in the world. It has extraterritorial reach — meaning it applies not only to UK companies but also to foreign companies that carry on any part of their business in the UK. There are four offences under the Act. The first two are individual offences — bribing another person, and being bribed. The third is bribing a foreign public official — a specific offence designed to target corrupt payments in international business. The fourth — and most significant for organisations — is the corporate offence of failing to prevent bribery. This offence is unique: unlike virtually any other criminal offence, it does not require proof of knowledge or intent. A commercial organisation is guilty if a person associated with it — an employee, agent, or subsidiary — bribes another person to obtain or retain business for the organisation. The only defence is that the organisation had adequate procedures in place to prevent bribery. The government has published six principles for adequate procedures: proportionate procedures; top-level commitment, meaning the board must set the right tone from the top; risk assessment; due diligence on business partners and third parties; communication and training; and monitoring and review. The consequences of a conviction under the Act include an unlimited fine, confiscation of assets, and the potential to be debarred from public procurement.

Market Abuse and Insider Trading: A Compliance Perspective
Compliance Briefing · B2 Level · approx. 108 sec
0:00

Market abuse is one of the most serious and closely monitored areas of financial regulation. The Market Abuse Regulation — known as MAR — applies across the UK financial markets and prohibits three types of conduct: insider dealing, unlawful disclosure of inside information, and market manipulation. Let us focus on insider dealing. Inside information is defined as specific, precise information that has not been made public, which relates to a financial instrument or its issuer, and which, if made public, would be likely to have a significant effect on its price. When a person who holds inside information — whether they are an employee, an adviser, or anyone else in a position of confidence — buys or sells securities to take advantage of that information, they commit the offence of insider dealing. For compliance officers in listed companies, this creates a daily operational challenge. The company must maintain a list of insiders — all persons who have access to inside information at any given time. The company must also implement procedures to prevent inside information from leaking. When inside information exists — for example, during a merger negotiation or ahead of an earnings announcement — the company has a choice: it must either disclose the information to the market promptly, or apply to the relevant authority for a delay in disclosure, which is permitted only in narrow circumstances. The consequences of market abuse enforcement are serious: the FCA can impose unlimited financial penalties, and individuals can face criminal prosecution and imprisonment. In recent years, the FCA has significantly increased its use of criminal powers in market abuse cases.

Case Study 1: The Collapse of Marconi plc — A Governance Failure
How a flawed strategy, inadequate board oversight, and poor accountability destroyed one of Britain's largest technology companies

Background

Marconi plc was one of Britain's most celebrated industrial companies — a FTSE 100 technology and defence business with revenues of over £7 billion and a reputation for engineering excellence. Between 1999 and 2001, the company undertook a dramatic transformation under its chief executive, Lord Simpson, and finance director, John Mayo. The board approved a strategy of divesting core defence and industrial businesses and reinvesting the proceeds in the booming US technology sector — acquiring a series of American telecommunications equipment companies at premium prices just as the technology bubble was reaching its peak. When the bubble burst in 2000 and 2001, Marconi's core revenues collapsed, it was left with billions of pounds of debt, and its share price fell from over 1,200p to less than 2p. The company never recovered.

The Governance Failures

The Marconi collapse was subsequently analysed as a textbook example of governance failure. Non-executive directors had either failed to challenge the strategic transformation or lacked the relevant industry expertise to do so effectively. The board did not receive adequate information about the risks of the acquisitions programme, and risk management processes were wholly inadequate. The remuneration structure rewarded the executives for short-term revenue growth rather than long-term value creation, creating a misalignment of incentives. There was no effective mechanism by which the board could slow or reverse the strategy once problems began to emerge. The nomination process had prioritised loyalty over independence and relevant experience, weakening the board's capacity to exercise genuine oversight.

Lessons for Corporate Governance Practice

Marconi's collapse contributed to the reform of the UK Combined Code (now the Corporate Governance Code) and accelerated demands for stronger NED independence, greater board diversity, and more robust risk oversight. For governance practitioners, the case teaches several enduring lessons. First, independence without relevant expertise is insufficient — NEDs must have the knowledge to challenge management credibly. Second, effective governance requires that the board receives timely, accurate, and complete information — not the information management wishes it to have. Third, remuneration structures must align long-term shareholder interests with management behaviour. Fourth, the board's role is not merely to approve management's strategy but to provide rigorous, independent scrutiny of whether that strategy is in the company's long-term interest. Fifth, governance processes — including nomination, audit, and risk oversight — must operate with genuine independence to be effective.

United Kingdom · Financial Crime · 2012 · AML and Regulatory Sanctions
Case Study 2: Standard Chartered Bank — AML Failures and Regulatory Action
How systemic failures in anti-money laundering controls led to landmark enforcement action on two continents

Background

In August 2012, the New York Department of Financial Services (DFS) issued a dramatic order against Standard Chartered Bank, a major UK-headquartered international bank, alleging that it had conducted approximately 60,000 transactions involving Iran in breach of US sanctions — with a total value of approximately 250 billion dollars. The DFS alleged that the bank had deliberately stripped identifying information from wire transfers to disguise their Iranian origin and avoid triggering automated compliance systems. The order described the bank as a 'rogue institution' and threatened to revoke its New York banking licence. The bank entered into a consent order with the DFS, paying 340 million dollars in penalties. Subsequent regulatory action by US federal authorities and, in 2019, by the UK Financial Conduct Authority added further billions to the total penalties paid.

The Regulatory and Compliance Issues

The Standard Chartered enforcement actions highlighted several systemic failures in the bank's compliance culture and AML controls. First, there was a failure of tone from the top: internal emails produced in evidence suggested that senior figures within the bank were aware of the transactions and had, in some cases, actively sought to suppress compliance concerns raised by US-based staff. Second, the bank's transaction monitoring systems were inadequate to detect the concealment of transaction origins. Third, the bank failed to maintain an effective compliance function that had the authority and resources to identify and escalate concerns. The case also highlighted the extraterritorial reach of US financial regulation — a risk that many international banks had underestimated — and the willingness of the US authorities to pursue enforcement actions with enormous financial consequences.

Lessons for Compliance Professionals

The Standard Chartered case has become a landmark study in financial crime compliance. For compliance professionals, it teaches several critical lessons. First, tone from the top is not merely a governance platitude — if senior management is seen to suppress or dismiss compliance concerns, the compliance function is effectively undermined. Second, transaction monitoring systems must be regularly tested and updated to detect new evasion techniques. Third, compliance must have genuine authority and independence — not merely a reporting line to management, but the ability to escalate concerns directly to the board and, if necessary, to regulators. Fourth, international organisations must map their exposure to extraterritorial regulatory regimes — particularly US sanctions and AML requirements, which have extraordinary reach. Fifth, the reputational and financial cost of AML failures dwarfs the cost of an effective compliance programme.
