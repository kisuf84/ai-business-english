📚 Key Legal Terms
🔬
Patent
A time-limited exclusive right granted by the state to an inventor, giving them the right to prevent others from making, using, or selling the patented invention without permission. In the UK, patents last up to 20 years from the filing date.
™️
Trademark
A sign — such as a word, logo, shape, or colour — that distinguishes the goods or services of one business from those of another. Registration gives the owner exclusive rights to use the mark in the relevant class of goods or services.
©
Copyright
An automatic right protecting original literary, artistic, musical, and dramatic works. Copyright prevents copying, adaptation, or distribution without the owner's permission. It lasts for the author's life plus 70 years in most jurisdictions.
🔐
Trade Secret
Confidential business information — such as a formula, process, or customer list — that gives a competitive advantage and is protected by keeping it secret. Unlike patents, trade secrets can last indefinitely but offer no protection if independently discovered.
⚠️
Infringement
The unauthorised use of intellectual property protected by patent, trademark, copyright, or design right. Infringement entitles the rights holder to seek remedies including injunctions, damages, and an account of profits.
📄
Licence
Permission granted by an IP rights holder (the licensor) to another party (the licensee) to use the IP in a specified way, territory, and for a specified period. A licence can be exclusive, sole, or non-exclusive.
💰
Royalty
A payment made by a licensee to a licensor for the right to use intellectual property. Royalties may be calculated as a percentage of revenue, a fixed sum per unit, or a lump sum.
📚
Prior Art
All publicly available knowledge existing before the filing date of a patent application. If an invention is already part of the prior art, it cannot be patented — even if the applicant was unaware of it.
✨
Novelty
One of the key requirements for patentability. An invention is novel if it is not part of the prior art — that is, it has not been publicly disclosed anywhere in the world before the patent application's filing date.
💡
Inventive Step
Also called 'non-obviousness'. A patentable invention must not be obvious to a person skilled in the relevant field at the time of the application. This requirement prevents minor modifications of known technology from being patented.
📅
Filing Date
The date on which a patent application is officially lodged with the patent office. The filing date establishes the applicant's place in the queue and is critical for assessing novelty and inventive step.
🏁
Priority Date
The date from which the novelty and inventive step of a patent are assessed — usually the filing date of the first application. Filing in one country and claiming priority within 12 months allows protection to be sought internationally.
📋
Claim
The numbered paragraphs at the end of a patent specification that define the scope of legal protection sought. Only what is claimed is protected — claims must be clear, concise, and supported by the description.
📝
Specification
The full written description of a patent application, including the technical field, background, detailed description of the invention, and the claims. It must enable a skilled person to reproduce the invention.
🚫
Passing Off
A common law tort protecting the goodwill of an unregistered business. It requires: goodwill, misrepresentation, and damage. It protects against another business trading in a way that is likely to cause confusion with an established business.
🎨
Design Right
Protection for the appearance of a product — its shape, configuration, pattern, or ornamentation. In the UK, unregistered design right arises automatically; registered design right provides stronger, more easily enforced protection.
🧑‍🎨
Moral Rights
Non-economic rights belonging to authors and creators — primarily the right of attribution (to be identified as the author) and the right of integrity (to object to derogatory treatment of the work). Cannot be transferred, only waived.
⚖️
Fair Dealing
A UK copyright exception allowing limited use of a copyright work without permission for specific purposes including research, private study, criticism, review, news reporting, and parody. The use must be 'fair' — not excessive or commercial.
🌐
Exhaustion of Rights
The principle that once an IP-protected product has been sold by or with the consent of the rights holder in a territory, the rights holder cannot use IP law to control further resale of that product within that territory.
🔄
Assignment
The permanent transfer of IP rights from the owner (assignor) to another party (assignee). Unlike a licence, an assignment transfers ownership — the assignor no longer has any rights in the IP.
🏭
Compulsory Licence
A licence granted by a government authority without the rights holder's consent — typically to enable access to patented medicines or technology in the public interest. Permitted under the TRIPS Agreement subject to conditions.

Text 1: How Patent Protection Works — From Application to Enforcement

A patent gives its owner the legal right to prevent others from making, using, selling, or importing the patented invention for a period of up to 20 years from the filing date. In exchange for this monopoly, the inventor must fully disclose the invention in a written specification — this disclosure becomes public and forms part of the prior art after publication, contributing to the global body of technical knowledge. To qualify for patent protection in the United Kingdom, an invention must satisfy four key criteria: it must be novel (not part of the prior art), involve an inventive step (not obvious to a skilled person), be capable of industrial application, and not fall within the excluded categories — which include discoveries, mathematical methods, mental acts, and computer programs 'as such'.

The patent application process begins with filing at the Intellectual Property Office (IPO). The applicant submits a specification describing the invention and a set of claims defining the scope of protection sought. The filing date is critical — it determines the applicant's priority and is the date from which novelty and inventive step are assessed. Within 12 months of the first filing, the applicant may claim priority under the Paris Convention and file applications in other countries, using the same priority date. The IPO then conducts a prior art search and substantive examination. If the application meets all requirements, the patent is granted and published. Annual renewal fees must be paid to keep the patent in force.

Owning a patent does not automatically mean competitors comply. Enforcement is the rights holder's responsibility. If infringement is suspected, the rights holder may send a cease and desist letter, seek an interim injunction to stop the infringing activity immediately, and ultimately bring proceedings in the Intellectual Property Enterprise Court (IPEC) or the High Court. Remedies for infringement include damages (compensating for actual loss), an account of profits (requiring the infringer to pay over the profits made from infringement), and delivery up or destruction of infringing products. Patent litigation is expensive and technically complex — many disputes are settled through licensing negotiations rather than pursued to judgment.

Text 2: Trademarks, Passing Off, and Brand Protection

A trademark is one of the most commercially valuable forms of intellectual property. Well-known trademarks — such as those of global luxury brands, pharmaceutical companies, and technology firms — can be worth billions of pounds, far exceeding the value of the physical assets of the business. A registered trademark gives its owner the exclusive right to use the mark in relation to the goods or services for which it is registered, and the right to prevent others from using identical or confusingly similar signs. In the United Kingdom, trademarks are registered with the Intellectual Property Office in one or more of 45 classes of goods and services, reflecting the international Nice Classification system. Registration is renewable every 10 years and can last indefinitely — unlike patents and copyright, trademarks do not have a fixed duration.

Not all brand protection depends on registration. The common law tort of passing off protects the goodwill of an unregistered business against misrepresentation by another trader that causes damage. The 'classic trinity' required to establish passing off was set out in Reckitt and Colman Products Ltd v Borden Inc [1990] — popularly known as the Jif Lemon case. The claimant must prove: (1) that it has goodwill — a reputation or customer recognition associated with its business; (2) that the defendant has made a misrepresentation — by trading in a way likely to confuse customers into believing they are dealing with the claimant; and (3) that this misrepresentation has caused or is likely to cause damage to the claimant's goodwill. Passing off is particularly useful where a trademark has not been registered, where the scope of registration does not cover the alleged infringement, or where registration has lapsed.

Brand owners face an escalating threat from counterfeiting — the manufacture and sale of goods bearing a counterfeit mark that is identical to a registered trademark. Counterfeiting is both a civil wrong and a criminal offence in the UK under the Trade Marks Act 1994. It causes significant economic harm to rights holders, exposes consumers to safety risks, and is closely connected to organised crime. Enforcement strategies for brand protection include monitoring marketplaces (both physical and online), working with customs authorities to intercept counterfeit goods at the border, and taking civil or criminal action against infringers. Many major brand owners work with specialist IP enforcement agencies and use technological solutions such as holographic labels, QR codes, and blockchain-based authentication to combat counterfeiting.

Grammar Point 1: Complex Noun Phrases in IP Writing
What Are Complex Noun Phrases?

A complex noun phrase is a noun with pre- and post-modifiers that together convey precise meaning efficiently. IP writing — patents, licence agreements, trademark filings — relies heavily on complex noun phrases to pack technical and legal information into compact, unambiguous expressions.

'an exclusive territorial manufacturing licence' (pre-modifiers: exclusive, territorial, manufacturing)
'a sufficiently detailed written technical description' (pre-modifiers: sufficiently detailed, written, technical)
'the scope of protection claimed in claim 1' (post-modifier: prepositional phrase)
'any product incorporating the patented process' (post-modifier: participial phrase)
Pre-Modification vs Post-Modification

Pre-modifiers (adjectives, nouns used as adjectives) go before the noun. Post-modifiers (prepositional phrases, relative clauses, participial phrases) go after. Legal precision often requires both.

Pre-modifier: 'the registered trademark owner' / 'a non-exclusive sublicence agreement'
Post-modifier: 'the rights holder under the licence' / 'any device falling within the scope of the claim'
Combined: 'the original registered design right holder in the United Kingdom'
Combined: 'a royalty-free perpetual non-exclusive licence to use the software'
Precision Through Noun Phrase Structure

In patent claims specifically, every word in a noun phrase matters. Adding or removing a modifier can change the scope of protection. The drafter must balance breadth (wider protection) against clarity (easier to enforce).

Broad claim: 'a device for transmitting data' (few modifiers — broad scope)
Narrow claim: 'a wireless handheld device for transmitting encrypted digital data over a cellular network' (highly specific — narrower scope)
Too vague: 'a useful method' (not patentable — fails clarity requirement)
Best practice: match the noun phrase to what you can distinguish from prior art

Grammar Point 2: Conditional Sentences in IP Agreements
Type 1 Conditionals — Real Conditions in Agreements

IP agreements use Type 1 conditionals (if + present simple, shall/will + infinitive) to define obligations and rights that will arise if a condition is met. 'Shall' creates a binding obligation; 'may' grants a discretion.

'If the licensee fails to pay royalties, the licensor shall be entitled to terminate the agreement.'
'If the patent is invalidated, all licence fees paid shall be refunded within 30 days.'
'If either party wishes to terminate, it shall give 90 days' written notice.'
'If the invention is used outside the licensed territory, the licensor may claim additional royalties.'
Type 2 and Inverted Conditionals — Hypothetical and Formal

Type 2 conditionals (if + past simple, would + infinitive) are used to discuss hypothetical scenarios. In formal IP drafting, the inverted form ('should', 'were', 'had') replaces 'if' for added formality.

'Should the licensee breach the territorial restriction, the licensor would be entitled to seek an injunction.'
'Were the patent to be invalidated on appeal, the enforcement proceedings would lapse.'
'Should prior art emerge that anticipates the invention, the application may be refused.'
'Had the applicant disclosed the invention before filing, the novelty requirement would not have been met.'
Conditional Phrases Specific to IP Law

IP law uses certain conditional phrases with precise technical meanings. Knowing these helps in reading licence agreements, patent office decisions, and court judgments.

'provided that' — introduces a condition: 'the licence shall be renewed, provided that no breach has occurred'
'unless' — introduces an exception: 'the patent shall not be granted unless all criteria are satisfied'
'subject to' — introduces a qualification: 'subject to payment of the renewal fee, the patent shall remain in force'
'in the event that' — introduces a contingency: 'in the event that the mark is cancelled, the licensee may terminate'
📋
Grammar Point 3: Relative Clauses in IP Specifications and Judgments
Defining Relative Clauses in Patent Claims

Patent claims use defining relative clauses (no commas) to specify exactly which product, process, or feature is being claimed. 'Which', 'that', and 'wherein' are standard. Every word restricts or defines the scope of protection.

'A device which converts electrical energy into mechanical motion.' (defining what type of device)
'A process that comprises the steps of heating, mixing, and filtering.' (defining the process)
'The compound of claim 1, wherein the substituent is selected from the group consisting of...' (defining within a class)
'Any method that uses the compound of formula (I) to treat inflammation.' (defining by function and purpose)
Non-Defining Relative Clauses in IP Judgments

IP judgments and agreements use non-defining relative clauses (with commas) to add non-essential information about already-identified IP rights. 'Which' and 'whose' are used — never 'that' in non-defining clauses.

'The patent in suit, which was granted in 2015, covers a novel fermentation process.'
'The licensor, whose IP portfolio includes over 200 patents, granted an exclusive worldwide licence.'
'The defendant's product, which incorporates all features of claim 1, constitutes a direct infringement.'
'The trademark, which has been registered since 1988, is protected in all 45 classes.'
'Wherein' and 'Whereby' — Patent Claim Conventions

'Wherein' (meaning 'in which') and 'whereby' (meaning 'by which' or 'as a result of which') are conventions specific to patent claim drafting. They introduce features of a claimed invention in dependent claims.

'The method of claim 1, wherein the temperature is maintained between 60°C and 80°C.'
'A device as claimed in claim 3, wherein the housing is formed from a thermoplastic material.'
'A system according to claim 5, whereby the data is transmitted via an encrypted channel.'
'The apparatus of any preceding claim, wherein the sensor is a piezoelectric transducer.'

Introduction to Intellectual Property: An Overview
Lecture · B1–B2 Level · approx. 93 sec
0:00

Welcome to Module Five of Legal and Compliance English. In this module, we explore the world of intellectual property — the legal framework that protects the creations of the mind. Intellectual property law covers four main areas: patents, which protect inventions; trademarks, which protect brands and signs that distinguish products and services; copyright, which protects original creative works; and design rights, which protect the appearance of products. In addition, trade secrets — confidential business information that gives a competitive advantage — are protected through a combination of contract law and the law of confidence. Understanding IP law is increasingly important for professionals in every sector. Whether you work in technology, pharmaceuticals, fashion, publishing, or financial services, IP issues will arise throughout your career. The value of intellectual property in the modern economy is enormous — for many technology companies, IP assets represent the majority of their total value. In this module, you will build the vocabulary and language skills to navigate IP agreements, read patent specifications, engage with trademark disputes, and draft basic IP correspondence at a B1 to B2 level.

Filing a Patent: A Step-by-Step Guide
Practical Briefing · B1–B2 Level · approx. 108 sec
0:00

Filing a patent application is a precise, multi-stage process that requires careful preparation and a thorough understanding of what the patent system can and cannot protect. The first and most important step — before anything is disclosed publicly — is to conduct a prior art search. This means searching existing patents, scientific literature, and other publicly available sources to determine whether the invention has already been described. If prior art exists that anticipates all the features of the invention, the application will fail the novelty requirement. If prior art exists that makes the invention obvious, it will fail the inventive step requirement. Once the prior art search is complete and the inventor is satisfied that the invention is patentable, the application is filed with the Intellectual Property Office. The application must include a specification — a written description of the invention that is clear and complete enough to enable a person skilled in the relevant field to carry out the invention. The specification concludes with the claims — the numbered paragraphs that define the precise scope of protection sought. After filing, the applicant has 12 months in which to decide whether to file in other countries while maintaining the benefit of the original filing date as the priority date. This is the Paris Convention window, and it is a critical strategic decision — filing in every country is expensive, so applicants must choose their key markets. The IPO will conduct a prior art search and formal examination before deciding whether to grant the patent.

Licensing IP: Negotiating a Technology Licence
Business Briefing · B2 Level · approx. 110 sec
0:00

Licensing is one of the most commercially important tools in the intellectual property world. Rather than selling IP outright — which would be an assignment — a licence allows the rights holder to retain ownership while permitting another party to use the IP on agreed terms. This can generate a steady revenue stream through royalties, expand the reach of the technology into new markets or territories, and allow the licensor to focus on innovation rather than commercialisation. When negotiating a technology licence, several key parameters must be agreed. First: exclusivity. An exclusive licence gives the licensee the sole right to use the IP in the defined territory — even the licensor itself cannot use it during the term. A non-exclusive licence allows multiple licensees simultaneously. A sole licence sits between the two — only the licensee and the licensor can use it. Second: territory. The licence must specify which countries or regions it covers. IP rights are territorial — a UK patent does not protect against infringement in Germany. Third: royalty structure. Royalties can be structured as a percentage of net sales, a fixed sum per unit, milestone payments, or a combination. Negotiating the right royalty rate requires detailed knowledge of the market, the technology's competitive advantage, and the likely sales volume. Fourth: sublicensing. Can the licensee permit a third party to use the IP? This must be expressly addressed — without a sublicensing right, the licensee cannot grant further licences. Fifth: term and termination. How long does the licence last, and what are the grounds for termination? These are often the most heavily negotiated provisions in any licensing deal.

Case Study 1: Pozzoli SpA v BDMO SA [2007] EWCA Civ 588
The Court of Appeal's reformulation of the Windsurfing test for assessing inventive step in UK patent law

Background

Pozzoli SpA v BDMO SA concerned a patent for a type of jewellery box with a hinged lid designed to display the contents. BDMO challenged the validity of Pozzoli's patent, arguing that the invention lacked an inventive step — that is, that the design would have been obvious to a skilled person at the priority date given the prior art. The case reached the Court of Appeal, where Jacob LJ took the opportunity to clarify and restructure the established test for inventive step that had been used in UK patent law since Windsurfing International Inc v Tabur Marine (GB) Ltd [1985]. The Windsurfing test was widely used but had been criticised for occasional confusion in its application, and Jacob LJ's reformulation was intended to provide greater clarity for future cases.

The Legal Issues

Jacob LJ reformulated the four-step test for inventive step as follows: (1) Identify the notional person skilled in the art. (2) Identify the relevant common general knowledge of that person. (3) Identify the inventive concept of the claim in question — which may or may not be the same as its overall subject matter. (4) Identify what, if any, differences exist between the matter cited as prior art and the inventive concept of the claim. (5) Viewed without any knowledge of the alleged invention, would those differences have been obvious to the person skilled in the art, or would they have required any degree of invention? Applying this test to the facts, the Court of Appeal held that the patent was invalid for obviousness — a skilled designer of jewellery boxes would have arrived at the same design without inventive effort.

Lessons for IP Practice

Pozzoli is now the standard test for inventive step in UK patent proceedings and is applied in virtually every patent dispute. For IP practitioners, the case teaches several important lessons. First, the 'person skilled in the art' is a legal construct — not a real person, but a hypothetical technician with knowledge of the field but no inventive capacity. Second, the inventive concept of a claim is not necessarily the same as what the claim says in its broadest terms — identifying it correctly requires careful analysis of the specification as a whole. Third, hindsight must be rigorously excluded: the question is whether the invention would have been obvious at the priority date, not with the benefit of knowing the invention. Fourth, commercial success of a product covered by a patent can be evidence of inventive step — if the product filled a long-felt want, this may suggest the solution was not obvious.

United Kingdom · Trademark Law · 1990 · Passing Off
Case Study 2: Reckitt and Colman Products Ltd v Borden Inc [1990] 1 WLR 491
The House of Lords decision establishing the 'classic trinity' for passing off — the Jif Lemon case

Background

This case — widely known as the Jif Lemon case — concerned the distinctive lemon-shaped plastic container used by Reckitt and Colman to sell their Jif lemon juice since 1956. The container's shape and appearance had become strongly associated in consumers' minds with the Jif brand. When Borden Inc began selling their own lemon juice in a similarly shaped container, Reckitt and Colman brought proceedings for passing off, arguing that the public would be confused into believing that Borden's product was the well-known Jif lemon juice. Reckitt and Colman did not have a registered trademark in the shape of the container — their claim relied entirely on the common law tort of passing off.

The Legal Issues

The House of Lords held that Reckitt and Colman had established all three elements of passing off. First, the claimant had substantial goodwill — the lemon-shaped container had been on the market for over 30 years and had become distinctively associated with their product. Second, there had been a misrepresentation — a substantial proportion of the public was likely to be confused into believing that Borden's product was the Jif product when they encountered the similar container in a supermarket. Third, there was damage — the diversion of sales to Borden's product would damage Reckitt and Colman's goodwill. Lord Oliver set out what has become the definitive statement of the three requirements for passing off — goodwill, misrepresentation, and damage — now universally known as the 'classic trinity'.

Lessons for Brand Protection

The Jif Lemon case remains one of the most important decisions in UK trademark and passing off law. For IP practitioners and brand owners, it teaches several key lessons. First, passing off can protect the distinctiveness of a product's shape or appearance — not just its name — provided that the shape has acquired sufficient goodwill through use. Second, even without a registered trademark, a brand owner who has invested in building a distinctive product identity over many years can prevent a competitor from trading on that reputation. Third, the three-part classic trinity — goodwill, misrepresentation, damage — provides a structured analytical framework that should be applied sequentially: all three elements must be established. Fourth, surveys and market research can be powerful evidence of consumer confusion in passing off cases.
