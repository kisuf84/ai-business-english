📚 Key Legal Terms
⚖️
Unfair Dismissal
The termination of an employee's contract without a fair reason or without following a fair procedure. Employees with two years' continuous employment have the right not to be unfairly dismissed. Fair reasons include capability, conduct, redundancy, illegality, and some other substantial reason.
🚪
Constructive Dismissal
Where an employee resigns in response to a fundamental breach of their contract of employment by the employer — such as a demotion, a pay cut, or a hostile working environment. The employee treats themselves as dismissed and may claim unfair dismissal.
📦
Redundancy
A fair reason for dismissal where the employer requires fewer employees to do a particular type of work. A fair redundancy requires a genuine redundancy situation, fair selection, meaningful consultation, and consideration of alternative employment.
🛡️
Discrimination
Treating someone less favourably because of a protected characteristic, or applying a provision that puts people with a protected characteristic at a disadvantage. Prohibited under the Equality Act 2010 in employment and other contexts.
🌈
Protected Characteristic
One of the nine characteristics protected under the Equality Act 2010: age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, and sexual orientation.
♿
Reasonable Adjustment
A change that an employer is required to make to remove or reduce a substantial disadvantage faced by a disabled employee. The duty to make reasonable adjustments arises where a provision, criterion, or practice puts a disabled person at a substantial disadvantage.
📋
Grievance
A formal complaint raised by an employee about a workplace matter — such as the conduct of a colleague, a management decision, or a breach of their contractual rights. Employers must have a written grievance procedure and must follow it when a grievance is raised.
🔴
Disciplinary
The process by which an employer addresses an employee's misconduct or poor performance. A fair disciplinary process requires investigation, a written invitation to a hearing, the hearing itself, a decision, and the right of appeal. Failure to follow a fair process may result in an unfair dismissal finding.
🤝
Settlement Agreement
A legally binding contract under which an employee agrees to waive their employment law claims in exchange for a financial payment and other agreed terms. The employee must receive independent legal advice before signing. Formerly known as a compromise agreement.
📞
ACAS
The Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service — an independent public body that provides free, impartial information and advice to employers and employees. ACAS early conciliation is a mandatory step before an employment tribunal claim can be submitted.
🏛️
Employment Tribunal
A specialist judicial body that hears employment disputes — including unfair dismissal, discrimination, and unlawful deduction from wages claims. Proceedings are less formal than court, but decisions are legally binding and can be appealed to the Employment Appeal Tribunal.
📅
Notice Period
The period of notice required to terminate an employment contract, either by the employer or the employee. Minimum statutory notice is one week per year of employment (up to 12 weeks). Contractual notice may be longer. Pay in lieu of notice (PILON) may be agreed.
⚠️
Wrongful Dismissal
A dismissal in breach of the employment contract — typically where the employer fails to give the required notice or pay in lieu of notice, or dismisses in breach of a contractual disciplinary procedure. A contractual claim, not a statutory one — no qualifying period required.
📣
Whistleblowing
Making a protected disclosure of information that a worker reasonably believes relates to wrongdoing — including criminal activity, health and safety risks, or breaches of a legal obligation. Protected by the Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998. Dismissal for whistleblowing is automatically unfair.
🔗
Vicarious Liability
The legal principle by which an employer is held responsible for the wrongful acts of its employees committed in the course of their employment. An employer may be vicariously liable for discrimination, harassment, or other torts committed by an employee against a colleague or third party.
🤱
Maternity Leave
The statutory right of a pregnant employee to take up to 52 weeks' maternity leave. The first 26 weeks is ordinary maternity leave; the second 26 weeks is additional maternity leave. Statutory maternity pay is payable for up to 39 weeks.
🤜
Collective Bargaining
Negotiation between an employer (or employers' association) and a recognised trade union over pay, terms and conditions, and other matters. A trade union may apply for statutory recognition where 10% of the workforce are members and a majority are likely to support recognition.
🔄
Transfer of Undertakings
The transfer of a business or part of a business from one employer to another. Governed by the Transfer of Undertakings (Protection of Employment) Regulations 2006 (TUPE). Employees' contracts of employment transfer automatically to the new employer on the same terms.
🕐
Zero Hours Contract
A contract of employment under which the employer is not obliged to offer any minimum number of hours and the worker is not obliged to accept any hours offered. Workers on zero hours contracts retain certain statutory rights including the national minimum wage and holiday pay.
💷
National Minimum Wage
The minimum hourly rate that workers aged 16 and over are legally entitled to receive, set by the government annually. The National Living Wage applies to workers aged 21 and over and is set at a higher rate. Failure to pay is a criminal offence.
⏰
Working Time
Regulated by the Working Time Regulations 1998, which implement the EU Working Time Directive. Key provisions include a maximum 48-hour average working week (which workers may opt out of), a minimum of 28 days' paid annual leave, and rest break entitlements.

Text 1: Unfair Dismissal — Rights, Process, and Remedies

The right not to be unfairly dismissed is one of the most fundamental protections in UK employment law, enshrined in Part X of the Employment Rights Act 1996. To bring an unfair dismissal claim, an employee must have been dismissed — either expressly, constructively, or by non-renewal of a fixed-term contract — and must have two years' continuous employment with the employer. The dismissal must then be assessed against two questions: was there a fair reason for dismissal, and did the employer act reasonably in treating that reason as sufficient to justify dismissal? The five potentially fair reasons are: capability (including health), conduct, redundancy, a statutory restriction preventing continued employment, and some other substantial reason (SOSR). Even where a fair reason exists, a dismissal may still be unfair if the employer failed to follow a fair procedure.

The procedural requirements for a fair dismissal are set out in the ACAS Code of Practice on Disciplinary and Grievance Procedures. For a conduct dismissal, a fair procedure typically requires: a thorough investigation of the alleged misconduct; a written notification to the employee setting out the allegations; a disciplinary hearing at which the employee has the right to be accompanied by a trade union representative or colleague; a decision communicated in writing; and the right of appeal. Failure to follow these steps does not automatically make a dismissal unfair — the employment tribunal has a wide discretion to consider whether the employer acted within the 'range of reasonable responses'. However, a failure to follow the ACAS Code may result in a tribunal uplifting any compensation award by up to 25%.

Where an unfair dismissal claim succeeds, the primary remedy is reinstatement (returning to the same job) or re-engagement (returning to a comparable job). In practice, compensation is by far the most common remedy. Compensation comprises a basic award (calculated by reference to age, length of service, and weekly pay, capped at a statutory maximum) and a compensatory award (designed to compensate for actual loss of earnings, capped at the lower of one year's gross pay or the statutory cap, currently £115,115). The total compensatory award may be reduced if the employee contributed to their own dismissal, or if the employer can show that a fair procedure would have made no difference to the outcome (the 'Polkey reduction').

Text 2: Discrimination in the Workplace — The Equality Act 2010

The Equality Act 2010 consolidated and extended the previous patchwork of UK discrimination legislation into a single, comprehensive statute. It protects employees, workers, and job applicants against discrimination in the workplace on the basis of nine protected characteristics: age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, and sexual orientation. The Act prohibits several forms of discriminatory conduct: direct discrimination (treating someone less favourably because of a protected characteristic); indirect discrimination (applying a provision, criterion, or practice that puts people with a protected characteristic at a particular disadvantage); harassment (unwanted conduct related to a protected characteristic that has the purpose or effect of violating dignity or creating a hostile environment); and victimisation (treating someone less favourably because they have made or supported a discrimination complaint).

The duty to make reasonable adjustments is one of the most operationally significant obligations under the Equality Act. It applies specifically to disability — the nine protected characteristics do not all attract this positive duty. Where a provision, criterion, or practice, or a physical feature of the employer's premises, puts a disabled person at a substantial disadvantage compared to a non-disabled person, the employer must take such steps as are reasonable to remove that disadvantage. What is 'reasonable' depends on factors including the effectiveness of the adjustment, its cost, the employer's resources, and the disruption it would cause. An employer who fails to make a reasonable adjustment that is identified as necessary is directly liable to the disabled employee — there is no defence of justification.

Liability for discrimination does not rest solely with the individual perpetrator. Under the doctrine of vicarious liability, employers are responsible for acts of discrimination and harassment committed by their employees in the course of employment — unless the employer can demonstrate that it took all reasonable steps to prevent the discriminatory act from occurring. This 'all reasonable steps' defence requires employers to have comprehensive equality and diversity policies, to provide regular training, and to enforce those policies consistently. In practice, demonstrating this defence is extremely difficult, which is why proactive equality training and monitoring are such important features of a sound HR compliance programme.

Grammar Point 1: Reported Speech in Employment Documents
Reporting Verbs in HR and Legal Contexts

Employment documents use specific reporting verbs that carry precise legal and evidential meaning. Choosing the right verb signals the speaker's status, the strength of the statement, and whether it is contested.

Employee / Claimant: stated, alleged, claimed, maintained, contended, asserted
Employer / Respondent: maintained, denied, submitted, argued, confirmed, acknowledged
Tribunal / Judge: found, held, determined, concluded, noted, considered
Witness: testified, stated, confirmed, denied, acknowledged, recalled
Tense Backshift in Reported Findings

When recording what was said in the past, tenses shift back. This is essential in HR investigation reports and tribunal judgments, which must accurately record historical statements without implying they are currently true.

Direct: 'I was not given a right of appeal.' → Reported: She stated that she had not been given a right of appeal.
Direct: 'The procedure was followed correctly.' → Reported: The respondent maintained that the procedure had been followed correctly.
Direct: 'I find the dismissal was unfair.' → Reported: The tribunal found that the dismissal was unfair.
Direct: 'We will conduct a full investigation.' → Reported: The employer confirmed that it would conduct a full investigation.
Reporting Denials and Admissions

Employment documents must record what was admitted and what was denied with precision. Incorrect reporting of a concession or denial can significantly affect the outcome of a claim or investigation.

'The respondent denied that any warning had been given prior to the disciplinary hearing.'
'The claimant acknowledged that she had received the written allegations three days before the hearing.'
'The employer admitted that no appeal procedure had been offered.'
'The witness confirmed that the manager had been present throughout the incident.'

Grammar Point 2: Formal Register in HR and Employment Documents
Opening and Closing a Disciplinary Letter

Disciplinary outcome letters follow a standard formal structure. The language must be precise, measured, and fair — it will be scrutinised by an employment tribunal if the matter is disputed.

Opening: 'We write to advise you of the outcome of the disciplinary hearing held on [date].'
Finding: 'Having carefully considered all the evidence presented, the panel has found the allegation [substantiated / not substantiated].'
Decision: 'In light of the above, it has been decided that [outcome — e.g., a final written warning / summary dismissal] is the appropriate sanction.'
Appeal: 'You have the right to appeal this decision to [person/panel] within [X] working days of receipt of this letter.'
Formal Verbs and Phrases in Employment Law

Employment documents use specific verbs and phrases that carry legal weight. Informal alternatives can undermine the document's authority or create ambiguity about what was decided.

'advise you of' (not 'tell you about') — informing of the outcome
'found / determined / concluded' (not 'decided' or 'thought') — recording a finding
'waive / relinquish' (not 'give up') — recording an agreement to abandon a claim
'in light of all the circumstances' (not 'because of everything') — justifying a decision
Qualifying and Hedging in Investigation Reports

HR investigation reports must record findings of fact without overstating certainty. Precise hedging signals what has been established and what remains unclear — critical where the matter may proceed to tribunal.

'On the balance of probability, it is considered that...' (civil standard — more likely than not)
'The weight of the evidence suggests that...' (conclusion based on evidence, not certainty)
'It has not been possible to determine conclusively whether...' (where evidence is inconclusive)
'The investigation found no corroborating evidence to support the allegation.'
📋
Grammar Point 3: Complex Sentences in Employment Law Documents
Expressing Conditions — When Obligations Arise

Employment law creates conditional obligations — duties that arise when specific circumstances occur. Conditional structures are essential in policies, tribunal submissions, and legal advice.

'If an employee raises a formal grievance, the employer must acknowledge it within five working days.'
'Where a disabled employee is placed at a substantial disadvantage, the duty to make reasonable adjustments arises.'
'Should the claimant fail to submit within the three-month limitation period, the claim will be out of time.'
'Provided that the employer follows a fair procedure, a dismissal for genuine redundancy will generally be fair.'
Expressing Concession — 'Although', 'Despite', 'Even Where'

Concession structures allow legal and HR writers to acknowledge a complicating factor while maintaining the primary argument. They are essential in disciplinary letters, tribunal decisions, and settlement negotiations.

'Although the employer carried out some investigation, this was insufficient given the seriousness of the allegations.'
'Despite the fact that a warning had been given, the dismissal was outside the range of reasonable responses.'
'Even where an employer has a genuine redundancy situation, the selection process must be fair.'
'The dismissal was upheld as fair notwithstanding the employer's failure to offer redeployment.'
Expressing Result and Consequence

Result clauses link a finding to its legal consequence. They are the backbone of tribunal judgments, investigation conclusions, and disciplinary outcome letters.

'The employer failed to follow the ACAS Code; as a result, the compensation award was increased by 20%.'
'The claimant contributed to her own dismissal by refusing to attend the investigation meeting — accordingly, the award has been reduced by 25%.'
'The tribunal found that the respondent had failed to carry out a reasonable investigation; consequently, the dismissal was held to be procedurally unfair.'
'The reasonable adjustment was not made; therefore, the employer is directly liable to the claimant.'

Introduction to Employment Law and Workplace Rights
Lecture · B1–B2 Level · approx. 93 sec
0:00

Welcome to Module Nine of Legal and Compliance English. In this module, we explore the language of employment law and workplace rights — one of the most practically important areas of law for anyone working in HR, compliance, management, or legal roles. Employment law governs the relationship between employers and employees from the moment a job offer is made to the moment employment ends — and beyond, in the case of post-termination obligations. It is also one of the most frequently litigated areas of law in England and Wales, with over 100,000 claims submitted to employment tribunals each year. Discrimination Claims: A Legal Briefing
Legal Briefing · B2 Level · approx. 107 sec
0:00

Discrimination claims are among the most complex and sensitive employment law matters that HR professionals and line managers will encounter. Understanding the legal framework — and the practical steps employers must take to protect themselves — is essential for anyone in a people management role. The Equality Act 2010 prohibits discrimination on the basis of nine protected characteristics. The most commonly litigated in employment tribunals are disability, sex, race, and age. Let us take disability discrimination as an example, as it is also the most operationally demanding for employers. Disability is defined broadly: it includes any physical or mental impairment that has a substantial and long-term adverse effect on the ability to carry out normal day-to-day activities. 'Long-term' means that the condition has lasted or is likely to last for at least twelve months, or is likely to recur. So mental health conditions, chronic pain, certain learning difficulties, and many other conditions can qualify — even if the individual does not regard themselves as disabled. The employer's obligations fall into two categories. First, the employer must not discriminate directly or indirectly against the disabled employee. Second — and this is the positive duty — the employer must make reasonable adjustments to remove or reduce any substantial disadvantage the employee faces because of their disability. What adjustments are reasonable depends on the circumstances: cost, effectiveness, the size of the organisation, and the resources available. Small employers may not be expected to make the same adjustments as large organisations. However, many reasonable adjustments cost little or nothing — flexible working, additional rest breaks, or adjusting targets, for example.

TUPE and Settlement Agreements: Protecting Employee Rights
Practical Briefing · B1–B2 Level · approx. 104 sec
0:00

Two areas of employment law that frequently arise in commercial transactions and HR practice are the Transfer of Undertakings (Protection of Employment) Regulations — known as TUPE — and settlement agreements. Let us take each in turn. TUPE applies when a business or part of a business is transferred from one employer to another — for example, when a company is acquired, when an outsourcing contract changes hands, or when a service is brought back in-house. When TUPE applies, the transferring employees' contracts of employment transfer automatically to the new employer on the same terms and conditions. The new employer cannot simply offer inferior terms. Employees who are dismissed in connection with the TUPE transfer — unless there is an economic, technical, or organisational reason entailing changes in the workforce — are automatically unfairly dismissed. Both the old and new employer have an obligation to inform and consult employee representatives about the TUPE transfer. Settlement agreements are a different but equally important tool in employment law. A settlement agreement is a legally binding contract in which an employee agrees to waive specified employment claims in exchange for a financial payment. For a settlement agreement to be legally valid, three things must happen: the agreement must be in writing; it must relate to specific complaints or proceedings; and the employee must have received independent legal advice from a qualified lawyer, trade union representative, or advice worker. The requirement for independent legal advice is non-negotiable — without it, the agreement is not binding and the employee retains their right to bring tribunal claims.

United Kingdom · Employment Law · 1999 · Unfair Dismissal Procedure
Case Study 1: Polkey v A E Dayton Services Ltd [1988] AC 344
The House of Lords decision that defined the relationship between procedural fairness and compensation in unfair dismissal

Background

Polkey v A E Dayton Services Ltd is one of the most important cases in UK employment law — and remains essential reading for HR professionals and employment lawyers. Mr Polkey was employed as a van driver by AE Dayton Services. The employer decided to make him redundant as part of a reorganisation of its van delivery service. Rather than following a proper redundancy procedure — which would have included consultation with Mr Polkey — the employer simply told him on arriving for work one day that he was redundant. No warning, no consultation, and no consideration of alternative employment. Mr Polkey brought an unfair dismissal claim. The employer argued that even if the procedure had been flawed, a proper procedure would inevitably have led to the same outcome — redundancy — and therefore the employee had suffered no real loss.

The Legal Issues

The House of Lords definitively rejected the approach that a procedural failure could be excused where the outcome would have been the same regardless. The House of Lords held that a tribunal must focus on whether the employer acted reasonably in the circumstances — including in deciding to dismiss without following a proper procedure. The fact that a fair procedure 'might have made no difference' was relevant to the assessment of compensation, not to the finding of fairness. However, the Lords simultaneously introduced what became known as the 'Polkey reduction': where a tribunal finds a dismissal was unfair because of a procedural failure, it may reduce the compensation award to reflect the chance that a fair procedure would have led to the same outcome. A 100% Polkey reduction — where the tribunal is satisfied that dismissal was inevitable — is possible but unusual.

Lessons for HR Practice

The Polkey case teaches HR practitioners two fundamental and interconnected lessons. First, an employer cannot excuse a procedural failure by arguing that the outcome would have been the same — procedural fairness is a free-standing obligation, not an optional step that can be skipped if the employer considers the outcome inevitable. Second, a procedural failure does not automatically mean full compensation — tribunals can and do reduce awards to reflect the probability that a proper procedure would have led to dismissal anyway. The practical lesson is straightforward: always follow a fair procedure. Doing so avoids both the unfairness finding and the uncertainty of a Polkey reduction. HR professionals should treat the procedural requirements as non-negotiable — not as bureaucratic box-ticking, but as the legal foundation on which the fairness of the dismissal rests.

United Kingdom · Discrimination Law · 2014 · Disability and Reasonable Adjustments
Case Study 2: Equality and Human Rights Commission v Cammell Laird — Disability Discrimination and the Duty to Adjust
A landmark case illustrating how the duty to make reasonable adjustments operates in practice, and the consequences of failing to meet it

Background

One of the most practically significant areas of employment law concerns the duty to make reasonable adjustments for disabled employees. While there is no single 'landmark' case that summarises the entire law, the principles have been developed through hundreds of employment tribunal and appellate decisions. A representative example involves the kind of fact pattern that arises regularly in practice: an employee develops a mental health condition — such as clinical depression or anxiety disorder — that amounts to a disability under the Equality Act 2010. Their employer, rather than exploring adjustments such as a phased return to work, flexible hours, or a temporary reduction in workload, instead pursues a capability procedure and ultimately dismisses the employee on grounds of ill-health absence. The employee brings a disability discrimination claim, alleging both direct discrimination and a failure to make reasonable adjustments.

The Legal Analysis

In cases of this type, employment tribunals consistently apply the following analytical framework. First, is the claimant disabled within the meaning of the Equality Act? A long-term mental health condition with a substantial adverse effect on day-to-day activities will typically satisfy the definition. Second, was the claimant placed at a substantial disadvantage by a provision, criterion, or practice — in this case, a requirement for full attendance and full-time working? Typically, yes. Third, did the employer know or could it reasonably have been expected to know about the disability? If the employer had been informed of the condition and its effects, knowledge will almost certainly be established. Fourth, were reasonable adjustments identified and offered? If the employer proceeded directly to a capability procedure without exploring adjustments such as a phased return, flexible working, or reduced hours — steps which might have enabled the employee to return to work — the tribunal is likely to find a failure to make reasonable adjustments. The absence of a justification defence makes this finding particularly damaging for employers.

Lessons for HR Practice

Cases of this type teach HR professionals several essential lessons about the duty to make reasonable adjustments. First, the duty is proactive — employers cannot wait for the employee to request a specific adjustment. Once the employer knows or should know of the disability, they must consider what adjustments might help. Second, occupational health advice is invaluable — and tribunals expect employers to obtain it. An employer who proceeds without occupational health advice will find it very difficult to demonstrate that they adequately explored their adjustment obligations. Third, the duty applies even when adjustments may not guarantee a successful return — the employer must try, document the attempts, and review them regularly. Fourth, the capability procedure should be a last resort, not a first response, where disability is a factor in an employee's absence or performance. Fifth, documentation is critical: a well-documented trail of reasonable adjustment discussions, occupational health referrals, and phased return plans provides the employer's best evidence that it met its legal obligations.
