📚 Key Legal Terms
🔐
Without Prejudice
A label placed on correspondence that cannot be used as evidence in court proceedings. It protects genuine attempts to settle a dispute and encourages open negotiation.
📬
Open Correspondence
Correspondence that can be disclosed and used as evidence in legal proceedings. Unlike 'without prejudice' letters, open letters form part of the formal legal record.
🛡️
Legal Professional Privilege
A fundamental right protecting confidential communications between a lawyer and client from disclosure. Divided into legal advice privilege and litigation privilege.
🚫
Cease and Desist
A formal demand requiring the recipient to stop a specified activity immediately. Often the first step before legal proceedings, used in intellectual property, defamation, and harassment cases.
⚖️
Letter Before Action
A formal letter sent before commencing legal proceedings, setting out the claim and giving the other party an opportunity to respond. Required by pre-action protocols in England and Wales.
📋
Pre-Action Protocol
A set of steps parties must follow before starting court proceedings. Designed to encourage settlement and the exchange of information before litigation begins.
📩
Demand Letter
A formal written request requiring the recipient to take a specific action — such as paying a debt or ceasing an activity — within a stated deadline.
📢
Formal Notice
A written communication that officially informs a party of a legal right, obligation, or intention. Giving formal notice is often a contractual or statutory requirement.
🗂️
Legal Hold
A directive requiring an organisation to preserve all documents and data relevant to anticipated or ongoing litigation. Failure to comply can result in sanctions for spoliation of evidence.
🔍
Subject Access Request
A request made under data protection law (e.g. GDPR) by an individual to access personal data held about them. Organisations must respond within one calendar month.
👤
Data Subject
An identified or identifiable living individual whose personal data is being processed. The data subject has specific rights under GDPR including access, rectification, and erasure.
🏢
Data Controller
An organisation or person that determines the purposes and means of processing personal data. The controller bears primary responsibility for GDPR compliance.
⚙️
Data Processor
An organisation or person that processes personal data on behalf of a controller. Must act only on the controller's documented instructions.
🔒
Injunctive Relief
A court order requiring a party to do something or stop doing something. Can be granted urgently (interim injunction) or after a full hearing (final injunction).
🤝
Undertaking
A solemn promise given in legal correspondence or to a court. Breach of an undertaking given to a court is contempt of court. Solicitors' undertakings are professionally binding.
✅
Acknowledgement
A formal written confirmation that correspondence or a document has been received. An acknowledgement does not imply agreement with the content.
📎
Enclosure
A document attached to a formal letter. Abbreviated as 'Enc.' at the foot of a letter. Always listed separately so the recipient can verify all materials have been received.
📝
Per Pro (pp)
Latin for 'on behalf of'. Used when signing a letter on behalf of another person or organisation, indicating the signatory has authority to act for the named party.
🔇
Without Admission
Language used to settle a claim or make a payment without conceding legal liability. Protects the paying party from the settlement being used as evidence of fault.
📣
Formal Complaint
A written communication that officially raises a grievance through an established procedure. Triggers a formal response obligation and creates a documented record.
⏰
Notice Period
The time a party must give before exercising a right — such as termination, rent review, or a legal claim. Missing a notice period can forfeit rights entirely.

Text 1: The Structure and Purpose of Legal Correspondence

Legal correspondence is one of the most consequential forms of professional writing. Unlike internal emails or informal business communications, formal legal letters create a documentary record that may be scrutinised by courts, regulators, and opposing parties. Every element of a legal letter — the heading, the date, the reference, the subject line, the body, the sign-off, and the enclosures — has a specific function. Getting these elements right is not merely a matter of professionalism; in some cases it determines whether a legal right can be enforced or a limitation period has been met. A letter before action, for example, must clearly state the basis of the claim, the remedy sought, and the deadline for response — failure to do so may prevent a party from recovering costs even if they win their case.

One of the most important distinctions in legal correspondence is between 'without prejudice' and 'open' communication. A letter marked 'without prejudice' is protected from disclosure in legal proceedings — it represents a genuine attempt to negotiate a settlement and cannot be produced in court to establish an admission of liability. Open correspondence, by contrast, forms part of the formal legal record and may be used in evidence. Lawyers use the without prejudice label carefully: a letter marked without prejudice that does not contain a genuine settlement offer may lose its protection, while an open letter that inadvertently concedes a point may seriously damage a client's position.

Register and precision are equally critical in legal correspondence. Legal letters must use formal, precise language that leaves no room for ambiguity. Hedging expressions such as 'without admission of liability', 'our client reserves all rights', and 'this letter is written without prejudice to our client's legal position' perform specific legal functions — they protect the writer from unintended concessions while keeping options open. Passive constructions, formal vocabulary, and careful qualification of every statement are hallmarks of professional legal writing. Junior professionals entering legal or compliance roles frequently underestimate the consequences of informal language or imprecise drafting in formal correspondence — consequences that can range from embarrassment to significant legal and financial liability.

Text 2: Data Subject Access Requests and Regulatory Correspondence

Under the General Data Protection Regulation, individuals have the right to access personal data held about them by submitting a subject access request (SAR). When an organisation receives a SAR, it has a legal obligation to respond within one calendar month. The response must include a copy of the personal data being processed, the purposes of processing, the categories of data concerned, and information about any third parties to whom the data has been disclosed. Where the request is complex or numerous, the organisation may extend the response period by a further two months, provided it notifies the data subject of the extension and the reasons within the first month.

Responding to a SAR requires careful review of what data is in scope. Not all data held about an individual must be disclosed — certain exemptions apply. Data relating to legal professional privilege, ongoing criminal investigations, or information about third parties whose privacy interests outweigh those of the requester may be withheld. When withholding data, organisations must clearly explain in their response which exemptions apply and why. Failing to respond correctly — whether by missing the deadline, disclosing too little, or disclosing too much — can result in a complaint to the Information Commissioner's Office and potential enforcement action.

Beyond SARs, organisations frequently receive correspondence from regulators — the Information Commissioner's Office, the Financial Conduct Authority, the Care Quality Commission, and others. Regulatory correspondence demands an even higher standard of care. Every word of a response to a regulatory investigation letter may be scrutinised, and any inconsistency with other documentation can have serious consequences. Best practice is to involve legal counsel early, ensure factual accuracy, and adopt a tone that is cooperative and professional without making admissions or concessions that could prejudice the organisation's position. Regulatory bodies expect prompt, complete, and professionally presented responses.

Grammar Point 1: Formal Register in Legal Letters
Verbs and Phrases that Signal Formality

Legal correspondence uses specific formal verbs and phrases that have no equivalent in everyday writing. These are not interchangeable with informal alternatives — they carry precise legal meaning and signal professional authority.

'We refer to...' (not 'We are writing about...')
'We advise you that...' (not 'We are telling you...')
'Please treat this letter as formal notice...' (not 'This letter is to tell you...')
'Our client hereby reserves all rights.' (not 'Our client is keeping their options open.')
Impersonal Constructions

Formal legal letters avoid first-person direct language where possible. Impersonal constructions create distance, objectivity, and authority — essential qualities in legal writing.

'It is noted that...' (not 'We noticed that...')
'It is alleged that...' (not 'We think that...')
'It is required that...' (not 'We need you to...')
'It would appear that...' (not 'It seems like...')
Signposting and Structural Language

Legal letters use structured signposting language to guide the reader through complex information. This makes the letter easier to navigate and ensures no key point is missed.

'We refer to the above matter and write to advise as follows.'
'In the circumstances, our client instructs us to...'
'Should you fail to comply with the above, our client reserves the right to...'
'We trust that this letter finds you well and await your response by return.'
📋
Grammar Point 2: Hedging and Qualification in Legal Correspondence
'Without Prejudice' — The Most Important Heading

Marking a letter 'without prejudice' is a legal mechanism, not just a phrase. It signals that the letter represents a genuine attempt to settle a dispute and cannot be used as evidence. It should only be used when there is an existing dispute and a genuine settlement offer is being made.

'WITHOUT PREJUDICE' (as a heading, in capitals, before the salutation)
'This letter is written without prejudice to our client's legal position.'
'Nothing in this letter shall constitute an admission of liability.'
'Our client makes this offer without prejudice to all rights and remedies.'
Reserving Rights and Making Qualifications

Phrases that 'reserve rights' ensure that making an offer, payment, or concession cannot later be used to argue that the writer has waived a legal right. These are essential tools in any legal correspondence involving a live dispute.

'Our client reserves all rights in respect of this matter.'
'This response is provided without prejudice to our client's right to adduce further evidence.'
'Nothing herein shall be construed as a waiver of any right or remedy.'
'Our client's position in relation to liability remains as previously stated.'
Cautious Language — Allegation and Appearance

Legal writers avoid stating disputed facts as certainties. Using 'alleged', 'it appears', 'it is understood', and 'on the information currently available' signals that the writer is not conceding the accuracy of the other party's version of events.

'It is alleged that your client failed to perform the agreement.'
'On the information currently available to us, it appears that...'
'It is understood — without admission — that a payment was received on 14 March.'
'Our client does not accept the characterisation of events as set out in your letter.'
📋
Grammar Point 3: Passive Voice and Formal Sentence Structure
Why Passive Voice Dominates Legal Letters

Passive voice removes the agent from the sentence, making statements more objective and impersonal. In legal correspondence, this avoids unnecessary accusations, deflects blame, and maintains a formal, authoritative tone.

'The payment was received on 14 March.' (passive — no named agent)
'The contract was breached on or around 1 April.' (passive — avoids direct accusation)
'Legal proceedings will be commenced without further notice.' (passive — authoritative)
'It has been brought to our client's attention that...' (passive — impersonal)
Formal Sentence Openings

The opening sentence of each paragraph in a legal letter should signal its purpose clearly. Formal openings create structure, aid comprehension, and ensure the letter reads as authoritative professional communication.

'We refer to the above matter...' (reference paragraph)
'We are instructed by our client to write to you in the following terms...' (instruction paragraph)
'In the circumstances, our client instructs us to make the following demand...' (demand paragraph)
'Should you fail to comply with the above, our client will have no alternative but to...' (consequence paragraph)
Closing Formulas and Sign-off

Legal letters close with standard formulas that serve specific functions. The choice of closing formula depends on the nature of the letter and the relationship between the parties.

'We await your response by [date].' (demand / deadline)
'We trust this sets out our client's position clearly.' (position statement)
'We remain available to discuss this matter should you wish to do so.' (open to dialogue)
'Yours faithfully,' (when the recipient is not known by name) vs. 'Yours sincerely,' (when named)

Without Prejudice Correspondence: Rules and Risks
Legal Briefing · B2 Level · approx. 105 sec
0:00

The without prejudice rule is one of the most important — and most frequently misunderstood — concepts in legal correspondence. When a letter is genuinely marked without prejudice, it cannot be produced in court as evidence to establish an admission of liability. This protection encourages parties to negotiate openly and make settlement offers without fear that those offers will be used against them if settlement fails. However, the without prejudice label is not a magic formula. To attract the protection, three conditions must generally be met. First, there must be an existing dispute between the parties. Second, the communication must be a genuine attempt to settle that dispute. Third, the parties must intend the communication to be protected. A letter that simply describes facts, without containing any settlement offer or negotiating position, will not attract without prejudice protection — even if the label is applied. Similarly, without prejudice protection can be waived if both parties agree to rely on the correspondence, or if it is needed to establish that a binding settlement was in fact reached. Solicitors must use the label carefully — and must also be vigilant when reading correspondence from the other side to ensure they understand whether they are reading a protected communication or an open one.

Writing a Letter Before Action
Practical Workshop · B1–B2 Level · approx. 100 sec
0:00

A letter before action is a formal letter sent before commencing legal proceedings. It is often required by pre-action protocols — rules set by the courts that both parties must follow before a case can proceed. In England and Wales, the pre-action protocols require claimants to send a letter before action setting out the nature of the claim, the amount sought, the relevant documents relied on, and the time allowed for a response — typically 14 days for simple debt claims and longer for more complex matters. The purpose of the letter before action is not merely procedural. It gives the other party a genuine opportunity to resolve the dispute without litigation — which benefits both parties by avoiding the cost, time, and uncertainty of court proceedings. A well-drafted letter before action should be professional and measured in tone, even if the underlying dispute is contentious. It should set out the facts clearly and without unnecessary detail, identify the specific legal basis of the claim, state precisely what is required — for example, payment of a specific sum by a specific date — and make clear the consequences of non-compliance. Courts take compliance with pre-action protocols seriously. A party that ignores a properly drafted letter before action and proceeds directly to defending litigation may face adverse cost consequences even if they ultimately win.

Responding to Regulatory Correspondence
Compliance Briefing · B2 Level · approx. 110 sec
0:00

Receiving correspondence from a regulator is one of the most significant events in a compliance professional's career. Whether it is an information request from the Financial Conduct Authority, an investigation notice from the Information Commissioner's Office, or a formal notice from the Care Quality Commission, regulatory correspondence demands an immediate, careful, and coordinated response. The first step is not to reply — it is to read the letter in full, identify precisely what has been requested, and establish the deadline. Regulatory deadlines are not the same as commercial deadlines. Missing a regulatory deadline is a serious matter that can be treated as non-cooperation and used to justify more intrusive regulatory action. The second step is to involve legal counsel immediately. Even if the request appears straightforward, a regulator's correspondence should never be handled without legal advice. The response will be scrutinised — and any inconsistency with other documents or previous statements could be damaging. The third step is to gather the relevant facts and documents. Do not send anything to the regulator until legal counsel has reviewed it. The fourth step is to draft the response — cooperatively and professionally, without making admissions or conceding points that have not yet been established. The tone should be helpful and transparent, while the content should be legally precise. Regulators appreciate prompt, complete, and professionally presented responses — and they notice when they do not receive them.

Case Study 1: Framlington Group Ltd v Barnetson [2007] — Without Prejudice Privilege in Practice
A Court of Appeal case clarifying when the without prejudice rule applies and when it can be overridden

Background

In Framlington Group Ltd v Barnetson, the Court of Appeal was asked to consider whether correspondence between a senior executive (Barnetson) and his employer (Framlington) was protected by without prejudice privilege. The correspondence arose during a dispute about the terms of the executive's departure from the company. Both parties had exchanged letters marked 'without prejudice' as they attempted to negotiate a settlement of various employment-related claims. When the dispute eventually proceeded to litigation, Framlington sought to rely on certain communications as evidence, arguing that they fell outside the scope of without prejudice protection because they were, in substance, factual statements rather than settlement offers.

The Legal Issues

The Court of Appeal confirmed the fundamental principle: for correspondence to attract without prejudice protection, there must be a genuine dispute and the communication must be a genuine attempt to settle that dispute. The court rejected Framlington's argument, holding that the correspondence was clearly part of a negotiating process aimed at settling the dispute — even if individual letters did not contain specific monetary offers. The court also addressed the issue of whether without prejudice protection could be maintained selectively — that is, whether one party could choose to disclose some without prejudice correspondence while keeping other parts protected. The answer was no: the protection must be applied to the entire negotiation, not cherry-picked.

Lessons for Legal Correspondence Practice

Framlington v Barnetson offers several important lessons for professionals drafting and handling legal correspondence. First, the without prejudice label should only be applied when there is a genuine dispute and a genuine attempt at settlement — applying it routinely to all correspondence in a dispute does not guarantee protection. Second, once without prejudice protection attaches to a series of negotiations, it applies to the whole sequence — neither party can selectively rely on parts of the without prejudice correspondence while keeping others protected. Third, professionals reviewing correspondence marked without prejudice from the other side should read it carefully: if there is any question about whether the label has been properly applied, legal advice should be sought before proceeding.

United Kingdom · Data Protection · 2022 · Subject Access Request
Case Study 2: Data Rights Enforcement — SAR Failures and ICO Action
How organisations' failures to respond correctly to subject access requests led to enforcement action by the Information Commissioner's Office

Background

Since the GDPR came into force in May 2018, the Information Commissioner's Office has consistently identified subject access request (SAR) handling as one of the most common sources of complaints from members of the public. The ICO's annual data shows that SAR failures — including failure to respond within the one-month deadline, failure to provide a complete response, and failure to identify and disclose all relevant data — account for a significant proportion of all data protection complaints. In response, the ICO has taken enforcement action against a range of organisations, from NHS trusts to financial services firms, for systemic failures in SAR handling. In several cases, enforcement notices were issued requiring organisations to implement remedial measures within specified timeframes.

The Legal Issues

The legal obligations on data controllers in respect of SARs are set out clearly in Articles 15 and 12 of the GDPR. Article 15 gives data subjects the right to access their personal data and requires controllers to provide a copy of the data, explain the purposes of processing, and identify any third-party recipients. Article 12 sets out the procedural requirements: responses must be provided within one calendar month of receipt, in a concise, transparent, and intelligible format, free of charge. Controllers may extend the deadline by up to two further months for complex or numerous requests — but must notify the data subject within the first month. Common failures include: responding after the deadline, providing only partial data, failing to identify relevant data in legacy systems, and failing to redact third-party data appropriately.

Lessons for Compliance Professionals

The ICO's enforcement record on SARs provides clear practical guidance for compliance professionals. First, organisations must have a clear, documented SAR process with designated responsible staff and a timetable from receipt to response. Second, the scope of a SAR is broad — it covers all personal data held in any format, including emails, handwritten notes, CCTV footage, and HR records. Third, exemptions must be applied carefully: withholding data without a clear legal basis is as serious an error as over-disclosing. Fourth, every SAR response should be reviewed by a legally qualified person before it is sent. Fifth, organisations should proactively map their data so they can locate all relevant information quickly — a task that becomes significantly harder if data has been stored unsystematically across multiple systems.
