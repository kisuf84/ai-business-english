📚 Key Legal Terms
🌐
Incoterms
International Commercial Terms — a set of standardised trade terms published by the International Chamber of Commerce (ICC) that define the responsibilities of buyers and sellers in international transactions, including who bears the risk of loss, who arranges and pays for transport, and who handles customs clearance.
🚢
FOB (Free on Board)
An Incoterm requiring the seller to deliver the goods on board the vessel nominated by the buyer at the named port of shipment. Risk passes from seller to buyer when the goods are on board. The buyer is responsible for freight and insurance costs from that point.
🏦
CIF (Cost, Insurance, and Freight)
An Incoterm under which the seller pays for transport and insurance to the named port of destination. However, risk passes to the buyer once the goods are on board the vessel — meaning the buyer bears the risk of loss during transit even though the seller has arranged the insurance.
💳
Letter of Credit
A payment mechanism issued by a bank on behalf of a buyer, guaranteeing payment to the seller upon presentation of specified documents (such as a bill of lading and commercial invoice) that confirm the goods have been shipped as agreed. Reduces payment risk in international trade.
📜
Bill of Lading
A document issued by a carrier acknowledging receipt of goods for shipment. It serves three functions: evidence of the contract of carriage, a receipt for goods, and a document of title — meaning the holder is entitled to claim the goods on arrival.
💰
Customs Duty
A tax imposed by a government on goods imported (or sometimes exported) across a national border. Calculated as a percentage of the value of the goods (ad valorem) or as a fixed amount per unit. A key tool of trade policy and a significant cost in international supply chains.
📊
Tariff
A schedule of customs duties levied on imported goods, or the specific rate of duty applied to a class of goods. Tariffs protect domestic industries, raise government revenue, and are the primary subject of international trade negotiations.
🤝
Trade Agreement
A legally binding agreement between two or more countries to reduce or eliminate barriers to trade — such as tariffs, quotas, and regulatory restrictions. May be bilateral (between two countries), regional (e.g. ASEAN), or multilateral (e.g. WTO agreements).
🏛️
WTO
The World Trade Organization — the international body that regulates global trade. Its core principles include non-discrimination (most favoured nation and national treatment), transparency, and predictability. Members commit to binding trade rules and can bring disputes through the WTO dispute settlement mechanism.
🚫
Sanction
A restrictive measure imposed by one country or international body against another country, entity, or individual to influence behaviour. May prohibit trade, freeze assets, or restrict financial transactions. Companies must conduct sanctions screening to avoid prohibited dealings.
🔒
Export Control
National laws and regulations that restrict the export of specified goods, technology, and software — particularly items with dual use (civilian and military applications). Regulated in the UK under the Export Control Order 2008 and administered by the Export Control Joint Unit.
⚖️
Jurisdiction
The legal authority of a court or tribunal to hear and determine a case. In international contracts, jurisdiction clauses specify which country's courts shall have authority to resolve disputes. Distinct from governing law, which specifies which country's law applies.
📋
Governing Law
The clause in an international contract specifying which country's legal system will be used to interpret and enforce the agreement. A critical choice — different legal systems have different rules on contract formation, breach, and remedies.
🔏
International Arbitration
A private, binding dispute resolution mechanism in which parties submit their dispute to one or more independent arbitrators rather than national courts. Awards are enforceable in over 170 countries under the New York Convention 1958. The preferred mechanism for resolving high-value international commercial disputes.
🌊
Force Majeure
A contractual clause excusing a party from performance of their obligations when an extraordinary event beyond their control — such as a natural disaster, war, or pandemic — prevents or delays performance. The clause must be specifically defined — it does not arise automatically under English law.
🌍
Choice of Law
The contractual or judicial selection of the legal system to govern a dispute or agreement. In international contracts, parties expressly choose their preferred governing law. Where no choice is made, courts apply conflict of laws rules to determine the applicable law.
📃
Treaty
A formal, legally binding agreement between sovereign states under international law. May be bilateral (between two states) or multilateral (between many). Treaties are binding on signatory states and may be directly applicable in domestic law depending on the constitutional system.
👑
Sovereign Immunity
The principle that a sovereign state cannot be sued in the courts of another state without its consent. In commercial contexts, most states have waived immunity for commercial activities under legislation such as the UK State Immunity Act 1978 and the US Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act.
🏷️
Most Favoured Nation
A WTO principle (and treaty clause) requiring that any trade advantage granted by one country to another must be extended to all WTO members. Ensures non-discrimination in tariffs and trade conditions between trading partners.
🏭
Rules of Origin
The criteria used to determine the national origin of a product for customs and trade purposes. Critical for determining whether goods qualify for preferential tariff treatment under a trade agreement, and for applying anti-dumping measures to imports.
⚓
Anti-Dumping
A trade remedy allowing countries to impose additional duties on imported goods sold at less than their fair market value (dumped) in the importing country. Designed to protect domestic industries from unfair foreign competition. Governed by the WTO Anti-Dumping Agreement.

Text 1: Incoterms and the International Sale of Goods

International trade involves a complex web of logistics, risk, and financial obligation between buyers and sellers separated by borders, currencies, and legal systems. The most important tool for managing these complexities in the sale of goods is Incoterms — International Commercial Terms published by the International Chamber of Commerce. The current version, Incoterms 2020, provides eleven standardised trade terms, each of which precisely defines the point at which risk passes from seller to buyer, which party is responsible for arranging and paying for transport and insurance, and which party handles export and import customs clearance. By incorporating a specific Incoterm into their contract, the parties avoid potentially catastrophic misunderstandings about who bears the risk if goods are lost or damaged in transit.

The two most widely used Incoterms in maritime trade are FOB (Free on Board) and CIF (Cost, Insurance and Freight). Under FOB, the seller's responsibility ends when the goods are placed on board the vessel at the agreed port of shipment — from that point, the buyer bears all risk and is responsible for arranging freight and insurance. Under CIF, the seller goes further: they arrange and pay for freight and insurance to the named port of destination. However — and this is a critical distinction — risk still passes to the buyer at the point of shipment, not at the destination. This means that under CIF, the buyer bears the risk of loss during the voyage even though the seller has arranged the insurance. In the event of a loss, the buyer must claim against the insurer, not the seller.

Letters of credit are the most common payment mechanism in international trade for reducing counterparty payment risk. A letter of credit is issued by the buyer's bank, committing to pay the seller a specified sum upon presentation of documents that comply strictly with the letter of credit's terms. The documents typically required include a commercial invoice, a bill of lading (or other transport document), a packing list, and an insurance certificate. The critical feature of a letter of credit is that it is a bank's obligation — independent of the underlying sale contract. If the seller presents conforming documents, the bank must pay regardless of any dispute between buyer and seller. This makes a confirmed, irrevocable letter of credit one of the most secure forms of payment available in international trade.

Text 2: Sanctions, Export Controls, and International Trade Compliance

For any business operating internationally, trade compliance is no longer a peripheral concern — it is a core legal obligation. Two overlapping frameworks impose the most significant restrictions on cross-border trade: economic sanctions and export controls. Economic sanctions are restrictive measures imposed by states or international organisations to achieve foreign policy or national security objectives. They may prohibit trade with designated countries (comprehensive sanctions, such as those against North Korea and Iran), restrict dealings with listed individuals and entities (targeted sanctions), or prohibit specific categories of transaction — such as the provision of financial services or the export of certain goods. The UK's sanctions regime is administered by the Office of Financial Sanctions Implementation (OFSI) and the Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office. Since leaving the European Union, the UK has implemented its own autonomous sanctions framework under the Sanctions and Anti-Money Laundering Act 2018.

Export controls are a distinct but complementary framework. They restrict the export of goods, software, and technology that could have civilian and military applications — so-called 'dual-use' items. These may include certain chemicals, electronics, cryptographic software, and aerospace components. In the UK, export controls are governed by the Export Control Order 2008 and administered by the Export Control Joint Unit (ECJU). A business wishing to export a controlled item must apply for an export licence — and failure to do so is a criminal offence. The consequences of an export control violation can be severe: unlimited fines, imprisonment, and reputational damage that can destroy business relationships built over decades.

For compliance professionals, the practical challenge is integrating sanctions and export control checks into the day-to-day operations of the business. This requires robust customer due diligence — screening every new customer, supplier, and transaction counterparty against up-to-date sanctions lists. It requires product classification — ensuring that every product and technology exported is correctly classified against the relevant control lists. And it requires ongoing monitoring — because sanctions designations can be added overnight, and a counterparty that was not designated yesterday may be on the list today. Enforcement agencies in the United States (OFAC), the United Kingdom (OFSI), and the European Union have all significantly increased their enforcement activities in recent years, and cross-border prosecutions have demonstrated that compliance failures in one jurisdiction can trigger liability in others.

Grammar Point 1: Complex Sentences in International Trade Documents
Contrast and Concession — 'Although', 'Even If', 'Notwithstanding'

International trade contracts frequently contain provisions that acknowledge a competing factor — such as the CIF risk rule — while maintaining a primary obligation. Concession and contrast clauses are essential for expressing these nuances precisely.

'Although the seller has arranged insurance under CIF, risk passes to the buyer at the point of shipment.'
'The buyer must pay the contract price even if the goods are damaged after risk has passed to them.'
'Notwithstanding the force majeure event, the buyer's obligation to pay for goods already delivered remains.'
'The arbitration clause shall survive termination of the agreement, even though all other obligations have ceased.'
Conditions — 'Where', 'Provided That', 'Unless'

Trade documents use conditional clauses to define precisely when obligations arise, when exceptions apply, and when rights can be exercised. 'Where' introduces a general condition; 'provided that' introduces a positive condition; 'unless' introduces a negative one.

'Where the goods are subject to export controls, an export licence must be obtained before shipment.'
'The letter of credit shall be payable, provided that all required documents are presented within the validity period.'
'Anti-dumping duties shall not apply unless it is established that the goods are sold at below normal value.'
'The sanctions screening must be completed unless the counterparty has been previously approved in writing.'
Purpose — 'In Order To', 'So As To', 'With a View To'

Purpose clauses explain why a procedural step or contractual obligation is required. They are common in compliance procedures, trade documentation requirements, and payment mechanisms.

'A full set of documents must be presented in order to trigger the letter of credit payment obligation.'
'The seller must obtain an export licence so as to ensure compliance with the Export Control Order 2008.'
'Sanctions screening is conducted with a view to identifying any restricted parties before the transaction proceeds.'
'Rules of origin documentation must be maintained in order to claim preferential tariff treatment under the agreement.'
📋
Grammar Point 2: Precision Noun Phrases in Trade Contracts
Incoterm References — How to Use Them Correctly

Incoterms must be referenced precisely in contracts. The correct format includes the three-letter abbreviation, the named place, and the edition year. Imprecise references create ambiguity about which version applies.

Correct: 'FOB Port of Shanghai, Incoterms 2020' (three elements: term, place, edition)
Correct: 'CIF Port of Felixstowe, Incoterms 2020'
Incorrect: 'FOB' alone (no named place — creates ambiguity)
Incorrect: 'CIF as per ICC rules' (no edition year — earlier, different versions may apply)
Document References in Letters of Credit

Letters of credit operate on the strict compliance principle — documents must match exactly. Precise noun phrases for document types are essential to avoid discrepancies that can block payment.

'A full set of clean on-board bills of lading marked freight prepaid'
'A signed commercial invoice in triplicate describing the goods as per the contract'
'A certificate of origin issued by the Chamber of Commerce of the country of manufacture'
'An insurance policy or certificate covering all risks for 110% of the CIF value of the goods'
Nominalisation in Trade Compliance Documents

Trade compliance documents — sanctions assessments, export licence applications, and legal opinions — use nominalisations to create formal, objective language that carries authority.

classify → classification ('the classification of goods under the export control schedule')
screen → screening ('the screening of counterparties against applicable sanctions lists')
comply → compliance ('compliance with the applicable export control regulations')
assess → assessment ('a comprehensive risk assessment of the transaction counterparty')
📋
Grammar Point 3: Passive Voice and Impersonal Structures in Trade Compliance
Passive Voice for Procedural Authority

Trade compliance procedures, export licence applications, and sanctions assessments use passive voice to express mandatory steps impersonally and authoritatively. The passive removes the named actor, focusing on the requirement rather than who must fulfil it.

'An export licence must be obtained before the goods are shipped.' (mandatory — passive)
'The sanctions screening must be repeated each time the designated lists are updated.'
'All documents must be presented to the nominated bank within the validity period of the letter of credit.'
'The goods have been classified as dual-use items under the Export Control Order 2008.'
'It Has Been Determined / Established / Noted That...'

Impersonal 'it' constructions lend authority to compliance findings and assessment conclusions. They are the standard register for reporting the results of sanctions screening, export control classification reviews, and trade due diligence.

'It has been determined that the counterparty does not appear on any applicable sanctions designation list.'
'It has been established that the goods meet the rules of origin criteria for preferential tariff treatment.'
'It is noted that the export of this technology requires an export licence under the Export Control Order.'
'It has been concluded that the transaction falls outside the scope of the applicable sanctions regime.'
Obligation and Requirement — Passive Modals

Trade documents use passive modal constructions to express binding obligations without naming the responsible party — particularly important in standard form contracts and regulatory guidance.

'Sanctions screening must be conducted before each payment is processed.'
'The letter of credit must be opened in favour of the seller no later than 30 days before the shipping date.'
'All goods subject to export controls shall be identified and classified before any export licence application is made.'
'The arbitral award shall be recognised and enforced in accordance with the New York Convention 1958.'

Introduction to International Law and Trade
Lecture · B1–B2 Level · approx. 95 sec
0:00

Welcome to Module Ten of Legal and Compliance English. In this module, we explore the language of international law and international trade — the legal frameworks that govern how businesses operate across borders, how goods move between countries, and how disputes are resolved when parties from different legal systems disagree. International trade is the engine of the global economy, and the legal infrastructure that supports it — trade agreements, Incoterms, letters of credit, customs duties, and dispute resolution mechanisms — is vast and technically demanding. At the same time, the regulatory environment governing international trade has become more complex than at any point in recent history: economic sanctions imposed in response to geopolitical events, export controls protecting sensitive technologies, and anti-dumping measures protecting domestic industries all create significant compliance obligations for businesses operating internationally. Separately, criminal liability can arise — with sentences of up to seven years' imprisonment. And the reputational damage of being publicly named as a sanctions violator can be catastrophic. So what do you need to do? First, know your customer. Before entering into any transaction with a new counterparty, screen them against the relevant designation lists. Second, know your goods. Some goods are subject to trade sanctions independently of whether the counterparty is designated. Third, review regularly. Sanctions lists change — sometimes overnight. A counterparty that was clean yesterday may be designated today. Fourth, when in doubt, stop and escalate. Do not proceed with a transaction that raises sanctions concerns without legal advice. The business cost of declining a transaction is manageable. The cost of a sanctions violation is not.

International Arbitration: Resolving Cross-Border Disputes
Legal Briefing · B2 Level · approx. 106 sec
0:00

When a dispute arises between parties from different countries, the question of how to resolve it is one of the most practically significant decisions in any international commercial relationship. National courts are the default option — but they come with significant disadvantages in international disputes. A party who wins a judgment in an English court may find it extremely difficult to enforce that judgment in another country, particularly in jurisdictions that are not party to enforcement treaties. The courts of the losing party's home country may refuse to recognise the foreign judgment, leaving the winning party with a piece of paper that cannot be used to recover assets. International arbitration offers a fundamentally different — and in most international contracts, superior — solution. Under the New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards 1958, arbitral awards are enforceable in over 170 countries. This gives international arbitration a reach and enforceability that no national court can match. The major international arbitration institutions include the International Chamber of Commerce in Paris, the London Court of International Arbitration, the Singapore International Arbitration Centre, and the Hong Kong International Arbitration Centre. Each has its own rules governing the conduct of proceedings — the appointment of arbitrators, the submission of evidence, the hearing, and the issuance of the award. Arbitration also offers confidentiality — a significant advantage over court proceedings, which are generally public. It allows the parties to choose arbitrators with relevant technical expertise — a key advantage in disputes involving complex technical or financial issues. And it gives the parties control over the process in a way that litigation does not. The primary disadvantage of arbitration is cost — major international arbitrations can be extremely expensive. But for high-value cross-border disputes, the enforceability advantage typically outweighs the cost.

Case Study 1: The Doctrine of Strict Compliance in Letters of Credit — Equitable Trust Co of New York v Dawson Partners Ltd [1927]
The foundational principle that banks must pay under letters of credit only when documents strictly comply — and the commercial consequences of documentary discrepancies

Background

The doctrine of strict compliance is the cornerstone of the documentary credit system and one of the most commercially important principles in international trade law. Its origins lie in early English cases, including Equitable Trust Co of New York v Dawson Partners Ltd [1927], in which the House of Lords confirmed that a bank acting under a letter of credit must pay only when the presented documents strictly comply with the credit's terms. In that case, the credit called for a certificate signed by 'one expert'. The presented certificate was signed by 'experts' — plural. The House of Lords held that the bank was not obliged to pay because the document did not strictly comply with the terms of the credit. Lord Sumner stated in words that have defined the law ever since: 'There is no room for documents which are almost the same, or which will do just as well.'

The Legal Principles

The strict compliance principle reflects the commercial logic of the letter of credit system. A letter of credit is a bank's independent commitment to pay against documents — it is not a guarantee of the underlying goods. If the bank were permitted to exercise judgment about whether a discrepancy was material, the certainty that makes letters of credit valuable would be destroyed. Sellers would never know with confidence whether their documents would be accepted. This certainty has a price: even minor documentary discrepancies — a misspelled address, a wrong date, a missing endorsement — can give the bank grounds to refuse payment. The International Chamber of Commerce's Uniform Customs and Practice for Documentary Credits (UCP 600), now the universally adopted standard for letters of credit, provides detailed rules for document examination and specifies that banks have five banking days to examine documents and raise discrepancies.

Lessons for International Trade Practice

The strict compliance doctrine carries critical lessons for every participant in international trade. For exporters, the lesson is: prepare your documents with extreme care. A single discrepancy — however minor — can give the issuing bank grounds to refuse payment and throw a complex supply chain into chaos. The most common discrepancies in practice include: description of goods that does not exactly match the credit; late presentation of documents after the expiry date; and bills of lading that do not comply with the terms specified. For buyers, the lesson is: draft the letter of credit with equal care. A credit that is too prescriptive or contains impossible requirements will generate discrepancies that delay payment. For both parties, the UCP 600 is the essential reference — and any international trade lawyer or trade finance professional must be deeply familiar with its provisions.

Case Study 2: The Impact of Russia Sanctions on International Trade Compliance
How the most sweeping sanctions regime in recent history transformed trade compliance obligations for businesses worldwide

Background

Following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, the United Kingdom, European Union, United States, and other allied states imposed a sweeping package of economic sanctions on Russia — widely described as the most comprehensive sanctions regime ever imposed on a major economy. The UK measures, implemented under the Sanctions and Anti-Money Laundering Act 2018, included asset freezes on designated Russian individuals and entities; prohibitions on trade in goods including luxury items, advanced technology, and aviation components; restrictions on financial services including banking, insurance, and capital markets; and a ban on providing a wide range of professional services including legal advice, accountancy, and management consultancy to Russian entities. For UK businesses with any Russia-related supply chains, transactions, or professional relationships, the sanctions created immediate and urgent compliance obligations.

The Compliance Challenges

The Russia sanctions created compliance challenges of a scale and complexity that few UK businesses had previously encountered. First, the sanctions were introduced incrementally — new designations and new prohibitions were added on a rolling basis, requiring businesses to update their compliance systems repeatedly. Second, the scope of the prohibited services — including legal, accountancy, and consultancy services — extended the sanctions far beyond the traditional financial services sector and caught professional services firms that had little previous experience of trade sanctions compliance. Third, the concept of 'ownership and control' — by which a Russian-owned entity may be a designated person even if not itself named on a list — required businesses to look beyond the immediate counterparty to the ultimate beneficial owner of the entities they dealt with. Fourth, the potential for inadvertent breaches was significant: supply chains that had operated for years suddenly needed to be screened and restructured.

Lessons for Trade Compliance Practice

The Russia sanctions crisis is the defining trade compliance event of the 2020s and will shape how businesses manage sanctions risk for years to come. First, sanctions compliance is not a one-time exercise — it requires continuous monitoring and rapid adaptation as designations and prohibitions change. Second, sanctions compliance is not just a financial services issue — every business with cross-border activities needs a proportionate sanctions compliance programme. Third, the 'ownership and control' concept means that screening the immediate counterparty is not sufficient — businesses must conduct beneficial ownership due diligence. Fourth, the professional services prohibitions demonstrated that sanctions can extend into entirely unexpected areas of business activity. Fifth, companies that had invested in robust trade compliance infrastructure — sanctions screening systems, specialist compliance staff, and clear escalation procedures — were able to adapt far more quickly and with far less legal and reputational risk than those that had not.
