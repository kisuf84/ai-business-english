📚 Key Legal Terms
👤
Claimant
The party who brings a civil claim in court — formerly called the 'plaintiff'. The claimant initiates proceedings by filing particulars of claim setting out the basis of their case.
🎯
Defendant
The party against whom a civil claim or criminal prosecution is brought. The defendant must file a defence within the required timeframe or risk a default judgment being entered against them.
📜
Pleadings
The formal written statements exchanged between the parties at the outset of litigation — primarily the particulars of claim, the defence, and any counterclaim. Pleadings define the issues in dispute.
📋
Particulars of Claim
The formal document in which the claimant sets out the facts, legal basis, and remedy sought in a civil claim. It must be served on the defendant and is a core pleading document.
🛡️
Defence
The defendant's formal written response to the particulars of claim. It must admit or deny each allegation and set out any positive case the defendant wishes to advance.
🔄
Counterclaim
A claim made by the defendant against the claimant within the same proceedings. The defendant becomes a Part 20 claimant and the original claimant must file a defence to counterclaim.
📂
Disclosure
The process by which parties to litigation must reveal documents that are relevant to the issues in the case — including documents that are adverse to their own case.
📝
Witness Statement
A formal written account of the evidence a witness intends to give at trial. It must be signed with a statement of truth and stands as the witness's evidence-in-chief.
🔬
Expert Witness
A person with specialist knowledge — such as a doctor, engineer, or accountant — appointed to give independent opinion evidence in proceedings. Must comply with duties to the court, not the party who instructs them.
⚖️
Summary Judgment
A court order disposing of a claim or defence without a full trial, where the court is satisfied that the opposing party has no real prospect of success and there is no other compelling reason for a trial.
🚫
Strike Out
A court order removing a statement of case or part of it from the proceedings, typically because it discloses no reasonable grounds, is an abuse of process, or for failure to comply with a court order.
💰
Costs
The legal expenses incurred in litigation. The general rule ('costs follow the event') is that the losing party pays the winning party's costs, subject to the court's discretion.
🏛️
Court of Appeal
The second-highest court in the English judicial hierarchy. It hears appeals from the High Court and County Court on points of law. Its decisions bind all lower courts.
👑
Supreme Court
The highest court in the United Kingdom. It hears appeals on points of law of general public importance. Its decisions bind all courts below it and create binding precedent.
🏢
Tribunal
A specialist adjudicative body with jurisdiction over specific areas such as employment, immigration, tax, or mental health. Generally less formal than courts but legally binding in their decisions.
🔍
Judicial Review
A procedure by which the High Court supervises the decisions of public bodies, lower courts, and tribunals. It examines the lawfulness of the decision, not its merits.
📖
Precedent
A legal principle established by a court decision that must be followed by lower courts in subsequent cases with similar facts. The doctrine of binding precedent is called 'stare decisis'.
❓
Cross-Examination
The questioning of a witness by the opposing party's advocate after the witness has given their evidence-in-chief. Its purpose is to test credibility and challenge the accuracy of the evidence.
🗣️
Hearsay
A statement made out of court that is offered as evidence of the truth of the matter asserted. Admissible in civil proceedings under the Civil Evidence Act 1995, subject to notice requirements.
🎓
Counsel
A barrister (or solicitor-advocate) engaged to provide legal advice or appear as an advocate in court. 'Leading counsel' (King's Counsel) handles complex or high-value cases.
⏳
Limitation Period
The time within which legal proceedings must be commenced. Under the Limitation Act 1980, most contract claims must be brought within 6 years; personal injury within 3 years. Time-barred claims cannot proceed.

Text 1: The Civil Litigation Process in England and Wales

Civil litigation in England and Wales follows a structured process governed primarily by the Civil Procedure Rules 1998 (CPR). Before issuing proceedings, parties are expected to comply with pre-action protocols — a series of steps designed to encourage early settlement and information exchange. These include sending a letter before action, providing relevant documents, and allowing a reasonable period for a response. Non-compliance with pre-action protocols can have cost consequences even if the non-complying party ultimately wins the case. Only once these preliminary steps have been completed should a claimant issue a claim form at court and serve particulars of claim on the defendant.

Once proceedings are issued, the court actively manages the case through a system of directions. Cases are allocated to one of three tracks depending on their complexity and value: the small claims track (claims up to £10,000), the fast track (£10,000–£25,000), and the multi-track (over £25,000 or complex cases). On the multi-track — which covers most significant commercial disputes — the court will give detailed directions governing disclosure of documents, exchange of witness statements and expert reports, and the trial timetable. The disclosure process is particularly significant: each party must disclose all documents relevant to the issues in the case, including documents that are harmful to their own position.

Trial is the culmination of the litigation process, but it is reached by only a small minority of cases — the vast majority settle before trial. At trial, witnesses give their evidence-in-chief by reference to their written statements (which stand as evidence), and are then cross-examined by the opposing advocate. Expert witnesses give their opinions and may be questioned on their methodology and conclusions. At the end of the trial, the judge delivers a judgment — typically reserved for a later date in complex cases. The losing party will generally be ordered to pay a substantial proportion of the winning party's costs, which provides a powerful incentive to settle early.

Text 2: The Hierarchy of English Courts and the Doctrine of Precedent

The English legal system is built on the doctrine of precedent — the principle that courts are bound to follow the decisions of higher courts when dealing with cases that raise the same legal issues. This doctrine, known by the Latin phrase 'stare decisis' (to stand by decided matters), gives the common law its characteristic stability and predictability. At the apex of the hierarchy stands the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom, whose decisions bind every court below it. Below the Supreme Court sits the Court of Appeal — divided into the Civil Division and the Criminal Division — whose decisions bind the High Court, the County Court, and all other lower courts. The High Court, while bound by the Court of Appeal and Supreme Court, is not strictly bound by its own previous decisions, though it will generally follow them in the interests of consistency.

Beneath the High Court sits the County Court, which handles the vast majority of civil claims in England and Wales, including most debt recovery, personal injury, and contract disputes below the value that justifies a High Court claim. Below the civil courts, a range of specialist tribunals exercise jurisdiction over specific subject matters — employment disputes, immigration appeals, tax matters, and mental health decisions. Tribunals generally operate with less formality than courts, are designed to be more accessible to litigants in person, and their decisions may be appealed to the Upper Tribunal and, on points of law, to the Court of Appeal.

A court decision creates a precedent through its 'ratio decidendi' — the legal reasoning that is essential to the decision. This ratio is binding on lower courts. Statements of law made 'obiter dicta' — 'by the way', in passing — are persuasive but not binding. Understanding this distinction is crucial for legal practitioners: a barrister arguing that a lower court is not bound by a higher court's decision will often seek to distinguish the earlier case on its facts, or argue that the relevant statement was obiter rather than part of the ratio. The development of the common law through the accumulation of precedents — each case building on earlier decisions — is one of the most distinctive features of the English legal tradition.

Grammar Point 1: Reported Speech in Legal Documents
Tense Backshift in Reported Speech

When converting direct speech to reported speech in legal documents, tenses shift back. Present simple becomes past simple; present perfect becomes past perfect; 'will' becomes 'would'; 'can' becomes 'could'. This backshift signals that the statement is being reported, not made afresh.

Direct: 'The contract is valid.' → Reported: The party argued that the contract was valid.
Direct: 'I have not received the notice.' → Reported: The witness stated that he had not received the notice.
Direct: 'We will perform the agreement.' → Reported: The party undertook that it would perform the agreement.
Direct: 'The clause is ambiguous.' → Reported: Counsel submitted that the clause was ambiguous.
Key Reporting Verbs in Legal Writing

Legal writing uses precise reporting verbs that carry specific meanings. The choice of verb signals the speaker's epistemic status — whether they are making a finding (the judge), advancing a case (counsel), or giving evidence (a witness).

Judge / Court: held, found, ruled, determined, concluded, decided
Counsel / Advocate: submitted, argued, contended, invited, urged
Witness: stated, testified, confirmed, denied, acknowledged
Party: alleged, claimed, asserted, maintained, admitted, denied
Reporting Admissions and Denials

Legal documents frequently record what was admitted or denied. Precise reporting of these positions is critical — an incorrectly reported admission or denial can affect the whole case.

The defendant admitted that the payment had not been made by the due date.
The claimant denied that it had been given adequate notice of the alleged defect.
It was common ground between the parties that the contract had been validly executed.
The defendant neither admitted nor denied the allegation, requiring the claimant to prove it.
📋
Grammar Point 2: Citation and Reference Language
Citing Authorities in Legal Writing

Citing case law and statutory authority is a core skill in legal writing. English legal writing uses precise formulaic expressions to introduce, apply, and distinguish precedents. The citation format typically includes the case name, year, and law report reference.

'As held in Donoghue v Stevenson [1932] AC 562...' (introducing an authority)
'The test set out in Caparo Industries plc v Dickman [1990] 2 AC 605 requires...' (applying a test)
'The claimant relies on the authority of...' (advancing a submission based on precedent)
'This case is distinguishable from X v Y on the following grounds...' (distinguishing a precedent)
Cross-Reference Language within Documents

Legal documents cross-reference earlier sections, exhibits, and authorities with precision. Standard phrases are used to guide the reader through complex multi-part documents.

'As noted above at paragraph [X]...' (internal cross-reference)
'As set out in Schedule 1 hereto...' (reference to an attached schedule)
'Further particulars are set out in the witness statement of [name] served herewith.' (reference to evidence)
'Without prejudice to the foregoing...' (introducing a qualification)
Latin Phrases Used in Legal Reporting

English law retains a number of Latin phrases that are used in legal writing and court documents. Knowing these terms is essential for reading judgments and legal texts accurately.

'ratio decidendi' — the essential reasoning of a case, binding on lower courts
'obiter dicta' — statements made in passing, persuasive but not binding
'inter alia' — among other things (used to introduce a non-exhaustive list)
'prima facie' — on the face of it; at first glance (used to indicate an initial impression subject to further examination)

Grammar Point 3: Legal Argument Structure and Connectives
The IRAC Structure

Legal argument commonly follows the IRAC structure: Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion. This framework helps organise complex legal reasoning clearly and ensures all essential elements of an argument are addressed.

Issue: 'The question is whether the defendant owed a duty of care to the claimant.'
Rule: 'The three-stage Caparo test requires foreseeability, proximity, and that it be fair, just, and reasonable to impose a duty.'
Application: 'Applying the test to the present facts...'
Conclusion: 'Accordingly, the claimant respectfully submits that a duty of care existed.'
Connectives for Legal Reasoning

Legal arguments use precise connectives to link propositions, introduce concessions, signal conclusions, and introduce alternatives. Using the wrong connective can undermine the logic of a submission.

Conclusion / therefore: 'Accordingly', 'It follows that', 'Therefore', 'In the circumstances'
Concession / despite: 'Notwithstanding', 'Although', 'Even if', 'Without prejudice to'
Contrast / however: 'However', 'Nevertheless', 'That said', 'By contrast'
Alternative submission: 'In the alternative', 'Should the court not accept...', 'Without prejudice to the primary submission'
Hedging in Legal Submissions

Legal advocates hedge their submissions carefully — overstating a case can damage credibility. Precise hedging language signals confidence without overreaching, and protects against being held to an absolute position.

'It is submitted that...' (not 'It is certain that...')
'The evidence suggests...' (not 'The evidence proves beyond doubt...')
'On the balance of probabilities...' (civil standard — not 'beyond reasonable doubt')
'The claimant invites the court to find...' (not 'The claimant demands that the court holds...')

A Day in Court: Civil Trial Procedure
Documentary Dialogue · B2 Level · approx. 108 sec
0:00

Today we follow a civil trial in the High Court of England and Wales. The case involves a contractual dispute between a technology company and a software developer. The trial is now in its third day. The claimant's counsel, Mr Ashworth, is about to call the claimant's key witness. Judge: Mr Ashworth, you may proceed. Ashworth: Thank you, My Lord. I call Ms Priya Sharma. Judicial Review: When Can Courts Challenge Public Decisions?
Legal Briefing · B2 Level · approx. 105 sec
0:00

Judicial review is a legal procedure by which the High Court supervises the decisions of public bodies — including government departments, local authorities, regulatory agencies, and inferior courts. It is one of the most important mechanisms for holding public power to account in a democratic society. Crucially, judicial review does not examine whether a decision was the right one — it examines whether the decision was made lawfully. The court asks three fundamental questions. First: did the decision-maker have the legal authority to make the decision? A public body can only act within the powers granted to it by statute — if it acts beyond those powers, the decision is ultra vires and unlawful. Second: did the decision-maker follow a fair procedure? The principles of natural justice require that the decision-maker be unbiased and that the affected person be given an opportunity to make representations. Third: was the decision rational? A decision that is so unreasonable that no reasonable decision-maker could have reached it may be struck down under the principle known as Wednesbury unreasonableness. Importantly, judicial review is a remedy of last resort — claimants must generally exhaust other available remedies before the court will grant permission to proceed. The permission stage is a significant filter: many applications for judicial review do not pass it.

Understanding Precedent: Ratio, Obiter, and Distinguishing Cases
Academic Briefing · B2 Level · approx. 100 sec
0:00

The doctrine of precedent — or stare decisis — is the cornerstone of the English common law system. Under this doctrine, a decision of a higher court creates a binding precedent that lower courts must follow when faced with the same legal issue. But understanding precedent requires more than knowing which court binds which. It requires understanding the anatomy of a judicial decision. Every court judgment contains two types of statement: the ratio decidendi and obiter dicta. The ratio decidendi — Latin for 'the reason for the decision' — is the legal principle applied by the court to reach its conclusion. This is the binding element. The ratio is determined not by the judge, but by subsequent courts interpreting the earlier decision. Obiter dicta — 'things said by the way' — are legal observations that are not essential to the decision. They may be highly persuasive, particularly if made by the Supreme Court or a senior judge, but they are not strictly binding. Skilled advocates use the distinction between ratio and obiter strategically. If an advocate wants to argue that a lower court is not bound by an earlier decision, they will try to show either that the earlier case is factually distinguishable — that is, the material facts are sufficiently different — or that the relevant statement was obiter rather than ratio. This process of distinguishing cases is one of the most important lawyering skills in the common law tradition.

Case Study 1: Donoghue v Stevenson [1932] AC 562
The House of Lords decision that established the modern law of negligence and the 'neighbour principle'

Background

In August 1928, May Donoghue visited a café in Paisley, Scotland, where a friend purchased for her a bottle of ginger beer. The bottle was opaque and sealed. When Donoghue poured out part of the contents, she discovered the decomposed remains of a snail. She alleged that she suffered gastroenteritis and nervous shock as a result. She could not sue the café owner in contract, because she had not purchased the ginger beer — her friend had. She could not sue the manufacturer, Stevenson, in contract either, for the same reason. Instead, she brought a claim in the tort of negligence, arguing that the manufacturer owed her a duty of care in preparing and selling the product. The case ultimately reached the House of Lords.

The Legal Issues

The House of Lords, by a majority of three to two, held that the manufacturer owed a duty of care to the ultimate consumer of its product. Lord Atkin, in the leading judgment, articulated the 'neighbour principle': one must take reasonable care to avoid acts or omissions which one can reasonably foresee would be likely to injure one's 'neighbour' — defined as persons who are so closely and directly affected by the act that one ought reasonably to have them in contemplation. This principle was revolutionary: it established that a duty of care can exist even in the absence of a contractual relationship. Lord Atkin's neighbour principle has since been developed and refined — most notably in Caparo Industries plc v Dickman [1990], which added the requirements of proximity and that it be fair, just, and reasonable to impose a duty.

Lessons for Legal Practice

Donoghue v Stevenson is one of the most important cases in the common law world. For legal practitioners, it illustrates three fundamental principles of judicial reasoning. First, the ratio decidendi of a case — the neighbour principle — can be stated at a level of abstraction that allows it to apply across a vast range of subsequent situations. Second, dissenting judgments — in this case two of the five judges dissented — are part of the legal record and may later become influential if the law develops in a different direction. Third, the case demonstrates the power of precedent: a 1932 decision of the House of Lords continues to shape the law of negligence today, and every first-year law student in England and Wales encounters it within weeks of beginning their studies.

England and Wales · Constitutional Law · 2019 · Supreme Court
Case Study 2: R (Miller) v The Prime Minister [2019] UKSC 41
The Supreme Court's unanimous ruling that the prorogation of Parliament was unlawful

Background

In August 2019, Prime Minister Boris Johnson advised Queen Elizabeth II to prorogue — that is, to suspend — Parliament for five weeks, from 9 September to 14 October 2019. The prorogation came at a critical moment in the Brexit process, with Parliament set to debate legislation concerning the UK's departure from the European Union. Gina Miller, a businesswoman, challenged the lawfulness of the prorogation by way of judicial review. The case was expedited through the courts at exceptional speed. The Court of Session in Scotland held that the prorogation was unlawful; the Divisional Court in England held that it was not justiciable — that is, not a matter the courts could review. Both decisions were appealed directly to the Supreme Court.

The Legal Issues

In a unanimous decision of all eleven justices, the Supreme Court held that the prorogation of Parliament was unlawful and void. The court applied the fundamental constitutional principle that Parliament is sovereign — it is the supreme lawmaking body in the United Kingdom, and the executive cannot act to frustrate Parliament's ability to carry out its constitutional functions. The court held that proroguing Parliament for five weeks without reasonable justification at such a critical constitutional moment had the effect of frustrating Parliament without reasonable excuse. The order of prorogation was quashed, and Parliament was recalled. The decision is remarkable for several reasons: it was unanimous; it directly constrained the exercise of a prerogative power by the Prime Minister; and it confirmed that the courts have jurisdiction to review the lawfulness of prorogation.

Lessons for Legal Practice and Constitutional Law

Miller 2 — as it is widely known — illustrates several principles of fundamental importance. First, judicial review is a powerful constitutional mechanism: even the most senior exercise of executive power is subject to the rule of law and can be reviewed and quashed by the courts. Second, the decision demonstrates the speed at which the English judicial system can respond to urgent constitutional crises: the Supreme Court delivered its judgment within days of the hearing. Third, the case shows how courts develop the law: the Supreme Court applied existing constitutional principles to a novel factual situation, creating precedent that will govern any future attempt to prorogue Parliament in similar circumstances. Fourth, the case confirms that constitutional conventions — previously thought to be non-justiciable — can underpin legally enforceable principles when fundamental constitutional values are at stake.
