Module Overview

Welcome to Module 8: Writing Sales Emails and Follow-ups. In modern sales, the written word is often the first — and sometimes the only — contact a prospect has with you before they decide whether to engage. A poorly written email can close a door that a well-written one would have opened wide. Yet most salespeople write emails as an afterthought, dashing off a few lines between calls without considering structure, tone, subject line psychology, or what the reader actually needs to see in order to respond.

This module builds on the prospecting skills from Module 2 and the pitch language from Module 3, applying them specifically to written communication. You will study the structure, language, and psychology behind the most effective types of sales email — cold outreach, post-meeting follow-ups, proposal cover emails, deal-nudge messages, and break-up emails. You will develop the grammar precision and vocabulary range needed to write emails that feel personal, professional, and purposeful in English.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Identify the key types of sales email — cold outreach, follow-up, post-meeting summary, proposal cover, deal-nudge, and break-up — and describe the purpose, structure, and tone of each.
2
Write a compelling cold outreach email with a personalised opener, a concise value proposition, relevant social proof, and a low-friction call to action under 120 words.
3
Structure and write a professional post-meeting follow-up email that summarises the conversation, confirms specific next steps with dates, and keeps the deal moving forward.
4
Use correct tense sequences and appropriate register — distinguishing formal from semi-formal written English — to write sales emails that feel professional without being stiff.
5
Apply linking phrases and transitional language correctly to create smooth, logical, and readable email prose — especially in multi-paragraph follow-up and proposal emails.
6
Form and use the core sales email collocations — including reach out, follow up, circle back, loop in, and touch base — accurately and naturally in context.
7
Write a full five-email follow-up sequence for a prospect who did not respond to initial outreach, varying the approach and adding genuine value at each touchpoint.
8
Listen to and understand discussions about email copywriting principles, follow-up sequence strategy, sales email critiques, and best-practice frameworks for written sales communication.

Key Vocabulary

21 essential terms for writing sales emails and follow-ups in professional English.

Module Vocabulary
❄️
Cold email
An unsolicited email to a prospect with no prior contact. Effective cold emails are short (under 120 words), highly personalised, relevant to the recipient's specific situation, and end with a single low-commitment call to action.
🔁
Follow-up email
An email sent after an initial contact or meeting, designed to maintain momentum, add value, address questions, and move the conversation to the next stage. The most underused tool in sales.
📋
Post-meeting summary
A follow-up email sent within 24 hours of a call or meeting. Recaps the key points discussed, confirms agreed actions and specific dates, and sets out the proposed next step. Prevents misunderstandings and keeps the deal moving.
📩
Subject line
The headline of an email seen in the inbox before it is opened. The single most important element of any outreach email — it determines whether the email is opened, ignored, or deleted. Best practice: four to seven words, personalised, curiosity-generating.
👋
Personalised opener
The first one or two sentences of a cold email that reference something specific about the recipient — their company news, role, recent activity, or a shared connection — proving the email was written for them, not sent to a list.
📣
Value proposition line
A single outcome-focused sentence explaining who you help, what problem you solve, and what result you deliver — not a product description. Example: 'We help logistics companies reduce customs compliance errors by 70%.'
🎯
Call to action (CTA)
The specific, single action you ask the reader to take at the end of the email. Best CTAs in cold emails are low-friction: a yes/no question, a 15-minute call, or a simple reply request — never a demand for major time commitment.
📆
Follow-up cadence
A planned sequence of follow-up emails sent at set intervals — typically days 1, 3, 7, 14, and 21 after initial contact — each taking a different angle and adding new value, designed to stay visible without becoming intrusive.
💔
Break-up email
The final email in a follow-up sequence, sent honestly and warmly to a non-responsive prospect, signalling that this will be the last message. Frequently triggers replies from prospects who were meaning to respond but hadn't. Must be genuine, never guilt-inducing.
📊
Open rate
The percentage of recipients who open a sales email. Measures subject line and timing effectiveness. Industry averages vary by sector, typically between 20–40% for well-crafted outbound sequences. Tracked in CRM or email automation tools.
📨
Reply rate
The percentage of recipients who respond to a sales email. The most important metric for outbound email — more valuable than open rate. A well-crafted, personalised five-email sequence typically achieves 5–15% reply rates.
🔗
Warm follow-up
A follow-up email to a prospect who has already had some interaction with the company — attended a webinar, downloaded content, or had a previous conversation. Has higher expected reply rates than cold outreach due to existing awareness.
📝
Proposal cover email
An email sent alongside a written proposal, summarising the key recommendations and making the proposal easy to navigate. Should reference the prospect's specific situation and the headline result they can expect — never just 'please find attached.'
⚡
Re-engagement email
An email sent to a prospect or customer who has gone quiet after prior interaction — a stalled deal, an inactive account, or an unanswered sequence. Takes a fresh angle, shares something new, or asks a direct question to restart dialogue.
🤝
Mutual connection reference
A mention in a cold email of a shared contact who knows both the sender and the recipient. One of the highest-converting personalisation elements available — significantly increases both open rate and reply rate.
📌
Email sequence
A pre-planned series of emails sent to a prospect over a defined period — for example, five emails over three weeks — with each message taking a distinct angle, adding different value, and using a different CTA.
✂️
Email brevity
The principle that sales emails should be as short as possible while still being complete. Cold emails: under 120 words. Follow-ups: three to five sentences unless specifically adding a case study or data point. Long emails are rarely read in full.
🔄
Circle back
A professional expression meaning to return to a topic or contact at a later point. Common in follow-up emails: 'I'd like to circle back on this next month when your Q3 budget process is underway.'
🔔
Touch base
A common business English expression meaning to make brief contact to check in or share a quick update. Often used in follow-up email subject lines and openers: 'Touching base after our call last Thursday.'
📐
P.S. line
A postscript added below the email sign-off. Often one of the most-read elements of a sales email because it stands out visually from the main body. Best used for a compelling social proof point, a time-sensitive note, or a secondary CTA.
🎣
Email hook
The opening element of a sales email designed to capture attention immediately. A strong hook references something specific and relevant to the reader — making them want to continue. The difference between an email that is read in full and one that is closed after two seconds.

Text 1: The Anatomy of a High-Performing Cold Email

What separates emails that get replies from those that get deleted

The cold email is one of the most challenging formats in professional written communication — it must capture the attention of someone who did not ask to hear from you, demonstrate relevance within the first two sentences, convey credibility without arrogance, and close with a request that feels so easy to fulfil that the reader's natural instinct is to reply. Most cold emails fail not because the product is wrong or the prospect is uninterested, but because the email is written from the sender's perspective rather than the reader's. The classic failure mode is an email that opens with a paragraph about the sending company's history and credentials, then lists product features, and closes with a request for a 60-minute discovery call. The reader, who receives dozens of such emails each week, has no compelling reason to respond.

The highest-performing cold emails share a consistent structural formula, even though they vary enormously in tone and content. They open with a personalised hook — a specific reference to the recipient's company, role, recent news, or shared connection that demonstrates the email was written for them, not for a list. The second sentence introduces the sender's value in outcome language rather than feature language: not 'we are a data analytics platform' but 'we help finance teams at companies like yours reduce their month-end close process by 40%.' The third element introduces social proof — a named client, a specific result, or a relevant statistic — that makes the claim credible rather than merely asserted. The email then closes with a single, low-friction call to action: not 'let's schedule a 60-minute call' but 'would a 15-minute conversation this week be worth your time?' or even simpler: 'is this even relevant to you right now?'

Subject lines deserve special attention. Research on email open rates consistently shows that short subject lines — four to seven words — outperform longer ones. Subject lines that reference the recipient's name or company outperform generic ones. Questions and curiosity-gap subject lines ('Quick question about your reporting process') outperform statement subject lines ('Introducing our analytics platform'). And subject lines that feel personal and conversational — as if written by a human being rather than a marketing department — consistently outperform polished promotional ones. The paradox of cold email subject lines is that the less they look like a marketing email, the more likely they are to be opened and read.

Text 2: The Art of the Follow-Up — Persistence Without Pushiness

How to build a follow-up sequence that adds value and earns responses

The follow-up email is statistically one of the most valuable tools in a salesperson's written communication repertoire — and one of the most consistently underused. Studies of B2B sales email sequences consistently show that the majority of positive responses come not from the first email but from the second, third, or even fourth follow-up. Yet most salespeople send one email, receive no reply, and conclude that the prospect is not interested. This conclusion is almost always wrong. Prospects are busy, inboxes are full, good intentions to reply get interrupted, and the first email simply gets buried. A well-timed, well-written follow-up email is not a sign of desperation — it is a professional service.

The most common mistake in follow-up email writing is sending the same email again with slightly different wording. 'Just following up on my previous email' is the least effective opener in professional sales English — it adds no value, provides no new reason to engage, and signals to the recipient that the sender has nothing new to say. An effective follow-up sequence treats each email as a distinct opportunity to add value, take a different angle, or share a new piece of relevant information. Email 2 might reference a relevant case study. Email 3 might share a brief industry insight the prospect would find genuinely useful. Email 4 might take a more direct approach: 'I'll be brief — is this simply not a priority right now, or would it be worth a quick conversation?'

The break-up email — the final message in a follow-up sequence — deserves special consideration. Done well, a break-up email is one of the most reliably effective emails a salesperson can send. By honestly and gracefully signalling that the sender is concluding their outreach — 'I don't want to keep filling your inbox, so this will be my last message unless I hear from you' — the break-up email removes the pressure that may have been preventing the prospect from responding, and creates a now-or-never moment that frequently triggers a reply. The tone must be warm and genuine, never passive-aggressive or guilt-inducing. A break-up email is not a manipulation tactic; it is an honest communication that respects both parties' time and leaves the door genuinely open for future contact.

Grammar Reference 1: Tense and Register in Professional Sales Emails

Why tense precision matters in sales emails

Sales emails must feel current, relevant, and professionally written. Incorrect tenses make emails sound hesitant, unnatural, or poorly edited — reducing the reader's confidence in the sender. Present perfect and present continuous are the most frequently used tenses in effective sales email writing.

Present perfect — for recent relevance and current status

Use present perfect to connect past events to the current moment. Essential for cold email openers that reference news, and for follow-up emails referring to prior action.

  • 'I have seen your recent news about the European expansion...' (recently noticed, still relevant)
  • 'I have attached our case study for your reference.' (attached to this email, currently relevant)
  • 'I have been following your company's growth in the B2B space...' (ongoing, present relevance)

Present continuous — for current purpose and actions in progress

Use present continuous to describe what you are doing right now — particularly for email openings where you state the purpose of writing.

  • 'I am writing to introduce myself and explore whether...'
  • 'I am reaching out because I noticed that...'
  • 'I am following up on our conversation from last Thursday...'

Register — formal vs semi-formal in B2B sales emails

Cold outreach to senior executives: slightly more formal register. Follow-up after rapport has been established: semi-formal and conversational. Always avoid extremes — overly formal emails feel cold and distant; overly casual emails can seem disrespectful of the professional relationship.

  • Formal: 'I would be grateful if you could confirm your availability for a call.'
  • Semi-formal: 'Would Tuesday or Thursday work for a quick 15-minute call?'
  • Too casual: 'Hey — just checking you got my last email!'
  • Standard professional close: 'I look forward to hearing from you. Best regards, [Name].'
Grammar Reference 2: Linking Phrases for Coherent Email Prose

Why linking phrases matter in sales emails

Sales emails must flow logically from one point to the next. Without linking phrases, paragraphs feel disconnected and the argument is hard to follow. The right linking phrase signals the relationship between two ideas — addition, contrast, concession, consequence, or context.

Adding information and building the argument

Use these to introduce an additional benefit, evidence, or supporting point:

  • 'In addition...' / 'Furthermore...' / 'On top of that...'
  • 'I have also included...' / 'I have attached as well...'
  • 'Beyond this...' / 'Additionally...' / 'What is more...'

Concession and contrast — acknowledging before redirecting

Use these to acknowledge a concern or limitation and then redirect to a positive point. Critical for follow-up emails when you need to address a potential objection or difficult timing:

  • 'That said...' / 'With that in mind...'
  • 'I understand that... however...'
  • 'While I appreciate you may be extremely busy, I believe this is worth 15 minutes.'
  • 'Notwithstanding that...' (formal)

Signalling context and linking to previous communication

Use these to connect the current email to a prior conversation, agreed action, or attached document:

  • 'Following our conversation last Tuesday...'
  • 'As we discussed...' / 'As I mentioned on the call...'
  • 'As promised, I have attached the case study.'
  • 'In response to your question about integration...'
Grammar Reference 3: Core Sales Email Collocations

Why collocations define email credibility and fluency

Professional sales emails have their own fixed vocabulary of standard expressions. Using the correct combinations signals fluency and professionalism. Wrong combinations sound unnatural even when the intended meaning is clear — reducing the sender's credibility in the eyes of a native or fluent English speaker.

Reaching out and following up — the most essential patterns

These verb patterns are fixed and must be memorised:

  • reach out → to someone, about something, regarding a topic
  • follow up → on a conversation, on a proposal, on an unanswered email
  • circle back → next week, after the review, in Q1, when timing is better
  • touch base → briefly, quickly, to check in, ahead of the call

Attaching, sending, and sharing materials

These expressions describe accompanying documents and materials in a sales email:

  • attach → a document, a case study, a proposal, a one-pager, a link
  • send over → a document, a summary, details, a link to the recording
  • include → a copy, a brief overview, an introduction
  • forward → the email chain, the recording, the document to a colleague

Offering, proposing, and closing the email

These standard combinations are used in sales email body and close:

  • find a time → that works, that suits you, for a brief 15-minute call
  • have a call → next week, on Thursday, at your convenience
  • look forward to → hearing from you, speaking with you, your thoughts
  • be happy to → arrange a demo, send further details, answer any questions

Case Study 1
Predictable Revenue — Aaron Ross and the Cold Email at Salesforce
USA · SaaS / Sales Methodology · 2002–Present · Email-Driven Pipeline Generation · How Aaron Ross used a structured cold email model to add over $100 million to Salesforce's pipeline — and changed outbound sales permanently
Background

In the early 2000s, Aaron Ross joined Salesforce with an unconventional mission: generate a predictable, scalable flow of new revenue from outbound prospecting — without depending on the heroics of individual star salespeople. The approach he developed, documented later in the influential book Predictable Revenue, became one of the most widely adopted sales methodologies in the global technology industry. At its core was a simple but powerful insight: most cold emails fail not because outbound sales does not work, but because salespeople write the wrong type of email to the wrong person and ask for too much too soon.

The Cold Email Formula That Generated Results

Ross's approach to outbound email was built on four non-negotiable principles. First, emails were sent to the right person — not the CEO or the most senior executive available, but the individual most directly affected by the problem being solved, who was most likely to refer the salesperson to the actual decision-maker. Second, emails were short — a maximum of three to five sentences. In Ross's model, a cold email is not a sales pitch; it is a referral request. The goal is not to explain the product in detail but to receive a response such as 'you should speak to our Head of Operations.' Third, subject lines were deliberately conversational and non-promotional — written to look like a personal email, not a campaign message. Fourth, every email ended with a single, easy-to-answer question, never a complex request for time or commitment.

Results and Legacy

The approach ultimately contributed over $100 million in recurring revenue to Salesforce through outbound prospecting channels alone. Ross's model demonstrated conclusively that cold email, when structured correctly with realistic expectations, could be one of the most cost-efficient and systematically scalable sources of new B2B pipeline. The Predictable Revenue methodology has been adopted by thousands of technology and professional services companies worldwide and spawned an entire ecosystem of sales development tools, training programmes, SDR roles, and outbound process frameworks. The core lesson, which remains as relevant today as it was in 2002, is disarmingly simple: the best cold email is not a pitch — it is a short, relevant, respectful, and easy-to-answer request for a conversation.

Case Study 2
HubSpot's Research on Follow-Up Email Timing and Sequence Performance
USA · Marketing Technology · Research & Insights · 2015–Present · What data from millions of B2B sales email sequences reveals about optimal timing, sequence length, and the content that drives replies
The Research Foundation

HubSpot — both a leading CRM and marketing technology platform and a prolific publisher of sales effectiveness research — has analysed data from millions of sales email sequences to understand what genuinely drives reply rates, deal progression, and pipeline generation. Published across its annual State of Sales reports and platform usage analyses, this research draws from a broad cross-section of B2B sales teams across industries and geographies, giving it unusual credibility and generalisability as a benchmark for best practice. Unlike anecdotal advice that varies wildly by sector and sales culture, HubSpot's data represents the aggregated behaviour of a diverse global sales population.

Key Findings on Timing and Sequence Length

HubSpot's research consistently identifies the optimal gap between follow-up emails as two to five business days after the previous touchpoint. Reaching out again in less than 24 hours signals desperation and typically generates negative reactions; waiting more than seven days allows the conversation to cool excessively and reduces relevance. Critically, the data reveals that most salespeople stop following up far too early. In sequences where deals eventually progressed to a positive outcome, the majority of positive responses came after the third or fourth email — yet the average salesperson abandons follow-up after only one or two attempts. The optimal sequence length for cold outbound prospecting appears to be between four and six distinct emails, with each email introducing a meaningfully different element: the first introduces, the second adds value, the third takes a different angle, the fourth becomes more direct, and the fifth or sixth is the break-up.

What Drives Reply Rates in Individual Emails

On the content side, HubSpot's findings align closely with the principles taught in this module. Subject lines under fifty characters consistently outperform longer ones in open rate tests. Emails containing a single clear call to action significantly outperform emails with multiple competing requests. Personalised emails — even simple personalisation such as mentioning the prospect's company name, referencing a recent piece of company news, or noting a shared connection — outperform generic templates by a factor of two to five times. On timing within the working week, emails sent on Tuesday, Wednesday, or Thursday between 9am and 11am local time show consistently higher open and reply rates than those sent on Mondays, Fridays, or at weekends. The overarching conclusion the data supports is entirely consistent with everything known about human attention and professional communication: people respond to messages that feel relevant to their situation, respectful of their time, and genuinely written for them as an individual.
