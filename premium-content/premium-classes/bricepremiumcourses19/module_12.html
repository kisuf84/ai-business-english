Module Overview

Welcome to Module 12: Metrics and Performance Feedback. In high-performance sales and customer service environments, the ability to discuss numbers clearly and confidently is as important as the ability to pitch, close, or resolve a complaint. Whether you are presenting your results in a team meeting, receiving feedback from your manager in a one-to-one, reviewing a team's KPI dashboard with a director, or setting performance goals at the start of a new quarter, you need precise, professional English to engage with this data credibly.

This module covers the full landscape of performance language in sales and customer service — the vocabulary of KPIs, targets, trends, and benchmarks; the grammar of describing data, making comparisons, and expressing cause and effect; and the conversational structures used in feedback sessions, performance reviews, and coaching conversations. You will leave this module able to talk about your own performance and your team's performance with the precision and confidence of a native English-speaking professional.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Define and explain the most important KPIs used in sales and customer service — including conversion rate, average handle time, first-call resolution, NPS, CSAT, quota attainment, and ARR — and describe what each metric reveals about performance.
2
Read, interpret, and discuss a performance dashboard or KPI report accurately — identifying trends, anomalies, and areas of strength or concern — using the correct English vocabulary.
3
Use the standard language for describing trends and data movement — including verbs such as rise, fall, plateau, recover, and outperform, and adverbs such as sharply, steadily, significantly, and marginally — in spoken and written performance discussions.
4
Apply cause and effect language structures — including 'as a result of', 'this led to', 'due to', 'which contributed to', and 'this was driven by' — correctly when explaining performance outcomes in meetings and written reports.
5
Give structured, balanced, and professionally worded performance feedback to a colleague or direct report — using the SBI (Situation-Behaviour-Impact) model and appropriate positive and developmental language.
6
Receive, respond to, and engage constructively with performance feedback in English — asking clarifying questions, acknowledging concerns, and committing to specific improvement actions.
7
Form and use the core performance management collocations — including set a target, track progress, identify a gap, review performance, and agree on an action plan — accurately and fluently.
8
Write a structured individual performance review summary and a monthly team KPI report — both communicating results clearly, identifying root causes of underperformance, and specifying next steps.

📚 Word Bank

Key Vocabulary

21 essential terms for metrics, performance management, and feedback in professional English.

Module Vocabulary
📊
KPI (Key Performance Indicator)
A specific, measurable metric used to evaluate whether an individual, team, or organisation is achieving its most important objectives. KPIs are 'key' because they are chosen to reflect the factors that most directly drive success — not every available piece of data. In sales, typical KPIs include quota attainment, conversion rate, and average deal size. In customer service, they include CSAT, FCR, and AHT.
🎯
Quota attainment
The percentage of the assigned revenue or activity target that has been achieved in a given period. Expressed as a percentage: 100% means the target was exactly met; above 100% means it was exceeded; below 100% means it was missed. 'On quota' typically means 90–100%; 'above quota' means 100%+. Quota attainment is the primary measure of individual sales performance.
⭐
NPS (Net Promoter Score)
A metric that measures customer loyalty by asking a single question: 'How likely are you to recommend us to a friend or colleague?' on a scale of 0–10. Respondents scoring 9–10 are Promoters, 7–8 are Passives, and 0–6 are Detractors. NPS = % Promoters − % Detractors. Scores range from −100 to +100. An NPS above 50 is considered excellent in most industries.
😊
CSAT (Customer Satisfaction Score)
A metric that measures satisfaction with a specific interaction — typically collected through a post-interaction survey on a 1–5 or 1–10 scale. Unlike NPS, which measures overall loyalty, CSAT measures the quality of a single moment. A high CSAT indicates the interaction was handled well; a low CSAT points to a specific failure in the process, the language used, or the outcome delivered.
📞
FCR (First-Call Resolution)
The percentage of customer issues resolved during the first contact — without requiring a callback, follow-up, or escalation. FCR is one of the strongest predictors of customer satisfaction in service environments: customers who have their issue resolved in a single interaction are significantly more satisfied than those who must contact the company multiple times. A strong FCR is typically 70–80%+.
⏱️
AHT (Average Handle Time)
The average duration of a customer service interaction — from the moment the agent answers to the moment the interaction is fully completed, including any post-call work. AHT is important for workforce planning but must be balanced against quality metrics: reducing AHT without improving FCR typically increases call volume as customers call back. The optimal AHT varies significantly by industry and complexity.
📈
Leading indicator
A metric that predicts future performance — providing an early signal of results that have not yet occurred. In sales, leading indicators include the number of outbound calls made, meetings booked, demos delivered, and proposals sent. Because leading indicators can be acted on before results are affected, they are the most valuable metrics for coaching and early intervention.
📉
Lagging indicator
A metric that measures results that have already occurred — such as revenue closed, quota attainment, or churn rate. Lagging indicators confirm whether past activity produced the desired outcome, but by the time they are visible it is too late to change the behaviour that drove them. Effective performance management requires both: leading indicators for coaching, lagging indicators for accountability.
💰
ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue)
The total recurring revenue a subscription or SaaS business expects to receive annually from its current customer base, normalised to a 12-month period. ARR is the primary valuation metric for subscription businesses because it represents predictable, repeatable income. ARR growth — through new customer acquisition and account expansion — is the most important company-level metric for investors and leadership teams in SaaS organisations.
🏆
Benchmark
A standard or reference point against which performance is measured. Benchmarks may be internal (comparing current performance to last quarter or last year) or external (comparing the team's performance to industry averages or competitors). Benchmarking helps organisations identify whether their performance is genuinely strong or merely average within a broader context.
🎯
SMART target
A performance target that is Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. A vague target like 'improve customer satisfaction' is not SMART. A SMART version would be: 'Increase the team's CSAT score from 7.2 to 7.8 by the end of Q3 by implementing the new call quality framework.' SMART targets are used in performance reviews to ensure that goals can be objectively assessed.
🔄
SBI feedback model
A structured framework for giving specific, evidence-based feedback: Situation (describe the specific context when the behaviour occurred), Behaviour (describe the observable behaviour — what the person did or said), Impact (explain the effect this behaviour had — on the team, the customer, the result). SBI feedback is more useful than general praise or criticism because it gives the recipient concrete information they can act on.
📋
Performance improvement plan (PIP)
A formal document used when an employee's performance falls below the required standard over a sustained period. A PIP specifies the performance gaps, the expected standard, the support to be provided, the actions required of the employee, and the timeline for review. PIPs are typically a final step before more serious consequences and should always be delivered with clear, respectful, and specific language.
📐
Run rate
A method of projecting future performance by extrapolating current performance over a full period. If a sales rep has closed £90,000 in the first six weeks of a 13-week quarter, their run rate for the full quarter would be approximately £195,000. Run rate is useful for early-quarter forecasting but must be adjusted for known changes in pipeline composition or seasonal variation.
🔁
Year-on-year (YoY)
A comparison of performance in a given period to the same period in the previous year — for example, comparing Q2 revenue this year to Q2 revenue last year. YoY comparisons eliminate the effect of seasonal variation, making them more meaningful than sequential (month-on-month) comparisons for identifying genuine trends. 'Revenue grew 18% year-on-year' is standard business reporting language.
📌
Gap analysis
The process of comparing current performance to a target or desired state — identifying the size and nature of the difference that needs to be closed. In a performance review, gap analysis identifies where an individual or team is falling short, what is causing the gap, and what actions are required to close it. 'There is a 12-percentage-point gap between the current conversion rate and the team benchmark.'
🌊
Trend
The general direction in which a metric is moving over time — upward, downward, or flat (plateauing). Identifying trends requires comparing data points over multiple periods rather than reacting to a single data point. Professional language for describing trends includes: rising sharply, declining steadily, levelling off, recovering gradually, and fluctuating. Single-period variations should not be described as trends.
🗓️
1:1 meeting (one-to-one)
A regular, private meeting between a manager and a direct report — typically weekly or fortnightly — to discuss performance, challenges, development, and priorities. An effective 1:1 is structured: it covers recent results, blockers, coaching, and next steps. It is the primary vehicle for ongoing performance feedback and is more effective than annual reviews for driving continuous improvement.
📝
Action plan
A specific, time-bound list of steps agreed between a manager and an employee to address a performance gap or achieve a development goal. An effective action plan names the specific action, the person responsible, the support required, and the deadline for completion. Without a concrete action plan, performance conversations often produce goodwill but no change in behaviour.
🔍
Root cause analysis
The process of identifying the underlying reason for underperformance — rather than treating the symptom. For example, if a rep's conversion rate has fallen, the symptom is the metric decline; the root cause might be poor discovery questioning, a change in the prospect mix, or inadequate product knowledge. Effective coaching addresses root causes, not symptoms.
💬
Constructive feedback
Feedback that is specific, evidence-based, respectful, and actionable — focused on helping the recipient improve rather than simply criticising what went wrong. Constructive feedback identifies what happened (the behaviour), why it matters (the impact), and what a better approach would look like (the path forward). It is distinguished from purely evaluative feedback by its orientation towards growth rather than judgment.

Text 1: KPIs in Sales and Customer Service — What to Measure, Why It Matters, and How to Talk About It

The metrics that drive decisions — and the professional language used to discuss them

Key performance indicators exist because not all data is equally important. In a typical sales or customer service organisation, hundreds of data points are available at any moment — call volume, email open rates, average deal size, ticket resolution time, customer tenure, pipeline coverage, and dozens more. The challenge for managers and professionals is not collecting data; it is identifying which metrics are most directly connected to the outcomes that matter, and developing the discipline to focus attention — and conversation — on those indicators above all others. A KPI, properly defined, is not simply a number that is tracked; it is a specific measurement that has been deliberately chosen because its improvement reliably predicts or reflects improvement in the most important business outcomes.

In sales, the metrics that matter most divide naturally into two categories: leading indicators and lagging indicators. Lagging indicators — quota attainment, revenue closed, average deal size, win rate — measure results that have already been produced. They are essential for accountability and for understanding whether the quarter was a success, but they arrive too late to influence the behaviour that created them. Leading indicators — calls made, meetings booked, demos delivered, proposals sent — measure the activities and behaviours that will produce future results. They are the metrics that managers should spend the most time coaching against, because they can still be changed. A rep who is behind quota with three weeks left in the quarter cannot change the lagging indicators; they can only change the leading ones.

The vocabulary used to discuss these metrics in a professional English-speaking environment is precise and consistent. Metrics 'increase', 'rise', 'grow', or 'improve' when moving in a positive direction; they 'decline', 'fall', 'drop', or 'deteriorate' when moving in a negative one. The degree of change is expressed using adverbs: 'sharply', 'significantly', 'steadily', 'gradually', 'marginally', or 'slightly'. Comparisons use 'compared to', 'relative to', 'versus', or 'against': 'Conversion rate improved by 8 percentage points compared to the same period last year.' The cause of a change is introduced with 'due to', 'as a result of', or 'driven by': 'The decline in FCR was driven by a spike in complex technical queries that exceeded the first-line team's capability.' Mastering this vocabulary allows professionals to participate with authority in the data-driven conversations that define high-performance teams.

Text 2: The Art of Performance Feedback — How to Give It, Receive It, and Make It Work

Why feedback is the most powerful and most misused tool in performance management

Performance feedback is, in theory, one of the most powerful tools available to any manager. A well-timed, well-constructed piece of feedback can change a person's trajectory — shifting a habit, opening a blind spot, accelerating a strength, or redirecting an approach that was quietly costing the team results. In practice, however, most feedback fails to achieve its potential. Research by Gallup found that only 26% of employees strongly agree that the feedback they receive helps them do better work. The reason this figure is so low is not that managers do not care about the people they manage — it is that the feedback they give is too vague to be useful, too late to be relevant, or too focused on the person's character rather than their specific, observable behaviour.

The SBI model — Situation, Behaviour, Impact — was developed by the Centre for Creative Leadership as a framework for giving feedback that is specific, actionable, and fair. The Situation grounds the feedback in a concrete, observable context: not 'you often rush customer calls' but 'in Monday's call with the Meridian account at 2pm'. The Behaviour describes exactly what the person did or said — not what they are like or what they seemed to feel, but what was observable: 'you ended the call after four minutes without confirming whether the customer's issue was fully resolved'. The Impact explains why this matters — not as a personal judgment but as a measurable effect: 'the customer called back 40 minutes later, which added to the queue and resulted in a second agent having to handle a situation that was already frustrated'. SBI feedback gives the recipient exactly what they need to change: the what, the when, and the why.

Receiving feedback well is equally a skill — and one that is rarely taught. The professional response to critical feedback in English has a clear structure: acknowledge what has been said without immediately becoming defensive, ask a clarifying question to ensure you have understood correctly, demonstrate that you have taken the feedback seriously by summarising it back, and commit to a specific action. 'Thank you for raising that — I hadn't seen it from that angle. Can I check — are you saying that the issue was specifically with the call close, rather than the overall handling? I want to make sure I understand correctly. I'll make sure I confirm resolution on every call this week and we can review at the end of the week.' This response demonstrates emotional intelligence, professional confidence, and a genuine orientation towards improvement — three qualities that distinguish high performers in any English-speaking sales or service organisation.

🔍 Grammar Review

Grammar Reference

Detailed reference notes covering all three grammar points in this module.

Grammar Reference 1: Language for Describing Trends and Data Movement

Why precise trend language matters in performance discussions

In performance reviews, team meetings, and written reports, the vocabulary used to describe data must be precise. Vague language — 'it went up a bit' or 'things got worse' — reduces credibility. Professional English uses a specific system of verbs, adverbs, and noun phrases to describe changes in metrics with appropriate precision and register.

Verbs for upward movement

Use these verbs when a metric is moving in a positive direction. The choice of verb signals the speed and magnitude of the change.

  • rise / increase / grow / improve → general upward movement
  • climb / recover → upward movement after a period of decline or stagnation
  • surge / soar / jump → sharp, rapid upward movement
  • peak → reach the highest point in a given period

Verbs for downward movement

Use these verbs when a metric is declining. Again, verb choice signals magnitude.

  • fall / decline / drop / decrease → general downward movement
  • slip / dip → small or gradual downward movement
  • plunge / collapse / deteriorate → sharp or serious decline
  • plateau → level off at a particular point — neither rising nor falling

Adverbs to modify the degree of change

Always pair movement verbs with an adverb to indicate how significant the change was. This is essential for professional communication.

  • sharply / dramatically / significantly → large, noticeable change
  • steadily / consistently / gradually → slow, sustained change over time
  • marginally / slightly / modestly → very small change
  • rapidly → fast change, regardless of magnitude
Grammar Reference 2: Cause and Effect Language in Performance Reports

Why cause and effect language is essential in performance discussions

Describing what happened is only the first step in a performance conversation. Explaining why it happened — and what it led to — requires a specific set of connectors and structures that professional English uses consistently. Using the right connector signals analytical thinking and command of professional register.

Introducing a cause — prepositions and phrases

These expressions introduce the reason why a change occurred.

  • due to + noun phrase → 'The decline was due to increased competitor activity.'
  • as a result of + noun phrase → 'As a result of the training programme, CSAT improved.'
  • following + noun phrase → 'Following the restructure, call volume increased by 30%.'
  • owing to + noun phrase → 'Owing to system downtime, ticket volumes spiked.'

Introducing an effect — connectors and verbs

These structures introduce what happened as a consequence of something else.

  • this led to / this resulted in + noun phrase → 'The new protocol led to a 12-point CSAT improvement.'
  • consequently / as a result + new clause → 'Call volumes dropped; as a result, wait times improved.'
  • which contributed to + noun phrase → 'The coaching programme contributed to a 15% rise in conversion rate.'
  • was driven by + noun phrase → 'Revenue growth was driven by strong Q4 new business.'

Contrast — acknowledging a positive despite a negative (or vice versa)

Use these to acknowledge complexity in performance data — where one metric moved positively while another did not.

  • despite + noun phrase → 'Despite strong new business, churn reduced net growth.'
  • although + clause → 'Although FCR improved, AHT remained above target.'
  • however + new sentence → 'CSAT rose to 8.1. However, NPS declined to 38.'
Grammar Reference 3: Core Performance Feedback Collocations

Why performance collocations define professional credibility

Performance management in English has a specific vocabulary of fixed expressions. Using the wrong verb with a key noun — even a near-synonym — sounds unnatural to native speakers and can undermine the credibility of feedback conversations, written reviews, and coaching sessions.

Setting and reviewing goals

Standard verb-noun combinations for target and goal-related language:

  • set / agree on / establish → a target, a goal, a KPI
  • review / assess / evaluate → performance, progress, results
  • meet / exceed / miss → a target, a benchmark, a quota
  • track / monitor / measure → progress, performance, metrics

Giving and receiving feedback

Standard collocations for feedback and coaching conversations:

  • give / deliver / provide → feedback, a review, an assessment
  • receive / act on / take on board → feedback, criticism, a suggestion
  • identify / pinpoint / highlight → a gap, a strength, an area for improvement
  • recognise / acknowledge / celebrate → a success, an achievement, progress

Action planning and development

Standard expressions for the output of performance conversations:

  • agree on / commit to / sign off on → an action plan, next steps
  • put in place / implement / roll out → a change, an improvement, a process
  • conduct / carry out / run → a review, an analysis, a coaching session
  • close → a gap, the distance between current and target performance

Real-World Cases

Case Study 1
Google's Project Oxygen — How Data Proved That Feedback Quality Is the Most Important Management Skill
USA · Technology · 2008–Present · Performance Management Research · How Google used data analytics to identify what makes a great manager — and why feedback topped the list
Background

In 2008, Google launched an internal research initiative called Project Oxygen — a data-driven attempt to answer a question that most companies assume rather than investigate: do managers actually matter, and if so, what specifically do the best managers do differently from the rest? The research team analysed performance reviews, feedback surveys, semiannual reviews, and nominations for Great Manager Awards across thousands of Google managers, looking for patterns that correlated with team performance, employee satisfaction, and retention. The findings, published internally and later externally, were widely reported as one of the most rigorous studies of management effectiveness ever conducted within a single large organisation. What made Project Oxygen unusual was that it relied entirely on data rather than assumption — and several of its findings challenged conventional wisdom about what makes management effective.

The Eight Behaviours of Great Managers

Project Oxygen identified eight behaviours that consistently distinguished the highest-performing managers from the rest. The number-one behaviour — the most significant differentiator between outstanding and average managers — was being a good coach. Specifically, this meant: giving specific, actionable, and timely feedback rather than vague annual assessments; holding regular one-to-one conversations to understand what each individual needed; and using performance data to coach at the activity and behaviour level rather than simply reviewing outcomes. The second most important behaviour was empowering the team rather than micromanaging — giving people autonomy and trusting them to execute. Together, these two behaviours accounted for the largest share of the performance variance between teams. Technical expertise — the ability the company had historically prioritised in management selection — ranked eighth.

Implications for Performance Feedback Professionals

The Project Oxygen findings have several important implications for anyone working in a sales or customer service environment where performance feedback is a regular activity. First, the quality of feedback matters more than its frequency: vague, general feedback given weekly is less effective than specific, SBI-structured feedback given less often. Second, the data showed that managers who discussed performance data in terms of behaviours and activities — 'you made 20% fewer discovery calls than the team average in October' — were more effective coaches than those who discussed only outcomes — 'your quota attainment was below target'. Third, the transition from technical expert to feedback-giving manager is one of the hardest professional transitions in any industry — and it requires deliberate practice with the language of performance, not just the logic of it.

Case Study 2
Adobe's Check-In System — How One Company Abolished Annual Reviews and Increased Performance
USA · Enterprise Software · 2012–Present · Performance Management Innovation · How Adobe replaced formal annual performance reviews with frequent, informal check-ins — and saw immediate measurable results
Background

In 2012, Adobe made a decision that was radical by corporate standards: it abolished its annual performance review system entirely. The traditional review process — in which each employee received a single formal assessment once per year, linked directly to pay decisions — had been in place for years and consumed an estimated 80,000 manager-hours annually across the company. But internal surveys were revealing a troubling reality: employees did not find the reviews useful, managers dreaded conducting them, and the feedback that emerged was almost always too infrequent, too vague, and too closely tied to compensation decisions to be genuinely developmental. Adobe replaced the annual review with what it called the Check-In system — a series of regular, informal, forward-looking conversations between managers and employees throughout the year.

The Check-In System in Practice

The Check-In system had three components. First, regular conversations — at least quarterly but ideally monthly — focused on expectations, feedback, and growth. These were explicitly not tied to pay decisions, which removed one of the key barriers to honest two-way feedback. Second, a shift in manager training from 'how to rate' to 'how to coach' — with Adobe investing heavily in teaching managers to give SBI-structured, specific, and forward-looking feedback. Third, real-time feedback tools that allowed peers and managers to give brief, immediate feedback after specific interactions or deliverables — rather than saving all observations for the end of the year. The results were swift: voluntary attrition dropped by 30% in the two years following implementation, manager satisfaction with the performance process improved significantly, and the company reported that employees were substantially more likely to agree that the feedback they received helped them grow.

Lessons for Performance Conversations

Adobe's experience demonstrates several principles that apply to anyone managing or participating in performance feedback in English. First, feedback separated from pay decisions is dramatically more effective — people engage more honestly with feedback when it is purely developmental rather than tied to a compensation outcome. Second, regularity beats intensity: a brief, honest monthly check-in conversation produces more behavioural change than a comprehensive annual review. Third, forward-looking language — 'here is what I'd like to see you focus on this month' — is more effective than backward-looking language — 'here is what you did wrong last quarter'. And fourth, teaching managers the specific language of structured feedback — not just the concept — is a prerequisite for the whole system to work. The check-in system at Adobe was not just a process change; it was a language change.
