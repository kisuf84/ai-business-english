Module Overview

Welcome to Module 2: Finding and Qualifying Leads. Before any sale can happen, a salesperson must first identify the right people to speak to — and then quickly assess whether those people have the need, the authority, the budget, and the right timing to become genuine customers. This module focuses entirely on that critical early stage of the sales process: lead generation, prospecting, and qualification.

You will learn the vocabulary, grammar structures, and conversational techniques that top sales professionals use to find leads through inbound and outbound channels, to conduct compelling cold outreach, and to run structured qualification conversations that separate genuine prospects from tyre-kickers. Every section is built around real-world B2B and B2C sales scenarios and designed for B1–B2 English learners working in international or English-language sales environments.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Describe what a sales lead is, distinguish between inbound and outbound lead generation, and explain the key differences between a suspect, a prospect, and a qualified lead.
2
Use a structured qualification framework — including BANT (Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline) — to assess whether a prospect is worth pursuing, using precise professional English.
3
Conduct cold outreach conversations confidently — by phone, email, and LinkedIn — using appropriate greeting language, value propositions, and objection-handling techniques.
4
Form direct and indirect questions correctly to gather qualification information without sounding interrogative or making prospects feel uncomfortable.
5
Use modal verbs (can, could, would, might, should) with precision to soften requests, express possibility, and make polite sales enquiries in qualification conversations.
6
Form and use the core sales collocations for lead generation and qualification — including generate a lead, qualify a prospect, and move through the pipeline.
7
Write a professional cold email that generates responses and a qualification call summary memo that documents the prospect's key information clearly.
8
Listen to and understand cold calls, qualification conversations, sales team pipeline reviews, and account development discussions across different sales contexts.

📚 Word Bank

Key Vocabulary

21 essential terms for finding and qualifying leads in professional English.

Module Vocabulary
🎯
Lead
A person or organisation that has shown some level of interest in a product or service, or that fits the profile of a potential customer. A lead is the starting point of the sales process.
🔍
Prospect
A lead that has been assessed and found to have real potential — they have a relevant need, are the right type of company or person, and are potentially reachable. More qualified than a raw lead.
📋
BANT
A classic qualification framework: Budget (can they afford it?), Authority (are they the decision-maker?), Need (do they have a genuine problem your product solves?), Timeline (when do they plan to buy?).
📥
Inbound lead
A lead that comes to the company on their own initiative — by visiting the website, downloading content, attending a webinar, or contacting the sales team. Generally warmer and more qualified than outbound leads.
📤
Outbound prospecting
Proactively reaching out to potential customers who have not yet expressed interest — through cold calls, cold emails, LinkedIn messages, or in-person events. Requires more effort but allows precise targeting.
☎️
Cold call
An unsolicited phone call to a potential customer who has no prior relationship with the salesperson. The goal is to introduce the company, assess initial fit, and secure a follow-up conversation.
💼
Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)
A detailed description of the type of company or person that is the best fit for a product or service — based on industry, size, geography, pain points, and buying behaviour.
🔢
Lead scoring
A system that assigns a numerical value to each lead based on how closely they match the ICP and how they have engaged with the company's marketing. Higher scores indicate higher priority.
📊
Sales pipeline
A visual representation of all active deals at different stages of the sales process — from initial contact through to closed won or closed lost. Used by sales managers to forecast revenue.
🧩
Pain point
A specific problem or challenge that a potential customer is experiencing and that the salesperson's product or service can solve. Identifying pain points is central to effective qualification.
💡
Buying trigger
An event or circumstance that increases the likelihood of a prospect making a purchase decision — such as a company merger, a new regulation, a budget cycle reset, or a change in leadership.
🚪
Gatekeeper
A person — typically a receptionist, personal assistant, or junior team member — who controls access to a decision-maker and must be handled professionally and respectfully in outbound sales.
🎤
Value proposition
A clear, concise statement that explains how a product or service solves a customer's problem, what specific benefits it delivers, and why it is better or different from alternatives.
📈
MQL (Marketing Qualified Lead)
A lead that marketing has assessed as showing sufficient interest and fit to be passed to the sales team for follow-up. Typically triggered by content downloads, webinar attendance, or website activity.
✅
SQL (Sales Qualified Lead)
A lead that the sales team has personally assessed and confirmed as worth pursuing — they have verified need, authority, budget, and timing through direct conversation.
🔄
Lead nurturing
The process of building relationships with leads who are not yet ready to buy, through regular, relevant content and communication, so they think of the company when their buying intent increases.
📞
Discovery call
An early-stage call in the sales process where the salesperson's goal is to ask questions, listen carefully, and understand the prospect's situation, needs, challenges, and goals — before presenting a solution.
🏆
Account-based selling (ABS)
A targeted sales approach in which the team focuses on a specific list of high-value accounts — researching each deeply and coordinating personalised outreach across multiple contacts within the same company.
🤝
Decision-maker
The person within a prospect organisation who has the authority to approve a purchase. Identifying and reaching the decision-maker is a key goal of the qualification process.
📌
Pipeline stage
A defined step in the sales process — such as Prospecting, Qualified, Proposal Sent, Negotiation, or Closed. Each stage represents a different level of commitment from the prospect.
🗓️
Follow-up cadence
A planned sequence of follow-up communications — calls, emails, and messages — at set intervals after an initial contact, designed to maintain engagement and move the lead through the pipeline.

Text 1: The Science and Art of Lead Generation

How top sales teams find the right prospects efficiently

Lead generation is the engine of every sales organisation — without a steady flow of potential customers entering the pipeline, even the best salespeople cannot hit their targets. In recent years, the practice of lead generation has evolved dramatically, driven by digital tools, data analytics, and a growing understanding of buyer psychology. The best modern sales teams combine inbound and outbound strategies intelligently, using each approach where it performs best and building systems that generate a predictable flow of qualified opportunities rather than relying on individual effort and luck.

Inbound lead generation — attracting potential customers through content, search engine optimisation, social media, and paid advertising — has the significant advantage of producing warm leads: people who have already shown interest in the company's products or services. These leads have done their own research, understand the problem they want to solve, and are often much further along the buying journey than an outbound cold contact. The challenge with inbound is that the company cannot always control the quality of the leads it attracts — the same piece of content that brings in a perfectly qualified prospect also attracts students writing essays, competitors doing research, and people who are simply curious.

Outbound prospecting — proactively reaching out to potential customers who have not yet engaged with the company — requires more effort and typically produces lower initial response rates. However, it gives the sales team complete control over who they contact: by building precise Ideal Customer Profiles and carefully researching target accounts before making contact, the best outbound teams achieve remarkably high conversion rates from contact to qualified opportunity. The key to effective outbound is personalisation: a generic cold call or mass email campaign will produce near-zero results, but a well-researched, highly personalised message that references the prospect's specific situation and relevant pain points can achieve response rates of 20% or more.

Text 2: Lead Qualification — Separating Genuine Prospects from Tyre-Kickers

Why qualification skills are the most undervalued ability in sales

Not all leads are created equal — and the ability to quickly and accurately assess which leads are worth pursuing is one of the most valuable skills a salesperson can possess. In an organisation with limited sales resources, time spent on poorly qualified leads is time not spent on genuine opportunities. Qualification is, at its heart, a diagnostic process: the salesperson uses questions, listening, and research to determine whether a lead has the genuine combination of need, authority, budget, and timing that could lead to a deal. The most widely used framework for this is BANT — Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline — and despite being developed in the 1950s by IBM, it remains relevant because it addresses the four fundamental questions that determine whether a sale is possible.

The language of qualification is critically important. An aggressive or poorly sequenced qualification conversation — one that leads with 'Do you have the budget for this?' before establishing rapport or demonstrating value — will destroy trust immediately and cause the prospect to disengage. The best qualifiers ask open questions designed to understand the prospect's situation before they begin assessing fit: 'What does your current process look like?', 'What are the main challenges you face in this area?', 'What has prompted you to look at solutions now?' These questions gather qualification information naturally, as part of a genuine consultative conversation, rather than as a checklist interrogation.

Many experienced salespeople argue that need is the most important element of BANT to establish first, because without a genuine problem to solve, no budget or authority will create a purchase. Once need is confirmed, the conversation naturally expands to explore the other three dimensions. It is also important to recognise that qualification is not a one-time event — it is an ongoing assessment that continues throughout the sales process. A prospect who was qualified at the initial call may become disqualified later if the budget is cut, the decision-maker changes, or the project is deprioritised. The best salespeople continuously update their qualification assessments and ruthlessly remove dead leads from their pipeline rather than carrying them out of false hope.

🔍 Grammar Review

Grammar Reference

Detailed reference notes covering all three grammar points in this module.

Grammar Reference 1: Question Forms in Qualification Conversations

Why question forms matter in qualification

Qualification is fundamentally a questioning process. The way you ask questions determines whether the prospect feels interrogated or understood. Skilled salespeople use a mix of open, indirect, and reflective questions to gather information naturally while building trust.

Open questions (Wh- questions)

Begin with What, Who, How, Where, When, or Why. These invite detailed answers and are essential for discovery and qualification. They signal genuine curiosity.

  • What does your current setup look like?
  • What are the main challenges you are facing in this area?
  • How does your team typically make decisions on software investments?

Indirect questions — softer and more professional

Indirect questions use a statement-like structure and sound less demanding. Extremely useful when asking about sensitive topics like budget or internal politics.

  • Could you tell me a bit more about how decisions like this are typically made?
  • I'd be interested to understand what your timeline looks like.
  • It would be helpful to know what your key priorities are for this quarter.

Clarifying and summarising questions

These demonstrate active listening and help confirm your understanding — critical for building trust and avoiding misqualification.

  • Just to make sure I've understood correctly, are you saying that...?
  • So if I've got this right, the main issue is...?
  • Could I summarise what you've told me and check I haven't missed anything?
Grammar Reference 2: Modal Verbs in Professional Sales Conversations

Why modals are essential in sales English

Modal verbs allow salespeople to make requests, offers, suggestions, and recommendations with precisely the right degree of softness and professionalism. The wrong modal choice can make you sound either too aggressive or too uncertain.

Could and Would — polite requests and offers

Use could for polite requests and would for offers or conditional suggestions. Both are softer and more professional than will or can in formal B2B conversations.

  • Could you help me understand what your current setup looks like?
  • Would it be useful if I sent you a case study from a similar company?
  • Could we schedule a brief call for next week to continue this conversation?

Might, May, Could — expressing possibility

Use these to express what is possible without over-committing. Essential when discussing whether a solution might be a fit, or when the outcome is uncertain.

  • Based on what you've described, our platform might be worth exploring.
  • This could be a strong fit for your team, depending on a few details.
  • There may be a way to structure the pricing to suit your Q1 budget.

Should — recommendations

Use should to make professional recommendations in qualification and discovery conversations, especially about timelines and next steps.

  • If your project needs to be live by Q1, you should start the evaluation process in October.
  • You should also involve your IT team early — they will want to review the integration.
  • We should probably schedule a technical call before moving to a proposal.
Grammar Reference 3: Core Sales Collocations for Lead Generation

Why collocations matter in sales English

In professional sales English, specific verb-noun pairs are fixed. Using the wrong combination sounds unnatural and reduces your credibility with English-speaking customers and colleagues. These must be practised until they become automatic.

Generate, qualify, and manage — the core trio

These three verbs are the most important in lead generation and qualification language:

  • generate → a lead, a pipeline, interest, demand, revenue
  • qualify → a lead, a prospect, an opportunity
  • manage → a pipeline, a relationship, expectations, objections

Key verb + noun pairs for sales activities

These pairings are used every day in sales teams:

  • run → a campaign, a demo, a discovery call, a process
  • build → rapport, a relationship, a pipeline, trust
  • schedule → a call, a demo, a follow-up, a meeting
  • close → a deal, a sale, the loop
  • handle → an objection, a concern, a rejection

Describing prospect behaviour and signals

These expressions describe what prospects do and feel:

  • show → interest, buying signals, engagement, hesitation
  • raise → an objection, a concern, a question
  • express → interest, a need, a concern
  • request → a demo, more information, a proposal

Case Study 1
HubSpot: Building a Lead Generation Machine Through Content Marketing
USA · SaaS / CRM · 2006–Present · Inbound Marketing Excellence · How HubSpot invented a new category — inbound marketing — and used it to generate millions of qualified leads
Background

HubSpot was founded in 2006 by Brian Halligan and Dharmesh Shah, who noticed that traditional outbound marketing — cold calls, unsolicited emails, and interruptive advertising — was becoming less effective as buyers gained more control over their information consumption. Rather than fighting this trend, HubSpot decided to build its entire business model around the opposite approach: inbound marketing, which involves attracting potential customers by creating valuable content that answers their questions and solves their problems. The company would generate leads not by interrupting people but by being genuinely helpful — so that buyers found HubSpot when they were looking for answers.

The Lead Generation Engine

HubSpot's approach to lead generation was remarkable in its consistency and scale. The company produced an enormous volume of free educational content — blog posts, e-books, webinars, templates, and free tools — all designed to rank highly on search engines and address the real questions that marketing and sales professionals were asking. When a visitor downloaded a guide or signed up for a webinar, they provided their contact information, creating a lead that could be nurtured through a sequence of targeted emails. Over time, HubSpot built an audience of millions of marketing professionals who trusted the brand long before they were ready to become paying customers. By the time a lead spoke to a HubSpot salesperson, they already knew the company, respected its expertise, and had often been using free tools for months.

Qualification and Conversion

Because HubSpot generated so many leads, the company needed sophisticated qualification systems to prioritise the sales team's time. They developed one of the most refined lead scoring models in the SaaS industry, assigning scores based on company size, industry, the specific content a lead had consumed, and their website activity. Leads that matched the ideal customer profile and had demonstrated high engagement were automatically routed to account executives for personalised outreach. The result was a highly efficient sales operation in which salespeople spent most of their time with leads that were already warm, informed, and pre-qualified — dramatically reducing the cost of customer acquisition and increasing conversion rates.

Case Study 2
Salesforce's Account-Based Selling Transformation
USA · Enterprise SaaS · 2000s–Present · ABS & Outbound Excellence · How Salesforce developed a pioneering outbound sales engine that became the model for B2B technology sales worldwide
Background

In the early 2000s, Salesforce was growing rapidly but recognised that it needed a more systematic, scalable approach to finding and qualifying enterprise customers. Under the influence of Aaron Ross — whose work was later published in the book Predictable Revenue — Salesforce pioneered a model that separated the roles of prospecting and closing into distinct functions. Rather than having account executives both find and close their own deals, Salesforce introduced a dedicated layer of Sales Development Representatives (SDRs) whose sole job was to identify and qualify leads, conduct initial outreach, and hand off qualified opportunities to senior account executives for closing.

The SDR Model and Qualification System

The Salesforce SDR model was built on a rigorous qualification process. SDRs were trained to identify target accounts using precise Ideal Customer Profile criteria — including company size, industry, existing technology stack, and growth trajectory. Before making any contact, SDRs would research each account thoroughly, identifying the likely decision-makers, understanding the company's recent news and strategic challenges, and crafting personalised outreach that referenced the prospect's specific situation. Qualification conversations were structured around the BANT framework, with a particular emphasis on identifying genuine pain and confirming that the contact had or could access the authority to evaluate and purchase a solution. Only deals that met specific qualification criteria — confirmed need, identified authority, budget indication, and realistic timeline — would be moved to an account executive.

Results and Legacy

The results of this model were transformative. By separating prospecting from closing, Salesforce dramatically increased the efficiency of both functions: SDRs became extremely good at finding and qualifying leads quickly, while account executives could focus all their time on deals that were genuinely worth pursuing. The model was so successful that it has been widely adopted across the SaaS industry — the SDR function is now a standard entry point for sales careers in technology companies globally. The Salesforce model demonstrated that systematic, process-driven outbound prospecting — when combined with rigorous qualification — can be a predictable, scalable source of revenue that does not rely on the exceptional individual performance of a few star salespeople.
