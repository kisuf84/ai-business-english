Module Overview

Welcome to Module 1: Sales Process and Customer Journey. Sales is one of the most language-intensive professions there is. Every interaction with a prospect or customer requires precision, empathy, and adaptability — whether you are cold-calling a new lead, running a discovery call to understand a prospect's needs, presenting a proposal, handling objections, negotiating terms, or nurturing a long-term customer relationship.

This module introduces the complete sales process in English, from the first contact with a potential customer through to closing the deal and managing the post-sale relationship. You will learn the professional vocabulary of sales, the grammar structures used in sales conversations and written communications, and the specific language patterns that distinguish effective sales professionals from average ones. Each section builds on real-world sales scenarios designed for B1–B2 English learners working in international or English-language sales environments.

By the end of this module, you will be able to describe and discuss the full sales cycle in professional English, conduct discovery conversations, handle objections confidently, write professional sales emails and proposals, and apply the grammar structures most commonly used in sales communication.

Text 1: The Sales Process — From Prospecting to Closing

Paragraph 1 — What Is the Sales Process?

The sales process is a structured sequence of stages that a salesperson moves a prospect through from initial contact to completed purchase. While every industry and organisation has its own variation, most professional sales processes share a common architecture. They begin with prospecting — identifying potential customers who have the need, authority, and budget to buy — and move through qualification, discovery, proposal, negotiation, and finally closing. Each stage has its own objectives, language patterns, and success criteria, and moving a prospect from one stage to the next requires both product knowledge and communication skill.

Understanding the sales process is valuable not just for salespeople but for anyone who works with customers, because the underlying dynamic — identifying a need, presenting a solution, handling concerns, and securing a commitment — appears in almost every professional relationship. A project manager asking for budget approval, a consultant recommending a strategy, or an employee presenting a new idea to their manager is, in a structural sense, going through a sales process. The language and techniques of professional sales are therefore applicable far beyond a traditional sales role.

Paragraph 2 — Discovery and the Art of Asking Questions

Of all the stages in the sales process, the discovery stage is arguably the most important and the most frequently undervalued. Discovery is the process of deeply understanding a prospect's situation, challenges, priorities, and objectives before presenting any solution. The most common error made by inexperienced salespeople is to skip discovery and move immediately to presenting the product or service. This approach almost always fails, because a solution presented without a deep understanding of the customer's specific needs will either be irrelevant or appear generic. The customer's response — "we are not really looking for that right now" — is almost always a signal that the salesperson has not yet established what the customer is actually looking for.

Effective discovery relies on asking the right questions — specifically, open questions that invite the prospect to share information, and probing questions that dig deeper into the answers given. Open questions typically begin with "what," "how," "tell me about," or "describe," and they cannot be answered with a simple yes or no. Probing questions follow up on what the prospect has shared: "Can you tell me more about that?" or "What impact has that had on your team?" The salesperson's role in discovery is primarily to listen, not to talk. Research consistently shows that the most effective salespeople spend approximately 60% of a discovery call listening and only 40% speaking.

Paragraph 3 — Closing and Objection Handling

The closing stage is when the salesperson asks the prospect to make a commitment — to proceed with a purchase, to sign a contract, or to take a defined next step. Many salespeople are uncomfortable with this stage because it involves the risk of rejection, and they therefore delay asking for the commitment, hoping the prospect will volunteer it themselves. In practice, this rarely happens: most prospects need to be asked explicitly before they will make a decision. Effective closing language is direct but not aggressive: "Based on what we have discussed, I think this is the right solution for your needs. Would you like to move forward?" or "What would you need to see to be confident moving ahead this month?"

Closely related to closing is objection handling — the process of addressing the concerns a prospect raises that prevent them from proceeding. Objections are not rejections; they are requests for more information or reassurance. The most effective structure for handling an objection has three steps: acknowledge the concern genuinely, respond with relevant information or evidence, and confirm that the concern has been addressed. For example: "I completely understand why cost is a concern at this stage [acknowledge]. What I can tell you is that our customers typically recoup their investment within six months through the efficiency savings the platform delivers [respond]. Does that help address the concern, or is there something more specific you would like me to cover? [confirm]"

Text 2: The Customer Journey — Understanding How Buyers Buy

Paragraph 1 — The Customer Journey vs the Sales Process

The customer journey and the sales process describe the same commercial relationship from two different perspectives. The sales process describes what the salesperson does. The customer journey describes what the buyer experiences. Understanding both perspectives is essential for effective sales, because a salesperson who only knows their own process without understanding the buyer's experience will often apply pressure at the wrong moment, skip stages that the buyer needs, or miss signals that the buyer is ready to move forward.

The customer journey typically has five stages: Awareness (the customer becomes aware that they have a problem or need), Consideration (the customer actively researches solutions), Decision (the customer evaluates specific options and makes a choice), Purchase (the transaction is completed), and Post-purchase (the customer forms a view of the experience and decides whether to remain a customer and whether to recommend the brand). A skilled salesperson maps their activities to the buyer's current journey stage: in the awareness stage, the salesperson provides education and builds credibility; in consideration, they demonstrate relevance and differentiate their solution; in decision, they manage objections and close.

Paragraph 2 — The Role of Trust in the Customer Journey

One of the most consistent findings in sales research is that trust is the single most important factor in a customer's decision to buy, particularly in B2B contexts and high-value transactions. Trust is not built through product features or price; it is built through consistent behaviour over time — showing up when promised, delivering what was committed to, being honest about limitations, and demonstrating genuine interest in the customer's success rather than just in closing the deal. The salesperson who is perceived as a trusted advisor rather than a vendor will consistently outsell the technically superior product sold by someone who has not built the same depth of relationship.

Trust is also easily destroyed. A single instance of overpromising and underdelivering, a failure to follow up on a commitment, or a sense that the salesperson is prioritising their commission over the customer's needs can erase months of relationship-building. The language of trust-based selling is characterised by honesty and specificity: acknowledging when the product is not the right fit, being precise about timelines and capabilities rather than vague, and following through consistently on every small commitment made during the sales process.

Paragraph 3 — Post-Sale: Where Long-Term Value Is Created

The sale is not the end of the customer journey; it is the beginning of the most commercially important part of it. Research consistently shows that it costs five to seven times more to acquire a new customer than to retain an existing one, and that existing customers are significantly more likely to buy additional products and services than new prospects. The post-sale relationship — managed through account management, regular check-ins, proactive problem-solving, and genuine interest in the customer's ongoing success — is therefore where the majority of long-term commercial value is created.

The language of post-sale account management is distinct from the language of the initial sales process. It is less persuasive and more collaborative; less focused on what the product can do and more focused on whether it is doing what the customer needs. Key practices include regular review meetings at which performance data is presented and discussed, proactive identification of new needs or challenges before the customer raises them, and the management of any issues or complaints with the same speed and care applied to the original sale. The salesperson who treats the close as the end of their responsibility has missed the most valuable part of the customer relationship.

Open & Probing Questions in Discovery
Why question type matters in sales discovery
The discovery call is the most important stage of the sales process, and the quality of your questions determines the quality of the information you receive. Open questions invite broad, detailed responses. Probing questions dig deeper into what the prospect has shared. Closed questions (yes/no) shut down conversation and should be minimised in discovery. The best salespeople spend 60% of a discovery call listening, not talking.

Hypothetical questionIf / Imagine / What would happen ifIf you could solve that problem, what would that mean for the business?

Good morning everyone. I want to start today with a statement that surprises many salespeople when they first hear it: the most important skill in sales is not persuasion. It is listening. And the best tool for creating the conditions where great listening is possible is the question.

The discovery call is the stage of the sales process where you learn everything you need to know about a prospect before presenting your solution. Done well, it is a conversation in which the prospect does most of the talking and you do most of the listening. Done badly, it is a monologue in which the salesperson talks about their product while the prospect waits politely to say no.

Here is why discovery matters so much. When you present a solution before you understand the problem, you are guessing. Your guess might be right. It is more likely to be slightly wrong in ways that matter to the specific prospect you are speaking to. And the prospect will know immediately that you are presenting a generic solution rather than one tailored to their specific situation. They will feel like a number, not a person. And people do not buy from salespeople who treat them like numbers.

The questions you ask in discovery do three things. First, they gather information you need to tailor your proposal. Second, they signal to the prospect that you are genuinely interested in their situation rather than just trying to close a deal. Third, and most importantly, they help the prospect understand their own problem more clearly. Many prospects have a vague sense that something is not working. A good discovery conversation helps them articulate exactly what is wrong, in their own words. And when a prospect has articulated their problem clearly, they are far more motivated to find a solution. You have not just learnt about their problem. You have helped them feel it more acutely.

The questions that achieve this are open questions: questions that invite detail and reflection. What does your current process look like? How does that affect your team? What would need to be different for this to feel like a success? These questions cannot be answered with yes or no. They require the prospect to think and share. And while they are thinking and sharing, you are building trust and gathering the information you need to win the deal.

Good afternoon. Today I want to talk about objections. Because in my experience, the moment a prospect raises an objection is the moment that separates the effective salespeople from the ineffective ones. Effective salespeople welcome objections. They know that an objection is not a rejection. It is a question. It is the prospect saying: I am still interested, but I have a concern. And when someone is still interested, you have an opportunity.

The three most common objections you will encounter in B2B sales are: the price objection, which sounds like "this is more expensive than we expected" or "we do not have budget for this right now"; the timing objection, which sounds like "this is not the right time for us" or "we are not looking to make a change this quarter"; and the competitor objection, which sounds like "we are already using a competitor product" or "we have seen something similar elsewhere."

Each of these objections has a specific best-practice response. Let me walk you through a structure that works for all of them. It has three steps: acknowledge, respond, and confirm.

Step one: acknowledge. Do not argue. Do not immediately counter the objection. Instead, validate the concern. "I completely understand why budget is a concern at this stage." "That makes total sense given where you are in the financial year." When a prospect feels heard and understood, they become more open to hearing your response.

Step two: respond. Now you address the objection with a specific, relevant response. For a price objection: provide data on return on investment. "Most of our customers recoup their investment within six months through the time savings alone." For a timing objection: explore the real cost of waiting. "I understand, but I would be interested to understand what the cost of maintaining the current situation is for the next six months." For a competitor objection: ask questions rather than attacking the competitor. "That is interesting. What has your experience been with them so far, and what is working well or less well?"

Step three: confirm. Check that the concern has been addressed. "Does that help address the concern, or is there something more specific you would like me to clarify?" This closes the loop and either moves the conversation forward or surfaces a deeper concern you can then address.

The most important thing to remember is this: an objection is not the end of the conversation. It is the continuation of it.

Good evening. I want to talk about something that makes a surprisingly large number of salespeople uncomfortable: asking for the close. And I want to explain why that discomfort exists, what it costs you commercially, and what you can do about it.

The reluctance to close is rooted in the fear of rejection. Asking explicitly for a commitment exposes the salesperson to the risk of a direct no. And a direct no, particularly at the end of a long sales process, feels personal. It can feel like a rejection of you, not just of your product. This emotional response is completely understandable. It is also, commercially speaking, very expensive.

Here is what the research shows. In a study of over 10,000 sales conversations, the most significant predictor of whether a deal was won or lost was not the quality of the product, the price, or the presentation. It was whether the salesperson explicitly asked for the next commitment at the end of the conversation. Deals where the salesperson asked for a specific next step closed at a rate of 34%. Deals where the salesperson ended the meeting with a vague statement like "let me know when you are ready" closed at a rate of 11%. The difference was the explicit ask.

So why do salespeople avoid closing? Three reasons. First, they do not want to seem pushy or aggressive. Second, they hope the prospect will volunteer a commitment without being asked. Third, they are genuinely uncertain whether the prospect is ready to move forward. All three of these concerns can be addressed with the right language.

The most effective closing language is not aggressive. It is direct, collaborative, and respectful of the prospect's autonomy. There are three types of closing question worth knowing. The assumptive close: "Based on what we have discussed, it sounds like this would be a good fit. What would you need to see to move forward this month?" The alternative close: "Would it work better to start with the pilot programme first, or would you prefer to go directly to the full implementation?" The direct close: "Is there anything that would prevent you from making a decision today?"

None of these is pushy. All of them respect the prospect's right to say no. But all of them create the conditions for a yes — which, without the explicit ask, will almost never happen on its own.

Case Study 1: Vertex Solutions — How a Discovery-First Approach Doubled the Close Rate

Sales Process · Discovery · Consultative Selling · Pipeline Management

Background

Vertex Solutions is a UK B2B software company selling project management tools to professional services firms. In early 2022, the sales team of eight was consistently missing its quarterly revenue targets despite a healthy volume of product demos. The pipeline was full: approximately 80 active opportunities at any given time. But the close rate — the percentage of those opportunities that converted to a paying customer — was just 14%, well below the industry benchmark of 22–25% for comparable SaaS products.

The Head of Sales commissioned an audit of the team's call recordings. The findings were clear and consistent: in 73% of cases, the salesperson had moved directly from an initial qualification call to a product demonstration without conducting any meaningful discovery. The demos were polished and technically impressive, but they presented the product's features generically rather than connecting them specifically to the prospect's stated challenges. The most common outcome was the prospect saying: "This looks interesting, but I am not sure it is quite what we need." This was a classic signal that the salesperson had not yet established what the prospect actually needed.

The Intervention & Results

The Head of Sales introduced a mandatory discovery call protocol that every salesperson had to follow before any demo could be booked. The protocol required the salesperson to identify and document four things: the prospect's current process and its specific pain points, the business impact of those pain points in measurable terms (time, cost, or revenue), the decision-making process and the key stakeholders involved, and the prospect's timeline and urgency for making a change. Only when all four elements were documented could the opportunity advance to the demo stage.

The results were significant. In the first quarter after the protocol was introduced, the number of demos booked fell by 30% — because salespeople were now disqualifying prospects who were not ready or not a good fit, rather than booking demos with everyone in the pipeline. But the close rate climbed from 14% to 28% — double the previous rate. Total revenue in the quarter was 18% higher than in the same quarter the previous year, despite fewer demos. The win-rate improvement was entirely attributable to better discovery: salespeople were now demonstrating the product specifically against the challenges the prospect had described, rather than presenting generic features. The lesson the team drew: a shorter, better-qualified pipeline is always more valuable than a long, unfocused one.

Case Study 2: Northstar Recruitment — Rebuilding a Client Relationship After a Poor Post-Sale Experience

Customer Journey · Account Management · Trust · Objection Handling

Background

Northstar Recruitment is a specialist executive search firm operating across the UK technology sector. In 2021, Northstar won a significant retained search contract with a fast-growing fintech company called PulsePay to find four senior engineers. The salesperson who won the deal was highly effective in the sales process — she had conducted excellent discovery, presented a compelling proposal, and negotiated commercially attractive terms. But immediately after the contract was signed, she moved on to the next opportunity and handed the delivery to a colleague with a one-paragraph handover note.

The delivery did not go well. The first candidate presented was rejected by PulsePay as not meeting the brief. The second search produced two candidates who both withdrew from the process. By the third month of the engagement, PulsePay had lost confidence in Northstar and escalated its concerns to the managing director. The account was at serious risk of cancellation, with the client threatening to move the remaining two searches to a competitor. The situation illustrated a problem common in service businesses: the salesperson who wins the deal often does not manage the delivery, and the post-sale experience — the part of the customer journey that determines whether a client renews, refers, and expands — had been treated as someone else's responsibility.

Recovery & Outcome

The managing director intervened personally. She called the PulsePay HR director and opened the conversation not by defending Northstar's performance but by acknowledging the problem directly: "I understand we have not delivered the standard of service you should expect from us, and I want to take personal responsibility for getting this back on track." She then scheduled a face-to-face meeting within 48 hours to conduct what was effectively a new discovery session: listening to exactly what PulsePay needed from the remaining two searches, what had gone wrong in their view, and what success would look like.

Two specific changes were made. First, the original salesperson was brought back into the account and given joint accountability with the delivery team for the remaining searches. Second, a weekly update call was established with the HR director to report on progress before being asked. Both remaining searches were successfully completed within six weeks. PulsePay renewed and expanded their Northstar relationship the following year, awarding an additional three-search contract. The managing director's reflection: "We almost lost a client that should have been a reference account. The problem was not our capability — it was that we treated the sale as the end of the job rather than the beginning of the relationship." The case illustrated one of the most important principles in sales: the post-sale experience determines whether a single transaction becomes a long-term commercial relationship.
