Module Overview
B1–B2

Welcome to Module 3: Branding and Positioning Language. This module develops the professional English you need to engage confidently with the concepts and communications of branding. A strong brand is far more than a logo or a colour palette — it is a coherent set of values, promises, and associations that lives in the minds of consumers. You will learn to talk about brand identity, brand voice, positioning strategy, and brand equity using the precise language that marketing and communications professionals use every day. Whether you are writing a brand brief, presenting a positioning statement to a client, or leading a brand review, this module gives you the vocabulary and grammatical tools to do it with authority.

🎯 Learning Outcomes

  • Identify and correctly use key branding and positioning vocabulary in professional contexts
  • Read and interpret texts about brand identity, brand equity, and positioning strategy
  • Discuss brand values, brand architecture, and competitive positioning with confidence in English
  • Use verb patterns after reporting and presenting verbs accurately in brand strategy presentations
  • Apply noun-preposition collocations common in branding and positioning language
  • Use purpose, result, and reason clauses to explain strategic brand decisions in reports and presentations
  • Listen to and comprehend English in brand workshops, strategy presentations, and client briefs
  • Write a brand positioning statement and a brand identity brief

📣

🎭

Text 1: Brand Identity — More Than a Logo

In popular usage, the word 'brand' is often reduced to its most visible elements — the logo, the colour palette, the typeface. While these visual components are undoubtedly important, they represent only the surface layer of what a brand truly is. At its deepest level, a brand is a relationship between an organisation and the people it serves — a set of associations, promises, and expectations that accumulate over time through every interaction a consumer has with the company and its products. Brand identity is the deliberate, managed expression of this relationship: the coherent system of visual, verbal, and behavioural signals through which a company communicates who it is, what it stands for, and why it matters.

The most enduring brands are built on a clear and authentic foundation of brand values — the core principles that guide every decision the organisation makes, from product development to customer service to the tone of its advertising. Brand values should not be aspirational fiction; they must reflect the genuine character of the organisation. When consumers sense a mismatch between what a brand claims to value and how it actually behaves, the result is a loss of trust that can be extremely difficult to recover. Brand voice — the personality and style of language the brand uses consistently across all channels — is another critical dimension of identity. A brand that speaks confidently and directly on one platform but sounds formal and distant on another creates confusion and signals a lack of coherent strategy.

The brand promise sits at the heart of identity. It is the commitment the brand makes to its customers — the experience or benefit they can reliably expect to receive every time they interact with the brand. Apple's implicit promise, for example, is that its products will be beautifully designed, intuitive to use, and seamlessly integrated with each other. Nike's promise is that it will inspire and equip athletes of all levels to push beyond their perceived limits. When a brand consistently delivers on its promise, it builds what marketers call brand equity — the additional value that the brand name itself adds to the product, enabling the company to charge a premium price and retain customers even in the face of competitive pressure. When it fails to deliver, equity erodes and the brand must work harder to justify consumer preference.

Text 2: Positioning Strategy — Finding and Owning Your Space in the Market

Positioning is one of the most consequential decisions in marketing strategy. At its simplest, positioning answers a fundamental question: in the minds of our target consumers, what unique and valuable place do we want our brand to occupy — and why should they believe us? The positioning statement is the internal strategic tool that captures this answer in a compact, actionable form. A well-crafted positioning statement defines the target audience, the frame of reference (the category or context in which the brand competes), the unique benefit the brand offers, and the reason to believe — the evidence or proof point that makes the claim credible. It is not an advertising tagline; it is a strategic compass that guides every subsequent marketing decision, from product development to campaign tone.

The concept of differentiation is inseparable from positioning. A brand that cannot clearly articulate what makes it different from its competitors has no positioning at all — it simply occupies whatever space the market allocates to it by default. Genuine differentiation can be achieved in many ways: through a product or functional advantage (the brand does something competitors cannot), through a pricing strategy (the brand offers the best value or the most exclusive premium), through the brand's associations and personality (consumers prefer it because of who it makes them feel they are), or through the brand's heritage and story (consumers trust it because of where it comes from and what it represents). The brand perception map is a powerful tool for visualising competitive positioning: by plotting brands along two axes — for example, 'traditional versus modern' and 'affordable versus premium' — marketers can identify white spaces in the market where consumer needs are unmet and brand opportunities exist.

Repositioning — deliberately shifting a brand's position in the minds of consumers — is one of the most challenging tasks in marketing. It requires not only changing communications but often fundamentally transforming the product, the customer experience, and the organisational culture behind the brand. Old Spice's transformation from a brand associated with elderly men to one celebrated by younger consumers required a radical rethinking of every element of the brand expression. Burberry's evolution from a brand with a controversial association with a specific British subculture to one of the world's most recognised luxury fashion houses required sustained investment in product quality, distribution, celebrity association, and brand communication over more than a decade. These examples illustrate that successful repositioning is rarely quick or cheap — but when executed well, it can generate transformative commercial results.

📌 Grammar Point 1: Verb Patterns in Brand Strategy Presentations

In branding and marketing meetings, professionals regularly use verbs that report, recommend, or describe decisions. Each of these verbs is followed by a specific grammatical pattern. Using the wrong pattern is a very common error.

VerbPatternExample
suggest / recommend / propose / consider / mention / admit / avoidverb + -ing"She suggested revising the positioning statement."
decide / agree / refuse / advise someone / want / plan / manageverb + to-infinitive"They decided to launch a full rebrand."
advise / tell / persuade / encourage / ask / remindverb + object + to-infinitive"The consultant advised the board to consider the risk."
confirm / deny / admit / claim / state / argueverb + that-clause"The research confirmed that there was a perception gap."
✓ "The agency recommended conducting a brand audit." (recommend + -ing)
✓ "The director refused to adopt the new brand voice." (refuse + to-infinitive)
✗ "The agency recommended to conduct a brand audit." — incorrect
✗ "The director refused adopting the new voice." — incorrect

Note: Some verbs can follow both patterns with a change in meaning: stop to do (pause in order to do) vs stop doing (cease). In brand presentation contexts, be especially careful with suggest, recommend, and propose — these always take -ing, never to-infinitive.

📌 Grammar Point 2: Noun-Preposition Collocations in Branding Language

Many key branding nouns are followed by a specific preposition. These are fixed collocations — the preposition cannot be changed without making the phrase incorrect or unnatural. Learning these as chunks will help you write and speak more fluently.

Noun + PrepositionExample
awareness of"There is growing awareness of the brand among young consumers."
commitment to"The brand has a strong commitment to sustainability."
confidence in"The board expressed confidence in the new strategy."
differentiation from"The brand achieved clear differentiation from its competitors."
loyalty among (a group)"The brand built strong loyalty among younger consumers."
partnership with"The partnership with the tech company opened new markets."
perception of"The perception of the brand has changed significantly."
reputation for"The brand has a reputation for outstanding quality."
agreement among (a group)"There was broad agreement among the team on the new direction."
lack of"A lack of consistency in communications damaged the brand."
Memory tip: When you learn a new branding noun, always learn the preposition that follows it as part of the same phrase. Keeping a personal "collocation notebook" where you record noun + preposition pairs in example sentences is one of the most effective vocabulary learning strategies.

📌 Grammar Point 3: Purpose, Result, and Reason Clauses in Brand Strategy

When explaining brand decisions, strategies, and outcomes, purpose, result, and reason clauses are essential. Using them accurately and naturally signals fluency and strategic thinking.

FunctionConnectorNotes & Example
Purpose (aim of an action)in order to / so as to + infinitiveMore formal. "The brand repositioned in order to attract younger consumers."
Purpose (aim of an action)to + infinitiveNeutral. "The team created personas to guide the campaign."
Purpose (subject may differ)so that + subject + verb"The brief was simplified so that all teams could apply it consistently."
Result (consequence of a quality)so + adjective/adverb + that"The brand was so well-known that it needed no advertising."
Reason (cause of a situation)because + subject + verb"Equity fell because the product failed to match the promise."
Reason (cause — noun phrase)because of / due to / owing to + noun"Sales grew because of the successful repositioning."
Purpose: "The company redesigned its logo in order to signal its transformation." (formal)
Purpose: "The team used a perception map so that everyone could see the competitive landscape." (different subjects)
Result: "The campaign was so effective that brand awareness doubled in six months."
Reason: "The brand lost market share because it failed to differentiate itself."
Reason: "The rebranding was necessary because of a serious reputation crisis."

Key distinction — in order to vs so that: Use in order to / so as to when the subject of the purpose clause is the same as the main clause. Use so that when the subjects are different, or when the purpose clause contains a modal verb (can, could, will, would).

Host: Let's start with the basics. How would you define brand equity?

Guest: At the simplest level, brand equity is the extra value that a brand name adds to a product or service beyond its functional attributes. Think about it this way: you can buy a white T-shirt from a no-name manufacturer for five pounds. You can also buy an essentially identical white T-shirt from a luxury brand for two hundred and fifty pounds. The cotton is the same. The stitching is similar. The only real difference is the label — and what that label means in the minds of consumers. That meaning — that set of associations, feelings, and trust — is brand equity.

Host: That's a clear example. What are the main components of brand equity?

Guest: Most frameworks identify four key components. First, brand awareness — do people know you exist? Can they recall your name or recognise your logo? This is the most basic level. You can't have equity if no one has heard of you. Second, perceived quality — do consumers believe your product or service is better, more reliable, or more trustworthy than alternatives? This doesn't have to be based on objective testing. It's about perception. Third, brand associations — what concepts, feelings, people, or experiences do consumers connect with your brand? These associations give the brand its personality and meaning. And fourth, brand loyalty — do consumers come back? Do they choose you even when a cheaper or more convenient option is available? Loyalty is the commercial expression of equity. It translates directly into revenue.

Host: Which of these do you think is most important?

Guest: That's a great question, and the honest answer is that they're interdependent — you can't really have strong equity in one dimension without some level of strength in the others. But if I had to choose, I'd say brand loyalty is the most commercially valuable component because it directly reduces your acquisition costs. Keeping an existing customer is far cheaper than finding a new one. And loyal customers are also your best marketing channel — they recommend you to their friends, and that word-of-mouth is more credible than anything you could buy.

Host: How do you build brand equity?

Guest: Consistently. That's really the key word. Brand equity is built over time through consistent delivery on the brand promise — every product, every interaction, every piece of communication reinforcing the same values and meaning. You don't build equity with a single brilliant campaign. You build it through thousands of small, consistent acts that say: this is who we are, and we mean it.

Host: David, that's been incredibly insightful. Thank you.

Guest: My pleasure, Jessica.

Good afternoon, everyone. Today we're continuing our series on strategic brand management, and our topic is brand architecture — the structure that organises how a company's brands relate to each other. This is a topic that sounds technical but is actually deeply strategic, because the architecture decisions a company makes have enormous implications for investment efficiency, consumer clarity, and long-term brand equity.

Let me start by setting out the landscape. When a company has multiple products, services, or businesses, it faces a fundamental question: how do we organise them? Do we give them all the same brand name? Do we give each one a completely separate identity? Or do we do something in between? The answer determines the brand architecture.

There are two fundamental strategies at opposite ends of the spectrum. The first is called the 'branded house' approach. In a branded house, everything — every product, every service, every new initiative — operates under a single master brand. Apple is the classic example. Whether you're buying an iPhone, a MacBook, an Apple Watch, or a subscription to Apple Music, you are always buying Apple. The master brand is the hero. Every product launch builds the master brand, and the master brand in turn endorses every product. The strength of this approach is efficiency and coherence. You are building one brand, not many. Every marketing investment contributes to the same equity pool. The weakness is risk — if one product fails dramatically or generates bad press, the entire brand can be damaged.

The second approach is called the 'house of brands'. Here, the parent company owns multiple independent brands, each with its own name, identity, and positioning. The parent company is often invisible to consumers. Procter and Gamble is a household products company that owns brands including Tide, Pampers, Gillette, and Head & Shoulders — but most consumers have no idea these brands share a parent. The strength of the house of brands approach is flexibility and risk isolation. You can position each brand differently, target different segments, and if one brand gets into trouble, the others are protected. The weakness is cost — you are effectively building and maintaining multiple separate brands, which requires substantially more investment.

Most large companies sit somewhere between these two poles — using what academics call an 'endorsed brand' model, where sub-brands exist with their own identities but carry the visible endorsement of a parent brand. Kellogg's is a good example: Special K, Cornflakes, and Rice Krispies all have distinct personalities, but they all carry the Kellogg's name as a stamp of quality and trust.

The key principle in choosing an architecture is consumer logic: what structure will be clearest and most useful to the people you are trying to reach? Architecture should serve the consumer, not the organisational chart.

Case Study 1: Old Spice — From Grandfather's Aftershave to Cultural Icon

By the mid-2000s, Old Spice faced a brand positioning problem that many heritage brands recognise: it was perceived as a product for elderly men — specifically, for grandfathers. In consumer perception studies, the brand consistently attracted words like 'old-fashioned', 'dated', and 'my dad's brand'. Despite being a functionally sound product with strong distribution and a loyal (if ageing) customer base, Old Spice's relevance among men under 35 was negligible. The brand had become a victim of its own history: decades of consistent communication with one generation had made it invisible — or worse, actively unappealing — to the next.

The repositioning challenge was significant. Old Spice needed to attract younger male consumers without alienating its existing base, enter a highly competitive men's grooming category dominated by brands with modern, sporty, or premium positioning, and do all of this with a brand name and heritage that most young men associated with the medicine cabinet of their grandparents' bathroom.

Procter & Gamble, which owns Old Spice, worked with Wieden+Kennedy to develop a strategy that would become a landmark in modern branding. Rather than attempting to erase the brand's heritage or pretend the image problem did not exist, the agency chose to lean into it — using humour, self-awareness, and exaggerated masculinity to reframe the brand's character in an entirely new way. The result was the 'The Man Your Man Could Smell Like' campaign, launched in 2010, which featured absurdist humour, rapid-fire visual jokes, and a charismatic spokesperson who addressed women directly rather than men.

The campaign was not just a creative success — it was a strategic triumph. Sales increased by 107% in the month following the launch. Brand awareness among young male consumers increased dramatically. Crucially, the campaign did not abandon the brand's identity — it reimagined it. Old Spice was still about masculine confidence; it was simply now expressing that confidence with wit and self-deprecation rather than earnestness. The brand had found a new tone of voice that felt genuinely its own, not borrowed from competitors.

The Old Spice repositioning became widely studied in business schools as an example of how a brand can successfully shift its positioning without losing its core identity — and as proof that the most powerful differentiator is often not a product feature but a point of view.

Case Study 2: Burberry — Reclaiming a Luxury Brand

In the early 2000s, Burberry found itself in a brand positioning crisis that threatened to permanently damage its status as a British luxury heritage brand. The iconic Burberry check — one of the most recognisable patterns in global fashion — had been widely counterfeited and had become, in the UK at least, strongly associated with a specific working-class youth subculture that was frequently depicted in the media in a negative light. At the same time, the brand had licensed its name and check extensively, appearing on products ranging from luggage to pet accessories to baseball caps, in ways that were fundamentally inconsistent with a luxury positioning. The result was a brand whose image was simultaneously devalued by association and diluted by overexposure.

The turnaround began with the appointment of Angela Ahrendts as CEO in 2006, and subsequently with the arrival of Christopher Bailey as creative director. Together, they implemented a comprehensive repositioning strategy that addressed the brand's architecture, distribution, communication, and product quality simultaneously. First, Burberry radically reduced the visibility of the check in its primary communications, relegating it from a dominant graphic element to an occasional, more discreet brand signal. Second, the company terminated or renegotiated hundreds of licensing agreements, bringing product quality and brand expression back under central control. Third, the distribution strategy was tightened — wholesale partnerships with retailers who did not meet the brand's luxury standards were ended, and the company invested instead in its own flagship stores in premium locations globally.

On the communications side, Burberry made a pioneering move into digital marketing at a time when most luxury brands were reluctant to engage with social media, fearing it would damage their exclusivity. The Burberry Acoustic campaign, which featured emerging British musical talent and was distributed primarily online, positioned the brand as a champion of British creative culture — a move that resonated deeply with a younger, internationally mobile, culturally curious audience. The brand also invested heavily in celebrity association, product design, and catwalk storytelling, ensuring that Burberry's communications consistently expressed a coherent and compelling vision of contemporary British luxury.

By the early 2010s, the repositioning had generated remarkable results. Revenue had grown from approximately £700 million to over £2 billion. The brand was recognised in global luxury rankings as one of the fastest-growing luxury brands in the world. The check — once a symbol of reputational danger — had been reclaimed as a coveted luxury identifier. Burberry's story is widely studied as a masterclass in the strategic use of brand architecture, distribution control, communications investment, and cultural relevance to rescue and then transform a great brand.
