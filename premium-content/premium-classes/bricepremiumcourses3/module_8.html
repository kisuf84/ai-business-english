Module Overview

Welcome to Module 8: Product Launch and Campaign Planning. Launching a product is one of the most complex and high-stakes activities in marketing. It requires coordinating multiple teams — product, brand, digital, PR, sales, and operations — around a single, time-critical event, while maintaining a coherent strategy and a consistent message. The language used to plan, brief, and manage this process is highly specialised, and fluency in it is essential for any marketing professional working in an international or English-language environment.

This module focuses on the vocabulary and communication skills needed at every stage of the launch process: from the initial go-to-market strategy and campaign brief, through pre-launch activities and stakeholder alignment, to post-launch analysis and reporting. You will learn how to write and interpret campaign briefs, discuss launch timelines and critical paths, present strategy to senior stakeholders, and evaluate campaign performance against objectives.

By the end of this module, you will be able to lead or contribute to product launch discussions in English, write a structured campaign brief, present a go-to-market strategy clearly, and use the grammar structures most common in planning documents, presentations, and post-launch reviews.

Text 1: Go-To-Market Strategy — Planning a Successful Product Launch

Paragraph 1 — Why Most Product Launches Fail

Research consistently suggests that between 80 and 95% of new product launches fail to meet their commercial objectives within the first year. The reasons are varied — poor product-market fit, inadequate distribution, pricing errors, insufficient marketing investment — but a significant proportion of failures can be attributed to poor launch planning rather than poor products. A product that is genuinely valuable to customers can still fail if it reaches the wrong people, in the wrong channels, with the wrong message, at the wrong time. The go-to-market strategy exists precisely to prevent this kind of avoidable failure.

A go-to-market (GTM) strategy is a comprehensive plan that defines how a company will bring a product to market, reach its target customers, generate initial sales, and establish a sustainable competitive position. It is not a marketing plan in the narrow sense; it encompasses decisions about product positioning, pricing, distribution, sales enablement, and marketing communications, all coordinated around a unified launch timeline. The GTM strategy is typically developed during the final phase of product development and must be finalised before any campaign activity can begin.

Paragraph 2 — The Components of a GTM Strategy

A well-constructed GTM strategy addresses five fundamental questions. First, who is the target customer? This means defining the primary target segment with precision: their demographics, psychographics, purchase behaviours, and the specific problem the product solves for them. Second, what is the value proposition? This is the clear, compelling statement of the benefit the customer receives from the product and why it is superior to existing alternatives. Third, how will the product be priced and distributed? Pricing strategy must reflect both the value delivered to the customer and the competitive landscape. Distribution determines where and how the customer can access the product. Fourth, how will the product be communicated? This covers the campaign channels, key messages, creative approach, and media investment. Fifth, how will success be measured? Defining KPIs before the launch ensures that the team has clear, shared criteria for evaluating performance.

The GTM strategy also establishes the launch phasing: most successful launches follow a sequence of pre-launch, launch, and post-launch phases. The pre-launch phase builds awareness and anticipation, typically through teaser content, early access programmes, waitlists, press previews, and influencer seeding. The launch phase deploys the full campaign across all selected channels simultaneously, driving awareness, consideration, and conversion. The post-launch phase sustains momentum through retargeting, customer reviews, case studies, and ongoing content, while also gathering data for the performance review.

Paragraph 3 — Stakeholder Alignment and the Critical Path

Even the best GTM strategy will fail in execution if the teams responsible for delivering it are not aligned. In most organisations, a product launch involves contributions from product, marketing, sales, legal, operations, customer service, and often external agencies or partners. Each team has its own priorities, timelines, and language. The marketing lead's responsibility is to create a shared understanding of the launch objectives and a clear picture of how each team's contributions fit into the overall plan.

Central to this coordination effort is the launch timeline, which maps every task, dependency, and milestone from the start of campaign development to the post-launch review. The concept of the critical path — the sequence of tasks that determine the minimum launch date — is essential here. If the packaging design is on the critical path and it is delayed by two weeks, the entire launch moves by two weeks. Identifying critical path items early and monitoring them closely is one of the most important skills in launch project management. It is also where most launches encounter their first serious problems: a task that seemed straightforward turns out to be dependent on another team's approval, a supplier's delivery, or a regulatory review that takes longer than anticipated.

Text 2: The Campaign Brief — The Foundation of Effective Marketing Execution

Paragraph 1 — What a Campaign Brief Is and Why It Matters

The campaign brief is arguably the single most important document in marketing communications. It is the document that translates a business objective into a marketing task, and a marketing strategy into actionable creative and media direction. A poorly written brief produces unfocused work, wasted budget, and painful revision cycles. A well-written brief gives creative teams and media agencies a clear, shared understanding of what success looks like — and empowers them to do their best work within a defined strategic framework.

The brief is written by the marketing team (usually the brand or campaign manager) and is used to brief both internal teams and external agencies. It is a living document in the sense that it may be refined through conversations with the creative team, the media agency, and senior stakeholders before the campaign begins. However, once it is signed off, it becomes the reference point against which all campaign work is evaluated. If the work does not answer the brief, it is sent back for revision regardless of how creatively impressive it may be.

Paragraph 2 — The Key Elements of a Campaign Brief

While brief formats vary by organisation, the most effective briefs consistently address a common set of elements. The background section provides context: what is happening in the market, why is this campaign being run now, and what has been learnt from previous activity? The objective section states what the campaign must achieve in measurable terms. A good objective is specific, time-bound, and connected to a defined KPI: not "increase brand awareness" but "achieve a 15% increase in spontaneous brand awareness among our target segment within three months."

The target audience section describes the customer in detail: not just their demographics but their attitudes, motivations, barriers, and the specific insight that makes the campaign relevant to them. This is often the most important section of the brief, because if the creative team does not understand the audience deeply, the work will not resonate. The key message section distils everything the campaign needs to communicate into a single, prioritised sentence. There is usually space for supporting messages, but the single most important message should be unmistakable. The mandatories section lists non-negotiable requirements: the brand's visual identity guidelines, legal disclaimers, regulatory restrictions, and any mandatory product information that must be included.

Paragraph 3 — Budget, Timeline, and Evaluation

The brief must also specify the budget available for the campaign. Providing a budget figure is not optional: without it, creative teams and agencies cannot make informed decisions about scope, production values, or media scale. A campaign brief without a budget is like asking an architect to design a building without telling them how much the client can spend. The result is almost always wasted time and a mismatch between ambitions and reality.

The timeline section sets out the key milestones: the briefing date, the deadline for initial concepts, the date for client review and feedback, the production timeline, and the campaign go-live date. Finally, the success criteria or evaluation framework defines how the campaign will be measured after it launches. This section should specify both the primary KPI and any secondary metrics that will be tracked, as well as the tools and methodologies that will be used to measure them. A campaign that is well-planned but poorly evaluated generates no learning for future campaigns — and in a data-rich environment, that is an inexcusable waste.

Grammar Review

Three reference boxes covering future forms, passive and impersonal structures, and expressing purpose and objectives in professional planning English.

Reference Box 1
Future Forms in Launch Planning
Why future forms matter in launch documents
Launch plans and campaign timelines are written almost entirely in future forms. Choosing the right future form signals whether an action is planned and certain, scheduled, or a prediction. Using the wrong form can make professional documents sound uncertain or informal.
FormUseLaunch Planning Example
will + infinitiveDecisions, predictions, promisesThe campaign will launch on 14 March across all channels.
be going to + infinitiveIntentions already decided, predictions based on evidenceWe are going to soft launch in the UK first.
Present continuousFixed future arrangementsThe agency is presenting the creative concepts on Friday.
Present simpleScheduled/timetabled eventsThe product goes live on the website on 1 April.
will + be + -ingActions in progress at a future timeBy next month, the team will be briefing all regional managers.
will + have + past participleActions completed before a future pointBy the launch date, all assets will have been approved.
Common errors in launch planning language
❌ "The campaign is going to will launch next month." (double future form)
✅ "The campaign will launch next month." or "The campaign is going to launch next month."

❌ "By the end of Q2, we launch the product." (missing future marker for future completion)
✅ "By the end of Q2, the product will have launched."
Reference Box 2
Passive & Impersonal Structures in Planning Documents
Why planning documents use the passive
Formal planning documents — campaign briefs, launch plans, strategy presentations — use the passive voice extensively to achieve an objective, authoritative tone, focus on outcomes rather than actors, and distribute responsibility across teams rather than attributing it to individuals. The passive is also used in impersonal constructions that are standard in professional English.
StructureUsePlanning Document Example
Passive (past)Completed actionsThe market research was conducted in Q4 2024.
Passive (future)Planned actionsAll creative assets will be reviewed by the legal team.
It is expected that...Formal predictionsIt is expected that the launch will generate 50,000 first-week sales.
It has been agreed that...Formal decisionsIt has been agreed that the launch date will move to Q2.
It is recommended that...Formal recommendationsIt is recommended that a soft launch precede the full campaign.
It should be noted that...Drawing attention to somethingIt should be noted that the budget excludes production costs.
Examples in campaign briefs and launch plans
All campaign materials must be submitted for brand approval no later than 10 working days before the go-live date.
It is anticipated that the pre-launch campaign will generate a waitlist of over 5,000 potential customers.
The budget has been allocated as follows: 40% to paid media, 30% to content production, and 30% to influencer partnerships.
Reference Box 3
Expressing Purpose & Objectives Professionally
Why precise objective language matters
In campaign briefs and launch documents, stating objectives vaguely is one of the most common and costly mistakes. The right objective language is specific, measurable, and actionable. Using the correct grammatical structures also signals professional competence to senior stakeholders and agency partners.
StructureUseCampaign Planning Example
The objective is to + infinitiveFormal objective statementThe objective is to achieve 20% brand awareness among 18–30-year-olds.
In order to + infinitiveFormal purpose (written)In order to maximise launch impact, we will activate all channels simultaneously.
So as to + infinitiveVery formal purposeSo as to ensure consistency, all teams will use the approved messaging document.
The aim of this campaign is to...Introductory purpose statementThe aim of this campaign is to generate 10,000 pre-launch sign-ups.
This campaign is designed to...Passive purposeThis campaign is designed to shift brand perception among young urban consumers.
With a view to + gerundFormal strategic intentionWith a view to expanding into the German market, we are running a pilot in Berlin.
Writing SMART objectives in briefs
The best campaign objectives are SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. Compare the examples below.
❌ Vague: "Increase brand awareness."
✅ SMART: "Achieve a 15-point increase in spontaneous brand awareness among UK women aged 25–40 within 12 weeks of launch, as measured by our monthly brand tracker."

Case Study 1: Lyra Audio — A Phased Product Launch That Created a Waitlist of 40,000

Go-To-Market Strategy · Pre-Launch Campaign · Soft Launch Execution

Background

Lyra Audio is a UK startup that spent three years developing a premium wireless headphone with an active noise-cancellation system that the founders believed outperformed everything available at a similar price point. By early 2023, the product was ready for market. However, rather than launching immediately with a traditional product release, the founding team made a deliberate decision to invest six months in a structured pre-launch strategy. Their reasoning was both strategic and financial: they had a limited marketing budget of £95,000, and they needed that budget to work as hard as possible. A noisy, immediate launch with insufficient marketing support, they argued, would be worse than a delayed, well-prepared one.

The pre-launch phase began twelve weeks before the product went on sale. The team started by building an email waitlist, driving sign-ups through paid social advertising, tech review partnerships, and a referral programme where existing waitlist members received early-access privileges for each friend they referred. The product was seeded to thirty-five audio-focused YouTube reviewers and podcasters — not for paid promotions, but with the expectation that genuine reviews would follow. A teaser website was launched showing only the product's name, a countdown timer, and three headline specifications.

Launch & Results

By launch day, the waitlist had grown to 42,000 people. Thirty-one of the thirty-five seeded reviewers had published content — generating over 8 million organic views and an estimated earned media value of £340,000 against a seeding cost of approximately £12,000 in product and logistics. The press coverage from the reviewer community had attracted the attention of three national technology publications, which ran features in the week before launch.

On launch day, the team activated the email list with a personalised launch announcement and an early-access window of 48 hours. Within 24 hours, 6,200 units had been sold. Within the first week, the entire initial production run of 9,000 units had been purchased. The paid media budget was not deployed until week two, when a new production batch was available. The campaign achieved a total ROAS of 6.2x across all paid activity — significantly above the 3.5x target set in the GTM strategy. The Lyra Audio launch was widely studied as an example of how a well-executed pre-launch strategy could dramatically reduce the cost of customer acquisition at launch by creating genuine pre-existing demand.

Case Study 2: Solaris Energy — A B2B Product Launch That Required Full Stakeholder Alignment

B2B Launch Planning · Stakeholder Management · Campaign Brief Execution

Background

Solaris Energy Solutions is a UK provider of commercial solar installation services for mid-sized businesses. In 2023, the company developed a new product: a smart energy management dashboard that allowed commercial clients to monitor and optimise their energy usage in real time, reducing bills and supporting ESG reporting. The product had been eighteen months in development and represented the company's most significant investment in technology to date. The marketing director was brought in six months before the planned launch to lead the go-to-market strategy.

The first challenge she faced was not strategic but organisational: the product, sales, legal, and customer success teams each had different assumptions about what the product was, who it was for, and when it would be ready. The product team believed the launch date was fixed; the legal team had not yet approved the data privacy documentation required for the dashboard to comply with UK regulations; the sales team had not received any training on the product and did not feel confident selling it; and the customer success team had no onboarding materials prepared for clients who purchased. In short, despite the product being technically ready, the organisation was not ready to launch.

Strategy & Execution

The marketing director immediately commissioned a full launch readiness audit covering all teams. The audit revealed that the legal approval process would take a minimum of eight weeks, making the original launch date impossible to meet. She presented the findings to the CEO with a revised timeline: the launch would be pushed back by ten weeks, and the additional time would be used to complete the sales training programme, produce the onboarding materials, and run a beta test with five existing clients who had agreed to trial the dashboard before the public launch.

The campaign brief was written to reflect a B2B launch: the target audience was defined as Operations Directors and Finance Managers at UK companies with 50–500 employees and existing solar installations. The key message was framed around measurable financial and ESG benefit: "See exactly how much your solar investment is saving you — in real time." The launch channels were LinkedIn (sponsored content and direct mail to a targeted list), a dedicated landing page, a PR campaign targeting the business energy and sustainability press, and a webinar series featuring existing client case studies. Within three months of launch, Solaris had acquired 47 new dashboard clients, exceeding the 30-client target set in the GTM strategy by 57%.
