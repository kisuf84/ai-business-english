Module Overview

Welcome to Module 1: Marketing Fundamentals and Key Vocabulary. This module introduces you to the core concepts and professional English used in marketing and advertising. Whether you work in a marketing department, an agency, or interact with marketing teams in your role, this module will give you the language you need to participate confidently in conversations, meetings, and written communications about marketing strategy.

You will explore the foundational vocabulary of the discipline — from the four Ps of the marketing mix to concepts such as brand positioning, target audiences, and the customer journey. You will read professional texts, listen to authentic business conversations, and practise the grammar structures most commonly used when presenting and discussing marketing ideas.

By the end of this module, you will be able to discuss marketing strategies in English, describe products and target markets, use key marketing terminology accurately, and write a short marketing brief or brand description. This module sets the foundation for all subsequent modules in the Marketing & Advertising English course.

Text 1: The Marketing Mix and Its Evolution

Paragraph 1 — What Is Marketing?

Marketing is the process by which organisations identify customer needs and create value to satisfy those needs profitably. Far from being simply about advertising or selling, marketing encompasses the full range of activities involved in bringing a product or service from its conception to the customer — and beyond. At its core, marketing is about understanding people: who they are, what they want, why they make the decisions they do, and how a brand can position itself to become their preferred choice.

The discipline has its roots in economics and psychology, and has evolved significantly over the past century. Early marketing was largely product-focused — the assumption being that if you built a good enough product at the right price, customers would naturally come to you. Modern marketing, by contrast, starts with the customer and works backwards. This shift from a product orientation to a customer orientation represents one of the most important intellectual developments in business history.

Paragraph 2 — The Four Ps

The marketing mix, introduced by E. Jerome McCarthy in the 1960s, remains one of the most widely taught frameworks in business education. Its four components — Product, Price, Place, and Promotion — provide a structured way to think about all the decisions a marketer must make when bringing an offering to market. The Product refers not just to its physical attributes, but to everything the customer experiences: design, quality, features, packaging, and after-sales service. Price is a powerful signal of value and brand positioning. Place — or distribution — determines how and where the customer can access the product. Promotion covers all the communication activities used to make customers aware of and interested in the product.

In recent decades, the model has been extended. Some theorists have proposed adding People, Process, and Physical Evidence to create a "Seven Ps" model, particularly for service industries where the human element of the interaction is inseparable from the product itself. However, the original four Ps remain a foundational framework and are the starting point for most strategic marketing discussions.

Paragraph 3 — Market Segmentation and Targeting

One of the most important strategic decisions in marketing is identifying who you are trying to reach. Market segmentation is the process of dividing the total available market into distinct subgroups of consumers who share similar characteristics, needs, or purchasing behaviours. Common segmentation variables include demographic factors (age, gender, income, education), psychographic factors (lifestyle, values, attitudes), geographic factors, and behavioural factors (usage rate, brand loyalty, purchase occasions).

Once a market has been segmented, marketers must decide which segments to target. This targeting decision is influenced by the size and growth potential of each segment, the level of competition within it, and the company's ability to serve it profitably. Effective targeting allows companies to concentrate their marketing resources where they are most likely to generate a return, rather than spreading them thinly across an undifferentiated mass market.

Text 2: Brand Strategy and Consumer Psychology

Paragraph 1 — What Is a Brand?

A brand is far more than a logo or a name. It is the sum of perceptions, feelings, and associations that consumers hold about a company and its products. Successful brands create emotional connections with their customers — connections that are strong enough to influence purchasing decisions, command premium prices, and generate loyalty that outlasts individual product generations. The value that a brand adds to a product beyond its functional attributes is called brand equity, and it is one of the most powerful — and difficult to measure — assets a company can possess.

Brand positioning is the strategic process by which a company defines how it wants to be perceived in the market relative to its competitors. A clear positioning statement answers three questions: Who is the target customer? What category does the brand compete in? And what is the key benefit that sets it apart? Companies that are unclear about their positioning risk being perceived as generic, which makes it difficult to justify premium pricing or build loyalty.

Paragraph 2 — Consumer Psychology and Decision-Making

Understanding how consumers make purchasing decisions is central to effective marketing. Classical economic theory assumed that consumers are rational agents who evaluate products objectively based on price and utility. Behavioural economics and consumer psychology have fundamentally challenged this assumption. Research shows that most purchasing decisions are strongly influenced by emotional factors, social proof (what others are doing or recommending), scarcity signals (limited availability), and cognitive shortcuts that bypass rational analysis entirely.

For marketers, these insights have profound implications. Advertising that evokes emotion — nostalgia, aspiration, humour, or social belonging — tends to be more effective than purely informational advertising. Product design, packaging, and even the way a price is presented can all influence perceived value and purchase intent. Understanding these mechanisms allows marketers to design more persuasive communications and more compelling customer experiences.

Paragraph 3 — The Customer Journey

Modern marketing recognises that a consumer's relationship with a brand does not begin with a purchase and end when the transaction is complete. The customer journey — sometimes called the purchase funnel or buyer's journey — describes the full sequence of interactions a customer has with a brand, from initial awareness through to consideration, purchase, retention, and advocacy. Different marketing activities are appropriate at different stages of this journey. Above-the-line advertising is well-suited to building awareness among a broad audience. Content marketing, search engine optimisation, and comparison websites play a role in the consideration phase. Personalised offers, loyalty programmes, and excellent customer service are crucial for retention. And satisfied customers who become advocates — recommending the brand to others — are arguably the most valuable marketing asset of all.

Grammar Review

Three reference boxes covering all grammar points in this module. Study these alongside the grammar exercises for best results.

Reference Box 1
Relative Clauses in Marketing English
What are relative clauses?
Relative clauses add information about a noun. They are essential in marketing English for describing products, audiences, and strategies precisely. There are two types: defining (essential information, no commas) and non-defining (extra information, with commas).
TypePronounPunctuationRemovable?
Definingwho / which / that / whoseNo commasNo — changes meaning
Non-definingwho / which / whose (NOT that)Commas requiredYes — adds extra info
Examples in Marketing Contexts
The customers who spend over £500 a year receive loyalty rewards. [defining: specifies which customers]
Our premium segment, which accounts for 30% of revenue, is growing rapidly. [non-defining: adds extra info]
The agency that won the pitch had the most creative strategy. [defining: 'that' is acceptable for things]
Nike, whose brand identity is globally recognised, invests heavily in sponsorship. [non-defining with 'whose']
Common Errors to Avoid
Do NOT use 'that' in non-defining clauses. Do NOT omit the comma in non-defining clauses. Do NOT use 'which' where 'who' is required for people.
❌ Our brand, that launched last year, has been very successful.
✅ Our brand, which launched last year, has been very successful.

Purpose structures in marketing presentations
When explaining why a strategy or action is taken, marketers need precise purpose structures. These are common in briefs, presentations, and strategy documents.
StructureUseFormality
to + infinitiveGeneral purposeAll registers
in order to + infinitiveFormal purpose (often written)Formal
so as to + infinitiveFormal purpose (often written)Very formal

so + adj + thatResult (degree)Neutral/formal
such + noun phrase + thatResult (degree)Neutral/formal
Examples in Marketing Contexts

Reference Box 3
Modal Verbs for Recommendations & Possibility
Why modals matter in marketing discussions
In strategy discussions, pitches, and meetings, marketers rarely make absolute statements. Modal verbs allow you to express degrees of certainty, make recommendations diplomatically, and discuss possibilities — all of which are essential skills in professional marketing communication.
ModalMeaningMarketing Example
should / ought toRecommendationWe should target millennials for this campaign.
couldPossibility / suggestionWe could expand into the Asian market next year.
might / mayUncertain possibilityThis approach might appeal to a younger demographic.
mustStrong necessityThe brand must maintain consistency across all channels.
wouldHypothetical / conditionalA loyalty programme would increase customer retention.
need toNecessity (practical)We need to define our positioning before we brief the agency.
canAbility / possibilityDigital channels can reach our target audience cost-effectively.
Softening Recommendations Professionally
In professional settings, direct statements can seem aggressive. Using modal verbs makes recommendations sound collaborative and respectful while still being clear and persuasive.
❌ "Change the pricing strategy." (too direct)
✅ "We might consider revising the pricing strategy to better reflect the premium positioning."

Case Study 1: How Oat&Go Disrupted the Breakfast Market

Brand Launch · Market Segmentation · USP Development

Background

Oat&Go is a UK-based startup that launched a range of ready-to-drink oat milk smoothies in 2021. Entering a crowded breakfast drinks market dominated by large multinationals, the founders recognised that existing products were either too sweet and indulgent, or too bland and health-focused to appeal to busy urban professionals who wanted a nutritious but enjoyable breakfast on the go. This gap in the market became the foundation of their entire marketing strategy.

The company defined its target audience with unusual precision: professionals aged 25–40 in urban areas, commuting daily, with a household income above £45,000, an interest in sustainability, and an existing habit of buying their breakfast from high-street coffee shops. By being this specific, Oat&Go was able to craft messaging that felt deeply relevant to its audience rather than broadly appealing to everyone and connecting with no one.

Strategy

Oat&Go's USP was summarised in their tagline: "The breakfast that thinks ahead." This positioned the product as both intelligent (nutritionally optimised) and convenient (no preparation needed) — values that resonated strongly with their target segment. Rather than competing on price, they adopted a premium pricing strategy (£3.50 per bottle) that signalled quality and allowed the brand to be stocked alongside premium coffee and juice brands in independent cafes and Waitrose.

Their promotional strategy was almost entirely below the line. They partnered with fitness influencers on Instagram, offered free samples at train stations during peak commuting hours, and used geo-targeted digital advertising within a 500-metre radius of their stockists. Within 18 months, Oat&Go had achieved a brand awareness level of 34% among their defined target segment — a remarkable result for a startup with no above-the-line advertising budget.

Case Study 2: Reviva Skincare — Repositioning for a New Generation

Brand Repositioning · Consumer Psychology · Digital Strategy

Background

Reviva Skincare was founded in 1987 as a pharmaceutical-grade skincare range sold exclusively through pharmacies. By 2019, the brand was in serious decline. Although the products remained highly effective, the brand's visual identity and communications had not evolved in over a decade. Consumer research revealed a damaging perception: Reviva was seen as "my mother's skincare brand" — effective but old-fashioned, with no emotional appeal to younger consumers. Sales were falling at 8% per year.

The marketing director commissioned a full brand audit which identified three core problems. First, the brand's positioning was unclear: it sat uncomfortably between medical-grade products (which required a stronger clinical identity) and lifestyle beauty brands (which required an emotional, aspirational identity). Second, the packaging was functional but completely lacking in shelf appeal. Third, the brand had zero presence in digital channels, which is where its potential new customers spent most of their time.

The Repositioning

The repositioning strategy had three pillars. First, Reviva sharpened its positioning statement: "Dermatologist-developed skincare for people who value results over packaging." This deliberately targeted a sophisticated, evidence-driven consumer who was sceptical of marketing hype. Second, the packaging was redesigned with a clean, modern aesthetic that could sit comfortably in both pharmacy and premium beauty retail. Third, the brand launched a content marketing programme built around transparency: dermatologists explained the science behind each ingredient in short-form video content on Instagram and YouTube.

The results exceeded expectations. Within two years, Reviva's sales had grown by 41%, and brand awareness among 25–35-year-olds had increased from 12% to 38%. The brand had successfully attracted a new generation of consumers without alienating its existing base — a notoriously difficult balance to achieve in brand repositioning. The case became widely studied as an example of how deep consumer insight, a clear USP, and authentic communication can reverse a declining brand's fortunes.
