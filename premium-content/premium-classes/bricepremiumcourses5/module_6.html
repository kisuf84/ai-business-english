📚 Key Legal Terms
💰
Pre-Seed Round
The earliest formal external funding stage — typically £25,000 to £500,000 — raised before the startup has a working product or meaningful revenue. Pre-seed capital is most commonly provided by the founders themselves (savings), friends and family, angel investors, micro-VCs, or accelerator programmes. The primary purpose is to fund the team's time while they build the MVP and test initial market hypotheses. Pre-seed rounds are most commonly structured as SAFEs or convertible notes rather than priced equity rounds, to avoid the time and cost of a full valuation negotiation at such an early stage.
🌱
Seed Round
A funding round — typically £250,000 to £3 million in the UK — that follows pre-seed funding, usually once the startup has an MVP and early evidence of customer interest or revenue. Seed investors include angel syndicates, seed-stage venture capital funds, and occasionally corporate venture arms. The seed round is designed to fund the company through to product-market fit and the early revenue milestones needed to justify a Series A. Seed rounds may be structured as priced equity rounds (with a fixed valuation) or as SAFEs/convertible notes that convert at the Series A.
🚀
Series A
The first significant institutional venture capital round — typically £3 million to £15 million in the UK — raised after a startup has demonstrated initial product-market fit and early revenue traction. Series A investors (typically established VC funds) expect to see a working product, paying customers, and a clear hypothesis for how the capital will accelerate growth toward a Series B. The Series A is often the round where a lead investor takes a board seat and the governance structure of the company becomes more formal.
📈
Series B
The second institutional venture capital round — typically £10 million to £50 million — raised by companies that have proven their business model and are ready to scale aggressively. Series B capital is used for scaling the commercial team, entering new markets, and building the operational infrastructure needed to support rapid growth. Series B investors often include later-stage VC funds and growth equity investors alongside the company's existing investors. Growth rate, unit economics, and net revenue retention are the primary evaluation criteria.
🏦
Venture Capital Fund
A professionally managed investment vehicle that pools capital from institutional investors (limited partners — pension funds, endowments, family offices, fund-of-funds) and deploys it into a portfolio of high-growth startups, in exchange for equity stakes. A typical VC fund has a 10-year life: the first three to five years are the 'investment period' (making new investments), and the remaining years are used to manage and exit existing portfolio companies. The fund's returns are shared between the general partners (GPs — the fund managers, who typically take 20% of profits as carried interest) and the limited partners (who receive the remaining 80%).
🎲
General Partner
The fund manager of a venture capital fund — the individual or team responsible for sourcing deals, making investment decisions, managing portfolio companies, and ultimately generating returns for the fund's investors. GPs typically contribute a small percentage of the fund's capital (the GP commit — typically 1-2%) alongside the LPs, which aligns their financial interests with those of the fund's investors. GPs are compensated through a management fee (typically 2% of committed capital annually) and carried interest (typically 20% of profits above the hurdle rate).
🏛️
Limited Partner
An investor in a venture capital fund who provides capital but does not participate in fund management or investment decisions. Limited partners (LPs) in VC funds are typically institutional investors: pension funds, university endowments, sovereign wealth funds, family offices, and funds-of-funds. LPs receive 80% of the fund's profits (after carried interest is deducted) and have limited liability — they cannot lose more than their invested capital. The LP-GP relationship is defined by a limited partnership agreement that governs reporting, distributions, and governance.
💎
Carried Interest
The share of a VC fund's profits distributed to the general partners — typically 20% of profits above a defined hurdle rate (often 8%). Carried interest is the primary economic incentive for VC fund managers: if a fund returns 3x its invested capital, the GPs receive 20% of the profit above the hurdle. The alignment of carried interest with fund performance means that VCs make most of their money from successful exits rather than management fees — creating a genuine shared interest in portfolio company success. Carried interest is typically subject to a preferred return (hurdle) and a clawback provision.
🔻
Down Round
A funding round in which a company raises capital at a lower valuation than the previous round — implying that the company's value has declined since the last investment. Down rounds are a significant negative signal: they dilute existing investors and founders, often trigger anti-dilution provisions (which further dilute founders), and can damage team morale and the company's ability to attract talent. However, a down round at a realistic valuation is almost always preferable to running out of cash — 'down round survival beats up round bankruptcy'.
🌉
Bridge Round
A small, often informal funding round — typically structured as a convertible note or SAFE — designed to extend the company's runway while it works toward the milestones needed to raise a larger priced round. Bridge rounds are most commonly raised from existing investors rather than new ones. They are called 'bridges' because they are designed to bridge the gap between the current cash position and the conditions needed to raise the next formal round. A bridge round that extends repeatedly without the company reaching its milestones is often called a 'pier' rather than a bridge — it goes nowhere.
⚖️
Anti-Dilution Provision
A contractual right granted to preferred shareholders that protects them from the dilutive effect of a down round by adjusting the conversion price of their preferred shares downward. The two main types: broad-based weighted average (the most founder-friendly — the adjustment is weighted by the number of new shares issued) and full ratchet (the most investor-friendly — the conversion price is adjusted all the way down to the new lower price, maximally diluting founders). Anti-dilution provisions are a key negotiation point in term sheets; founders should understand their implications before accepting them.
🪑
Board Seat
A position on a company's board of directors granted to an investor as a condition of investment — giving the investor formal governance rights, including the right to vote on key decisions (executive appointments, major transactions, future funding rounds). Board seats are most commonly required by Series A investors. Founders should think carefully about board composition: a board dominated by investors can significantly reduce founder control, particularly if a founder is removed as CEO. Typical early-stage board structures: 2 founders, 1 lead investor, 2 independent directors.
🔗
Drag-Along Right
A provision that allows a majority of shareholders (typically a combination of common and preferred shareholders) to compel minority shareholders to approve and participate in an acquisition — even if the minority shareholders do not want to sell. Drag-along rights exist to prevent minority holdouts from blocking acquisitions that the majority have approved. From an investor's perspective, drag-along rights enable clean exits; from a founder's perspective, they may require the founder to sell their shares if a majority — including investors — votes for an acquisition the founder opposes.
🏷️
Tag-Along Right
A provision that gives minority shareholders the right to join a sale initiated by majority shareholders — on the same terms and at the same price. Tag-along rights protect early investors and founders from a situation where the majority shareholders sell their stake to a new acquirer but the minority are left behind, holding shares in a company now controlled by a buyer who may not share the original shareholders' interests. Tag-along rights are typically triggered when a significant portion of shares changes hands in a secondary transaction.
🎯
Right of First Refusal
A contractual right that gives an investor (or the company itself) the opportunity to purchase shares before the current owner sells them to a third party — at the same price and terms offered by the third party. ROFRs are common in startup shareholder agreements: they prevent unwanted third parties from acquiring stakes without existing shareholders having the opportunity to maintain their ownership percentage. ROFRs can complicate secondary sales and founder liquidity events if existing investors choose to exercise them.
📋
Information Rights
Contractual rights granted to investors specifying what financial and operational information the company must provide, how frequently, and in what format. Standard information rights packages include: annual audited accounts, monthly or quarterly management accounts, annual board-approved budgets, and cap table updates. Investors with significant equity stakes often negotiate enhanced information rights including board observer rights. Information rights are not just a compliance obligation — they are the foundation of the investor relationship: investors who are well-informed make better advisers.
💸
Secondary Sale
A transaction in which existing shareholders (founders, early employees, or early investors) sell shares to a new investor — as opposed to the company issuing new shares (a primary investment). Secondary sales allow founders and early employees to achieve partial liquidity before an IPO or acquisition, which can significantly reduce personal financial pressure and allow founders to make better strategic decisions. Secondary sales are increasingly common at Series B and C stages; they typically require the approval of existing investors and the company. Some investors specifically offer secondary liquidity as part of their investment thesis.
🏗️
Participating Preferred
A class of preferred shares that gives the holder two benefits in an exit: first, they receive their liquidation preference (their investment returned, sometimes with a multiple); second, they participate alongside common shareholders in any remaining proceeds as if their shares had converted to equity. Participating preferred is more investor-friendly than non-participating preferred (where the investor either receives their liquidation preference OR converts to equity — not both). A participating preferred holder with a 1x liquidation preference on a £10 million investment in a £30 million exit receives £10 million first, then participates in the remaining £20 million — significantly advantaging investors over founders.
🔢
Ownership Percentage
The proportion of a company's total shares outstanding owned by a specific shareholder, expressed as a percentage. Ownership percentage changes with every new share issuance — including new funding rounds, option pool expansions, and employee grants. Founders track their ownership percentage carefully across the full life of the company because it determines their economic stake in any exit. A founder who owns 25% of a company that exits for £100 million receives £25 million (before taxes and liquidation preferences are applied). Ownership percentage is modelled in the cap table.
🏆
Hurdle Rate
The minimum rate of return that a venture capital fund must achieve before the general partners are entitled to receive their carried interest. A typical hurdle rate is 8% annually — meaning the fund must first return all LP capital plus 8% annual interest before the GPs receive any share of the profit. The hurdle rate protects LPs from paying carried interest on mediocre performance, and ensures that GPs are genuinely incentivised to generate strong returns rather than simply collecting management fees.
📊
Follow-On Investment
An additional investment made by an existing investor in a subsequent funding round of a portfolio company. Follow-on investments allow existing investors to maintain or increase their ownership percentage as the company raises more capital. Top-tier VC funds typically reserve capital specifically for follow-on investments in their strongest portfolio companies — often two to three times the initial investment amount. The decision to follow on (or not) is one of the most visible signals an existing investor sends about their confidence in a portfolio company: investors who do not follow on in a company's Series A send a negative signal to new investors.

Text 1: How Venture Capital Funds Work — The Economics Behind the Investment Decisions

To understand how venture capital investors make decisions, it is essential to understand how VC funds work — because the economic structure of the fund directly shapes the investment decisions made within it. A VC fund is a limited partnership: the general partners (GPs) are the fund managers who make investment decisions; the limited partners (LPs) are the institutional investors who provide the capital. A typical fund has a 10-year life and a defined investment period (usually the first three to five years) during which new investments are made. After the investment period ends, the fund focuses on managing and exiting its existing positions.

The economics of a VC fund are built around two streams of compensation for the GPs. The first is the management fee — typically 2% of committed capital annually — which covers the operational costs of running the fund: staff, rent, due diligence expenses, and the GPs' base salaries. The second, and far more significant, is carried interest — the share of fund profits distributed to the GPs, typically 20% of profits above the hurdle rate (often 8% annually). A fund that raises £100 million and returns £300 million has generated £200 million in profit; the GPs receive 20% (£40 million) as carried interest, and the LPs receive the remaining £160 million. This structure creates a specific set of incentives: because the management fee rarely makes GPs wealthy, the real prize is the carry — and carry requires exits at high multiples.

The fund economics create specific implications for how VCs evaluate startups. First, VCs typically need their winners to return the entire fund. A £100 million fund that makes 25 investments of £4 million each expects most of those investments to fail or return modest multiples. For the fund to return 3x (£300 million), one or two investments must each return £50-100 million or more. This means VCs systematically look for companies with the potential to become very large — not merely good businesses, but companies capable of reaching £500 million or more in revenue. Second, the 10-year fund life creates time pressure: investments made in Year 3 must be exited by Year 10, which means VCs are always thinking about exit paths. Third, because a fund that does not return the hurdle rate pays no carry, GPs are heavily motivated to manage portfolio company failure efficiently — either turning around underperforming companies or writing them off rather than letting them consume capital and management time without prospect of recovery.

Text 2: Reading a Term Sheet — The Provisions That Matter Most

A term sheet — the non-binding document that establishes the framework for a venture investment — can run to 20 or more pages and contain dozens of provisions covering economic rights, control rights, and operational rights. Most first-time founders focus on the valuation and the ownership percentage: these feel like the most important numbers because they determine how much of the company the founder retains. They are important — but experienced investors and founders consistently report that the valuation is not the provision that most affects how much money founders actually receive in an exit. That distinction belongs to the liquidation preference structure.

A liquidation preference determines the order and amount that investors receive before common shareholders (typically founders and employees) in an exit. A 1x non-participating liquidation preference means the investor receives their investment returned first, and then converts to equity to participate in the remaining proceeds alongside common shareholders. A 1x participating liquidation preference means the investor receives their investment returned first, and then also participates in the remaining proceeds as if they had converted — effectively double-dipping. A 2x participating liquidation preference means the investor receives twice their investment before founders receive anything, and then participates in the remaining proceeds. In smaller exits, participating preferred provisions can result in founders receiving very little despite the company having been sold at a seemingly attractive price.

Control provisions are the second most important category. Board composition determines who can hire and fire the CEO, approve major transactions, and influence strategic direction. Protective provisions (also called veto rights) give investors the right to block specific actions — typically including raising new funding, selling the company, changing the capital structure, and making major acquisitions — without investor approval. Information rights determine what the company must share and when. While founders often focus intensely on valuation, it is the combination of liquidation preference, board control, and protective provisions that determines the practical reality of who controls the company and how exit proceeds are distributed.

Grammar Point 1: Precise Language for Funding Rounds and Valuation Mechanics
Key Verbs for Funding Events

Funding round communications use specific verbs that carry precise meanings about the type of event. Using the wrong verb signals financial inexperience.

'Close a round' — the round has completed and the capital is in the bank. (NOT 'finish' or 'complete' in casual language)
'Price a round' — the round has been agreed at a specific per-share price and valuation.
'Convert' — a SAFE or convertible note converts into equity at the next priced round.
'Trigger' — a specific event activates a contractual provision, such as anti-dilution being triggered by a down round.
Describing Valuation and Dilution Precisely

Valuation discussions require internally consistent arithmetic language. The pre-money and post-money relationship is always: post-money = pre-money + new investment. Ownership percentage = new investment / post-money valuation.

'A £2 million raise at a £6 million pre-money gives a post-money of £8 million and an investor stake of 25%.' (£2m / £8m = 25%)
'After the round, the founders' combined ownership fell from 70% to 52.5%.' (new percentage, expressed as change)
'The valuation represents a 3x step-up from the previous round.' (expressing the multiple of the previous valuation)
'The cap table shows 47% founders, 23% seed investors, 15% Series A, and 15% employee option pool.'
Funding Round Stages — Precise Terminology

The funding stage vocabulary must be precise: different stages imply different company maturities, investor types, and typical round sizes. Using the wrong stage term misrepresents the company's position.

Pre-seed: before product, typically <£500k, structured as SAFE or convertible note.
Seed: early product and revenue, typically £250k-£3m, angel investors and seed funds.
Series A: proven model, early traction, typically £3m-£15m, institutional VC lead.
Series B: proven model at scale, typically £10m-£50m, later-stage VC and growth equity.

Grammar Point 2: Conditional Structures in Term Negotiation
Stating Conditions for Acceptance — 'If' and 'Provided That'

Term negotiation uses conditional language to express what the founder will accept, what they cannot accept, and what conditions must be met for agreement. The language must be precise — vague conditionals create ambiguity that experienced investors will exploit.

'We would be willing to proceed provided that the liquidation preference is non-participating.' (condition for yes)
'If the anti-dilution provision is changed to broad-based weighted average, we can move forward this week.' (specific, actionable condition)
'We cannot accept the participating preferred provision in its current form.' (clear, unambiguous position)
'Should the board composition be modified to add an independent director, the full ratchet becomes more acceptable.' (conditional trade-off)'
Type 2 and Type 3 Conditionals in Deal Modelling

Modelling the financial implications of term sheet provisions uses Type 2 conditionals (hypothetical future scenarios) and Type 3 conditionals (past scenarios that did not happen). Both are essential for explaining to co-founders, advisers, and lawyers how provisions work.

Type 2 (future scenario): 'If the company were sold for £15 million, the 2x participating preferred holder would receive £6 million first, then 20% of the remaining £9 million — leaving founders with £7.2 million rather than £12 million.'
Type 3 (past scenario): 'Had the anti-dilution provision been full ratchet rather than broad-based, the founders would have been diluted by an additional 8 percentage points in the down round.'
'Were we to accept the drag-along provision as written, a simple majority of shareholders could compel us to sell the company.' (formal Type 2 — hypothetical consequence)
'Should we have agreed to the full ratchet, our ownership today would be 12% rather than 23%.' (Type 3 in formal register)'
Expressing Non-Negotiable Positions

Some term sheet provisions are non-negotiable from the founder's perspective. The language for expressing this must be firm, precise, and non-apologetic — while remaining professional.

'We are not in a position to accept participating preferred at any multiple.' (firm, professional)
'The full ratchet anti-dilution is a dealbreaker for us — we would not proceed on this basis.' (clear, unambiguous)
'We would walk away from this term sheet before accepting a 2x participating liquidation preference.' (direct consequence stated)
'This is a point we are unwilling to move on — we would, however, be happy to explore other ways of addressing the investor's downside concern.' (firm but constructive)

Grammar Point 3: Contrast Language for Comparing Funding Options
'Whereas' and 'While' — Parallel Comparisons

When comparing two funding instruments, investor types, or term sheet provisions, 'whereas' and 'while' introduce parallel contrasts efficiently — presenting both sides in a single sentence.

'A SAFE has no maturity date or interest, whereas a convertible note accrues interest and must be repaid if conversion does not occur.'
'Participating preferred holders receive their preference AND convert to equity; non-participating holders do one or the other.'
'Series A investors typically require a board seat, while seed investors more commonly take board observer rights.'
'Strategic investors bring industry expertise and distribution; financial investors, by contrast, bring capital and exit experience.'
'However' and 'That Said' — Sequential Qualification

When making a positive claim about a funding option followed by an important limitation, 'however' and 'that said' introduce the qualification in the next sentence — maintaining analytical balance.

'A bridge round extends runway and avoids a down round. However, it delays the day of reckoning if the underlying business model is not working.'
'Follow-on investment from an existing investor signals confidence. That said, some investors choose not to follow on for portfolio management reasons unrelated to company quality.'
'The SAFE is faster and cheaper to close than a priced round. However, SAFEs that stack up without a priced round create cap table complexity that can complicate the Series A.'
'The strategic investor brings genuine distribution advantages. That said, accepting strategic capital can create conflicts of interest if the strategic and the startup later compete.'
Explicit Conclusion Language after Comparison

After presenting a balanced comparison, formal documents and investor presentations require explicit conclusion language that draws the comparison to a clear recommendation or finding.

'On balance, the broad-based weighted average is the more appropriate provision given the company's stage and risk profile.'
'In this context, the SAFE is preferable to a convertible note — the absence of a maturity date removes a significant technical default risk.'
'Taking these factors together, the non-participating preferred structure better aligns with the long-term interests of founders, employees, and investors alike.'
'The evidence suggests that, in most exit scenarios below £20 million, the participating preferred provision is materially disadvantageous to founders.'

Case Study 1: Sequoia Capital and the Power Law — Why VC Funds Need Outliers, Not Averages
How the economics of VC fund returns shape every investment decision — and why this creates a specific dynamic between investors and founders

Background

Sequoia Capital, founded by Don Valentine in 1972, is one of the most successful venture capital firms in history — with investments in Apple, Google, Cisco, Oracle, Yahoo, YouTube, WhatsApp, Stripe, and many others. The firm's track record illustrates one of the most important principles in venture capital: the power law distribution of returns. In a typical VC portfolio, most investments return less than the capital invested, a small number return 2-5x, and one or two exceptional investments return 50x, 100x, or more. These outliers — the power law winners — generate the vast majority of the fund's total returns, and they are the reason that VC fund economics are structured the way they are.

How the Power Law Shapes VC Behaviour

Understanding the power law helps founders understand why VCs behave in ways that can seem puzzling. First, VCs systematically pursue outsized opportunities even when more modest opportunities are more certain. A company that could reliably return 3x does not interest most VC funds; a company that might return 100x but probably fails is exactly what they are looking for. This is not irrationality — it is the logical consequence of the fund economics. Second, VCs push portfolio companies to grow faster than might be optimal for the underlying business, because slow, careful growth does not produce the outlier returns that fund economics require. Third, VCs accept and even encourage concentration: rather than diversifying evenly across all portfolio companies, top funds deploy significant capital into their highest-conviction bets. Sequoia's investments in Apple and WhatsApp were not equal-weighted diversified bets — they were concentrated bets on companies Sequoia believed had outlier potential.

What This Means for Founders

The power law dynamic has specific implications for how founders interact with VC investors. First, not every startup is a good fit for venture capital. A business that will reliably generate £5 million in annual profit but will not reach £500 million in revenue is an excellent business — and a poor VC investment. Understanding this distinction prevents founders from pursuing VC funding for businesses that would be better served by bootstrapping or alternative financing. Second, when founders accept VC funding, they are implicitly committing to pursue an outlier outcome — because that is what the fund economics require. This creates real tension when a founder might rationally prefer a £20 million acquisition that makes them personally wealthy versus holding for a £200 million outcome that the VC needs. Third, the best VC-founder relationships are partnerships where both parties understand the power law dynamic and have genuinely aligned incentives about the type of outcome they are building toward.

Case Study 2: The Enterprise Investment Scheme — How UK Tax Policy Shaped the Angel Investment Market
How a government incentive programme transformed the availability of early-stage capital in the UK — and what founders need to know about EIS and SEIS

Background

The Enterprise Investment Scheme (EIS) and Seed Enterprise Investment Scheme (SEIS) are UK government tax incentive programmes designed to encourage private individuals to invest in early-stage companies by offering significant tax relief in exchange for the higher risk of startup investment. SEIS was introduced in 2012 for the very earliest stage companies (those that have been trading for under three years with assets under £350,000); EIS has been available since 1994 for a broader range of qualifying companies. Together, the schemes have transformed the UK angel investment market: in the 2022-23 tax year, approximately £2.4 billion was raised through EIS, and over 5,000 companies received SEIS investment.

How EIS and SEIS Work

For an angel investor investing under SEIS: they receive 50% income tax relief on investments up to £200,000 per year (effectively halving the cost of the investment), capital gains tax exemption on profits from qualifying shares held for at least three years, and loss relief if the company fails (further reducing effective downside). For EIS: investors receive 30% income tax relief on investments up to £2 million per year, capital gains deferral or exemption, and loss relief. The combined effect is significant: an angel investing £100,000 under SEIS effectively invests £50,000 after the tax relief, and if the investment grows to £300,000, they pay no capital gains tax on the £250,000 gain. The practical result is that EIS and SEIS have made angel investing in the UK substantially more attractive than in most other countries — and have significantly expanded the pool of individuals willing to provide early-stage capital.

What Founders Need to Know

For founders, EIS and SEIS eligibility creates a meaningful fundraising advantage: investors who are eligible to invest under the scheme will often accept a slightly lower valuation or invest in companies they might otherwise pass on, because the tax relief significantly improves the economics of the investment. However, EIS and SEIS come with conditions that founders must understand. The company must be incorporated in the UK (or have a permanent establishment in the UK), must be trading (not primarily holding investments), must not have gross assets above £15 million (EIS) or £350,000 (SEIS), and the investor must not be an employee or director of the company (with limited exceptions). Many startups engage a specialist accountant to obtain advance assurance from HMRC before approaching EIS investors — confirming eligibility before investment is made.
