📚 Key Legal Terms
📬
Investor Update
A regular written communication — typically monthly — sent by a founder to current investors and advisers, reporting on the company's performance, key wins, challenges, and specific asks. Effective investor updates are concise (one to two pages), honest (acknowledging problems alongside progress), and action-oriented (including a specific 'asks' section with help the founder needs from their network). Investors who are well-informed are more likely to make introductions, provide strategic advice, and follow on in future rounds. Founders who stop sending updates typically damage investor relationships more than any business setback would.
🤝
Warm Introduction
An introduction to a potential investor, customer, or partner made by a mutual contact who can vouch for both parties — as opposed to a cold approach with no prior relationship. A warm introduction dramatically increases the probability of a meeting: investors who receive hundreds of cold emails per week will prioritise a request that arrives with a personal endorsement from someone they know and trust. The most valuable skill in building a startup network is learning how to ask for introductions in a way that makes the introducer comfortable and makes the recipient want to respond.
❄️
Cold Outreach
An unsolicited approach to a potential investor, customer, or partner with whom the sender has no prior relationship — typically by email, LinkedIn, or direct message. Cold outreach requires exceptional craft: the recipient has no prior reason to engage, so the message must be relevant, specific, and brief. The most common errors: generic messages that could have been sent to anyone ('I am reaching out because I think you would be a great fit for...'), too long, and no clear call to action. The best cold emails are 3-5 sentences, reference something specific about the recipient, and ask for one small, specific thing.
📋
One-Pager
A single-page document — typically one side of A4 or a single scroll on a screen — that summarises a startup's opportunity, product, and ask in a scannable format. One-pagers are used as a leave-behind after meetings, as the attachment to a cold email, or as a teaser before sending a full pitch deck. They are not a replacement for the pitch deck but a filter — a well-designed one-pager should generate enough interest for the recipient to request the full deck or a meeting.
🔁
Retrospective
A structured team meeting — typically held at the end of a sprint, quarter, or project — to reflect on what worked, what did not work, and what should change going forward. The most common retrospective format is 'start, stop, continue': what should the team start doing, stop doing, and continue doing? Retrospectives are a cornerstone of agile development methodology and, when run well, create a culture of continuous improvement and psychological safety by normalising honest, constructive feedback.
🎯
OKR
Objective and Key Results — a goal-setting framework in which an organisation, team, or individual sets an ambitious qualitative Objective (a direction or aspiration) and defines two to five quantitative Key Results (specific, measurable outcomes that indicate the objective has been achieved). OKRs were popularised by Intel under Andy Grove and adopted by Google from its earliest days. Unlike KPIs (which measure ongoing operational performance), OKRs are typically time-bounded (quarterly or annual) and should be aspirational — a team that achieves 70% of an OKR is considered to have set it correctly.
📰
Press Release
A formal written announcement issued by a company to journalists and media outlets — typically to announce a significant milestone such as a funding round, product launch, partnership, or executive appointment. For startups, press releases are less about traditional media coverage (which is declining) and more about creating a permanent public record of milestones, generating SEO-value content, and providing a credible external reference for investors, customers, and potential hires. The inverted pyramid structure (most important information first) is the standard format.
📝
Decision Memo
A short written document — typically one to six pages — that frames a decision, presents the relevant evidence and analysis, states the options considered, and recommends a course of action. Amazon's culture of six-page narrative memos (rather than PowerPoint presentations) for major decisions is the most cited example. The discipline of writing forces the author to think through the decision rigorously before presenting it; reading the memo silently at the start of a meeting ensures every participant has the same information before discussion begins.
💬
All-Hands Meeting
A company-wide meeting — held weekly, monthly, or quarterly depending on company size and culture — at which the founding team shares company-wide updates on performance, strategy, and culture with the entire organisation. Effective all-hands meetings are transparent (sharing real numbers and honest assessments), two-way (including Q&A and anonymous questions), and culture-reinforcing (celebrating team achievements and reinforcing company values). As companies scale beyond 20-30 people, all-hands meetings become the primary vehicle for the founding team to communicate culture directly to the full organisation.
📊
Board Update
A formal presentation or written report prepared by the founding team for the company's board of directors, typically presented at quarterly board meetings. The board update covers: company performance against plan (revenue, growth, key metrics), financial position (cash, runway, burn rate), strategic priorities for the next quarter, key risks, and any decisions requiring board approval. Board updates force founders to communicate their business with rigour and objectivity — the discipline of preparing for an informed, challenging audience sharpens strategic thinking.
🗺️
Stakeholder Map
A visual or written tool that identifies all individuals and organisations with an interest in or influence over a company's activities — including investors, customers, employees, regulators, media, and community members — and assesses their level of influence and alignment with the company's goals. Stakeholder mapping is particularly important before major announcements (fundraising rounds, product changes, pivots) because it helps founders identify who needs to be communicated with, in what order, and in what way. Mismanaging key stakeholders — such as communicating a pivot to customers before employees — can cause significant damage.
🔍
Customer Discovery
The process of learning from potential customers before building a product — through structured interviews, observation, and prototype testing — to understand their problems, needs, and current solutions before assuming what to build. The term was popularised by Steve Blank and further developed in Eric Ries's The Lean Startup. Effective customer discovery requires active listening, open-ended questions, and a willingness to be surprised by what customers actually say — as opposed to what the founder assumed they would say. The classic error is asking leading questions ('Would you use a product that...?') that produce answers that confirm the founder's assumptions.
📤
Outbound Sales
A sales approach in which the company proactively reaches out to potential customers — through cold email, phone, LinkedIn, events, or other channels — rather than waiting for inbound inquiries. Outbound sales requires a clear ideal customer profile, a systematic prospecting process, a compelling opening message, and a structured follow-up cadence. For early-stage startups, outbound sales by the founder (rather than a dedicated salesperson) is often called 'founder-led sales' — and is considered essential for building the deep customer understanding needed to shape the product.
📥
Inbound Marketing
A marketing approach that attracts potential customers through valuable content, search engine optimisation, social media, and community building — rather than proactively reaching out to them. Inbound marketing creates a 'pull' rather than 'push' dynamic: customers discover the company because of the value it provides, and arrive with higher intent and lower acquisition cost than outbound-sourced leads. Blog content, podcast appearances, LinkedIn thought leadership, and SEO-optimised resources are common inbound channels for B2B SaaS startups.
🗣️
Founder Story
A personal narrative that explains why the founder is uniquely positioned to solve the problem their startup addresses — typically involving a specific moment of professional frustration, personal experience, or insight that motivated them to start the company. An authentic, specific founder story builds credibility with investors ('you have genuine domain expertise and personal conviction'), resonates with customers ('this person understands my problem because they have lived it'), and attracts talent ('this founder has a compelling reason why this company exists'). Generic founder stories ('I saw a gap in the market') are significantly less persuasive than personal ones.
🔗
Partnership Proposal
A structured written document or presentation proposing a commercial or strategic relationship between two organisations — outlining the mutual value, the specific terms proposed, and the next steps. Partnership proposals require the writer to understand what the potential partner values and to frame the proposal in terms of the partner's objectives rather than the company's own. The most common error: describing what the startup gains from the partnership rather than what the partner gains. A persuasive partnership proposal answers: why is this good for you, specifically, and why should you trust us to deliver?
📧
Email Register
The level of formality, vocabulary, and tone appropriate to a specific email communication — ranging from very formal (official letters, legal notices) to professional (investor communication, customer emails) to semi-formal (team communication) to casual (messages to close colleagues). Choosing the wrong register is a common communication error: an email that is too formal feels cold and creates distance; an email that is too casual in a professional context signals lack of awareness. Startup communication typically operates in a professional-but-direct register — clear, specific, and respectful without the elaborate formality of traditional corporate correspondence.
🏃
Async Communication
Written communication that does not require an immediate response — allowing participants to engage on their own schedule rather than simultaneously. Async-first communication (documented in writing, available to review at any time) is the default mode for remote and distributed teams, and has been championed by companies including Basecamp, GitLab, and Notion. Well-written async communication replaces synchronous meetings for most status updates, decisions, and feedback cycles — reducing context-switching for engineering teams and creating a searchable record of decisions. The discipline required is higher: a well-written Notion document or Loom video replaces a 30-minute meeting, but must be clear and self-contained.
📣
Company Narrative
The overarching story of why a company exists, what it believes, and where it is going — the strategic and cultural framing that unifies all external and internal communication. A strong company narrative provides the context within which individual messages (product updates, investor communications, recruitment materials) make sense. It typically includes: the problem the company exists to solve, the vision for what the world looks like when the problem is solved, the founding team's unique perspective on how to solve it, and the values that guide how the work is done. The company narrative is not a mission statement — it is a living story that evolves as the company does.
📏
Writing Style Guide
A document defining the conventions for written communication within or on behalf of a company — covering vocabulary preferences, tone of voice, formatting standards, punctuation conventions, and dos and don'ts for specific communication types. Style guides are typically maintained by the marketing or communications team and serve as a reference for anyone writing on behalf of the brand. For startups, having a clear writing style guide early — even a one-page version — creates consistency across external communications before the team is large enough to maintain it through osmosis.
🔑
Key Message
The single most important point that a communication — whether a pitch, an investor update, a customer email, or an all-hands presentation — is designed to leave with its audience. Every effective communication is built around a key message: all other content supports, evidences, or contextualises it. Founders who cannot articulate their key message before writing a communication typically produce documents that are long, unfocused, and fail to move the reader to action. The discipline of identifying the key message first is the foundation of clear, effective startup writing.

Text 1: The Investor Update — The Most Neglected Communication in Startup Life

Of all the communications a founder sends, the monthly investor update is among the most valuable and the most neglected. It is valuable because it maintains the relationships that make fundraising, introductions, and strategic support possible — an investor who knows your business, trusts your judgment, and has watched your progress over months is incomparably more likely to introduce you to their network, provide a warm reference, or follow on in a future round than one who has not heard from you since the investment closed. It is neglected because founders facing difficult months — which is most months in a startup — find it psychologically uncomfortable to write honestly about their challenges.

The structure of an effective investor update is simple and should not change significantly from month to month: highlights (what went well and what the metrics show), lowlights (what did not go as planned and why), key metrics (the two to four numbers that matter most for the business — MRR growth, churn, pipeline, burn), asks (the specific things the founder needs from their investor network — introductions, candidates, advice), and a brief look ahead (what the company is focused on in the coming month). This structure should be maintained even when the news is bad — particularly when the news is bad. An investor who reads three months of strong updates and then receives nothing for two months will assume the worst.

The single most common mistake in investor updates is omitting the lowlights section. Founders who report only wins are telling an incomplete story that experienced investors immediately recognise as such. The counter-intuitive truth is that honest reporting of problems — accompanied by a clear-eyed assessment of what is being done about them — builds more investor confidence than a sanitised success story. The reasoning is simple: investors know that every startup has problems. What they are trying to determine is whether this founder has the self-awareness to see them, the judgment to prioritise them, and the resilience to address them. The investor update is the primary evidence they have.

Text 2: The Art of Direct Communication — Why Startup Writing Must Be Different from Corporate Writing

Most founders have spent years inside large organisations before starting their companies — and they bring with them the communication habits of that world. Corporate communication is, by design, cautious, layered, and consensus-driven: messages are softened to avoid giving offence, ambiguity is preserved to maintain flexibility, and passive voice creates institutional distance from accountability. This approach is well-suited to large, complex organisations with multiple hierarchies and constituencies to manage. It is poorly suited to startups, where speed, clarity, and directness are competitive advantages.

Startup writing must be direct in a specific way: it must state the point first, support it with the minimum evidence required, and make the action required immediately clear. The classic corporate email buries the point in paragraph three after two paragraphs of context-setting. The startup equivalent puts the point in the subject line and the first sentence, uses the body to provide the minimum necessary evidence, and ends with a single, unambiguous call to action. This is not rudeness — it is respect for the reader's time. In a startup where everyone has too much to do, unclear communication is an expensive tax on collective attention.

The most consequential communication in which directness matters is delivering difficult news — a missed milestone, a product failure, a strategic change that will affect team members' roles. Corporate organisations manage difficult news through layers of softening language ('we are exploring options'), delayed announcements ('we will share more information in due course'), and passive constructions that avoid identifying who decided what ('a decision has been made to...'). Startups that adopt these habits damage the psychological safety that makes innovation possible. Teams who know that leaders will communicate difficult truths directly and early develop the trust that allows them to take risks, experiment, and share their own bad news before it becomes a crisis.

Grammar Point 1: Signposting Language in Complex Startup Communications
Structuring Multi-Part Updates and Reports

Investor updates, board papers, and all-hands presentations have multiple sections covering different topics. Signposting language tells the reader where they are in the document, where they are going next, and how sections relate to each other.

Opening a section: 'Turning to the product update...' / 'On the subject of hiring...' / 'Regarding the enterprise pipeline...'
Sequencing: 'Firstly... / Secondly... / Finally...' (formal) OR 'First... / Then... / And finally...' (semi-formal)
Transitioning: 'That said...' / 'With that context...' / 'Moving on...' / 'Turning now to...'
Summarising before moving on: 'In summary, the product track is on plan. Turning to commercial...' (keeps the reader oriented)
Contrast and Qualification Signposts

Honest communication acknowledges both positive and negative developments. Contrast signposts signal that a qualification follows, preparing the reader before the difficult news arrives.

'Revenue grew 23% last month. However, churn increased to 4.8%.' (blunt, effective)
'We hit the MRR target. That said, two enterprise accounts may downgrade at renewal.' (softer qualification)
'The product shipped on time. The integration, however, took longer than expected.' (mid-sentence contrast)
'While the overall metrics are positive, three specific areas require attention.' (frames difficulty before introducing it)'
Precision Signposts — Clarifying and Emphasising

When a statement needs clarification or emphasis, precision signposts make the nuance explicit — preventing misreading.

'Importantly, this is a channel-mix issue, not a conversion problem.' (signals that what follows is key)
'To clarify: the 14% miss was entirely in the SME segment — enterprise is ahead of plan.' (corrects potential misreading)
'Specifically, we need introductions to heads of compliance at tier-1 banks.' (narrows a broad statement)
'In other words: the product works; the pricing does not.' (plain-language restatement of a complex point)

Grammar Point 2: Conditional Structures for Persuasive Requests
'If' and 'Should' for Polite Professional Requests

Professional requests in startup contexts — for introductions, meetings, partnerships, or support — use conditional structures rather than direct imperatives ('Please introduce me to...') to signal respect for the recipient's choice. 'Should' is slightly more formal than 'if'.

'If you know anyone at the legal ops team at HSBC, an introduction would be enormously helpful.' (open condition)
'Should you have five minutes to review our one-pager, I would love your reaction.' (formal, polite)
'If you feel the timing is not right, I would completely understand — and would welcome the chance to revisit in Q2.' (respects choice to decline)
'Should you be interested in discussing this further, I am happy to set up a call at your convenience.' (formal invitation)'
'Were' for Hypothetical Value Propositions

'Were + subject + to + infinitive' (the formal 'Type 2' conditional) is used in partnership proposals and advisory board requests to frame the value of a relationship hypothetically — as a way of describing what the arrangement could enable without presuming it will happen.

'Were you to join our advisory board, you would have direct input into our enterprise roadmap.' (partnership proposal)
'Were we to agree on the commercial terms this week, we could include you in Tuesday's launch announcement.' (time-sensitive opportunity)
'Were this to progress to a partnership, the integration would take no more than four hours of engineering time.' (removes adoption barrier)
'Were you to make this introduction, I believe it would be genuinely valuable for both parties.' (framing for the introducer)'
Softening Requests Without Weakening Them

The most effective professional requests are both polite and clear — they respect the recipient's autonomy while making it easy for them to say yes. Key structures that achieve this balance:

'I would be most grateful if you could...' (formal and respectful)
'I would not ask unless I genuinely believed this would be valuable for both sides.' (addresses anticipated hesitation)
'The integration should take no more than four hours — we have built the SDK specifically to minimise your team's time.' (pre-answers the objection before it is raised)
'I can work around your schedule entirely.' (removes the logistical barrier to engagement)

Grammar Point 3: Delivering Difficult Messages — Structure and Precision
Point-First Structure for Difficult News

Corporate communication often buries difficult news in the middle or softens it with extensive context first. Startup communication should deliver the difficult news first, then provide context and solution — respecting the reader's time and their ability to handle directness.

'We missed our Q3 revenue target by 14%. Here is what happened and what we are doing about it.' (headline first)
'I want to be direct: we have six months of runway and need to make a decision within 60 days.' (stakes upfront)
'The enterprise pilot did not go as planned. Specifically: [the precise issue and cause].' (problem then precision)
'This is difficult news: we are not on track to reach our Series A milestones on the original timeline.' (clear, non-euphemistic)'
Taking Ownership Without Self-Flagellation

Effective accountability language owns the problem clearly without excessive apology or self-criticism — which can erode confidence. The pattern: acknowledge responsibility + explain the decision made + describe what changes.

'I take ownership of the pricing decision that contributed to the miss.' (clean ownership)
'I set the price too high, based on early research that did not reflect the broader market. We are fixing this.' (cause + correction)
'This was my call, and it was the wrong call. Here is what I would do differently.' (direct but not self-flagellating)
'The decision to delay the enterprise launch was mine — I will explain the reasoning and what we have learned.' (owns the choice and commits to transparency)'
Closing Difficult Messages — Action, Not Apology

Difficult messages lose impact when they end with excessive apology or vague reassurance. Effective closings are forward-looking: they end with a concrete next step, a timeline, or an invitation to respond.

'I will send a full post-mortem by Friday. In the meantime, please reach out with any questions.' (next step + open door)
'We have a clear plan and I am confident in our ability to address this. Here is the plan: [specific steps].' (reassurance through specificity)
'If you have concerns about this, I would rather address them directly — please reply or call me.' (invites engagement rather than avoidance)
'The key message is this: the business is fundamentally healthy, the miss is addressable, and we have a plan.' (summary closes on confidence, not apology)

Case Study 1: Amazon's Six-Page Memo Culture — How Jeff Bezos Banned PowerPoint and Improved Decision Quality
Why one of the world's largest companies outlawed presentation software — and what it teaches about the relationship between writing and thinking

Background

At some point in Amazon's early growth, Jeff Bezos made a decision that surprised many of his executives: he banned PowerPoint presentations from senior leadership meetings. In place of presentations, every meeting that required a significant decision would begin with the team reading a written narrative memo — typically four to six pages, written in prose rather than bullets — in silence for 15 to 20 minutes. The reasoning was explicit: PowerPoint presentations allow the presenter to hide behind bullet points, glossing over complexity and concealing weak thinking with confident delivery. A well-written narrative memo, by contrast, cannot hide behind a skilled presenter — if the thinking is shallow, the prose will reveal it.

The Memo as a Thinking Tool

Amazon's memo culture produced two benefits that Bezos considered essential for a high-growth company. First, the discipline of writing a full narrative forces the author to think through every aspect of a proposal before presenting it — including the objections, the alternatives, the data, and the assumptions. A presenter who builds slides can skip over the parts they have not figured out; a writer who builds a narrative cannot. The blank page forces completeness. Second, reading the memo silently at the start of the meeting ensures that every participant in the discussion has received the same information and had the same time to absorb it — eliminating the advantage of having heard the information before and the disadvantage of processing information aurally while simultaneously formulating a response. The discussion that follows a shared silent reading is qualitatively different from the discussion that follows a presentation: more precise, more equal, and more focused on the merits of the proposal rather than the charisma of the presenter.

Lessons for Startup Communication

Amazon's memo culture offers specific lessons for startup founders at all stages. First, the discipline of writing to think — of using the act of writing to clarify thinking rather than simply to communicate it — is available to any founder at any scale. The decision to write a four-paragraph note to yourself before making a major strategic decision produces better outcomes than making the same decision in a conversation. Second, the memo replaces the meeting: a well-written decision memo that circulates in advance means the meeting is used for decision and action rather than for information transfer. Third, the connection between writing quality and thinking quality is not cosmetic — clear writing requires and reveals clear thinking, and unclear writing reveals confused thinking. For founders, this is both a personal discipline and a hiring signal: the quality of a candidate's writing sample in an interview process is one of the most reliable indicators of their analytical quality.

Case Study 2: Basecamp and Asynchronous Communication — How a Remote-First Company Wrote Its Way to Operational Excellence
How Jason Fried and David Heinemeier Hansson built a profitable software company of 60 people without a single office — and what their communication philosophy teaches every startup

Background

Basecamp — a project management software company founded by Jason Fried and David Heinemeier Hansson (creator of Ruby on Rails) — has operated as a fully remote company since its founding in 1999 and grew to 60 employees and millions of users without ever opening an office. Fried and Hansson have written extensively about their communication philosophy in two books: Remote (2013) and It Doesn't Have to Be Crazy at Work (2018). Their central argument is that synchronous communication — the assumption that work happens in real time, through meetings and instant messaging — is one of the most destructive forces in knowledge work. It fragments attention, creates dependency on presence, and systematically advantages the loudest voices over the most thoughtful ones.

The Async-First Philosophy

Basecamp's communication approach is built on the principle that good thinking requires sustained, uninterrupted attention — and that most communication that organisations treat as synchronous (requiring immediate response) does not actually need to be. The implications are profound: internal decisions are communicated in long-form written posts on Basecamp's own platform, where team members read and respond at their own pace; meetings are treated as a last resort rather than a default; and the written record of every discussion is preserved and searchable. This approach has three specific advantages for startup communication. First, it forces clarity: you cannot hide behind confident delivery in a written post. Second, it creates a searchable record of decisions and reasoning that new employees can access and that prevents the organisational amnesia (forgetting why decisions were made) that plagues growing companies. Third, it levels the playing field: in a meeting, the most senior or most verbose participant tends to dominate; in an async written discussion, every contribution is equal in format even if not in authority.

Lessons for Startup Founders

Basecamp's async philosophy offers specific, practical lessons for startup founders. First, not every problem that currently generates a meeting actually requires one — the discipline of asking 'could this be a written post?' before scheduling a meeting creates immediate productivity gains. Second, the quality of a startup's internal written communication is a direct indicator of the quality of its thinking — companies where nobody writes clearly are often companies where nobody thinks clearly. Third, the asynchronous record of decisions is one of the most undervalued assets of a growing company: knowing why a decision was made, with what information, is essential for maintaining strategic coherence as teams grow and founders' bandwidth is stretched. Fourth, remote-first communication does not require a remote-first organisation — any startup benefits from treating written communication as a first-class discipline rather than an afterthought.
