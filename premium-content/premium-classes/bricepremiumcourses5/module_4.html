📚 Key Legal Terms
🎯
Pitch
A structured presentation by a founder to potential investors, designed to communicate the business opportunity, team, traction, and funding ask in a compelling and concise way. Pitches range from a 30-second elevator pitch (a spontaneous, informal summary) to a formal 20-minute investor presentation with a slide deck. The goal of a pitch is not to close an investment on the spot — it is to earn the next meeting. Investors are primarily evaluating the founder as much as the business.
📊
Pitch Deck
A visual presentation — typically 10 to 15 slides — used to communicate the key elements of a startup's business to investors. Standard sections: problem, solution, market size, product, business model, traction, team, financials, and the ask. The Sequoia Capital pitch deck framework and Guy Kawasaki's 10/20/30 rule (10 slides, 20 minutes, 30-point minimum font) are widely referenced standards. An effective pitch deck tells a story — it does not simply present information.
👼
Angel Round
An early-stage investment typically made by individual high-net-worth investors (angel investors) — often at pre-seed or seed stage — before institutional venture capital becomes involved. Angel rounds are typically smaller (£25,000 to £2 million), faster to close (weeks rather than months), and more relationship-driven than institutional rounds. In the UK, the Enterprise Investment Scheme (EIS) and Seed Enterprise Investment Scheme (SEIS) provide significant tax incentives that have made angel investing substantially more attractive.
📋
Due Diligence
The investigation and verification process conducted by investors before committing capital — examining the company's legal structure, financials, technology, team, contracts, IP ownership, and any outstanding liabilities or disputes. Due diligence for a seed round is relatively light; for a Series A or B, it is comprehensive and may involve external lawyers, accountants, and technical experts. Founders who have maintained clean, organised records significantly accelerate the due diligence process.
💼
Lead Investor
The investor who commits the largest portion of a funding round, sets the terms (typically through a term sheet), and often takes a board seat or board observer role. Finding a lead investor is typically the hardest part of a funding round: once a credible lead commits, other investors can follow more easily. A lead investor signals credibility to the rest of the market — if a respected fund is leading, it significantly reduces the perceived risk for co-investors.
📑
Term Sheet
A non-binding document that sets out the proposed terms of an investment — including the investment amount, pre-money valuation, equity stake, board composition, investor rights (pro-rata, anti-dilution, information rights), and any special conditions. The term sheet is the starting point for negotiation between founders and investors. Founders should understand every clause before agreeing to any term — liquidation preferences, anti-dilution provisions, and board composition have significant long-term implications.
🔢
Cap Table
Short for capitalisation table — a spreadsheet or document showing the ownership structure of a company, listing all shareholders, the number and class of shares they hold, and their percentage ownership. The cap table must be maintained accurately from founding and updated with each new investment round. A messy or incorrectly maintained cap table is a serious red flag during due diligence. Founders should model how future funding rounds will dilute their ownership before accepting investment.
📉
Dilution
The reduction in an existing shareholder's percentage ownership of a company that occurs when new shares are issued — typically to new investors, employees (via options), or advisers. Dilution is not inherently bad: if new shares are issued at a higher valuation, the existing shareholders' percentage decreases but the value of their remaining stake may be higher. However, excessive early dilution — through poorly structured rounds or over-generous option grants — can leave founders with insufficient ownership to remain motivated or financially rewarded for their success.
💰
Liquidation Preference
A provision in an investment agreement that determines the order in which investors and founders receive proceeds in an exit (acquisition or IPO) — and how much. A 1x non-participating liquidation preference means the investor receives their investment back before founders receive anything, but once recouped, they convert to equity and participate alongside founders. A 2x participating liquidation preference means the investor receives twice their investment back AND participates in the remaining proceeds as equity — significantly disadvantaging founders in smaller exits.
🎟️
Pro-Rata Right
The right of an existing investor to participate in future funding rounds to maintain their percentage ownership — by investing their pro-rata share of the new round. Pro-rata rights protect early investors from dilution in later rounds and are particularly valuable when a company is growing well: a seed investor who has pro-rata rights can increase their stake in a hot Series A. Founders should understand that granting pro-rata rights to many early investors can complicate later rounds by increasing the number of parties who must be accommodated.
📈
Pre-Money Valuation
The value attributed to a company before a new investment is made. If a company has a £4 million pre-money valuation and raises £1 million, the post-money valuation is £5 million and the new investor owns 20% (£1m / £5m). Pre-money valuation is the primary negotiation point between founders and investors: founders want it high (to minimise dilution); investors want it low (to maximise their equity stake). Early-stage valuations are highly subjective and typically reflect the team, market, traction, and comparable company benchmarks.
🔄
Convertible Note
A debt instrument — a loan to the startup — that converts into equity at a future funding round rather than requiring immediate repayment. Convertible notes typically include: an interest rate (often 5-8%), a maturity date (when repayment is due if conversion has not occurred), a discount rate (typically 10-25% off the next round's share price), and a valuation cap (the maximum valuation at which the note converts, protecting early investors from excessive dilution at high future valuations). Convertible notes are faster and cheaper to close than priced equity rounds.
📋
SAFE
Simple Agreement for Future Equity — an instrument created by Y Combinator in 2013 that allows investors to provide capital to a startup in exchange for the right to receive equity at a future priced round, without the debt structure of a convertible note (no interest, no maturity date). The SAFE has two key terms: the valuation cap (the maximum valuation at which the SAFE converts) and the discount rate (the percentage discount on the next round's share price). SAFEs have become the dominant instrument for pre-seed and seed investment in the US and are increasingly common in the UK.
🏆
Traction
Evidence of real-world momentum that demonstrates a startup is finding product-market fit and growing — typically expressed through metrics that investors can evaluate against comparable companies. Traction metrics vary by business model: SaaS companies show MRR growth and NRR; marketplaces show GMV and take rate; consumer apps show DAU/MAU and retention. The question 'what is your traction?' is one of the most important in any investor pitch — founders should know their key metrics precisely and be able to explain the trajectory.
💵
Ask
The amount of funding a startup is seeking in a specific round — typically expressed as a total round size and sometimes accompanied by the proposed use of funds. 'We are raising a £1.5 million seed round' is the ask. A credible ask is specific, justified by a detailed use-of-funds breakdown, and linked to what the capital will enable the company to achieve (the milestones). An ask that is not linked to milestones raises the question of why that specific amount is needed — suggesting the founder has not planned the use of capital carefully.
🤝
Smart Money
Capital that comes with strategic value beyond the financial investment — including industry expertise, a relevant network, operational experience, introductions to potential customers or future investors, and board-level mentorship. Smart money investors actively add value between funding rounds; 'dumb money' investors merely provide capital. For early-stage founders, the quality and relevance of an investor's network and expertise can be as important as the valuation they offer. The best investors open doors that would otherwise take years to open.
📣
Narrative
The story structure that connects a pitch's key elements into a compelling, coherent journey — from the problem to the solution to the market to the team to the ask. The most effective investor pitches are not presentations of information — they are stories with a clear protagonist (the customer), an antagonist (the problem), a resolution (the product), and a call to action (the investment ask). Founders who have a strong command of their narrative can handle difficult questions without losing the thread of their pitch.
❓
Investor Q&A
The question and answer session that follows a formal investor pitch — often the most important part of the interaction. Investors use Q&A to probe assumptions, test the founder's depth of knowledge, identify weaknesses the deck glossed over, and assess how the founder responds under pressure. Founders should prepare for Q&A as rigorously as the pitch itself — anticipating the toughest questions (competition, unit economics, team gaps, exit strategy) and preparing honest, confident responses. 'I don't know but I'll find out by tomorrow' is a better answer than an unconvincing defence.
🔍
Investor Fit
The alignment between a startup's stage, sector, geography, and business model and a specific investor's mandate, portfolio, and thesis. Cold pitching an investor who does not invest in your sector, stage, or geography is inefficient — the rejection is not a signal about the quality of your business. Research investor fit before approaching: check the investor's portfolio, stated thesis, typical check size, and recent investments. A warm introduction from a portfolio company founder significantly increases the chance of a meeting.
📊
Metrics
The quantitative measurements that demonstrate a startup's performance and growth — presented in investor pitches to provide evidence of traction and business model health. Key metrics vary by business model but commonly include: monthly recurring revenue (MRR) and its growth rate, customer acquisition cost (CAC), customer lifetime value (LTV), net revenue retention (NRR), gross margin, churn rate, and daily or monthly active users. Founders must know their metrics precisely — rounding or hedging on metrics during a pitch signals either poor financial management or lack of confidence.
🎪
Demo Day
A structured event — typically organised by an accelerator or investor network — at which a cohort of startups present 3-7 minute pitches simultaneously to a large audience of investors. Y Combinator's Demo Day is the most prominent example: hundreds of investors attend, funding decisions are made rapidly, and the competitive pressure of Demo Day forces founders to prepare their pitches to a high standard. Demo Day pitches are highly condensed — every word must earn its place — and are typically followed by individual investor meetings.

Text 1: What Investors Are Really Evaluating — Beyond the Slides

Founders preparing for investor pitches often spend weeks perfecting their slide decks — agonising over font choices, debating whether to include the competitive matrix, and rehearsing transitions. This is understandable: the pitch deck is the most tangible output of pitch preparation, and it is the artefact that circulates among investors before and after meetings. But experienced investors consistently report that the pitch deck is far less important than founders believe. What investors are actually evaluating — particularly at early stage — is the founder.

The founder evaluation happens across several dimensions. First, domain expertise: does this person understand the problem they are solving at a level that suggests they have an information advantage over others who might attempt the same thing? The best early-stage founders know something about their market that others do not — a specific insight derived from years of professional experience, an unusual data point from primary research, or a counterintuitive observation about how customers actually behave. Second, commercial instinct: can this person sell? Not just the product to customers but the vision to employees, partners, and the investors themselves. The ability to communicate compellingly and to listen actively to what investors are signalling is a predictor of the founder's ability to navigate the hundreds of high-stakes conversations that building a company requires. Third, resilience: how does this person respond to challenge? Investors routinely ask difficult questions not because they are hostile but because they need to see how the founder behaves under pressure — whether they become defensive, crumble, or engage thoughtfully and adapt.

Fourth — and most underappreciated — intellectual honesty. Founders who present a flawless picture of their business, who have an answer to every question and acknowledge no uncertainty, typically raise red flags rather than confidence. Experienced investors know that every early-stage business has significant unknowns. A founder who acknowledges what they do not know, who distinguishes between what the data shows and what they believe, and who is honest about the risks the business faces is demonstrating the quality of judgment that will serve the company well through the years of uncertainty ahead. Paradoxically, intellectual honesty about weaknesses often makes a stronger pitch than an unrealistically polished presentation.

Text 2: The Structure of a Compelling Investor Pitch — Narrative, Evidence, and the Ask

The most effective investor pitches follow a narrative arc — a story that begins with a problem, builds through a solution and evidence, and culminates in a specific ask. This structure is not arbitrary: it mirrors the cognitive process through which investment decisions are made. An investor who does not feel the problem acutely will not care about the solution. An investor who is not shown evidence that the solution works will not trust the projections. An investor who is not given a clear, justified ask will not know how to proceed. Each section of the pitch earns the attention for the next.

The problem slide is the most important — and the most commonly mishandled. Many founders either rush through it ('there is a large and growing market') or overcomplicate it with technical detail that only they can appreciate. The most effective problem presentations make the investor feel the problem: they tell the story of a specific customer who is experiencing it right now, with specific consequences (time wasted, money lost, risk incurred). 'Marketing managers at mid-sized companies spend 14 hours per week on reporting tasks that could be automated. That is 35% of their working week — time they cannot spend on the campaigns that actually grow revenue.' This specificity is what creates the emotional engagement that distinguishes memorable pitches from forgettable ones.

The ask — the final slide — is where many founders undermine an otherwise strong pitch. The two most common errors: asking for too broad a range ('we are raising between £500,000 and £2 million') which signals uncertainty about the business plan; and failing to link the ask to specific milestones ('we are raising £1.5 million to grow') which fails to demonstrate that the capital has been deliberately sized. A strong ask states the amount, the round structure, the use of funds in three to five clear categories, and the milestones the capital will enable. 'We are raising a £1.5 million seed round. The capital will be used to hire a VP of Sales (£180,000), build the enterprise features required for our next five pipeline customers (£280,000), and fund 18 months of runway. By month 18, we will have reached £500,000 ARR and be Series A-ready.' This is an ask that an investor can evaluate.

Grammar Point 1: Persuasive Narrative Structures in Investor Pitches
Opening with the Problem — Creating Emotional Engagement

The most effective pitch openings make the investor feel the problem before evaluating the solution. This requires specific narrative techniques: a precise statistic that quantifies the problem, a concrete human story of the affected customer, and a transitional phrase that positions the startup as the resolution. Generic market size statements do not achieve this.

'Every year, UK businesses lose £6.4 billion to invoice fraud — and most finance teams have no way to detect it until after the money has left the account.'
'Imagine being a CFO who has just discovered that her company paid a fraudulent supplier £45,000 over eight months.'
'That is the problem we exist to solve.' (positioning the transition from problem to solution)
'Here is what most people do not know about how HR managers spend their time...' (creating the information gap)
Building Through Evidence — The Transition to Traction

The most credible transition from problem to solution to traction uses specific numbers, not adjectives. 'Our growth has been strong' is an assertion; 'MRR grew from £8,000 to £42,000 in eight months' is evidence. The language of traction is precise, present-tense, and self-contained.

'In eight months, we have acquired 34 paying enterprise customers.' (specific, measurable, time-bounded)
'Monthly recurring revenue stands at £42,000, growing at 18% month-on-month.' (current state + growth rate)
'Net revenue retention is 114% — our existing customers grow our revenue without any new acquisition.' (metric + plain-language explanation)
'No single customer represents more than 8% of our revenue.' (addressing concentration risk proactively)
The Ask — Structuring the Close

The final section of a pitch must be specific, justified, and milestone-linked. Founders who present a clear, deliberate ask signal financial competence; those who present a range or a vague use-of-funds signal the opposite.

'We are raising £1.8 million in a seed round.' (specific amount, specific round stage)
'The capital will be used for three things: hiring a VP of Sales and three account executives, completing the enterprise API integration, and funding 18 months of runway.' (specific categories, not vague descriptions)
'By month 18, we will have reached £500,000 ARR — the milestone needed to be credibly Series A-ready.' (milestone that justifies the investment)
'We have £600,000 committed and are looking for a lead investor to complete the round.' (current state of the raise — transparent about momentum)

Grammar Point 2: Handling Investor Questions — Confident, Honest, and Precise
Buying Time and Acknowledging the Question

When an investor asks a difficult question, the instinct to answer immediately can lead to imprecise or defensive responses. Brief acknowledgement phrases buy thinking time and signal that the founder takes the question seriously.

'That is a great question — let me address that directly.' (acknowledging the quality of the question)
'You are right to probe that — it is one of the key risks we have thought carefully about.' (acknowledging the legitimacy)
'Let me make sure I understand what you are asking...' (clarifying before answering — prevents misaligned responses)
'I want to give you a precise answer on that, so let me think for a moment.' (signalling that precision matters)'
Admitting Uncertainty Without Losing Confidence

The most credible founders acknowledge what they do not know while demonstrating that they know how to find out. The key is to pair the acknowledgement with a concrete next step — signalling intellectual honesty and operational competence simultaneously.

'I do not have that exact figure with me today, but I will send it to you this evening.' (honest + concrete follow-up)
'We have not yet fully modelled the European market — that is on the roadmap for Q3.' (honest + timeline)
'That is an assumption we have not tested directly — though it is consistent with what we have seen from comparable companies.' (honest + contextualised)
'We have an early hypothesis on that but not enough data yet to be confident. Here is our current thinking...' (honest + showing active thinking)'
Handling the Toughest Questions — Competition, Churn, and Exit

Three questions appear in almost every investor pitch: 'What about [competitor]?', 'What is your churn?', and 'What is your exit strategy?'. Each requires a specific type of response.

Competition: 'We know [competitor] well. They are stronger in [X] but have not addressed [Y], which is our customers' primary pain point. Our product is specifically designed for the segment they cannot serve.'
Churn: 'Our monthly churn is 3.2%. Here is what drives it and what we are doing about it: [specific analysis and action plan].' (own the number — never round or hedge)
Exit: 'We are building a business to last — not optimising for a quick exit. That said, the natural acquirers in this space are [list], and comparable companies have been acquired at [multiple] ARR.' (demonstrate long-term thinking + commercial awareness)
Valuation pushback: 'We are comfortable with our valuation based on [comparable companies] and [our traction]. We are happy to discuss the terms further, but we are not prepared to accept a significant haircut on the pre-money at this stage of our growth.'

Grammar Point 3: Precise Language for Presenting Metrics in Pitches
Specific Metric Presentation — Avoiding Vagueness

Metric language in investor pitches must be precise. Vague language signals weak financial management or lack of confidence; precise language builds credibility. The key disciplines: always specify the time period, always specify what the metric measures, and always provide context (growth rate, benchmark comparison, or explanation of the driver).

'MRR grew from £8,000 to £42,000 over eight months — a 425% increase.' (NOT 'revenue is growing strongly')
'NRR stands at 114%, meaning existing customers grow our revenue by 14% annually without new acquisition.' (metric + plain-language explanation)
'CAC has fallen from £340 to £180 since Q1 as content and referral channels matured.' (metric + driver)
'Gross margin is 76%, consistent with best-in-class B2B SaaS at our stage.' (metric + benchmark context)'
Presenting Valuations and Round Terms Precisely

Valuation and round structure language must be precise — errors in these numbers during a pitch signal poor preparation or financial misunderstanding. The calculation should be explicit and internally consistent.

'We are raising £1.8 million at a £7.2 million pre-money valuation, resulting in a post-money of £9 million and a 20% investor stake.' (amount + pre-money + post-money + stake — all consistent)
'We have £600,000 committed and are seeking a lead for the remaining £1.2 million.' (current round status — specific)
'The round is structured as a SAFE with a £7.5 million valuation cap and a 20% discount.' (instrument + key terms)
'This is our first institutional round — we have previously raised £250,000 from angels under SEIS.'
Growth and Return Language

When describing growth trajectories and potential investor returns, use specific language that is internally consistent with the financial model. Vague optimism ('this market is exploding') is less persuasive than specific evidence.

'We are growing at 18% month-on-month — if we maintain this rate, we will reach £500,000 MRR in 14 months.'
'At a comparable SaaS exit multiple of 8x ARR, investors at today's valuation would see a 12x return on a £50 million exit.'
'We have reached £42,000 MRR in eight months. Our Year 2 target of £600,000 ARR is achievable at our current growth rate without any change to the sales model.'
'The LTV:CAC ratio stands at 4.2:1 — above the 3:1 threshold expected for sustainable unit economics at Series A.'

What the early Airbnb pitches reveal about investor psychology, pattern matching, and the persistence required to raise a seed round

Background

In 2008 and 2009, Brian Chesky and his co-founders pitched AirBed and Breakfast (later Airbnb) to dozens of investors. The concept — renting out air mattresses in your home to strangers — was so counterintuitive that most investors passed immediately. Chesky has spoken extensively about the rejection process: seven of the seven 'super-angels' he approached said no. The pitches that have since been published show that the core elements were sound — the problem was real, the traction was early but growing, the team was credible — but the concept required investors to override their instinctive reaction to an idea that sounded absurd. The company was eventually funded through Y Combinator in the Winter 2009 batch, and the rest is history: Airbnb went public in 2020 at a $100 billion valuation.

What Made the Pitch Difficult — and What Eventually Worked

The Airbnb story illuminates several enduring lessons about investor pitching. First, pattern matching works against genuinely novel ideas: investors evaluate pitches by comparing them to what has worked before. A company that does not fit any existing pattern is harder to fund — not because it is a worse idea but because investors struggle to form a thesis for it. Chesky and his team addressed this by focusing relentlessly on the evidence of real behaviour: people were actually paying to sleep in strangers' homes, the retention numbers were strong, and the unit economics worked. Second, persistence matters more than any individual pitch: the founders continued approaching investors despite consistent rejection because they believed in what the data was showing them. Third, the YC acceptance changed everything — not because of the capital ($20,000 at the time) but because the YC brand signal unlocked doors that had previously been closed.

Lessons for Founders Preparing to Pitch

The Airbnb rejection story teaches founders several things about the pitching process. First, rejection is not always a signal about the quality of the idea — it is often a signal about investor psychology, portfolio fit, and the timing of the idea relative to market readiness. Second, the best response to rejection is to ask why — and to distinguish between feedback that reveals genuine business weaknesses (which should prompt strategic reassessment) and feedback that reveals investor pattern mismatch (which should prompt a search for different investors). Third, the quality of the evidence — actual behaviour from real customers — is the most powerful rebuttal to scepticism. When investors said 'no one will pay to sleep in a stranger's home,' the founders could point to people who were already doing it. Fourth, finding the right accelerator or investor community — one that can see the potential that others miss — is as important as the pitch itself.

Case Study 2: Sequoia Capital's Investment in WhatsApp — What Investors Are Really Buying When They Back a Startup
How one of the most successful investments in venture capital history was made — and what it teaches about what investors are evaluating beyond the numbers

Background

In 2011, Sequoia Capital invested $8 million in WhatsApp — then a scrappy messaging startup with no revenue, a tiny team, and a product that charged $0.99 per year after a free first year. By 2014, WhatsApp was acquired by Facebook for $19 billion, making Sequoia's return approximately $3 billion — one of the most celebrated investments in the firm's history. What made Sequoia invest in a company with no revenue model and a product priced at less than one dollar per year? The answer is illuminating for anyone trying to understand how investors think about early-stage bets.

What Sequoia Was Evaluating

Jim Goetz, the Sequoia partner who led the WhatsApp investment, has spoken extensively about what he was evaluating. First, the founders: Jan Koum had emigrated from Ukraine with his family, worked at Yahoo for nine years, and had a deeply personal relationship to the problem WhatsApp was solving — reliable, private, cross-border communication for people who could not afford SMS fees. Koum's conviction and product obsession were evident from the first meeting. Second, the growth metrics: WhatsApp was growing at a rate that few applications had ever achieved — hundreds of millions of messages per day, with extraordinary international reach and retention. The metrics told a story that the absence of a revenue model could not diminish. Third, the market timing: messaging was clearly going to be a massive category, and WhatsApp's position — free acquisition of hundreds of millions of users — was a strategic asset regardless of the current monetisation model. Sequoia was betting on the team, the growth, and the strategic value of the asset — not the current revenue.

Lessons for Founders Pitching to Investors

The WhatsApp story offers several enduring lessons for founders preparing investor pitches. First, investors at the highest level are making long-term bets on teams and markets, not just financial models: Koum's personal story and product conviction were as important to Sequoia's decision as the growth metrics. Second, growth is the most powerful argument in an investor pitch: extraordinary growth in a large market is compelling even without a clear revenue model, because growth creates the strategic asset that can be monetised later. Third, an imperfect business model is not a fatal flaw if the team has the quality and the market has the scale to allow experimentation: WhatsApp's $0.99 annual fee was not its long-term monetisation strategy — it was a commitment device to avoid advertising. Sequoia understood this. Fourth, the investor who backs you matters as much as the terms they offer: Sequoia's brand, network, and strategic advice supported WhatsApp through its growth phase in ways that underpinned the eventual $19 billion exit.
