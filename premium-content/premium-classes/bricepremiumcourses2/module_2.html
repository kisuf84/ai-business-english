Module Overview

Welcome to Module 2: Understanding Balance Sheets. This module takes a deep dive into the balance sheet — one of the three core financial statements and arguably the most comprehensive window into a company's financial health. You will learn how to classify and describe every major section of the balance sheet, from current and non-current assets to short-term and long-term liabilities and shareholders' equity, using accurate and professional English.

Building on Module 1, this module goes beyond basic definitions to explore how analysts interpret the balance sheet — assessing solvency, leverage, liquidity ratios, and capital structure. You will practise the specific grammar and vocabulary used by finance professionals when discussing balance sheet items, trends, and what the numbers reveal about a company's strategic position and financial resilience.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Classify and accurately describe all major balance sheet line items — current assets, non-current assets, current liabilities, non-current liabilities, and equity — in professional English.
2
Explain the accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) and articulate why the balance sheet must always balance, including in complex reporting scenarios.
3
Use comparative adjectives and language of contrast correctly to compare balance sheet figures across periods and between companies.
4
Apply reported speech and indirect reporting structures to summarise and present balance sheet findings from analyst reports and board briefings.
5
Form and use advanced balance sheet collocations and noun phrases correctly — including goodwill impairment, net tangible assets, and capital structure.
6
Calculate and interpret key balance sheet ratios — including the current ratio, quick ratio, and debt-to-equity ratio — and discuss what they indicate about financial health.
7
Write a formal balance sheet analysis report and a professional memo summarising balance sheet changes for a senior management audience.
8
Listen to and understand analyst briefings, investor calls, and finance training sessions focused on balance sheet interpretation and capital structure analysis.

Key Vocabulary

21 essential terms for reading and discussing balance sheets at B1–B2 level.

Module Vocabulary
⚖️
Accounting equation
The fundamental equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity. This must always hold true and is the foundation of double-entry bookkeeping.
🏠
Non-current assets
Assets that a company does not expect to convert into cash within twelve months. Includes property, plant and equipment, intangibles, and long-term investments.
💵
Cash and cash equivalents
The most liquid assets on the balance sheet: cash on hand, bank deposits, and highly liquid short-term instruments such as treasury bills.
📦
Inventory
Goods held by a company for sale in the ordinary course of business, including raw materials, work-in-progress, and finished goods.
🔄
Accounts receivable
Amounts owed to the company by customers who have purchased goods or services on credit. A current asset.
📋
Accounts payable
Amounts the company owes to suppliers for goods or services received but not yet paid for. A current liability.
🏗️
Property, plant & equipment (PP&E)
Tangible long-term assets used in operations, recorded at cost less accumulated depreciation. Also called fixed assets.
✨
Intangible assets
Non-physical assets with economic value, such as patents, trademarks, customer lists, and software. Includes goodwill from acquisitions.
💡
Goodwill
An intangible asset arising when a company pays more than the fair value of an acquired business's net assets. Represents brand value, customer relationships, and synergies.
📉
Accumulated depreciation
The total depreciation charged against a tangible fixed asset since it was placed into service. Shown as a deduction from gross PP&E.
💳
Current liabilities
Obligations due for payment within twelve months, including accounts payable, short-term borrowings, and the current portion of long-term debt.
🏦
Long-term debt
Borrowings and financial obligations that are not due for repayment within the next twelve months. Recorded under non-current liabilities.
📊
Shareholders' equity
The residual interest in the assets of a company after deducting all liabilities. Comprises share capital, share premium, retained earnings, and reserves.
💼
Retained earnings
Cumulative net profits that have been kept in the business rather than distributed as dividends. Part of shareholders' equity.
🔢
Current ratio
Current assets divided by current liabilities. A liquidity ratio measuring a company's ability to pay short-term obligations. A ratio above 1.0 is generally considered healthy.
⚡
Quick ratio
(Current assets minus inventory) divided by current liabilities. A stricter liquidity measure than the current ratio, also called the acid-test ratio.
📐
Debt-to-equity ratio
Total debt divided by total shareholders' equity. Measures financial leverage and indicates how much the company is financed by debt versus equity.
🌐
Net tangible assets (NTA)
Total assets minus intangible assets and total liabilities. Represents the tangible net worth of a company, excluding goodwill and other intangibles.
📑
Capital structure
The mix of debt and equity a company uses to finance its assets and operations. A key determinant of financial risk and cost of capital.
🔻
Goodwill impairment
A write-down of goodwill when its carrying value on the balance sheet exceeds its recoverable amount. Results in an expense on the income statement.
🔁
Working capital
Current assets minus current liabilities. A measure of day-to-day operational liquidity. Positive working capital indicates the company can meet short-term obligations.

Text 1: Assets on the Balance Sheet — Classification and Analysis

How to read and interpret the assets side of a balance sheet

The assets section of a balance sheet is divided into two broad categories: current assets and non-current assets. Current assets are resources that a company expects to convert into cash, sell, or consume within twelve months. The most liquid of these — cash and cash equivalents — appears first and represents the company's immediately available funds, including bank balances and short-term instruments such as treasury bills. Following cash, accounts receivable reflects amounts owed by customers who have purchased goods or services on credit. The speed at which a company collects these receivables — measured by the debtor days ratio — is an important indicator of operational efficiency. Inventory, the third major current asset, represents goods awaiting sale and must be carefully managed to avoid tying up excessive working capital.

Non-current assets, sometimes called fixed or long-term assets, are those that provide economic benefit over a period longer than twelve months. Property, plant and equipment (PP&E) is typically the largest component for manufacturing and infrastructure companies. It is recorded at historical cost less accumulated depreciation — the systematic reduction in carrying value over the asset's useful life. Intangible assets form a second important non-current category and include items such as patents, trademarks, brand names, and customer relationships. These assets can be difficult to value objectively. Goodwill, a specific and often significant intangible asset, arises when one company acquires another for a price exceeding the fair value of the acquired company's identifiable net assets.

Analysts scrutinise the asset composition of a balance sheet to understand both the nature of the business and the risks it carries. A company with a high proportion of intangible assets — particularly goodwill — faces the risk of impairment charges if acquisitions fail to deliver expected value. A company with large receivables but slow collection may face cash flow stress even if it appears profitable. High inventory levels relative to sales can signal poor demand, excess production, or obsolescence risk. Conversely, a company with strong cash balances and well-managed receivables demonstrates financial discipline and operational resilience, characteristics that investors and creditors value highly.

Text 2: Liabilities, Equity, and Reading Capital Structure

Understanding the financing side of the balance sheet

The liabilities and equity sections of the balance sheet reveal how a company has financed its assets — whether through borrowing, through shareholders' investment, or through retained profits. Liabilities are divided into current liabilities, due within twelve months, and non-current liabilities, due beyond twelve months. Current liabilities typically include accounts payable — the amounts owed to suppliers — along with accrued expenses, the current portion of long-term debt, and short-term borrowings. Managing current liabilities effectively is critical: companies that allow accounts payable to become overdue risk damaging supplier relationships, while those that repay borrowings too quickly may sacrifice liquidity. The relationship between current assets and current liabilities — expressed as the current ratio — is one of the most commonly used indicators of short-term financial health.

Non-current liabilities are the long-term obligations of the company, primarily long-term debt such as bonds and bank loans maturing beyond twelve months, along with deferred tax liabilities and pension obligations. The level of long-term debt relative to equity — measured by the debt-to-equity ratio — determines a company's financial leverage. High leverage amplifies returns for shareholders when times are good, but it also increases financial risk: a highly indebted company must service its debt regardless of performance, reducing flexibility during downturns. Lenders and bond investors pay close attention to debt covenants — contractual conditions that restrict how much debt a company can carry relative to its earnings — as a form of protection against overleveraging.

Shareholders' equity, the third section, comprises share capital (the par value of shares issued), share premium (the amount received above par value), retained earnings, and other reserves. Retained earnings are the engine of organic equity growth: each profitable year in which dividends are kept below net profit increases the retained earnings balance. However, equity can also decrease through share buybacks — when a company purchases its own shares on the open market, the cost is recorded as treasury stock and reduces total equity. Understanding the capital structure — the mix of debt and equity financing — is essential for assessing a company's risk profile, its cost of capital, and its capacity to absorb financial shocks.

🔍 GRAMMAR REVIEW

Grammar Reference

Detailed reference boxes covering all three grammar points in this module.

Grammar Reference 1: Comparative Language in Financial Analysis

Why comparative language matters in finance

Financial analysts constantly compare figures — across periods (year-on-year), across companies (peer analysis), and across scenarios (actual vs budget). Using the correct comparative form is essential for clarity and professionalism.

Comparative adjectives — regular forms

For one-syllable adjectives: add -er. For adjectives with two or more syllables: use more/less. Always use 'than' after a comparative.

  • The current ratio is higher than last year. (one syllable: high → higher)
  • The balance sheet is more transparent than before.
  • Leverage is less significant than analysts expected.

As ... as / not as ... as — expressing equality or inequality

Use 'as + adjective + as' to show that two things are equal or to show they are not equal.

  • The quick ratio is not as high as the current ratio.
  • This year's equity base is not as strong as it was in 2022.
  • The debt level is approximately as large as total equity.

The ... the ... — expressing proportional change

This structure expresses that two changes happen in proportion. Very common in financial commentary.

  • The higher the debt-to-equity ratio, the greater the financial risk.
  • The lower the current ratio, the more concerned lenders tend to be.
  • The faster inventory turns, the less working capital the company needs.
Grammar Reference 2: Reported Speech in Financial Reporting

What is reported speech?

Reported speech (indirect speech) is used to summarise or report what someone said, wrote, or announced. It is essential in financial reporting — when presenting analyst findings, board decisions, or management comments in meetings, emails, and reports.

Tense backshift rules

When the reporting verb is in the past tense, the verb in the reported clause typically shifts back one tense:

  • Direct: 'The balance sheet is strong.' → Reported: She said the balance sheet was strong.
  • Direct: 'We have reduced debt.' → Reported: They announced they had reduced debt.
  • Direct: 'We will review covenants.' → Reported: The bank stated they would review covenants.

Reporting verbs in financial contexts

Choose your reporting verb carefully — each one carries a different meaning:

  • said / stated → neutral reporting
  • warned / cautioned → negative or risk-related news
  • confirmed / announced → formal, official statements
  • explained / noted → added context or clarification
  • argued / contended → expressing a position or opinion

Modal verb backshift

Modal verbs also shift in reported speech:

  • will → would: 'We will launch a buyback' → They said they would launch a buyback.
  • can → could: 'Covenants can be tightened' → She warned covenants could be tightened.
  • may → might: 'Equity may fall' → Management warned equity might fall.
Grammar Reference 3: Balance Sheet Collocations & Fixed Expressions

Why collocations matter

In professional finance English, specific verbs, adjectives, and nouns combine in fixed, natural-sounding ways. Using the wrong word, even with the right meaning, sounds unnatural to native speakers and damages professional credibility.

Verb + Noun collocations: assets and liabilities

These combinations are fixed and should be memorised:

  • record / recognise / carry → an asset, a liability, goodwill
  • write down / write off / impair → an asset, goodwill, inventory
  • discharge / settle / repay → a liability, a loan, debt
  • hold / maintain / carry → a cash balance, reserves

Verb + Noun collocations: equity and structure

Use these standard combinations when discussing equity and capital:

  • issue / allot → shares
  • launch / conduct / complete → a share buyback / buyback programme
  • strengthen / erode / rebuild → the equity base
  • breach / comply with / satisfy → debt covenants

Fixed prepositional phrases with balance sheet items

These prepositional patterns are fixed in professional finance writing:

  • at cost / at fair value / at carrying value → how assets are measured
  • net of depreciation / net of tax → after deductions
  • on the balance sheet / off the balance sheet → whether recorded or not
  • in excess of / below / in line with → for comparisons

Good morning and welcome to today's session. My name is Claire Donovan and I'll be walking you through how to read and interpret a basic balance sheet. Let's start with the structure. Every balance sheet has three sections: assets, liabilities, and equity. The assets side lists everything the company owns or controls. Assets are divided into current assets — those expected to become cash within twelve months — and non-current assets, which provide value over a longer period. Current assets include cash, accounts receivable — which is money owed by customers — and inventory, which is unsold stock. Non-current assets include property and equipment, intangible assets like patents and brand names, and goodwill, which arises when you acquire another company for more than its net asset value. Now let's move to the other side. Liabilities are everything the company owes. Current liabilities — due within twelve months — include accounts payable to suppliers, short-term loans, and accrued expenses. Non-current liabilities are the longer-term obligations, mainly long-term bank loans or bonds. Finally, equity is what is left for the shareholders after you subtract all liabilities from all assets. It includes share capital, the money originally invested by shareholders, and retained earnings, the profits kept in the business over the years. The golden rule is this: total assets must always equal total liabilities plus total equity. If they do not balance, there is an error. This is why it is called the balance sheet. Any questions before we look at a real example?

Case Study 1
Toys R Us: A Leveraged Buyout and a Balance Sheet Destroyed
USA · Retail · 2017 Bankruptcy · Debt Overload  ·  How an excessive debt burden placed on the balance sheet in a leveraged buyout led to inevitable collapse
Background

Toys R Us was the world's largest dedicated toy retailer, operating over 1,700 stores across more than 30 countries at its peak. In 2005, the company was taken private in a leveraged buyout (LBO) by a consortium of private equity firms — Bain Capital, KKR, and Vornado Realty Trust — for approximately $6.6 billion. In a leveraged buyout, the acquirers use a relatively small amount of equity and a very large amount of borrowed money — typically secured against the assets of the company being acquired — to complete the purchase. As a result of this transaction, Toys R Us's balance sheet was transformed overnight: what had been a modestly indebted retail business became one carrying approximately $5 billion in long-term debt.

The Balance Sheet Under Pressure

The consequences of this capital structure decision became progressively more severe over the following decade. With $400 to $500 million in annual interest payments, the company had almost no financial flexibility. While competitors such as Amazon and Walmart invested heavily in e-commerce infrastructure, supply chain efficiency, and customer experience, Toys R Us was unable to make equivalent investments because the majority of its operating cash flow was consumed by debt servicing. The balance sheet ratio that tells this story most clearly is the interest coverage ratio: in multiple years, the company's EBITDA barely covered its interest expense, leaving a razor-thin margin for capital expenditure, restructuring, or unexpected downturns. Working capital was also under constant strain, as the company struggled to finance its seasonal inventory build in the months before Christmas.

Collapse and Lessons

Toys R Us filed for bankruptcy protection in September 2017, eventually liquidating all its US and UK stores by 2018. The bankruptcy eliminated thousands of jobs and wiped out the equity of the private equity owners entirely. The primary lesson from the Toys R Us case is that capital structure decisions have profound and lasting consequences. Debt that is placed on a balance sheet in pursuit of short-term financial engineering can destroy a company's ability to compete, invest, and adapt over the long term. The case is also a reminder that a company can be operationally viable — generating reasonable revenues and even positive EBITDA — while being financially unviable due to an unsustainable debt load on its balance sheet.

Case Study 2
Berkshire Hathaway: A Masterclass in Balance Sheet Management
USA · Conglomerate · Ongoing · Exemplary Capital Allocation  ·  How Warren Buffett uses the balance sheet as a strategic weapon — and what finance professionals can learn
The Fortress Balance Sheet

Berkshire Hathaway, the conglomerate controlled by legendary investor Warren Buffett, is frequently cited as one of the most conservatively and intelligently managed balance sheets in corporate history. At virtually any point over the past four decades, Berkshire has maintained an extraordinary cash and short-term investment position — often exceeding $100 billion — that the company deliberately chooses not to deploy unless an exceptional acquisition or investment opportunity presents itself. This 'fortress balance sheet' strategy is the exact opposite of the Toys R Us approach: rather than using leverage to amplify returns, Buffett has consistently prioritised financial resilience, optionality, and the ability to act decisively when others cannot.

Equity, Earnings, and Intrinsic Value

The equity section of Berkshire's balance sheet tells a remarkable story of compounding retained earnings. Berkshire has not paid a cash dividend since 1967, instead reinvesting all profits back into the business. Over decades, this has resulted in a retained earnings balance that now runs into the hundreds of billions of dollars. The book value per share — total equity divided by the number of shares outstanding — grew from approximately $19 in 1965 to over $300,000 in the 2020s, reflecting an annualised compound growth rate of approximately 20%. Buffett himself has argued that reported book value understates the intrinsic value of Berkshire because the balance sheet carries many assets — particularly equity holdings in great businesses — at historical cost rather than current market value.

Lessons for Balance Sheet Analysis

Berkshire Hathaway's approach offers several critical lessons for finance professionals and analysts. First, cash on the balance sheet is not idle — it is strategic optionality. A company that holds substantial cash can acquire distressed assets, make opportunistic investments, or weather economic storms that destroy competitors. Second, retained earnings compounded over long periods create extraordinary wealth — the decision of whether to retain or distribute profits is one of the most consequential capital allocation choices any board makes. Third, balance sheet analysis requires understanding both what is on the balance sheet and what the accounting obscures: book value is a starting point, not a conclusion. The gap between book value and intrinsic value is where sophisticated financial analysis begins.
