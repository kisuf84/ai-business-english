Module Overview

Welcome to Module 8: Writing Financial Reports. This module focuses on the language, structure, and style required to write financial reports that are not only numerically accurate but also clearly written, logically structured, and appropriate in register for their intended audience. Building on all previous modules in this course, you will now learn how to communicate financial information in writing — one of the most valued and least taught skills in professional finance.

You will study the range of financial report types produced in professional organisations — from monthly management accounts packs to board papers, investor updates, and annual report narratives — and develop the specific writing techniques, grammar structures, and vocabulary patterns that distinguish outstanding financial writers from average ones. This module is essential for controllers, FP&A analysts, finance business partners, CFOs, and any finance professional who writes reports for internal or external audiences.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Identify the purpose, audience, structure, and register of the main types of financial reports — management accounts, board papers, investor letters, and annual report narratives.
2
Apply the key principles of effective financial writing — clarity, concision, accuracy, objectivity, and appropriate register — in a range of professional report contexts.
3
Use the passive voice and impersonal structures correctly and purposefully in formal financial reports to achieve an objective and professional tone.
4
Apply nominalisation — converting verbs and adjectives into formal noun phrases — to achieve a more concise and authoritative register in financial writing.
5
Use discourse markers and paragraph connectors correctly to create coherent, well-structured financial report sections that guide the reader logically from one point to the next.
6
Write effective executive summaries that distil complex financial information into clear, actionable insights for senior decision-makers.
7
Write a complete management accounts commentary and a formal board paper section — using professional structure, tone, and financial language — for realistic business scenarios.
8
Listen to and understand workshops, peer feedback sessions, and expert discussions on financial writing quality, register, and common mistakes made by non-native English writers.

Key Vocabulary

21 essential terms for writing and discussing financial reports at B1–B2 level.

Module Vocabulary
📋
Executive summary
A concise overview of the key findings, conclusions, and recommendations of a longer financial report — written for senior readers who may not read the full document. It must stand alone as a complete document.
📝
Management accounts
A set of regular internal financial reports — typically monthly or quarterly — showing actual performance against budget and prior year, with commentary explaining key variances.
🏛️
Board paper
A formal written document submitted to the board of directors for information, discussion, or approval. Typically includes an executive summary, background, analysis, recommendations, and financial implications.
🗣️
Register
The level of formality in language. Financial reports typically use formal or semi-formal register — avoiding slang, contractions, and casual language, and preferring objective, precise vocabulary.
🎯
Narrative commentary
The written explanation accompanying financial tables and charts in a report, interpreting the numbers and explaining their significance to the reader in plain professional prose.
🔄
Nominalisation
The process of converting a verb or adjective into a noun phrase to create a more formal, concise, and authoritative writing style. Example: 'The company declined' → 'A decline was recorded.'
🔗
Discourse markers
Words and phrases that connect ideas, paragraphs, and sentences in written reports — such as 'however', 'consequently', 'in addition', 'turning to', and 'as outlined above'.
📊
Management pack
A compiled set of financial reports and analysis circulated to senior management on a regular basis — typically including P&L, balance sheet, cash flow, KPIs, and variance commentary.
🔍
Analytical writing
Writing that goes beyond describing what the numbers show to explaining why results have occurred, what they mean, and what action is recommended — the highest value form of financial reporting.
📖
Annual report narrative
The written sections of a company's annual report — including the strategic report, the CEO's letter, and the operating and financial review — which contextualise the financial statements in professional narrative form.
⚠️
Going concern
A key accounting concept and a required disclosure in financial reports — the assumption that the company will continue operating for the foreseeable future. Any doubt must be disclosed prominently.
💡
Material information
Information that is significant enough that its omission or misstatement could influence the decisions of users of the financial report. All material items must be disclosed.
📣
Investor letter
A letter from the CEO, CFO, or Chairman to shareholders — typically found in the annual report — summarising strategic progress, financial performance, and the outlook in an accessible, measured tone.
🧩
Key message
The single most important piece of information or conclusion the writer wants the reader to take away from the report. It should be stated clearly in the executive summary and reinforced throughout.
🔇
Concision
The quality of expressing the necessary information using as few words as possible, without sacrificing clarity or completeness. A core principle of effective professional financial writing.
🔎
Clarity
The quality of being easy to understand, free of ambiguity, and well-organised. In financial writing, clarity requires plain language, logical structure, and consistent terminology.
⚖️
Objectivity
Presenting financial information in a balanced, unbiased way, without overstating positives or minimising negatives. Essential for credibility in internal reports and external communications.
📐
Report structure
The logical organisation of a financial report into its standard sections — typically: title/cover, executive summary, background, analysis, findings, recommendations, and appendices.
🌐
Impersonal structures
Grammatical constructions that avoid naming a specific subject — such as 'It has been noted that…', 'It is recommended that…', or 'It should be emphasised that…'. Used in formal financial reports to achieve objectivity.
🗂️
Appendix
A supplementary section at the end of a financial report containing detailed data, workings, or supporting analysis that is relevant but too detailed to include in the main body of the report.
🔁
The 'so what?' test
A self-editing technique for financial writers: after writing any sentence or paragraph, ask 'So what does this mean?' If the writing only describes without analysing or concluding, it fails the test.

Text 1: The Principles of Effective Financial Report Writing

Why the quality of financial writing matters and how to achieve it

Financial professionals are, by training, skilled at analysing numbers. They are far less frequently trained in communicating what those numbers mean. Yet the ability to write a clear, accurate, and well-structured financial report is one of the most commercially valuable skills a finance professional can possess. A management accounts pack that is unclear or poorly explained will fail to prompt the decisions it is intended to support. A board paper that buries its key message in impenetrable prose will be ignored by the very directors it is trying to inform. An investor letter that is defensive, vague, or over-optimistic will undermine trust. In each case, the quality of the writing has direct consequences for the quality of the decision-making it is meant to enable.

Effective financial writing rests on five core principles. The first is clarity — the report must be immediately understandable to its intended audience, free from ambiguity and jargon that the reader may not share. The second is concision — every sentence should earn its place; redundant words, padding, and repetition waste the reader's time and dilute the key message. The third is accuracy — financial reports must be numerically correct and linguistically precise; vague language like 'good performance' or 'some concern' fails to communicate the substance of the finding. The fourth is objectivity — professional reports acknowledge both positive and negative findings with equal directness, without spin or selective emphasis. The fifth, and most often missing, is analysis — describing what the numbers are is not enough; the reader wants to know what they mean and what should happen next.

Two common weaknesses undermine financial reports in practice. The first is purely descriptive writing — a commentary that simply restates the numbers from the table above it without adding any interpretation. 'Revenue was $45 million in Q3' tells the reader nothing beyond what the table already shows. 'Revenue of $45 million represents a 12% shortfall against budget, driven primarily by the delayed product launch in the North American market, which is expected to ship in Q4' adds context, cause, and implication. The second weakness is excessive length and complexity. Senior readers — particularly board members and investors — are time-poor. They need the key messages delivered in the first paragraph of each section, with supporting detail available for those who need it. The executive summary is the most important section of any financial report, and it must be able to stand alone.

Text 2: Report Types, Structure, and Register in Professional Finance

Understanding who you are writing for and what they need from your report

Different financial reports serve different purposes and require different approaches to structure, length, and language. The monthly management accounts pack is an internal document primarily for operational managers and the finance team. It typically includes an income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement for the period, supported by a narrative commentary explaining key variances. The tone is analytical and direct, often semi-formal, and the audience is assumed to be financially literate. The primary goal is speed — management needs the information quickly to make decisions — so the commentary should be concise and focused on the most material items. A management pack commentary rarely needs to run to more than three to four hundred words per section.

The board paper is a more formal document, written for a different audience. Board members range widely in their financial expertise — some may be experienced finance professionals, while others are independent non-executive directors with expertise in other fields. A well-constructed board paper therefore includes a clear executive summary at the front that non-specialist members can read independently, followed by more detailed analysis for those who want it. The structure typically follows a standard format: executive summary, background and context, key findings or analysis, risks and mitigants, and recommendations with financial implications. The language is formal, the tone is measured and objective, and the paper is typically shorter than it might initially appear necessary — boards read many papers and reward concision.

External financial communications — including investor letters, shareholder reports, and annual report narratives — require a further level of care. These documents are published and legally regulated, and the language used carries legal significance. Statements about future performance must be carefully hedged and qualified. Facts must be verifiable. Both positive and negative information must be disclosed fairly and without misleading emphasis. The register is formal but must also be accessible — many shareholders are not finance professionals, and the CEO's letter in an annual report is often the most-read section of the entire document. A consistent, credible, and clear voice is essential. The best annual report narratives combine analytical rigour with readability — explaining complex financial performance in language that educates and reassures rather than obscures.

Grammar Reference 1: Passive Voice and Impersonal Structures in Financial Reports

Why passive and impersonal structures are used in financial writing

Financial reports aim to be objective, authoritative, and professional. Passive voice and impersonal structures remove the personal subject ('I', 'we', 'the team') and focus on the action or finding itself. This creates a more formal, neutral tone that is expected in professional financial documents.

The passive voice — form and use

Form: subject + be (conjugated) + past participle. The passive removes or de-emphasises the agent (who did it).

  • Active: The finance team prepared the budget in October. → Passive: The budget was prepared in October.
  • Active: We identified three key risks. → Passive: Three key risks were identified.
  • Active: Management will present the results next week. → Passive: The results will be presented next week.

Impersonal 'it' structures

These constructions completely remove any personal subject. They are very common in board papers, audit reports, and formal financial commentary.

  • It is noted that… / It has been noted that…
  • It is recommended that… / It is proposed that…
  • It should be emphasised that… / It should be noted that…
  • It is expected that… / It is anticipated that…

Common errors to avoid

Even experienced writers make mistakes with passive structures in formal reports:

  • WRONG: 'It is recommending that the board approve...' → RIGHT: 'It is recommended that the board approve...'
  • WRONG: 'The results were present to the board.' → RIGHT: 'The results were presented to the board.'
  • WRONG: 'It has identified that costs exceeded budget.' → RIGHT: 'It has been identified that costs exceeded budget.'
Grammar Reference 2: Nominalisation in Financial Writing

What is nominalisation and why does it matter?

Nominalisation is the process of converting a verb, adjective, or clause into a noun phrase. It is one of the defining features of formal professional writing and is extremely common in financial reports. It creates a denser, more authoritative style that feels more objective and impersonal.

How to nominalise verbs

Add a noun-forming suffix to convert the verb into a noun. Common suffixes: -tion/-sion, -ment, -ance/-ence, -al, -ure.

  • decline → a decline / the decline in revenue
  • improve → an improvement / the improvement in margins
  • fail → failure (a failure to meet targets)
  • reduce → a reduction / reduction in costs
  • perform → performance / a strong performance

Nominalised sentence patterns in financial reports

These patterns are standard in professional financial writing:

  • 'A decline was recorded in Q3.' (not: 'Q3 declined.')
  • 'An improvement in cash flow was achieved.' (not: 'Cash flow improved.')
  • 'The approval of the revised budget is recommended.' (not: 'We recommend approving the budget.')
  • 'The identification of cost savings is ongoing.' (not: 'We are still identifying cost savings.')

When not to over-nominalise

While nominalisation is a powerful formal writing tool, overuse can make text unnecessarily dense. A good rule: use it for key conclusions and formal statements; use clearer active language when explaining complex processes for non-specialist readers.

  • Appropriate: 'A deterioration in working capital was observed during H1.'
  • Too dense: 'The realisation of the expectation of the achievement of the target was not forthcoming.' (= 'The target was not achieved.')
  • Balance: Use nominalised structures for formal conclusions, clearer structures for explanations.
Grammar Reference 3: Discourse Markers in Financial Reports

What are discourse markers and why do they matter?

Discourse markers are words and phrases that signal the relationship between ideas, sentences, and paragraphs. In financial reports, they help the reader follow the logic of the argument, understand contrasts, and anticipate what comes next. Without them, a report can feel like a list of unconnected statements.

Discourse markers for contrast and concession

Use these when introducing a contrasting or unexpected finding:

  • however → 'Revenue was strong; however, margins declined significantly.'
  • nevertheless / nonetheless → 'Q3 was below plan; nevertheless, the full-year outlook remains on track.'
  • despite this / in spite of this → introduces a contrasting result after a positive or negative statement

Discourse markers for addition and reinforcement

Use these to build on a point or add supporting information:

  • in addition / furthermore / moreover → 'Revenue grew by 9%; furthermore, margins also improved.'
  • as well as this → informal addition (better suited to semi-formal reports)
  • it should also be noted that → draws attention to an important additional point

Discourse markers for cause, effect, and sequence

Use these to signal logical relationships and report structure:

  • as a result / consequently / therefore → 'Costs exceeded budget; as a result, EBITDA was below target.'
  • in response to / following → 'Following the Q3 shortfall, a cost reduction programme was launched.'
  • turning to / with regard to / in relation to → signals a new topic or section in the report
  • as outlined above / as set out in Section 2 → cross-references within the report

Case Study 1
The Power of Clear Financial Writing: Berkshire Hathaway's Annual Letters
USA · Investment · 1965–Present · Warren Buffett's Shareholder Letters  ·  How one investor's commitment to plain English transformed annual report communication
Background

Every year, Warren Buffett — chairman and CEO of Berkshire Hathaway — writes a letter to shareholders that is widely regarded as the gold standard of financial communication. Unlike the typical annual report narrative, which is characterised by dense legal language, cautious disclaimers, and selective emphasis on positive performance, Buffett's letters are written in plain, direct English — the kind of language, as he famously describes it, that he would use to write to 'a bright, interested person who has about as much financial knowledge as I do.' The letters discuss both successes and failures with equal candour, explain complex financial concepts in accessible language, use metaphors and stories to illustrate abstract points, and consistently prioritise the reader's understanding over the writer's legal protection.

What Makes the Letters Work

Several specific writing characteristics distinguish Buffett's letters from typical corporate communications. First, they use active voice extensively — Buffett writes 'I was wrong' rather than 'errors were made'. This creates directness and accountability that is almost unique in corporate communication. Second, they apply the 'so what?' principle rigorously — every piece of information is connected to a conclusion or a learning. A decline in performance is not merely reported; it is explained, contextualised, and used as the basis for a lesson. Third, the letters are free of jargon — Buffett famously tests his writing by asking whether an intelligent but non-specialist reader would understand every sentence. Where they cannot, he rewrites rather than explains. Fourth, they are structured around the reader's questions — the natural flow follows what a shareholder would want to know: how did the company perform? Why? What does management think about the businesses? What should shareholders expect? This is the same question-based structure that separates outstanding financial writing from competent financial writing.

Lessons for Financial Writers

Buffett's letters offer five practical lessons for any financial writer. First, write for your reader, not for your legal team. This does not mean ignoring legal requirements — it means making the legal language and the reader-friendly language coexist rather than allowing one to crowd out the other. Second, be equally specific about both good news and bad news. Nothing destroys reader trust faster than the sense that the writer is managing their perception rather than informing their judgment. Third, test every sentence against the 'so what?' standard — if a piece of information does not lead to a conclusion, an implication, or a recommended action, consider whether it belongs in the report at all. Fourth, use active voice when you want to convey accountability and ownership; use passive voice when you want to report a finding objectively. Fifth, the best financial writing is also good writing — clear, direct, occasionally surprising, and always in service of the reader's understanding.

Case Study 2
The Collapse of Patisserie Valerie: When Financial Reports Concealed the Truth
UK · Retail · 2018 Collapse · Fraudulent Financial Reporting  ·  How systematically misleading financial reports allowed a £40 million fraud to go undetected for years
Background

Patisserie Valerie was a UK café chain that appeared, from its published financial reports and management accounts, to be a growing, cash-generative, and solidly profitable business. The company was listed on the London Stock Exchange's AIM market, and its financial statements — reviewed and signed off by its external auditors, Grant Thornton — showed strong revenue growth, healthy cash balances, and comfortable profit margins. The CEO, Paul May, and the chairman, Luke Johnson — a prominent investor and entrepreneur — publicly described the business as financially robust. In October 2018, however, the company announced it had discovered a £40 million hole in its finances. The actual cash position was dramatically lower than reported; debt balances had been understated; and the company had been sustaining itself on a series of fake or manipulated entries in its management accounts.

The Role of Financial Reports

The Patisserie Valerie fraud is instructive precisely because the financial reports looked normal. The management accounts were produced on time, the numbers appeared internally consistent, and the external audit signed off on the statutory accounts. What the reports concealed — and what is now understood to have been actively manipulated by the Finance Director — were the cash balances, which were systematically overstated through a series of fabricated bank entries, and the debt position, which was understated by hiding overdraft facilities. The written narrative commentary in the management pack never drew attention to the discrepancies between reported and actual cash, did not flag the growing overdraft facilities as a material risk, and used language that was deliberately vague about the sources of the company's cash generation. Financial reports that rely solely on numbers without clear narrative explanation of the sources and uses of cash are uniquely vulnerable to this type of manipulation.

Lessons for Financial Report Writers

The Patisserie Valerie case offers several important lessons. First, financial narrative commentaries must specifically address the quality and sources of reported cash — not simply report that the cash position is strong. A commentary that states 'cash balances remained healthy at £28 million' without explaining where the cash came from, how it was held, or whether it had been independently confirmed, fails its readers. Second, the principle of objectivity requires that any anomaly, discrepancy, or unusual pattern in the financial data be flagged clearly rather than passed over or obscured by vague language. Third, the external audit process — which is supposed to provide independent assurance on the financial statements — failed because auditors relied on management representations rather than independently verifying cash balances. This case accelerated discussions about audit reform and the responsibility of financial report writers and reviewers to ensure that reports are not only accurate in their presentation but honest in their substance.
