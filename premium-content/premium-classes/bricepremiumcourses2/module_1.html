Module Overview

Welcome to Module 1: Financial Statements and Key Vocabulary. This module introduces the three core financial statements used in businesses worldwide — the income statement, the balance sheet, and the cash flow statement — along with the essential English vocabulary you need to read and discuss them confidently. You will learn how each statement is structured, what key figures mean, and how to talk about financial performance, liquidity, and profitability in a professional context.

This module is designed for finance professionals, accountants, analysts, managers, and anyone who works with financial information at B1–B2 English level. By completing all activities, you will be able to participate confidently in meetings, write reports, and discuss financial data with colleagues and stakeholders in English.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Identify and explain the purpose and structure of the three core financial statements: income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement.
2
Use key financial vocabulary accurately to describe revenue, expenses, assets, liabilities, equity, and cash flows in spoken and written English.
3
Apply prepositions of financial change correctly to describe increases, decreases, and movements in financial data.
4
Use the present perfect and past simple tenses accurately to report financial results and describe historical performance.
5
Form and use financial collocations and noun phrases that are essential for professional finance communication in English.
6
Interpret financial data from income statements and balance sheets and comment on a company's financial health using appropriate language.
7
Write a short financial summary report and a professional email reporting key financial results to a non-specialist audience.
8
Listen to financial briefings, earnings calls, and analyst presentations and extract key figures, trends, and recommendations.

Key Vocabulary

21 essential terms for reading and discussing financial statements at B1–B2 level.

Module Vocabulary
📊
Income statement
A financial statement showing a company's revenues, expenses, and profit or loss over a specific period. Also called the profit and loss account (P&L).
⚖️
Balance sheet
A financial statement showing a company's assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific point in time.
💧
Cash flow statement
A financial statement that shows how cash moves in and out of a business through operating, investing, and financing activities.
💰
Revenue
The total income a company earns from its primary business activities, such as the sale of goods or services. Also called turnover or sales.
📉
Gross profit
Revenue minus the cost of goods sold (COGS). It represents profit before operating expenses are deducted.
🏦
Net profit
The final profit remaining after all expenses, taxes, and interest have been deducted from revenue. Also called the bottom line.
🧾
Operating expenses
The costs incurred in running the business day-to-day, such as salaries, rent, and utilities — excluding the cost of goods sold.
🏠
Assets
Resources owned or controlled by a company that are expected to provide future economic benefit. Divided into current and non-current assets.
📋
Liabilities
Obligations that a company owes to external parties, such as loans, accounts payable, and accrued expenses.
🤝
Equity
The residual interest in the assets of a company after deducting liabilities. Represents the owners' stake in the business.
🔄
Working capital
Current assets minus current liabilities. A measure of a company's short-term liquidity and operational efficiency.
📆
Accounts receivable
Money owed to a company by customers who have purchased goods or services on credit.
🧮
Accounts payable
Money a company owes to its suppliers for goods or services received but not yet paid for.
📦
Inventory
The raw materials, work-in-progress, and finished goods held by a company for sale.
🏗️
Depreciation
The systematic allocation of the cost of a tangible fixed asset over its useful life, recorded as an expense.
📈
EBITDA
Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortisation. A widely used measure of a company's core operating profitability.
💳
Retained earnings
The portion of net profit that is kept within the business after dividends are paid out, used to fund future growth.
🔍
Liquidity
A company's ability to meet its short-term financial obligations using available cash or assets quickly convertible to cash.
💼
Cost of goods sold (COGS)
The direct costs attributable to the production of the goods sold by a company, including materials and direct labour.
📐
Financial ratio
A numerical relationship between two financial figures used to assess a company's performance, efficiency, or financial health.
🌐
IFRS
International Financial Reporting Standards — a globally recognised set of accounting standards for the preparation of financial statements.

Text 1: Understanding the Income Statement

Structure, key line items, and what the numbers tell us

The income statement — also known as the profit and loss account or P&L — is one of the most closely studied financial documents in business. It provides a summary of a company's financial performance over a defined period, typically a quarter or a full financial year. Starting with revenue at the top — often referred to as the 'top line' — and ending with net profit at the bottom — the 'bottom line' — the income statement tells the story of how efficiently a company converts its sales into profit. Every line between these two figures represents a cost or deduction that erodes the company's earnings.

The first major deduction from revenue is the cost of goods sold (COGS), which represents the direct costs of producing the goods or services a company sells. Subtracting COGS from revenue gives us gross profit, which is a measure of production efficiency. From gross profit, operating expenses are then deducted — these include salaries, rent, marketing, and general administrative costs. The result is operating profit, also known as EBIT (Earnings Before Interest and Tax). After interest expenses and tax charges are deducted, the remaining figure is net profit, the definitive measure of a company's profitability during the period.

Analysts and investors study the income statement not simply for the headline profit figure but for the margins it reveals. The gross profit margin — gross profit divided by revenue — shows how efficiently a company manages its production costs. The net profit margin — net profit divided by revenue — reflects overall management efficiency after all costs are accounted for. A consistent improvement in margins over time typically signals that a company is becoming more efficient and competitive. Conversely, declining margins may indicate rising costs, pricing pressure, or operational problems that require management attention.

Text 2: The Balance Sheet — A Snapshot of Financial Position

Understanding assets, liabilities, equity, and the accounting equation

The balance sheet is a financial statement that provides a snapshot of what a company owns, what it owes, and what belongs to its shareholders at a specific point in time. Unlike the income statement, which covers a period, the balance sheet captures a single moment — typically the last day of a financial quarter or year. It is organised around the fundamental accounting equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity. This equation must always hold true, which is why the statement is called a balance sheet — the two sides must balance. Assets represent everything the company controls that has economic value; liabilities represent all obligations owed to external parties; and equity represents the residual interest belonging to shareholders.

Assets are divided into two main categories: current assets and non-current assets. Current assets are those expected to be converted into cash within twelve months — they include cash and cash equivalents, accounts receivable, and inventory. Non-current assets, also called fixed or long-term assets, provide value over a longer period and include property, plant and equipment (PP&E), intangible assets such as patents and goodwill, and long-term investments. On the other side of the balance sheet, liabilities are similarly divided into current liabilities — those due within twelve months, such as accounts payable and short-term loans — and non-current liabilities, such as long-term debt and pension obligations.

Equity, also known as shareholders' equity or net assets, is the third section of the balance sheet. It includes share capital — money originally invested by shareholders — and retained earnings, which are the accumulated profits the company has kept rather than distributing as dividends. Equity is sometimes described as the 'book value' of the company, though this rarely equals the company's market value. Analysts use the balance sheet to assess a company's leverage — how much debt it uses relative to equity — as well as its liquidity, which reflects its ability to meet short-term obligations. A healthy balance sheet typically shows a reasonable proportion of equity to debt and sufficient current assets to cover current liabilities.

🔍 GRAMMAR REVIEW

Grammar Reference

Detailed reference boxes covering all three grammar points in this module.

Grammar Reference 1: Prepositions of Financial Change

Why prepositions matter in finance

Prepositions are small words that carry important meaning when discussing financial figures. Using the wrong preposition changes the meaning significantly. In financial English, specific prepositions are paired with specific types of movement or position.

BY — Expressing the amount of change

Use 'by' to show how much something changed. This is the most commonly misused preposition in financial English.

  • Revenue increased by 12%. (= the amount it went up)
  • Costs fell by $2 million.
  • The margin improved by 3 percentage points.

FROM / TO — Expressing the range of change

Use 'from... to...' to show the starting point and the ending point of a change.

  • The share price rose from $40 to $48.
  • Debt was reduced from $20 million to $15 million.
  • Gross margin improved from 28% to 33%.

AT — Expressing a fixed position or level

Use 'at' when stating where a figure stands, not how it changed.

  • Cash stood at $5.2 million at year end.
  • The interest rate is currently at 4.5%.
  • The stock is trading at $62 per share.
Grammar Reference 2: Present Perfect vs Past Simple in Financial Reporting

Why this distinction matters

In financial English, choosing between the present perfect and past simple signals whether results are complete and historical, or whether they connect to the current situation. Mixing them up sounds unprofessional.

Past Simple — completed, historical events

Use the past simple for results, decisions, or events in a completed, specific time period in the past.

  • Revenue grew by 8% in fiscal year 2022.
  • The board declared a dividend of $0.50 last quarter.
  • The company reduced its debt when it sold the subsidiary in 2021.

Present Perfect — relevance to now

Use the present perfect for results or changes with current relevance, or when no specific past time is given. Common with: since, over the past X years, so far, recently, yet, already.

  • Revenue has grown by 8% since the restructuring.
  • The company has not yet published its full-year results.
  • Margins have improved significantly over the past three quarters.

Key time expressions to watch

These time expressions help you choose the right tense:

  • Past simple: last year, in Q3, in 2021, at that time, when…
  • Present perfect: since, over the past…, so far this year, recently, yet, already, this quarter (if still open)
Grammar Reference 3: Financial Noun Phrases & Collocations

What are collocations?

Collocations are word combinations that native speakers use naturally together. In finance, specific adjectives, verbs, and nouns combine in fixed ways. Using the wrong combination sounds unnatural even if it is grammatically correct.

Common Financial Collocations: Verbs + Nouns

These verb-noun combinations are essential in financial reporting:

  • report / record / post → a profit, a loss, earnings
  • declare / pay / cut → a dividend
  • raise / secure / repay → debt / financing / a loan
  • improve / squeeze / protect → margins

Common Financial Collocations: Adjective + Noun

These adjective-noun pairings are fixed in professional financial language:

  • gross profit / net profit / operating profit
  • current assets / fixed assets / intangible assets
  • working capital / share capital / venture capital
  • adjusted earnings / diluted earnings / basic earnings

Noun Phrases in Financial Statements

Financial documents use complex noun phrases. Understanding these helps you read statements more fluently:

  • earnings per share (EPS)
  • cost of goods sold (COGS)
  • property, plant and equipment (PP&E)
  • earnings before interest and tax (EBIT)

Case Study 1
Enron: When Financial Statements Deceive
USA · Energy · 2001 Collapse · Accounting Fraud  ·  How creative accounting and off-balance-sheet debt destroyed one of America's largest companies
Background

Enron Corporation was an American energy company headquartered in Houston, Texas, that rose to become the seventh-largest company in the United States by revenue. Throughout the late 1990s, Enron reported spectacular growth, strong earnings, and was repeatedly named Fortune's 'Most Innovative Company' in America. Its financial statements appeared to show a thriving, profitable business with substantial assets and consistent revenue growth. However, these statements concealed a fundamentally different reality — one built on sophisticated accounting manipulations designed to inflate earnings, hide debt, and present an artificially healthy financial picture to investors and analysts.

The Accounting Fraud

Enron's management, led by CEO Jeffrey Skilling and CFO Andrew Fastow, used a range of techniques to manipulate their financial statements. The most significant was the use of special purpose entities (SPEs) — off-balance-sheet vehicles that allowed Enron to transfer debt out of its balance sheet, making its leverage appear far lower than it actually was. This prevented investors from seeing the true level of risk. Additionally, Enron used mark-to-market accounting — originally intended for financial instruments — to immediately recognise the full estimated future value of long-term energy contracts as revenue, even when the cash had not been received and might never be. Costs, meanwhile, were deferred or hidden. The result was a reported income statement that bore little resemblance to the economic reality of the business.

Collapse and Consequences

When investigative journalists and analysts began questioning the opacity of Enron's financial disclosures in 2001, confidence collapsed rapidly. As the true state of the balance sheet became clear — with debts far exceeding what had been reported — Enron's share price plummeted from over $90 to less than $1. The company filed for bankruptcy in December 2001, at the time the largest corporate bankruptcy in US history. The scandal led directly to the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002, which introduced strict new requirements for financial reporting and internal controls. It also destroyed the reputation of Arthur Andersen, Enron's auditor, which had signed off on the misleading statements.

Case Study 2
Apple Inc.: Analysing a World-Class Balance Sheet
USA · Technology · Ongoing · Exemplary Financial Management  ·  What makes Apple's financial statements remarkable — and what every finance professional can learn from them
Revenue and Profitability

Apple Inc. is one of the most financially analysed companies in the world, and with good reason. Its income statement consistently demonstrates exceptional profitability at scale. In its fiscal year 2023, Apple reported total net revenue of approximately $383 billion — driven by iPhone sales, services, Mac, iPad, and wearables. What makes Apple's income statement particularly striking is not just the scale of revenue but the quality of its margins. The services segment, which includes the App Store, Apple Music, and iCloud, generates gross margins of over 70%, dramatically higher than the hardware segment's margin of approximately 36%. This shift towards services has been a deliberate strategy to improve overall profitability and reduce dependence on the more volatile hardware product cycle.

The Balance Sheet

Apple's balance sheet has attracted significant commentary from analysts for one particular reason: the extraordinary size of its cash and marketable securities position. At various points in recent history, Apple has held over $160 billion in cash and short-term investments — more than the GDP of many countries. This cash pile reflects the company's exceptional ability to generate free cash flow from its operations. However, Apple has also carried a significant amount of long-term debt — not because it needs to borrow to fund operations, but because borrowing at low interest rates and using the cash for share buybacks is financially advantageous in a low-rate environment. This strategic use of the balance sheet — holding cash while also carrying debt — is a deliberate financial management choice rather than a sign of financial weakness.

Lessons for Financial Analysis

Studying Apple's financial statements offers several important lessons. First, revenue size alone does not determine financial quality — margin structure and the mix between high-margin and low-margin business lines matter enormously. Second, a large debt balance does not automatically signal financial distress; context and cash generation capacity are essential to interpreting leverage correctly. Third, cash flow from operations — which Apple generates in excess of $110 billion annually — is the ultimate validation of a business model's strength. Finally, Apple's consistent return of capital to shareholders through dividends and buybacks demonstrates how a strong balance sheet can be actively used as a strategic tool rather than simply a record of past activity.
