Module Overview

Welcome to Module 12: Risk, Compliance, and Fraud Prevention. This module equips you with the specialised English vocabulary and communication skills needed to engage with enterprise risk management, regulatory compliance obligations, and corporate fraud prevention frameworks. From identifying and categorising financial risks, to drafting risk registers and presenting compliance reports, to discussing anti-money laundering controls and whistleblower procedures — this module covers the language professionals need to operate at the intersection of finance, law, and governance. Whether you work in risk, audit, compliance, or general finance, the skills developed here will enable you to communicate with confidence and authority on topics that are increasingly central to modern corporate life.

Text 1: Enterprise Risk Management — Frameworks, Frameworks, and the Three Lines of Defence

Enterprise risk management (ERM) represents a fundamental shift in how organisations approach risk — moving from a fragmented, departmental approach to a holistic, integrated framework that identifies and manages risks across the entire organisation. The most widely adopted ERM framework is that developed by the Committee of Sponsoring Organisations of the Treadway Commission (COSO), which defines ERM as a process carried out by an entity's board of directors, management, and other personnel to identify potential events that may affect the entity and manage risks within the entity's established risk appetite. Central to effective ERM is the risk register — a living document that catalogues each identified risk alongside its likelihood, potential impact, current controls, residual risk rating, and named risk owner. By maintaining a comprehensive risk register, management ensures that risks are not merely identified but actively monitored and reported at an appropriate level of the organisation.

The risk heat map is a powerful visual tool that complements the risk register by plotting risks on a matrix defined by two axes: likelihood (how probable is the risk?) and impact (how severe would the consequences be?). Risks that fall in the high-likelihood, high-impact quadrant are classified as 'critical' and demand immediate management attention and robust mitigating controls. Those in the low-likelihood, low-impact quadrant are typically monitored and accepted. The heat map enables the board and senior management to communicate the organisation's risk profile at a glance, facilitating rapid prioritisation and resource allocation. As the risk environment evolves — due to macroeconomic shifts, regulatory changes, technological disruption, or geopolitical events — the heat map must be regularly updated to reflect current exposures.

The three lines of defence model provides a governance structure for managing risk across an organisation. The first line consists of business operations — the managers and employees who own and manage risks in their day-to-day work and who are responsible for implementing and maintaining effective controls. The second line comprises the risk management and compliance functions, which develop risk frameworks, set policies, monitor adherence, and provide expert challenge and oversight to the first line without taking on operational ownership of the risks themselves. The third line is internal audit, which provides independent, objective assurance to the board and senior management that the first and second lines are operating effectively. This tiered structure ensures that risk management is embedded at every level of the organisation and that no single point of failure can undermine the overall control environment.

Text 2: Financial Crime — Money Laundering, Bribery, and the Language of Prevention

Financial crime encompasses a broad range of illegal activities — including money laundering, fraud, bribery and corruption, insider trading, and sanctions violations — that exploit the financial system for unlawful gain. For financial institutions and multinational corporations, managing financial crime risk is not merely a legal obligation but a strategic and reputational imperative. The consequences of financial crime compliance failures are severe: regulatory fines, criminal prosecution of individuals, suspension of operating licences, and lasting reputational damage that can cost institutions far more than any penalty imposed by a regulator. Accordingly, the investment that major banks and corporations make in financial crime compliance programmes — including staffing, technology, training, and third-party due diligence — has grown dramatically over the past two decades.

Money laundering — the process by which proceeds of criminal activity are made to appear as legitimate income — typically occurs in three stages. The first is placement: introducing the illicit funds into the financial system, often through cash-intensive businesses or informal value transfer mechanisms. The second is layering: creating a complex web of transactions designed to conceal the origin of the funds and frustrate investigators. The third is integration: the laundered funds re-enter the mainstream economy in a form that appears legitimate, enabling the criminal to use the proceeds freely. Anti-money laundering (AML) frameworks require financial institutions to monitor transactions for suspicious patterns, conduct customer due diligence and Know Your Customer (KYC) procedures, file suspicious activity reports (SARs) with the relevant authorities, and screen clients and transactions against international sanctions lists.

The fraud triangle — a conceptual model developed by criminologist Donald Cressey — identifies three conditions that must coexist for fraud to occur: pressure (the motivation, whether financial, professional, or personal), opportunity (the existence of weak or absent controls that make fraud feasible), and rationalisation (the psychological process by which the fraudster convinces themselves that the behaviour is justified). Organisations that understand this model are better placed to design preventive controls: reducing pressure by addressing underlying causes of employee stress or financial difficulty; eliminating opportunity through robust internal controls and segregation of duties; and countering rationalisation through a strong ethical culture, clear codes of conduct, and accessible whistleblower channels. When fraud is suspected, forensic accounting techniques — including transaction reconstruction, data analytics, and document examination — are deployed to gather evidence and quantify losses.

📌 Grammar Point 1: Relative Clauses — Defining and Non-Defining

Relative clauses are used extensively in compliance and risk writing to add precise information about people, documents, processes, and events. Choosing the correct type — and the correct punctuation — is essential for clarity and formality.

TypeFunctionPunctuationPronouns used
DefiningIdentifies which specific person/thing is meant — essential to meaningNo commaswho, that, which, whose
Non-definingAdds extra information about something already identified — can be removed without changing core meaningCommas requiredwho, which, whose (NOT 'that')
Defining: "The employee who reported the fraud was placed under witness protection." (tells us which employee)
Non-defining: "The Chief Compliance Officer, who joined in 2022, submitted the report." (adds information about an already-identified person)

Defining: "Any transaction that exceeds €10,000 must be reported." (essential condition)
Non-defining: "The risk register, which is updated quarterly, was presented to the board." (adds detail to an already-identified document)

Key rule: Non-defining relative clauses CANNOT use that. Only who, which, or whose. They are always surrounded by commas (or a comma and a full stop if at the end of the sentence).

Whose: Used to show possession — "The risk owner, whose department had the highest exposure, was required to present a mitigation plan."

📌 Grammar Point 2: Prepositional Phrases in Risk and Compliance Writing

Prepositional phrases of cause, purpose, and manner are essential connective tools in formal compliance and risk documents. They enable writers to explain why something happened, how it was done, and what it was done for — with the precision and formality required in regulatory writing.

FunctionPrepositional PhrasesExample
Cause / Reasonas a result of, due to, owing to, on account of"The fine was imposed as a result of repeated AML failures."
Purpose / Aimfor the purpose of, with a view to, in order to"The review was conducted for the purpose of identifying control gaps."
Manner / Methodby means of, through, via"The funds were transferred by means of a series of shell companies."
Conformityin accordance with, in line with, in compliance with"All transactions were processed in accordance with AML guidelines."
Responsein response to, following, in the light of"In response to the regulatory inquiry, the firm enhanced its KYC procedures."
Register note: In formal compliance documents, always prefer multi-word prepositional phrases over single prepositions: "as a result of" over "because of"; "in accordance with" over "following"; "for the purpose of" over "to". These choices signal professional register and convey the gravity appropriate to regulatory writing.

📌 Grammar Point 3: Nominalisation in Compliance and Risk Reports

Nominalisation — the process of converting verbs and adjectives into nouns or noun phrases — is a defining feature of formal compliance, legal, and risk writing. It creates a more impersonal, objective tone and allows complex ideas to be expressed concisely.

Verb / AdjectiveNominalised formExample in context
fail (to comply)failure (to comply) / non-compliance"Non-compliance with AML rules resulted in a significant fine."
investigateinvestigation / the investigation of"The investigation of the suspicious transactions is ongoing."
decide (to implement)decision (to implement) / the implementation of"Management's decision to implement dual controls reduced risk significantly."
violateviolation / breach"The violation of sanctions regulations triggered a regulatory inquiry."
disclosedisclosure / non-disclosure"Non-disclosure of material risks to shareholders constitutes a serious offence."
assessassessment / the assessment of"The committee's assessment of residual risk was presented to the board."
Informal: "We found that the controls failed and this caused a big loss."
Formal (nominalised): "The failure of controls resulted in a significant financial loss."

Informal: "The bank didn't disclose the risks to shareholders."
Formal (nominalised): "The bank's non-disclosure of material risks to shareholders constituted a breach of its regulatory obligations."

Tip: Overusing nominalisation can make writing difficult to read. Use it to establish formal tone and precision, but vary sentence structure to maintain readability. In spoken compliance briefings, a slightly less nominalised register is appropriate and more engaging.

Good morning, everyone. Welcome to today's financial crime compliance training. My name is Diana Chen, and I'm the Head of Financial Crime Compliance here at Hartwell Bank. This session is mandatory for all client-facing staff and covers two of the most critical areas of regulatory compliance: anti-money laundering, which we call AML, and Know Your Customer — KYC.

Let me start with money laundering. Money laundering is the process of making illegally obtained money appear legitimate. It's called 'laundering' because the goal is to clean dirty money — to remove the traces of its criminal origins. Every year, it's estimated that trillions of dollars are laundered through the global financial system. Banks are a primary target for money launderers because they provide access to the tools — accounts, wire transfers, foreign exchange — that make it possible.

Now, why does this matter to you? Because under the law, banks have an obligation to know who their customers are and to monitor transactions for signs of suspicious activity. If we fail to do this — if we allow criminals to use our bank to launder money — the consequences are severe. We can face enormous fines, loss of our banking licence, and criminal prosecution of individual employees. So this is not a box-ticking exercise. This is central to how we operate.

KYC — Know Your Customer — is the process by which we verify who our customers are before we open an account or provide a service. For individual customers, this means collecting proof of identity, such as a passport or national identity card, and proof of address, such as a utility bill or bank statement. For corporate customers, it means verifying the legal entity, its ultimate beneficial owners — the real people who own or control the company — and understanding the purpose of the relationship.

Why does KYC matter? Because if we don't know who our customers are, we can't assess the risk they pose. A customer whose source of funds we can't explain, or who owns a network of companies in multiple jurisdictions without clear business rationale, is a potential money laundering risk. Our job is to ask questions, document the answers, and — if the answers aren't satisfactory — to decline the relationship or file a suspicious activity report.

Speaking of which: what is a suspicious activity report, or SAR? A SAR is a report we file internally — and if the threshold is met, with the National Crime Agency — when we identify a transaction or pattern of behaviour that is inconsistent with what we know about the customer. Filing a SAR does not mean we're accusing someone of a crime. It means we've identified something we can't explain and we're alerting the appropriate authorities.

One final point: tipping off. Once a SAR has been filed, you must not tell the customer or any third party that a report has been made. This is called tipping off, and it is a criminal offence. If a customer asks about the status of a transaction that you've reported, you must seek guidance from the compliance team immediately — do not attempt to handle it yourself.

Thank you. I'll now take questions.

Host: Welcome back to Finance Uncovered. I'm your host, Sarah Winters, and today we're exploring one of the most important conceptual models in fraud prevention — the fraud triangle. Joining me is Dr. Patricia O'Brien, a forensic accountant with over twenty years of experience investigating corporate fraud. Patricia, welcome.

Guest: Thank you, Sarah. The fraud triangle is one of my favourite topics — because it's deceptively simple, but it explains so much about why intelligent, otherwise honest people end up committing fraud.

Host: Let's start from the beginning. Who developed the fraud triangle, and what does it say?

Guest: The model was developed by the American criminologist Donald Cressey in the 1950s, based on his research into white-collar criminals. He found that in virtually every case he studied, three conditions were present simultaneously. He called them pressure, opportunity, and rationalisation. And his insight was that you need all three for fraud to occur. Remove any one of them, and fraud becomes much less likely.

Host: Let's take them one by one. What does 'pressure' mean?

Guest: Pressure is the motivation — the reason why someone feels compelled to act dishonestly. This could be financial pressure: debt, gambling, an unexpected medical bill, a lifestyle that exceeds their salary. It could be professional pressure: fear of losing their job, the need to hit a performance target, or not wanting to report bad news to the boss. Or it could be personal: a sense of grievance, feeling undervalued or overlooked for promotion. The important thing to understand is that the pressure is usually private. The person doesn't share it. They sit with it alone.

Host: And 'opportunity'?

Guest: Opportunity is the most important element from a prevention standpoint, because it's the one that organisations have the most control over. Opportunity exists when there are weak or absent controls — when someone has access to assets or financial data that they shouldn't have, when there's no segregation of duties, when transactions aren't reviewed or reconciled, when the culture is such that people think they won't be caught. Every control weakness is an opportunity. And fraudsters are very good at finding them.

Host: How about 'rationalisation'?

Guest: This is the most psychologically interesting element. Rationalisation is the internal story the fraudster tells themselves to justify their behaviour. "I'm only borrowing the money — I'll pay it back." "The company owes me." "Everyone else is doing it." "I've worked here for twenty years and they treat me like nothing." These rationalisations can be quite elaborate, and they allow the person to preserve their self-image as an honest person even while committing fraud.

Host: So if you're an organisation wanting to reduce fraud risk, what do you do?

Guest: You work on all three elements. To reduce pressure, you need to look at your HR practices: are people paid fairly? Do they have access to employee assistance programmes? Are targets realistic? To reduce opportunity — and this is where most compliance programmes focus — you need strong internal controls: segregation of duties, dual authorisation, regular reconciliations, anonymous reporting channels. And to counter rationalisation, you need culture: a clear, visible commitment to ethics from the top, a zero-tolerance message, and — critically — a safe and genuinely confidential channel for people to report concerns. Because often, the person who knows about the fraud isn't the one committing it.

Host: Patricia, this has been genuinely illuminating. Thank you so much.

Guest: My pleasure. Remember: no control is perfect, but a strong culture is the best defence of all.

Case Study 1: The Collapse of Meridian Trading — A Rogue Trader and a Culture of Silence

Meridian Trading Partners was a mid-sized London-based investment bank with a strong reputation in fixed income markets. Between 2016 and 2021, a senior trader named Christopher Vale accumulated a series of increasingly large unauthorised positions in government bond derivatives. At their peak, these positions represented an exposure of approximately £680 million — more than four times the bank's stated risk limits. The positions were concealed through a combination of fictitious trades entered into the bank's systems, manipulation of daily profit and loss reports, and the exploitation of a gap in the firm's end-of-day reconciliation process that Vale had identified several years earlier.

The fraud persisted for five years for reasons that subsequent investigations found deeply troubling. Vale was one of the bank's most profitable traders, generating revenues that made senior management reluctant to scrutinise his activities too closely. When junior members of the risk team raised concerns about anomalies in his trading records on two separate occasions, their concerns were dismissed by a line manager who reportedly told them not to rock the boat. The bank's internal audit team had flagged weaknesses in the reconciliation process in 2018 but the remediation plan had been repeatedly deferred due to competing IT priorities.

The fraud unravelled in March 2021, when a routine counterparty confirmation from a government securities desk revealed a position that could not be reconciled with the bank's own records. The forensic accounting investigation that followed — conducted by an external firm — determined that Meridian had sustained losses of £423 million from the unwinding of Vale's positions in a period of market volatility. The bank was unable to absorb losses of this magnitude and entered administration within six weeks.

The Financial Conduct Authority's investigation concluded that Meridian had failed across multiple dimensions of its risk framework: risk limits were inadequately monitored, the control over system entries was insufficient, reconciliation weaknesses had been known and left unaddressed, and the culture did not support the escalation of legitimate concerns. Vale was convicted of fraud and sentenced to nine years in prison. The bank's CEO and Chief Risk Officer were personally fined and banned from working in financial services. The case prompted new FCA guidance on the minimum standards expected of investment firms in relation to trader oversight and the escalation of risk control concerns.

Case Study 2: Clearwater Payments — Building a Best-Practice AML Programme

Clearwater Payments was a rapidly growing US-based fintech company that processed cross-border payment transactions for small and medium-sized businesses in over forty countries. Founded in 2015, Clearwater had grown to process over $6 billion in annual transaction volume by 2020, driven by its competitive pricing and user-friendly platform. However, as the company prepared for a planned IPO in 2021, its investment bankers and legal advisers identified a significant problem: Clearwater's financial crime compliance programme was materially inadequate for a company of its size and cross-border complexity.

Specifically, the review found that KYC documentation was inconsistently collected and stored, with gaps in beneficial ownership information for a significant number of corporate clients. Transaction monitoring was rule-based and had not been updated since 2017, resulting in a high rate of false positives and a correspondingly low rate of genuine alerts being actioned. The company had filed only eleven SARs in its entire history despite processing millions of transactions in jurisdictions classified as high-risk. There was no dedicated financial crime compliance team: AML responsibilities had been distributed informally across the general compliance and operations functions.

Clearwater's board responded decisively. It appointed a Chief Financial Crime Officer — a role that had not previously existed — and engaged an external compliance consultancy to design and implement a best-practice AML programme. Over the following eighteen months, the company rebuilt its KYC infrastructure from the ground up, implementing a digital onboarding platform that automated identity verification and beneficial ownership checks. Transaction monitoring was upgraded to a machine-learning-based system that could identify genuine suspicious activity with significantly greater accuracy and a much lower false positive rate.

A dedicated financial crime team of twenty-two specialists was hired, and a formal SAR process was established with clear escalation procedures and legal sign-off requirements. The company also introduced a firm-wide AML training programme and a confidential employee reporting channel for financial crime concerns. By the time of its IPO in late 2022, Clearwater had received a satisfactory rating from its primary regulator following a comprehensive examination — a result that its Chief Financial Crime Officer credited to the board's commitment to treating compliance as a strategic investment rather than a regulatory burden. The IPO raised $480 million and valued the company at $3.2 billion.
