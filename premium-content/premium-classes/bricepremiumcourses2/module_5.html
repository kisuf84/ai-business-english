Module Overview

Welcome to Module 5: Financial Ratios and Performance Indicators. This module provides a comprehensive, professionally oriented guide to the financial ratios used by analysts, investors, credit officers, and finance professionals to assess business performance. Building on the financial statement knowledge of Modules 1–4, you will now learn how to calculate, contextualise, and communicate key ratios across five major categories: profitability, liquidity, efficiency, leverage, and market valuation.

This module is designed for professionals who need to present ratio analysis in meetings and reports, interpret ratios in analyst briefings and credit assessments, and use the precise English vocabulary and grammar structures that make ratio analysis credible, clear, and persuasive. You will practise the specific language patterns used when ratios are improving, deteriorating, or being compared across companies and time periods.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Calculate and explain the key profitability ratios — gross margin, EBITDA margin, net profit margin, return on equity, and return on capital employed — in professional English.
2
Calculate and interpret liquidity ratios — current ratio and quick ratio — and efficiency ratios — inventory days, receivable days, and payable days — and explain what they reveal about operational performance.
3
Describe and contextualise leverage ratios — net debt to EBITDA and interest cover — using precise language to discuss financial risk and debt sustainability.
4
Explain the main market valuation ratios — P/E ratio, EV/EBITDA, and price-to-book — and discuss what they imply about investor expectations and company value.
5
Use noun clauses and relative clauses correctly to define, explain, and contextualise financial ratios in written and spoken professional communication.
6
Apply language of cause and effect accurately to explain why ratios have improved or deteriorated and what they imply for management action.
7
Write a formal ratio analysis report comparing two companies and a professional briefing note interpreting a company's key performance indicators for a non-specialist audience.
8
Listen to and understand analyst briefings, investor presentations, and credit assessments that use financial ratios to evaluate business performance and investment risk.

Key Vocabulary

21 essential terms for reading and discussing financial ratios and performance indicators at B1–B2 level.

Module Vocabulary
📈
Return on Equity (ROE)
Net profit divided by shareholders' equity, expressed as a percentage. Measures how effectively management uses shareholders' capital to generate profit.
🏗️
Return on Capital Employed (ROCE)
EBIT divided by capital employed (total assets minus current liabilities). Measures how efficiently a company generates profit from its total capital base.
🔁
Return on Assets (ROA)
Net profit divided by total assets. Measures how efficiently a company uses all its assets — both debt-financed and equity-financed — to generate profit.
📦
Inventory days
Average inventory divided by cost of goods sold, multiplied by 365. Measures the average number of days a company holds its stock before selling it. Lower is generally better.
📆
Receivable days (DSO)
Average accounts receivable divided by revenue, multiplied by 365. Measures the average number of days taken to collect payment from customers. Also called Days Sales Outstanding.
📋
Payable days (DPO)
Average accounts payable divided by cost of goods sold, multiplied by 365. Measures the average number of days a company takes to pay its suppliers. Also called Days Payable Outstanding.
🔄
Cash conversion cycle (CCC)
Inventory days plus receivable days minus payable days. Measures the number of days a company's cash is tied up in the operating cycle. A lower or negative CCC indicates strong cash efficiency.
💹
Price-to-Earnings ratio (P/E)
Market price per share divided by earnings per share (EPS). A key valuation metric showing how much investors are willing to pay for each unit of earnings. High P/E implies high growth expectations.
🌐
EV/EBITDA
Enterprise Value divided by EBITDA. A widely used valuation multiple that is capital structure neutral — useful for comparing companies with different levels of debt. Lower multiples may suggest undervaluation.
📖
Price-to-book ratio (P/B)
Market capitalisation divided by net book value of equity (total equity). Shows the premium investors pay over the accounting value of a company's net assets.
🏦
Enterprise Value (EV)
Market capitalisation plus net debt. Represents the total value of a business as it would appear in an acquisition — including the cost of taking on its debt and receiving its cash.
📐
DuPont analysis
A framework that decomposes Return on Equity into three components: net profit margin × asset turnover × financial leverage. Helps identify which driver is causing ROE to change.
⚡
Asset turnover
Revenue divided by total assets. An efficiency ratio measuring how effectively a company uses its asset base to generate revenue. Higher is generally better.
🔢
Earnings per share (EPS)
Net profit divided by the number of shares outstanding. One of the most widely cited metrics for assessing a company's profitability on a per-share basis.
📉
Net debt to EBITDA
(Total debt minus cash) divided by EBITDA. A leverage ratio measuring how many years of EBITDA it would take to repay net debt. Widely used by lenders and analysts to assess debt sustainability.
⚖️
Interest cover ratio
EBIT divided by interest expense. Measures how comfortably a company can pay its interest obligations from operating earnings. A ratio below 2x raises significant concern.
💡
Peer comparison
The process of benchmarking a company's financial ratios against those of similar businesses in the same industry. Essential for contextualising whether a ratio is strong, average, or weak.
📊
Trailing vs forward P/E
Trailing P/E uses actual historical earnings; forward P/E uses analyst forecasts of future earnings. Forward P/E is generally considered more relevant for valuation decisions.
🔻
Diluted EPS
Earnings per share calculated including all potential shares — such as from stock options and convertible bonds — providing a more conservative and realistic measure of per-share earnings.
🌱
PEG ratio
P/E ratio divided by the expected earnings growth rate. Adjusts the P/E ratio for growth, so a stock with a high P/E but equally high growth may still be fairly valued. A PEG below 1 is often seen as attractive.
🔍
Working capital ratio
Current assets divided by current liabilities — the same calculation as the current ratio. Often used interchangeably in working capital analysis discussions.

Text 1: Profitability and Efficiency Ratios — Measuring Business Performance

How analysts use ratios to assess how well a company converts resources into profit

Financial ratios are the most powerful tools available for translating raw financial statement data into meaningful insights about business performance. Rather than looking at absolute figures — which can be misleading when comparing companies of different sizes or in different industries — ratios express financial relationships proportionally, enabling meaningful comparisons across time, peers, and sectors. Among the most important categories are profitability ratios, which measure how effectively a company converts revenue and capital into profit. The gross profit margin — gross profit divided by revenue — shows the efficiency of production or service delivery. The EBITDA margin shows operating profitability before capital investment and financing decisions. The net profit margin, at the bottom of the income statement, reflects the final profitability after all costs and taxes. A consistent improvement in margins over multiple periods signals genuine operating leverage and cost discipline.

Return ratios provide a deeper layer of analysis by measuring profitability relative to the capital invested in the business. Return on Capital Employed (ROCE), calculated by dividing EBIT by capital employed, answers the fundamental question: how much operating profit does the company generate for every unit of capital it deploys? Companies that consistently generate ROCE above their cost of capital are creating shareholder value; those that earn below it are destroying it. Return on Equity (ROE) specifically measures returns relative to shareholders' capital, and can be decomposed using DuPont analysis — which separates ROE into three components: net profit margin (how much of each revenue dollar becomes profit), asset turnover (how efficiently assets generate revenue), and financial leverage (how much of the asset base is funded by debt). DuPont analysis reveals whether a high ROE is driven by genuine operational efficiency or simply by high leverage.

Efficiency ratios measure how well a company manages its operational assets and working capital. Inventory days shows how long goods sit in the warehouse before being sold; receivable days shows how quickly customers pay; payable days shows how long the company takes to pay its own suppliers. Together, these three metrics calculate the cash conversion cycle — the number of days cash is tied up in the operating process. A company that sells quickly, collects promptly, and takes longer to pay its suppliers will have a short or even negative cash conversion cycle, meaning it generates cash before it needs to pay for the inputs that created it. This is a significant operational and financial advantage, and it is precisely the dynamic that underpins the business models of retailers like Amazon and Walmart.

Text 2: Leverage, Liquidity, and Valuation Ratios — Assessing Risk and Value

How analysts assess financial risk and what investors pay for a business

Leverage ratios measure the extent to which a company relies on debt financing and its ability to service that debt from earnings. The most widely used leverage ratio is net debt to EBITDA — calculated by dividing net debt (total debt minus cash) by EBITDA. This ratio answers a straightforward question: if the company devoted all its EBITDA to debt repayment, how many years would it take to clear its net debt? A ratio of 1x to 2x is considered low and conservative; 3x to 4x is moderate; above 4x raises significant concerns about the company's ability to weather an economic downturn or service its debt if EBITDA were to fall. Lenders typically embed this ratio into debt covenants, requiring companies to maintain it below a specified threshold — often 3.5x or 4x. The interest cover ratio — EBIT divided by interest expense — is the second key leverage metric. A ratio below 2x means the company is generating less than twice its interest expense in operating profit, which leaves very little buffer against earnings volatility.

Liquidity ratios focus on a company's ability to meet its short-term obligations. The current ratio — current assets divided by current liabilities — shows whether a company has sufficient short-term assets to cover its near-term liabilities. A ratio above 1.0 is generally considered satisfactory, though the appropriate level varies significantly by industry. Supermarkets, for example, typically operate with current ratios below 1.0 because they collect cash from customers immediately while paying suppliers on extended terms — a structural negative working capital position that reflects business model strength rather than financial stress. The quick ratio — current assets minus inventory, divided by current liabilities — is more conservative, removing inventory from the calculation because it may not be quickly convertible to cash. A quick ratio below 0.5 would typically concern analysts.

Valuation ratios are used by investors to assess whether a company's stock is cheap, fairly priced, or expensive relative to its earnings, assets, or growth prospects. The Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio is the most widely cited: a high P/E implies that investors expect strong future earnings growth, while a low P/E may indicate either a value opportunity or a business in structural decline. EV/EBITDA is often preferred by M&A practitioners and private equity because it is capital structure neutral — it removes the distorting effect of different debt levels and tax rates, making cross-company comparisons more reliable. The price-to-book ratio compares the market value of equity to its accounting book value; a ratio below 1.0 means the market values the company at less than the value of its net assets on the balance sheet, which can signal either deep undervaluation or fundamental concerns about the quality of the business.

🔍 GRAMMAR REVIEW

Grammar Reference

Detailed reference boxes covering all three grammar points in this module.

Grammar Reference 1: Noun Clauses and Relative Clauses in Financial Analysis

What are noun clauses?

A noun clause functions as a noun within a sentence — it can be the subject, object, or complement of a main clause. In financial analysis, noun clauses are used to report findings, explain what ratios show, and describe what analysts expect.

Noun clauses with 'that'

Use 'that' to introduce a noun clause after verbs such as: note, confirm, show, suggest, indicate, find, argue, explain.

  • The analysis shows that the company's margins have improved significantly.
  • Management confirmed that the ROE increase was driven by genuine operational gains.
  • The report found that net debt to EBITDA had risen above the covenant limit.

Noun clauses with 'what'

Use 'what' to introduce a noun clause that means 'the thing that'. Very common in financial commentary when discussing what ratios reveal.

  • The P/E ratio shows what investors are willing to pay per unit of earnings.
  • What concerns analysts most is the rising leverage ratio.
  • The DuPont framework reveals what is driving the change in ROE.

Relative clauses with 'which' and 'that'

Use relative clauses to define or describe ratios, metrics, and financial concepts. 'Which' introduces a non-defining clause (extra information); 'that' introduces a defining clause (essential information).

  • EV/EBITDA, which removes the effect of capital structure, is preferred in M&A analysis. (non-defining)
  • The ratio that analysts monitor most closely is net debt to EBITDA. (defining)
  • The covenant, which was set at 3.5x, has now been breached. (non-defining)
Grammar Reference 2: Cause and Effect Language in Ratio Commentary

Why cause and effect matters in ratio analysis

Ratio analysis is not just about calculating numbers — it requires explaining why a ratio has changed and what the consequences are. Professional analysts use a range of cause and effect structures to make their commentary clear and credible.

Expressing cause — what caused the ratio to change

Use these structures to identify the cause of a ratio movement:

  • 'due to' / 'owing to' / 'as a result of' + noun phrase: 'The gross margin fell due to rising raw material costs.'
  • 'because' + clause: 'The ROE improved because margins expanded significantly.'
  • 'was driven by' / 'was caused by': 'The improvement in ROE was driven by higher asset turnover.'

Expressing effect — what the ratio change led to

Use these structures to describe the consequences of a ratio change:

  • 'resulted in' / 'led to' + noun phrase: 'The inventory build-up resulted in higher inventory days.'
  • 'caused' + object + infinitive: 'The interest rate rise caused the interest cover ratio to fall.'
  • 'meaning that' / 'which meant that': 'Receivable days increased, meaning the company's cash conversion worsened.'

Combined cause-effect sentences

Professional commentary often combines cause and effect in a single sentence:

  • 'As a result of the margin compression, ROCE fell from 18% to 12%.'
  • 'The sharp increase in debt, combined with lower EBITDA, caused the leverage ratio to breach the covenant.'
  • 'Improved working capital management allowed the cash conversion cycle to shorten by 15 days.'
Grammar Reference 3: Financial Ratio Collocations and Fixed Expressions

Why collocations matter in ratio analysis language

Ratio analysis has its own professional vocabulary of fixed expressions and collocations. Using the wrong word combination — even when the meaning is clear — sounds unnatural in professional financial communication and reduces credibility.

Verb collocations with ratio movements

These verb-noun pairings are fixed and should be memorised:

  • trade at → 'The stock is trading at a P/E of 18x.'
  • stand at / come in at → 'The ROE stood at 22% at year end.'
  • breach → 'The company breached its interest cover covenant.'
  • exceed / outperform → 'ROCE exceeded the cost of capital for the fifth consecutive year.'

Adjective collocations with ratios

These adjective-noun pairs are natural in professional ratio commentary:

  • 'a comfortable interest cover ratio' (not 'a good interest cover ratio')
  • 'a stretched / elevated leverage ratio' (not 'a big leverage ratio')
  • 'capital-structure-neutral multiple' (used specifically for EV/EBITDA)
  • 'sector-leading / sector-average / below-sector margins'

Preposition patterns in ratio language

These prepositional patterns are fixed in ratio discussions:

  • 'at a premium to / at a discount to' → valuation relative to peers
  • 'relative to / compared with' → ratio comparisons
  • 'in line with / broadly consistent with' → ratios matching expectations
  • 'against the backdrop of' → providing context for ratio movements

Case Study 1
The Decline of General Electric: When Ratios Told the Story First
USA · Industrial Conglomerate · 2000–2018 · Financial Deterioration  ·  How financial ratio trends signalled GE's structural decline years before the crisis became front-page news
Background

General Electric (GE) was once the most admired and valuable company in the United States, consistently cited as a model of management excellence under legendary CEO Jack Welch. At its peak in 2000, GE's market capitalisation exceeded $500 billion, making it one of the largest companies in the world. However, the years following Welch's retirement in 2001 told a very different story — one that perceptive ratio analysts could have read years before the company's problems became public. GE had diversified aggressively into financial services through its GE Capital division, which came to represent nearly half of the company's total earnings by the mid-2000s. This made the industrial conglomerate deeply exposed to financial market risk in a way that its headline earnings figures obscured.

The Ratio Warning Signs

Several key ratios began to deteriorate significantly in the years before GE's crisis became undeniable. Return on Capital Employed fell steadily from the mid-teens in the early 2000s to single digits by 2017, reflecting the destruction of value from poor acquisition decisions and inefficient capital allocation. The interest cover ratio deteriorated as GE Capital's debt load grew: by 2008, the consolidated group's interest coverage was uncomfortably thin, and the company came close to losing its AAA credit rating during the financial crisis — a rare occurrence for an industrial company. Asset turnover declined substantially as GE acquired businesses at large premiums that were never fully realised in operating performance. Cash conversion quality deteriorated — operating cash flow consistently fell short of reported net income, a classic indicator of earnings quality concerns. Analysts who focused on these ratio trends rather than headline earnings would have been well positioned to identify the deterioration long before it was reflected in management guidance.

The Collapse and Lessons

By 2017 and 2018, GE's financial position had deteriorated to the point where the company cut its dividend for only the second time since the Great Depression. The stock fell by more than 60% in 2017 alone. New management undertook an urgent restructuring, selling off major divisions and writing down billions in asset values. The GE case offers several important lessons for ratio analysts. First, headline earnings and revenue growth can mask underlying deterioration — it is the return ratios and cash flow quality metrics that reveal the true picture. Second, a company's financial structure can make it vulnerable in ways that are invisible from the income statement but visible in the leverage and coverage ratios. Third, ratio analysis must be conducted longitudinally — a single year's ratios tell you little; it is the trend over five to ten years that reveals whether a business is compounding value or quietly destroying it.

Case Study 2
Microsoft's Transformation: How Ratios Reflect Strategic Reinvention
USA · Technology · 2014–Present · From Hardware to Cloud  ·  How the shift to a cloud subscription model transformed every key financial ratio at Microsoft
The Strategic Shift Under Satya Nadella

When Satya Nadella became CEO of Microsoft in February 2014, the company was widely seen as a fading technology giant — struggling to compete in mobile, over-reliant on declining PC sales, and trading at a modest P/E ratio that reflected low investor expectations. Under Nadella's leadership, Microsoft embarked on a fundamental strategic reinvention, pivoting aggressively from a product-licence model towards a cloud-based subscription model centred on Azure, Office 365, and Dynamics 365. This transformation had profound implications for every major financial ratio category — and the ratio story of Microsoft over the subsequent decade is one of the most instructive examples of how strategic change manifests in financial metrics.

The Ratio Transformation

The shift to subscription revenue drove a dramatic improvement in Microsoft's profitability ratios. The gross margin expanded from approximately 65% in 2014 to over 70% by 2023, as the high-margin cloud and software businesses grew to dominate the revenue mix. EBITDA margin expanded similarly, reflecting the operating leverage inherent in cloud businesses — once infrastructure is built, incremental revenue flows through to profit at very high rates. ROCE improved from approximately 20% in 2014 to over 35% in 2023, reflecting both the high profitability of the cloud model and the efficient capital intensity of software. On the valuation side, the transformation was even more dramatic. In 2014, Microsoft traded at a P/E multiple of around 13x — reflecting market scepticism about its future. By 2023, the P/E had expanded to over 30x, a more than doubling of the valuation multiple that reflected investors' transformed view of the company's quality and growth prospects.

Lessons for Ratio Analysis

Microsoft's transformation offers several important lessons for financial ratio analysts. First, ratios are not static properties of a business — they can change fundamentally when strategy changes, and tracking these changes over time is one of the most powerful forms of fundamental analysis. Second, margin expansion driven by a structural shift in business mix — rather than just cost-cutting — is among the most valuable and durable forms of improvement, because it reflects the underlying economics of the business model rather than temporary management actions. Third, the relationship between business quality and valuation multiples is real and quantifiable: as Microsoft's fundamentals improved, its P/E multiple expanded dramatically, demonstrating that valuation is ultimately driven by the quality and sustainability of returns. Analysts who understood the strategic direction in 2014 and tracked the early ratio signals — improving gross margins, rising ROCE — would have been well positioned to capture one of the most significant value creation stories of the decade.
