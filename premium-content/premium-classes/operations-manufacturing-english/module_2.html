Planning Vocabulary

Study these 21 essential production planning terms — the vocabulary of demand and supply management, scheduling, materials planning, and the cross-functional disciplines that keep production running reliably.

📚 Key Legal Terms
📅
Master Production Schedule
The primary output of the production planning process — a time-phased plan specifying exactly what finished products will be produced, in what quantities, and by when. The MPS translates the higher-level sales and operations plan (which might specify monthly volume by product family) into specific production orders for individual products at specific dates. It is the bridge between demand (what customers want) and supply (what the factory can make). The MPS drives the rest of the planning system: materials requirements planning (MRP) uses the MPS as its primary input to determine what materials need to be purchased or produced. The MPS must balance three competing objectives: customer service (delivering what customers want when they want it), capacity utilisation (keeping the factory busy without overloading it), and inventory (minimising the stock held while ensuring production continuity).
📊
Demand Forecast
A projection of the quantity of a product that customers will want to buy over a future time period — the primary input to the production planning process. Demand forecasting methods range from simple (using historical sales data with seasonal adjustments) to sophisticated (incorporating market intelligence, customer orders, promotional plans, and statistical modelling). All forecasts are wrong — the goal is to be usefully close to actual demand while understanding the likely range of error. Two types of demand drive production planning: independent demand (demand from external customers, which must be forecast because it is outside the company's control) and dependent demand (demand derived from the MPS — if 100 finished products are planned, the component and material quantities required are calculated directly from the bill of materials, not forecast). Only independent demand requires forecasting.
📋
Bill of Materials
A structured list of all components, sub-assemblies, raw materials, and quantities required to produce one unit of a finished product — the recipe that defines the product's composition. The BOM is hierarchical: at the top level is the finished product; below it are the major sub-assemblies; below them are the individual components and raw materials. A car's BOM, for example, would list the engine, gearbox, body, and interior at the first level; within the engine sub-assembly, it would list the cylinder block, pistons, crankshaft, and so on. The BOM is called a 'bill' because it is historically derived from the bill of materials that a purchasing department would send to a supplier — a list of what was needed. The BOM is the foundational data structure of MRP: multiplying the MPS quantity by the BOM quantities gives the gross material requirements.
🔄
Materials Requirements Planning
A computer-based planning system that calculates the materials and components needed to meet the master production schedule — when they are needed and in what quantities. MRP takes three inputs: the MPS (what finished products are required and when), the BOM (what materials are needed to make each finished product), and inventory records (what is already in stock or on order). From these inputs, MRP calculates: the gross requirements (total materials needed), the net requirements (gross requirements minus available inventory and scheduled receipts), and planned orders (when to place purchase orders or start production of sub-assemblies to ensure materials arrive on time). MRP was a revolution in manufacturing planning when it was first implemented in the 1960s and 1970s — replacing manual, error-prone material scheduling with systematic, computer-generated requirements.
🏭
Make to Order
A production strategy in which manufacturing begins only after a confirmed customer order is received — products are not produced in advance and held in finished goods inventory. Make-to-order is appropriate when: products are customised to individual customer specifications, demand variability is high (making it risky to hold finished goods inventory), products have short shelf lives or high obsolescence risk, or the product variety is too high to stock all variants. Examples: bespoke machinery, custom furniture, specialist engineering components, commercial aircraft. The challenge of make-to-order is lead time: because production does not begin until the order arrives, customers must wait for the entire manufacturing lead time, which can be significant for complex products. Make-to-order contrasts with make-to-stock (which produces to a forecast and holds finished goods inventory) and assemble-to-order (which holds sub-assemblies and configures to order on receipt).
📦
Make to Stock
A production strategy in which products are manufactured to a forecast and held in finished goods inventory — ready for immediate shipment when customer orders are received. Make-to-stock is appropriate when: products are standardised (the same specification for all customers), demand is reasonably predictable, products can be stored without deterioration, and customers expect immediate or very short delivery. Examples: consumer goods, standard industrial components, commodity chemicals, packaged food products. The challenge of make-to-stock is inventory risk: if the forecast is wrong and actual demand is lower than planned, the company holds excess inventory (with associated holding costs and obsolescence risk); if actual demand is higher than planned, the company faces stockouts and lost sales. Accurate demand forecasting is the critical capability for make-to-stock operations.
🛡️
Safety Stock
A buffer of inventory held above the expected demand requirement — designed to protect production continuity and customer service levels against variability in demand and supply. Safety stock is the planned answer to the question: 'what if actual demand is higher than forecast, or what if a supplier delivers late?' Without safety stock, any demand spike or supply disruption immediately causes a stockout. The appropriate level of safety stock is determined by: the variability of demand (higher variability requires more safety stock), the variability of supply lead times (more variable lead times require more safety stock), and the target customer service level (higher service level targets require more safety stock). Safety stock is not waste — it is the cost of managing an uncertain world. However, excessive safety stock (held because planning is poor rather than because variability genuinely requires it) is waste: it ties up capital and can mask underlying process problems.
📬
Purchase Order
A formal commercial document issued by a buyer to a supplier — authorising the purchase of specified goods or services at agreed prices, quantities, and delivery terms. In manufacturing, purchase orders are the formal mechanism for ordering raw materials, components, and sub-assemblies from external suppliers. A purchase order typically specifies: the supplier's name and address, the buyer's name and purchase order number, the item description and part number, the quantity ordered, the agreed price, the required delivery date, the delivery address, and any applicable terms and conditions. Purchase orders are legally binding documents: once accepted by the supplier, both parties are committed to the stated terms. In MRP-driven planning systems, planned purchase orders are generated automatically by the system and then converted to firm purchase orders by buyers.
⏰
Lead Time
The total time elapsed between initiating an action and the completion of that action. In production planning, lead time has several distinct meanings: supplier lead time (the time between placing a purchase order and receiving the goods from the supplier), manufacturing lead time (the time between starting production of an item and completing it), and customer lead time (the time between a customer placing an order and receiving delivery). Lead times are critical to the planning process: the MRP system uses lead times to calculate when purchase orders must be placed (working backwards from the required delivery date minus the supplier lead time) and when production of sub-assemblies must start (working backwards from the required completion date minus the manufacturing lead time). Inaccurate lead time data in the planning system produces systematically incorrect planned order dates — one of the most common causes of material shortages and excess inventory existing simultaneously.
🔒
Frozen Zone
The portion of the production schedule — typically the nearest one to four weeks — that is treated as fixed and not subject to revision except in exceptional circumstances. Within the frozen zone, work orders have been released, materials have been allocated, operators have been assigned, and suppliers have been given firm delivery expectations. Allowing the schedule within the frozen zone to be changed at short notice creates: wasted set-up costs (if a job is cancelled after the set-up has been started), material shortages (if a new urgent job is inserted without checking material availability), and general disruption to the planning system. The frozen zone concept is a recognition that all changes to a production schedule have a cost — and that cost increases dramatically the closer the proposed change is to the planned production date. Changes within the frozen zone should require explicit senior approval and a cost justification.
✅
Available to Promise
The quantity of a product that a company can commit to deliver by a specific date — calculated by taking the planned production schedule and subtracting the customer orders already committed against that production. ATP gives the sales team a reliable basis for making delivery promises: rather than guessing what is available, the sales team can query the ATP calculation and tell customers exactly how many units are available and when. For example, if 500 units are planned in week 5 and 300 have already been committed to other customers, the ATP in week 5 is 200 units. ATP is calculated at the product level and is updated as new customer orders are received. In make-to-stock environments, ATP is the key tool for preventing over-commitment — selling more than can be delivered, which causes customer dissatisfaction and planning chaos.
🔀
Scheduling Conflict
A situation in which two or more production jobs require the same resource — machine, operator, tooling, or production line — at the same time, making it impossible to execute all jobs as planned. Scheduling conflicts are a normal occurrence in manufacturing: they arise when production volume is high relative to capacity, when maintenance reduces available capacity unexpectedly, when a priority job is inserted into a full schedule, or when multiple orders converge on a shared bottleneck resource. Resolving a scheduling conflict requires a prioritisation decision: which job gets the resource first, and which must wait or be rescheduled? The prioritisation criteria commonly used include: due date (the job due earliest gets priority), customer priority (the most important customer's job gets priority), and job criticality (the job that will cause the greatest downstream disruption if delayed gets priority).
📈
Capacity Planning
The process of determining whether the production system has sufficient capacity to execute the production plan — and taking action to close any gap between required and available capacity. Capacity planning in production management has three levels: long-range capacity planning (strategic decisions about facility size and major equipment investments, typically 1-5 year horizon), rough-cut capacity planning (medium-term assessment of whether the MPS is feasible given key resource constraints, typically 3-12 months), and detailed capacity planning or CRP (short-term work centre loading analysis, typically 0-12 weeks). Capacity decisions take time to implement — adding a shift can be done in weeks, but building a new production line takes months or years. This is why the planning horizon for capacity decisions must be longer than the planning horizon for production scheduling.
🎯
Production Run
A period of continuous production of a single product or variant on a production line — from the start of the set-up or changeover to the completion of the last unit before the next changeover. The production run quantity (also called the batch size in batch production) is a fundamental planning decision: longer runs mean fewer changeovers (reducing set-up costs and time) but larger inventory accumulations; shorter runs mean more frequent changeovers but smaller batches, lower inventory, and faster response to demand changes. The economic order quantity (EOQ) model calculates the run length that minimises total cost by balancing set-up costs against inventory holding costs. In Lean manufacturing, the goal is to reduce changeover times so dramatically that short runs become economically viable — eliminating the trade-off between set-up cost efficiency and inventory minimisation.
📡
Sales and Operations Planning
A cross-functional business process that aligns sales demand plans with operational supply plans — typically conducted monthly at the product family or aggregate level, with a planning horizon of 3-18 months. S&OP brings together the sales team (who understand demand and customer expectations), the operations team (who understand production capacity and lead times), the finance team (who understand the financial implications of the plan), and sometimes procurement (who understand supplier constraints). The S&OP process produces a single integrated plan — one number for demand, one number for supply — that all functions commit to operating against. S&OP prevents the common situation where sales is planning for higher demand than operations can produce, or where operations is building inventory for demand that will not materialise. Effective S&OP is one of the most valuable planning practices in manufacturing management.
🧮
Economic Order Quantity
A formula that calculates the optimal order quantity — the quantity that minimises the total cost of ordering and holding inventory. The EOQ balances two cost elements: ordering costs (fixed costs incurred each time an order is placed, including administrative costs, set-up costs for production runs, or delivery charges) and holding costs (variable costs that increase with inventory level, including storage, insurance, capital cost, and obsolescence). The EOQ formula: square root of (2 × annual demand × ordering cost) ÷ holding cost per unit per year. A higher ordering cost per order pushes the EOQ up (fewer, larger orders); a higher holding cost pushes the EOQ down (more, smaller orders). The EOQ is a useful analytical tool but rests on simplifying assumptions (constant demand, known costs, no quantity discounts) that rarely hold perfectly in practice. Its primary value is conceptual: it makes explicit the trade-off between ordering and holding costs.
📉
Stockout
A situation in which a required item is not available in inventory when it is needed — either for production (causing a production stoppage) or for customer orders (causing a delivery failure or lost sale). Stockouts are among the most damaging operational events in manufacturing: they stop production lines, create urgent and expensive expediting activity, damage customer relationships, and sometimes cause permanent loss of customer business. The probability of a stockout is determined by: the level of safety stock held, the variability of demand and supply lead times, and the accuracy of the planning system. In MRP-driven systems, stockouts often occur not because total inventory is insufficient but because the wrong items are in stock — a 'wrong inventory' problem caused by inaccurate forecasts, inaccurate bills of materials, or incorrect inventory records.
🔁
Expediting
The process of urgently accelerating the procurement or production of an item that is required sooner than the standard lead time allows — typically in response to a stockout, a demand spike, or a planning failure. Expediting is the operational symptom of a planning breakdown: if the planning system were working well, materials would arrive when needed without expediting. Expediting costs are significant and largely hidden: premium freight charges, overtime labour, disruption to other jobs in the production schedule, supplier relationship strain, and planner time diverted from proactive planning to reactive firefighting. The 'expediting trap' is a self-reinforcing cycle: poor planning leads to shortages, which leads to expediting, which disrupts the schedule and creates new shortages, which require more expediting. Reducing expediting to near-zero is a sign of a well-functioning planning system.
🗓️
Planning Horizon
The length of time into the future that a production plan covers. Different planning decisions require different horizons: strategic capacity decisions (new facilities, major equipment) require horizons of 1-5 years; sales and operations planning requires 3-18 months; master production scheduling requires 3-12 months; detailed scheduling (which job runs on which machine at what time) requires 0-12 weeks. The planning horizon must be long enough to allow lead times to be accommodated: if the longest supplier lead time is 16 weeks, the MPS must extend at least 16 weeks into the future — otherwise purchase orders cannot be placed in time. At the near end of the planning horizon, plans are firm; further out, they are increasingly provisional. A common planning horizon structure: frozen zone (0-4 weeks, firm), slushy zone (4-12 weeks, subject to change with agreement), and liquid zone (12+ weeks, subject to change as demand evolves).
🔗
Pegging
A planning system feature that traces the relationship between a planned order (for a component, sub-assembly, or raw material) and the higher-level demand that created it — allowing planners to understand why a specific planned order has been generated and what will be affected if it is changed. Pegging is the ability to drill down from a finished product order through all the sub-assemblies and components it requires (forward pegging) or to trace a component order back to the finished product orders that require it (backward pegging). Pegging is essential for exception management: if a supplier cannot deliver a component on time, backward pegging shows which finished product orders will be affected and allows the planner to reschedule those orders and communicate the impact to customers before the shortage occurs rather than after.
📲
Available Capacity
The maximum production output a work centre, machine, or production line can achieve during a defined time period — after accounting for planned downtime (maintenance, changeovers, breaks, and shift patterns) but before accounting for actual production inefficiencies such as quality failures and unplanned stoppages. Available capacity is the denominator in the capacity utilisation calculation (actual output ÷ available capacity = utilisation). It is distinct from theoretical capacity (which assumes no downtime) and demonstrated capacity (the actual output achieved, which may be lower than available capacity due to OEE losses). In capacity planning, the comparison between required capacity (calculated from the production plan) and available capacity (determined by the facility's planned operating pattern) identifies where capacity bottlenecks will occur and where idle capacity exists.

Text 1: How Production Planning Actually Works — The Reality Behind the Theory

Production planning textbooks describe a logical, sequential process: forecast demand, calculate material requirements, schedule production, manage capacity. In practice, production planning is a continuous, simultaneous negotiation between competing demands, imperfect information, and constantly changing realities. The demand forecast changes. Suppliers deliver late. Machines break down. A key customer calls to request a priority order. A component fails its incoming quality inspection. An operator is absent. Each of these events — which happen multiple times every week in most manufacturing operations — invalidates some portion of the carefully constructed production plan and requires an immediate planning response. The most important skill in production planning is not the ability to construct a perfect plan but the ability to maintain operational continuity when reality diverges from the plan — which it always does.

The fundamental tension in production planning is between responsiveness and efficiency. Responsiveness means changing the production plan whenever the need arises — inserting priority orders, adjusting quantities, rescheduling jobs to accommodate customer requests. Efficiency means running the factory in a stable, predictable pattern — long production runs, minimal changeovers, stable schedules that allow operators and machines to work without constant disruption. These two objectives are inherently in conflict: a factory that is maximally responsive to every customer request will be maximally inefficient; a factory that maximises production efficiency will be maximally unresponsive. The production planner's job is to find a workable balance between these objectives — one that delivers adequate customer service without destroying operational efficiency.

The planning hierarchy — from long-range capacity planning through S&OP and MPS to detailed scheduling — is the framework that manages this tension across different time horizons. At the longest time horizon (1-5 years), capacity decisions are made without knowing the specific products that will be demanded — the planner is managing aggregate volume. At the medium horizon (3-18 months), the S&OP process aligns sales and operations plans at the product family level. At the short horizon (0-12 weeks), the MPS and detailed scheduling deal with specific products, specific quantities, and specific dates. The hierarchy exists because different decisions require different information, different lead times to implement, and different levels of specificity. A factory that tries to manage all planning decisions at the same level of detail will be overwhelmed by the complexity; one that manages them at the appropriate level for each horizon can handle the complexity systematically.

Text 2: The MPS and MRP — How the Core Planning Engine Works

The master production schedule (MPS) is the central document of production planning — the plan that everything else derives from. The MPS specifies, for each time period (typically a week), exactly which products will be produced and in what quantities. It is the production planner's primary commitment: what the factory has agreed to produce. Once the MPS is set, it drives two downstream processes simultaneously: MRP (which calculates what materials are needed to execute the MPS) and capacity planning (which checks whether the factory has sufficient capacity to execute the MPS). If MRP reveals a material shortage or capacity planning reveals a capacity overload, the MPS must be revised. In this sense, the MPS is not just a plan — it is a hypothesis that is tested against material availability and capacity before being firmed up.

MRP works through a simple but powerful logic. For each item in the MPS, it asks: how many units of each component do I need, and when? To answer this, it uses the bill of materials (BOM): if the MPS specifies 100 units of Product A in week 5, and Product A's BOM specifies 3 units of Component X per finished product, then 300 units of Component X are needed in week 5. But if there are already 120 units of Component X in stock and a purchase order for 50 units due to arrive in week 4, then the net requirement is only 130 units (300 − 120 − 50). And if the supplier lead time for Component X is 3 weeks, then the purchase order for 130 units must be placed no later than the end of week 2. This logic — gross requirements minus available inventory and scheduled receipts, offset by the lead time — is repeated for every component in every BOM, generating planned purchase orders and production orders across all time periods in the planning horizon.

The accuracy of MRP output depends entirely on the accuracy of its three inputs: the MPS, the BOM, and the inventory records. In practice, all three are imperfect. The MPS may change because of forecast errors or customer requests. The BOM may have errors — components listed at the wrong quantity, items omitted, or engineering changes not yet reflected. Inventory records are frequently inaccurate because of unrecorded transactions (items taken from the store without a system transaction, scrap not recorded, goods received but not booked). A commonly cited rule is that MRP requires 99% inventory record accuracy to function reliably — a standard that most manufacturing operations struggle to achieve without disciplined cycle counting programmes. When MRP is fed with inaccurate data, it generates plans that look precise but are systematically wrong — a condition sometimes described as 'garbage in, garbage out.'

Grammar Point 1: MPS and MRP Language — Describing Plans, Requirements, and Exceptions
MPS Language — Time-Phased Planning Communication

MPS communication requires precise time-phased language — specifying what will be produced, in what quantity, and in which specific time period. Vague MPS language ('we'll make more next month') is not actionable; precise MPS language specifies the numbers and the weeks.

MPS statement: 'The MPS for weeks 4 and 5 has Lines 1 and 2 scheduled at 115% of available capacity — a combined overload of approximately 1,400 units across the two weeks.'
MPS revision: 'I am proposing to pull 700 units of Order A from week 5 to week 3 to balance the capacity load. This requires confirming that: (1) Line 1 capacity in week 3 can accommodate the additional volume; (2) raw materials for Order A are available for a week 3 start; and (3) customer delivery dates are not advanced by the earlier production date.'
MPS achievement: '87% of the week's MPS target achieved through day 3 — 2,156 units produced against a planned 2,480. Line 2 is 380 units behind plan due to the hydraulic issue on Monday and Tuesday.'
MPS horizon: 'The MPS extends 12 weeks forward — weeks 1-3 are in the frozen zone, weeks 4-8 are subject to change with materials confirmation, and weeks 9-12 are provisional based on the current demand forecast.'
MRP Language — Requirements, Shortages, and Planned Orders

MRP language requires specific quantities, time periods, and supply chain terms. The calculation chain — gross requirement → available inventory → net requirement → planned order → order date — should be visible in the communication.

MRP shortage report: 'MRP has flagged a critical shortage on Component J for week 3. Gross requirement: 480 units (120 finished products × 4 per BOM). Current stock: 180 units. Scheduled receipts before week 3: zero. Net shortage: 300 units. Supplier lead time: 4 weeks. Emergency order must be placed today if we are to receive by week 7.'
Planned order communication: 'MRP has generated a planned purchase order for 870 units of Component K, required by week 6. At the current 6-week supplier lead time, the order must be placed this week to arrive on time.'
BOM error communication: 'The BOM for Product X has been found to specify 3 units of Component Q per finished product — the correct quantity is 4. This error has been causing a systematic shortfall of 1 unit of Component Q per finished product over the past 6 weeks, which explains the recurring stockouts at the assembly stage.'
Inventory accuracy: 'The MRP exception report is generating 47 alerts — most are false alarms caused by inventory record inaccuracies. Inventory record accuracy is currently 91.3%. Until we get to 99%, the MRP output requires significant manual filtering by the planning team.'
Exception Management Language — Prioritising and Escalating

Planning exceptions require clear categorisation (high/medium/low priority), quantified impact, and explicit decision requests. Every exception report should tell the reader: what the problem is, how serious it is, what the options are, and what decision is needed.

Priority classification: 'Three material exceptions this week: one high priority (Component J shortage — production stoppage risk in week 3), one medium priority (resin lead time extension — gap risk in weeks 11-12), and one low priority (Product D overstock — no operational impact).'
Option presentation: 'To resolve the Component J shortage, we have three options: (1) approve emergency order at secondary supplier — 15% premium cost, 2-week lead time; (2) reduce week 3 MPS by 75 units — customer communication required; (3) pull Component J from safety stock — depletes safety stock to zero, production risk if any other demand spike occurs.'
Decision request: 'I need a decision on the Component J exception by end of today to avoid missing the supplier's production cut-off. My recommendation is Option 1 — secondary supplier at 15% premium. The cost of the premium (£4,500) is less than the cost of a production stoppage on Line 2 (approximately £18,000 per day).'
Escalation: 'I am escalating the resin supply issue to operations director level — the 10-week lead time increase has implications for the November-December production plan that are beyond my authority to resolve within the normal planning process.'

Grammar Point 2: Capacity and Schedule Language
Capacity Language — Available, Required, and Utilisation

Capacity language must distinguish between available capacity (what can be produced), required capacity (what the plan demands), and actual output (what is actually produced). Capacity gaps and overloads must be quantified in units and time periods.

Available capacity: 'Available capacity on Line 1 this week: 3,840 units (480 units per hour × 8 hours × 1 shift). Planned maintenance reduces available capacity on Wednesday morning by 480 units.'
Required capacity: 'The revised MPS for Q3 requires 18,400 units per month — equivalent to approximately 4,600 units per week. Available capacity on two lines is 4,200 units per week — a gap of 400 units per week (8.7% overload). Closing the gap requires either one 4-hour Saturday shift per week or the early return of Line 3 from maintenance.'
Capacity utilisation: 'Line 2 is currently running at 112% of available capacity — running extended shifts to meet the Order B commitment. Utilisation above 100% is unsustainable for more than 2-3 weeks.'
Rough-cut capacity check: 'The RCCP check against the revised Q1 MPS shows capacity is broadly balanced except in weeks 8-10, where the convergence of three large orders creates a 23% overload on the injection moulding press. We need to either spread the orders or confirm that the press can be run on Saturdays during those weeks.'
Scheduling Language — Priority, Sequencing, and Conflict Resolution

Scheduling language describes the sequence of production jobs, the basis for prioritisation, and the resolution of conflicts. It is specific about machine names, job numbers, due dates, and quantities.

Priority statement: 'Job 4521 (Order A, 120 units, due Thursday) takes priority over Job 4547 (Order B, 80 units, due Friday) on the CNC lathe. This is an A-tier customer order and the due date is earlier. Job 4547 will be rescheduled to the afternoon slot — risk of a 4-hour delay to Friday delivery.'

Grammar Point 3: Supplier and Inventory Language
Supplier Communication — Professional and Specific

Supplier communication in planning must be professional, specific, and relationship-aware. It should state the request clearly, provide the business context, specify the timeline, and invite a specific response.

Expediting language: 'I am requesting an expedited delivery on PO-0892 (Component J, 300 units). We require 150 units by end of week 3 and 150 units by end of week 4. We will cover premium freight costs. Please confirm your ability to meet this schedule by close of business today.'
Lead time discussion: 'We are requesting a reduction in standard lead time from 8 weeks to 6 weeks for Component X — a 25% reduction. We would like to discuss whether this is achievable and what commercial arrangement might support it. Our annual volume for Component X is approximately £320,000.'
Quality rejection: 'We are formally notifying you of a quality rejection on PO-0887 (Component P, 340 of 1,200 units non-conforming — hardness 45 HRC against specified 50-55 HRC). We require replacement units within 5 working days and a root cause analysis within 10 working days.'
Avoiding aggression: Supplier communication should be firm but not aggressive. 'Your delivery is unacceptably late' damages the relationship; 'PO-0876 is 5 days overdue — this has created a production planning risk. Can we confirm the current status and revised ETA?' achieves the same objective while maintaining the relationship.
Inventory Language — Levels, Targets, and Trends

Inventory language must specify what type of inventory is being discussed (raw materials, WIP, finished goods), the quantity or value, the target, and the trend. 'Inventory is too high' is not actionable; specific inventory language enables management action.

Inventory status: 'Current inventory position: raw materials 18 days forward coverage (target 7 days — 11 days above target); WIP 3.2 days (target 3 days — on target); finished goods 22 days (target 8 days — 14 days above target due to delayed customer shipment).'
Inventory reduction language: 'The finished goods reduction from 22 days to 8 days requires two changes: switching the top 8 SKUs from weekly to daily shipping (reducing the average stock cycle from 7 days to 1 day) and implementing demand-triggered replenishment for the remaining SKUs (replacing fixed monthly order quantities).'
Safety stock calculation: 'Proposed safety stock for Component K: 870 units — calculated at 98% service level given the demand coefficient of variation (0.30) and lead time variability (±0.8 weeks). This is 45% above the current 600-unit holding.'
Stockout report: 'Three components on stockout this week — all linked to the same supplier who reduced batch sizes without notification. Impact: Line 2 stopped for 3.5 hours Monday; approximately 420 units of finished goods not produced. Emergency orders placed; normal supply expected by Wednesday.'

When presenting planning calculations (EOQ, safety stock, capacity utilisation), show the logic clearly — not just the answer. The business audience needs to understand the basis for the recommendation, not just the conclusion.

Case Study 1: The Invention of MRP — How Joseph Orlicky Transformed Manufacturing Planning
How Materials Requirements Planning replaced manual material scheduling and created the systematic planning tools still used in factories worldwide

Background

Before the development of Materials Requirements Planning in the 1960s, manufacturing companies managed their material requirements through a combination of manual order point systems and the accumulated experience of their purchasing and planning staff. The order point system worked simply: when stock fell below a defined reorder point, a new order was placed. This approach worked reasonably well for items with stable, independent demand, but failed systematically for manufactured goods where material requirements were dependent on production plans — if the factory planned to produce 500 finished products in week 8, the material requirements were not random but precisely calculable from the bill of materials. Joseph Orlicky, a Czech-born IBM computer scientist, recognised this distinction between independent and dependent demand and built the first MRP logic on an IBM System/360 computer at Black and Decker in 1964.

The MRP Revolution

Orlicky's MRP system replaced intuition and experience with systematic calculation. Instead of guessing how much material to order and when, MRP calculated the precise requirements from three data sources: the master production schedule (what finished products were planned and when), the bill of materials (what components were in each finished product), and inventory records (what was already in stock or on order). The calculation was straightforward in concept but required a computer to execute across the hundreds or thousands of components in a typical industrial BOM: multiply the MPS quantities by the BOM quantities, subtract available inventory, and offset by the supplier lead time to determine when purchase orders needed to be placed. Orlicky published his comprehensive guide to MRP in 1975 — 'Materials Requirements Planning: The New Way of Life in Production and Inventory Management' — which became the foundational text for industrial planning practice. By the early 1980s, MRP had been adopted by most major manufacturers in the United States and Europe.

MRP's Legacy and Limitations

MRP's legacy is profound: virtually every manufacturing planning system in use today is either an MRP system or an evolution of MRP. Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) systems — SAP, Oracle, Microsoft Dynamics — all contain MRP engines at their core. MRP II (Manufacturing Resource Planning, developed in the 1980s) extended the original MRP logic to include capacity planning, financial planning, and simulation. However, MRP has significant limitations that have driven the development of more advanced planning approaches. MRP assumes infinite capacity — it plans material requirements without checking whether the factory can actually execute the plan. It assumes fixed lead times — in reality, lead times vary with factory loading. It generates plans in batch mode — plans are run overnight and may be stale by morning. And it produces large volumes of planned orders that must be reviewed and acted on by human planners — creating significant workload when the planning environment is volatile. These limitations motivated the development of Advanced Planning and Scheduling (APS) systems, which incorporate capacity constraints and real-time responsiveness.

Case Study 2: Sales and Operations Planning — How the Best Manufacturing Companies Align Demand and Supply
How S&OP evolved from a simple production planning tool into the most important cross-functional management process in manufacturing — and the specific practices that distinguish excellent S&OP from routine planning meetings

Background

Sales and Operations Planning emerged as a formal business process in the mid-1980s, primarily through the work of practitioners and consultants associated with the Oliver Wight Companies — particularly Richard Ling and Tom Wallace, who helped popularise the process through writing and consulting work. Before S&OP, most manufacturing companies operated with a 'wall' between the sales function (which managed demand) and the operations function (which managed supply): sales planned independently of operations, creating plans that were often operationally infeasible; operations planned independently of demand, creating capacity and inventory positions that did not reflect the market reality. The result was a constant tension between sales teams promising what operations could not deliver and operations building inventory for demand that did not materialise. S&OP was designed to bridge this wall by creating a single, integrated demand and supply plan.

What Best-Practice S&OP Looks Like

Best-practice S&OP follows a structured monthly process with five stages. Stage 1 — Demand Review: the sales team reviews and updates the demand forecast by product family, incorporating the latest market intelligence, customer feedback, and promotional plans. Stage 2 — Supply Review: the operations team assesses whether the updated demand forecast can be met within current capacity and lead time constraints — identifying gaps and proposing solutions. Stage 3 — Pre-S&OP Meeting: a cross-functional team (typically including planning, sales, operations, procurement, and finance) reviews the demand-supply gaps and prepares recommendations for the executive meeting. Stage 4 — Executive S&OP Meeting: the senior leadership team reviews the recommendations, resolves any outstanding conflicts, and makes the decisions needed to create a single integrated plan. Stage 5 — Implementation: the agreed plan is communicated to the operating functions and drives the MPS, procurement plans, and financial projections. The entire process typically runs over 2-3 weeks and culminates in the executive meeting.

Why S&OP Fails and How to Fix It

Despite its conceptual simplicity, S&OP fails in many organisations for the same predictable reasons. The most common failure: the process becomes a reporting exercise rather than a decision-making forum — data is presented but no decisions are made, and the plan that emerges from the executive meeting looks like the plan that was input into the process, unchanged. The second common failure: the demand and supply plans remain independent — sales submits a forecast, operations prepares a supply plan, and the two are never genuinely reconciled in a single number. The third common failure: the process focuses on the past rather than the future — reviewing last month's performance rather than making forward-looking decisions on the next 3-18 months. The signature of excellent S&OP is a process where a single number emerges from the executive meeting — one demand plan and one supply plan that all functions commit to — and where the decisions made in the executive meeting are different from the inputs that entered the pre-S&OP meeting.
