📚 Key Legal Terms
📅
Steering Committee
A formal periodic meeting between the consulting team and the client's senior leadership — typically held every two to four weeks during a consulting engagement — at which progress is presented, key findings are shared, critical decisions are made, and the engagement's direction is calibrated. The steering committee is the most consequential client meeting in a consulting engagement because it is attended by the most senior decision-makers: the CEO, CFO, or business unit heads who have the authority to approve recommendations or redirect the team's work. Preparation for a steering committee is extensive: the deck is reviewed by the partner, talking points are rehearsed, and potential questions or objections are anticipated and prepared for in advance.
💼
Working Session
A less formal, more interactive meeting between the consulting team and the client's day-to-day team — focused on collaborative problem-solving, data sharing, hypothesis testing, or interim findings review rather than formal progress presentation. Working sessions are the primary mechanism through which the consulting team accesses the client's internal knowledge: the people in working sessions know where the data is, what the organisation's real constraints are, and which recommendations will face implementation resistance. Effective working sessions feel like a genuine collaboration rather than an interrogation — the consultant's role is to ask excellent questions, listen carefully, and synthesise what is said into analytical insights.
📋
Agenda
A structured document or verbal outline distributed before a meeting that specifies the topics to be covered, the time allocated to each, the format (discussion, decision, information), and any preparation the participants should complete in advance. A professional consulting meeting agenda is not simply a list of topics — it specifies the purpose of each item: is this item for information sharing, for discussion, or for decision? A well-crafted agenda also signals the meeting's overall purpose and allows participants to arrive prepared. Consulting teams typically send the agenda with the pre-read at least 24 hours before a meeting. An agenda-less meeting is a hallmark of poor meeting management.
📄
Pre-Read
Materials distributed to meeting participants before the meeting — typically a deck or summary document — that allow participants to review key information in advance so that the meeting time can be used for discussion and decision rather than information transfer. Pre-reads are standard practice in consulting meetings and reflect a fundamental principle: meeting time is the most expensive and non-renewable resource in a client engagement. A meeting in which participants spend the first 20 minutes reading the deck is a meeting that has wasted 20 minutes of client time. The pre-read shifts information transfer outside the meeting and reserves meeting time for the higher-value activities of analysis, discussion, and decision.
🎯
Decision Point
A specific moment in a meeting — typically preceded by a clear framing and a summary of the options — at which a decision is required from the meeting participants. Decision points must be explicitly identified by the meeting facilitator: without explicit identification, meetings frequently end without decisions, with participants unclear about what was agreed. Professional consulting language for identifying a decision point includes: 'At this point, we need to agree on...', 'To move the work forward, we need a decision on...', and 'The options as I see them are X and Y — which do you prefer?' The most common failure in consulting meetings is allowing a decision point to pass as discussion without explicitly calling it.
🔄
Action Item
A specific task, committed to by a named individual, with a clear deadline — arising from a meeting discussion. Action items are the primary mechanism through which meeting decisions become real-world progress. A professional action item has three elements: what will be done (specific task), who will do it (named individual, not 'the team'), and by when (specific date, not 'soon' or 'next week'). At the end of every consulting meeting, the facilitator should read back the agreed action items, confirm the owner and deadline for each, and send a written summary within 24 hours. An action item that lacks any of these three elements is likely to be either ignored or disputed after the meeting.
🅿️
Parking Lot
A designated space — typically a visible list on a whiteboard or in a shared document — where off-topic or out-of-scope issues raised during a meeting are captured for later discussion rather than being pursued immediately. The parking lot is a facilitation tool that serves two purposes: it prevents meetings from derailing into unproductive tangents, and it signals to the person who raised the issue that their concern has been heard and will be addressed — rather than simply dismissed. A parking lot item should always be reviewed at the end of the meeting with an explicit decision about what to do with it: schedule a separate discussion, add it to the next meeting agenda, or address it via email.
🔊
Facilitation
The process of guiding a meeting or discussion toward a productive outcome — by managing the agenda, drawing out contributions from participants, keeping the discussion focused, summarising key points, managing time, and moving the group toward decisions and action items. A skilled facilitator in a consulting meeting is not passive: they actively structure the discussion, ensure all relevant perspectives are heard, surface disagreements rather than allowing them to remain implicit, and apply the necessary pressure to reach conclusions before the meeting ends. In client-facing consulting work, the Engagement Manager typically facilitates working sessions, while the Partner facilitates steering committees.
⏸️
Silence
A deliberate pause in a meeting — used after asking a question, after presenting a provocative finding, or after a client makes a significant statement — to give participants time to think, to signal that the speaker has finished, or to create space for the client to speak. New consultants frequently make the mistake of filling silence with clarification, additional context, or nervous commentary — which prevents the client from responding and undermines the impact of the question or statement that preceded it. Experienced consultants use silence strategically: a two-second pause after 'What is your reaction to this finding?' is not awkward; it is productive. The client who fills the silence often reveals something more valuable than any question could have surfaced.
🔍
Clarifying Question
A question asked during a client meeting to better understand what the client has said — to ensure the consulting team has correctly understood the client's meaning, intention, or concern before responding. Clarifying questions are essential in client meetings because clients frequently use shorthand, assume context, or describe their actual concern indirectly. Typical clarifying questions: 'When you say the market is saturated, what specifically are you referring to?', 'Can you help me understand what you mean by that?', or 'Just to make sure I have the right picture — are you saying X or Y?' A clarifying question defers the consultant's response until the client's actual concern is understood — preventing a response that addresses the wrong issue.
🏗️
Straw Man
A preliminary or provisional proposal — deliberately incomplete or imperfect — presented to a client or team to provoke reaction and stimulate discussion rather than to be accepted as the final recommendation. The straw man approach is particularly useful in consulting meetings where the client is unable to articulate what they want but can easily identify what they don't want: 'We've put together an initial view on the go-to-market approach — think of it as a straw man designed to generate your reaction rather than represent our final recommendation. What works, what doesn't?' The response to a straw man often surfaces more specific and actionable client preferences than any open question could.
📝
Meeting Summary
A written document produced after a client meeting — typically within 24 hours — that records the key discussion points, decisions made, and action items agreed. The meeting summary is more than a record: it is a tool for alignment. In the absence of a written summary, different participants frequently recall the meeting's conclusions differently — which creates confusion, disputes, and the need for additional meetings to re-establish what was agreed. A professional meeting summary has four sections: key discussion points (what was discussed), decisions made (what was agreed), action items (what will be done, by whom, by when), and next steps (upcoming meetings or milestones). The meeting summary is typically sent by the Engagement Manager.
🚦
Pushback
A client's challenge or disagreement with a consulting finding, recommendation, or position — which may range from a genuine analytical concern to a politically motivated objection. Pushback is normal and healthy: a client who agrees with everything the consulting team says has either stopped engaging or has concerns they are not expressing. Professional response to pushback follows the 'acknowledge-understand-respond' pattern: acknowledge the concern without dismissing it, ask a clarifying question to understand the specific objection, and then respond to the actual concern rather than the surface statement. Never respond to pushback with defensiveness — it signals insecurity about the analysis.
🔗
Synthesis
The integration of disparate discussion points, findings, or perspectives into a coherent, structured conclusion — the step beyond summary in which the facilitator not only recalls what was said but identifies the pattern or insight that emerges from it. Synthesis is the highest-value skill in a consulting meeting: while anyone can summarise ('So you've said X, Y, and Z'), synthesis requires analytical judgment ('What I'm hearing is that the core concern is A — is that right?'). A consultant who can synthesise a complex client discussion in real time — extracting the central concern, the key constraint, or the emerging decision — dramatically increases the value of the meeting for the client.
📊
Status Update
A structured communication — in a meeting, email, or written report — that informs the client of the engagement's current progress against the agreed workplan, highlights any risks or delays, and confirms upcoming milestones. Status updates in consulting are formal and structured: they cover what has been completed since the last update, what is in progress, what is upcoming, and any issues or risks that require client attention or decision. A status update that only reports what is going well is incomplete — the most valuable element is the identification of issues and the proposed response, which demonstrates that the consulting team is in control of the engagement rather than simply reporting facts.
🗝️
Alignment
The state of shared understanding and agreement between the consulting team and the client team — on the engagement's direction, findings, recommendations, and next steps. Achieving alignment is one of the primary purposes of client meetings: through working sessions, steering committees, and informal check-ins, the consulting team validates that its direction is consistent with the client's evolving priorities and that key stakeholders are not heading toward a situation where the final recommendation will be a surprise. Alignment is not the same as agreement: the client may disagree with a specific recommendation but still be aligned with the process. Misalignment — particularly when it is discovered late — is one of the most common causes of consulting recommendations that are not implemented.
📣
Escalation
The process of bringing an unresolved issue, disagreement, or risk to a higher level of the client or consulting hierarchy for resolution — when the issue cannot be addressed at the working level. Escalation in a consulting context is typically managed by the Engagement Manager when a significant problem arises: a client team member who is not providing agreed data, a mid-engagement disagreement about scope, or a client working-level objection that cannot be resolved without senior approval. Escalation should be flagged to the Partner before it happens — so the partner can manage the senior client relationship appropriately — and should be framed as a joint problem to be solved rather than as a complaint.
⏱️
Hard Stop
A fixed meeting end time that cannot be extended — typically because a senior participant has another commitment immediately following. A hard stop must be communicated at the start of the meeting and actively managed by the facilitator: if the meeting agenda is running behind, the facilitator must make explicit choices about what to defer, accelerate, or cut. The professional response to a hard stop that is approaching before the agenda is complete: 'We have fifteen minutes remaining and we still need to cover decisions on X and Y. I'd suggest we take ten minutes on X — which requires a decision today — and defer Y to the follow-up email. Does that work?' Managing a hard stop professionally demonstrates engagement management competence.
✋
Open Question
A question that cannot be answered with 'yes' or 'no' — designed to draw out the client's thinking, perspective, or knowledge rather than to confirm or deny a specific hypothesis. Open questions are the primary tool for learning in a client meeting: 'What is your view on the competitive landscape?' 'How does your team currently approach this decision?' 'What would success look like for you?' Open questions invite narrative responses that surface context, constraint, and perspective that the consulting team cannot obtain from data alone. In working sessions, experienced consultants use open questions to draw out the client's most valuable knowledge — which is almost never the first thing the client says when asked a direct closed question.
🔒
Closed Question
A question that can be answered with 'yes' or 'no', or with a choice from a defined set of options — used to confirm a specific fact, elicit a decision, or move a meeting to a resolution. Closed questions are the primary tool for driving decisions and confirming alignment: 'Do you agree with this finding?', 'Is option A or option B your preference?', 'Can we confirm that this is the agreed next step?' The mistake is using closed questions when open questions are needed — asking 'Do you agree with the market sizing?' when the real question is 'What is your reaction to the market sizing and why?' A skilled consultant alternates between open questions (to draw out) and closed questions (to confirm and decide).
📬
Follow-Up Email
A professional communication sent after a client meeting — within 24 to 48 hours — that summarises key discussion points, confirms decisions made, specifies action items with owners and deadlines, and outlines next steps. The follow-up email is a critical piece of professional infrastructure in a consulting engagement: it creates a written record of what was agreed, prevents misalignment from developing between meetings, and signals to the client that the consulting team is organised and proactive. The follow-up email should be clear, brief, and specific — not a verbose meeting transcript. The most important sections: decisions confirmed (aligned with what the meeting was for) and action items (the specific tasks that will happen as a result of the meeting).

Text 1: How Consultants Run Effective Client Meetings — The Disciplines That Separate High-Performing Teams

A consulting engagement's success is substantially determined by the quality of its client meetings — not because the meetings themselves are where the analytical work happens, but because meetings are where the analytical work is validated, redirected, and converted into decisions. A team that produces excellent analysis but communicates it poorly in client meetings will struggle to achieve recommendations that are implemented. Conversely, a team that understands how to run meetings well — structuring discussions, drawing out the client's knowledge, managing disagreement professionally, and ending every meeting with clear decisions and action items — creates the conditions for implementation success even when the analysis is imperfect.

The most common reason consulting meetings fail to produce their intended value is the absence of explicit meeting architecture. This means: no pre-read distributed before the meeting (so participants spend the first twenty minutes reading rather than discussing), no agenda communicated in advance (so participants arrive without having thought about the meeting's purpose), no explicit identification of decision points (so the meeting ends in discussion rather than conclusion), and no summary of action items at the end (so participants leave with different understandings of what was agreed). These failures are not primarily failures of intelligence or analysis — they are failures of professional discipline. The most analytically excellent consulting team in the world produces no value if its meetings end without decisions.

The discipline that most distinguishes high-performing consulting teams in client meetings is the ability to synthesise. Most meeting participants can summarise what has been said; very few can extract, in real time, the insight that emerges from a complex discussion. When a client spending twenty minutes expressing concern about a recommendation is asked by a skilled consultant: 'What I'm hearing is that the core concern isn't the recommendation itself but the implementation risk — is that right?' — and the client says 'Yes, exactly' — the consultant has saved the meeting, redirected the engagement, and built significant client trust in ten seconds. The ability to synthesise — to name the pattern in what the client is saying — is among the most valued skills in consulting practice.

Text 2: Managing Difficult Moments in Client Meetings — Pushback, Silence, and Sensitive Disagreement

The most valuable professional skill in a client meeting is not the ability to present analysis confidently — it is the ability to handle the moments when the meeting goes wrong. A client who challenges a central finding, a room full of silence after a recommendation, a senior stakeholder who appears dismissive — these are the moments that test a consultant's professional composure and determine whether the engagement continues productively or begins to deteriorate. The good news is that these moments follow predictable patterns, and experienced consultants develop a set of reliable response approaches for each.

Client pushback — a challenge or disagreement with a finding or recommendation — is among the most common of these difficult moments. The instinctive but incorrect response is defensiveness: explaining at length why the analysis is correct, providing additional evidence, or becoming visibly flustered. This response signals insecurity, escalates the tension, and rarely resolves the objection. The professional response follows a consistent three-step pattern: first, acknowledge the concern without immediately agreeing or disagreeing ('That's an important point — thank you for raising it'); second, ask a clarifying question to understand the specific objection before responding ('Can you help me understand what specifically you're concerned about?'); third, respond to the specific concern that has been clarified, rather than to the surface statement. This approach — acknowledge, clarify, respond — de-escalates the tension, ensures the consultant understands the actual concern, and demonstrates professional confidence.

Silence is among the most mismanaged aspects of client meetings. Inexperienced consultants fill every silence with additional explanation, qualification, or nervous commentary — which prevents the client from responding, undermines the impact of what was just said, and signals a lack of confidence. A skilled consultant holds silence deliberately: after asking an important question, after presenting a significant finding, or after making a provocative recommendation. The silence gives the client space to think, signals that the consultant is genuinely interested in the client's response, and frequently surfaces information or perspectives that no amount of additional explanation could have produced. The professional rule: ask a question, then wait. If the silence feels uncomfortable, it is almost certainly more uncomfortable for you than for the client.

Grammar Point 1: Language for Opening, Structuring, and Closing Client Meetings
Opening Language — Setting Purpose, Agenda, and Tone

The first two minutes of a consulting meeting determine the tone for everything that follows. Professional opening language accomplishes four things: confirms the time available, states the meeting's purpose, outlines the agenda, and invites any additions. This signals to the client that the meeting is purposeful and that the team is in control.

'Good morning. We have two hours — I'd like to use the first hour to present the findings and the second hour to reach decisions on the strategic options. Before we start, are there any additional agenda items?'
'Our objectives for today are: [objective 1], [objective 2], and [objective 3]. We'll cover [topic A] from 9-9:45, [topic B] from 9:45-10:30, and use the final 30 minutes for decisions. Does that work?'
'We circulated the pre-read on Monday — for those who've reviewed it, we'll move straight to discussion. For those who haven't, the core finding is on slide four.'
'We have a hard stop at 3pm — I'll flag when we have fifteen minutes remaining to make sure we cover the decision items in time.'
Decision Point Language — Identifying and Driving to Decisions

The most common failure in consulting meetings is allowing a decision point to pass as discussion. Explicit language for calling a decision point — and for presenting the options clearly — is essential.

'At this point, we need to reach a decision on [topic]. The options as I see them are: [A], [B], [C]. Which direction does the group want to take?'
'To move the analysis forward, we need to agree today on [specific decision]. Without this, we cannot complete [next workstream] before the next steering committee.'
'We've discussed this thoroughly — I'd like to call it to a decision. Are there any remaining concerns before we confirm [direction]?'
'The recommendation is [X]. We're asking for your approval to proceed on this basis, or for direction to explore an alternative — [A] or [B].'
Closing Language — Confirming Decisions and Action Items

The close of a meeting must be structured and explicit. Participants should leave with identical understanding of what was decided and what will happen next. Ambiguity at the close is as damaging as absence of a decision.

'Before we close, let me confirm the decisions: [decision 1], [decision 2]. And the action items: [task / owner / deadline]. Are there any corrections?'
'To confirm next steps: we will [consulting action] by [date]; if you can [client action] by [date], we'll be on track for the steering committee on [date].'
'One final check — is there anything we discussed today that we haven't captured in the decisions or action items?'
'Thank you for a productive session. The meeting summary — including confirmed decisions and action items — will be with you by [time/date].'

Grammar Point 2: Language for Facilitation, Pushback, and Difficult Moments
Facilitation Language — Drawing Out and Focusing Discussion

Effective facilitation uses open questions to draw out thinking and closed questions to confirm agreement. The rhythm of alternating between the two keeps discussion productive and moves toward decisions.

Open questions to draw out: 'What is your reaction to this finding?' / 'What do you see as the primary barrier?' / 'How does your team currently approach this decision?'
Closed questions to confirm: 'Do you agree with this characterisation?' / 'Is Option A or Option B your preference?' / 'Can we confirm this as the agreed next step?'
Redirecting: 'That's an important point — I've added it to the parking lot. To stay on the agenda, can we return to [topic]?'
Synthesising: 'What I'm hearing is that the core concern is [X] — is that right?' (then wait for confirmation before proceeding)'
Pushback Language — Acknowledge, Clarify, Respond

The three-step pattern for handling client pushback professionally — acknowledge the concern, clarify the specific objection, respond to the specific concern — is the most reliable approach to maintaining composure and credibility under challenge.

Step 1 — Acknowledge: 'That's an important concern — thank you for raising it.' / 'I'm glad you've raised that — it's a point we want to address.'
Step 2 — Clarify: 'Can you help me understand what specifically you're concerned about?' / 'When you say the projection seems optimistic, are you questioning the growth rate, the market share, or the pricing assumption?'
Step 3 — Respond: [respond specifically to what the client has identified — not to the original statement] 'On the growth rate specifically — here's the basis for that assumption: [evidence].'
Avoid: Defensiveness ('Our analysis clearly shows...') / Over-explanation ('Let me walk through the methodology in detail...') / False agreement ('You're absolutely right — we'll revise it')
Language for Managing Silence and Uncertainty

Strategic silence and honest uncertainty management are marks of professional confidence. Both require resisting the instinct to fill gaps with noise.

After asking a question: ask it clearly, then stop. Wait. The silence is productive. Do not add 'does that make sense?' or 'any thoughts?' — these undermine the question.
After a significant finding: present it clearly, then pause. Let the client react before offering explanation. Additional context before the client reacts is almost always unnecessary.
When you don't know: 'I want to give you a precise answer rather than a rough one. Let me confirm by end of day tomorrow.' (never say 'I don't know' without a plan to find out).
When unexpected information emerges: 'That's very helpful — thank you. Can I ask a few questions about this?' (pursue the new information actively — do not dismiss it or become defensive about its implications)

Grammar Point 3: Professional Language for Written Meeting Communication
Agenda Writing — Clear, Specific, and Action-Oriented

A professional meeting agenda specifies not just the topics but the format and purpose of each item — making clear whether participants should arrive to receive information, contribute to discussion, or make a decision.

Standard format: '[Item name] | [Time] | [Format: Information / Discussion / Decision] | [Owner] | [Preparation required]'
For decision items: 'Entry Mode Decision [20 mins | Decision Required]: Background in pre-read pp. 8-12. Recommendation: Option A. Decision required: approval or direction. Owner: CEO (decision); Priya (present).'
Opening item: 'Objectives and agenda confirmation [5 mins | Information]: Purpose of meeting; confirmation of agenda; any additions.'
Closing item: 'Decisions, action items, and next steps [10 mins | Decision / Information]: Review and confirm decisions made; confirm action items with owners and deadlines; confirm next meeting.'
Follow-Up Email Structure — The Four Sections

A professional follow-up email after a consulting meeting has four sections, each with a specific purpose. This structure prevents ambiguity and creates a durable written record of the meeting's outcomes.

Section 1 — Key Discussion Points: 'The following topics were discussed: [brief summary of each — 1-2 sentences per topic]. Full discussion context is available in the deck circulated before the meeting.'
Section 2 — Decisions Confirmed: 'The following decisions were confirmed during the meeting: [decision 1 — including any conditions or caveats]; [decision 2].' [If no decision was made, state: 'No decision was reached on [topic] — this will be addressed at [next meeting]'.]
Section 3 — Action Items: 'Action Item 1: [Specific task] — Owner: [Named individual] — Due: [Specific date]. Action Item 2: [Specific task] — Owner: [Named individual] — Due: [Specific date].'
Section 4 — Next Steps: 'The next [steering committee / working session] is scheduled for [date, time, location]. We will circulate the pre-read by [date]. If there are any corrections to the above, please let us know by [date] so we can incorporate them.'
Register Calibration — Formal, Professional, and Clear

Follow-up emails and agendas require formal but not bureaucratic register. They should be clear enough that a participant who was not at the meeting could understand what was decided. They should never be vague about ownership or deadlines.

Vague (avoid): 'We'll follow up on the pricing question soon.' → Specific: 'We will provide a written analysis of the pricing premium assumption by Thursday 5pm.'
Vague (avoid): 'The team will revise the model.' → Specific: 'Priya (consulting) will revise the market sizing model to reflect the German state-level segmentation discussed today — circulating by Thursday 5pm.'
Vague (avoid): 'There was some disagreement about the entry mode — we'll discuss further.' → Specific: 'The entry mode decision was not resolved in today's meeting. The CEO has requested that Options A and B be further tested against the Q3 operational capacity data before the next steering committee on [date].'
Always: include a note if any items remain open — 'The following items were raised but not resolved during the meeting: [item] — to be addressed at [when] by [who].'

Case Study 1: The Pyramid Principle in Client Communication — How Barbara Minto's Framework Changed the Way Consultants Write and Speak
How one McKinsey consultant's communication framework became the standard for structured professional communication — and what it means for client meetings

Background

Barbara Minto joined McKinsey & Company as one of the firm's first female consultants in 1963. During her time at McKinsey and in her subsequent consulting career, she developed a framework for structured communication that she published in The Minto Pyramid Principle (1987) — a book that has since sold over a million copies and is used as a core training document by McKinsey and most other major consulting firms worldwide. The Pyramid Principle is, at its core, a framework for ensuring that communication — whether written or verbal — begins with the main point and then provides the supporting arguments and evidence, rather than building to the conclusion at the end. This sounds simple, but it runs directly counter to the way most people are taught to write and speak academically, where the conclusion typically comes last.

The Pyramid Principle in Client Meetings

The Pyramid Principle's most direct application in client meetings is in how consultants present findings and recommendations. The pyramid structure requires the presenter to begin with the answer — the conclusion, the recommendation, the 'so what' — and then provide the supporting arguments. 'We recommend Option A [answer]. We recommend it for three reasons [supporting arguments]. Reason one is... Reason two is... Reason three is... [evidence].' This structure is counterintuitive because it requires the speaker to commit to a position before providing all the evidence — but it is far more effective in client meetings because it answers the most important question first. A client who hears the recommendation before the analysis can evaluate the analysis against the recommendation; a client who hears the analysis first often disengages before the conclusion arrives.

Application to Meeting Communication Specifically

In client meetings, the Pyramid Principle has four specific applications. First, in status updates: begin with the headline ('We are on track for the steering committee' or 'There is a risk to the timeline') rather than building to it through a chronological recitation of what the team has done. Second, in presentations of findings: begin with the most important finding, then provide the supporting evidence. Third, in responses to questions: answer the question first ('Yes, we've modelled that scenario'), then provide the explanation. Fourth, in synthesis during a meeting: capture the central insight first ('What I'm hearing is that the core concern is implementation risk') rather than summarising all the points made before identifying the pattern. The pyramid principle is not just a communication framework — in client meetings, it is a discipline of clarity under pressure.

Case Study 2: How Jeff Bezos Banned PowerPoint at Amazon — and What It Teaches Consulting Teams About Meeting Design
Why Amazon's insistence on six-page narrative memos over PowerPoint presentations produces better decisions — and the implications for how consulting teams should design client meetings

Background

In the mid-2000s, Jeff Bezos made an unusual operational decision for a company of Amazon's scale: he banned PowerPoint presentations from most senior leadership meetings and replaced them with a six-page narrative memo — a structured written document that participants read in silence at the start of each meeting before any discussion begins. The rationale, as Bezos explained in his 2018 letter to shareholders, was that PowerPoint presentations allow presenters to hide weak thinking behind a structure of bullet points that appears organised but does not require genuine analytical coherence. A six-page narrative memo, by contrast, forces the author to construct a complete, coherent argument — one that cannot rely on visual structure, presenter energy, or the audience's willingness to fill analytical gaps.

What the Amazon Meeting Model Produces

The Amazon model produces two things that are relevant to consulting practice. First, it forces complete analytical thinking before the meeting: if the argument cannot be written as a coherent narrative, it is not yet ready to be decided. This is a direct challenge to the consulting practice of presenting preliminary or incomplete analysis in client meetings — the argument for doing so being that 'we want client input before we finalise'. The Amazon model suggests that presenting incomplete thinking imposes analytical work on the client that the consulting team should have completed before the meeting. Second, it dramatically improves decision quality: a room full of senior people who have all read the same six-page document before discussing it is a fundamentally different decision-making environment than one in which some have read the pre-read and others have not.

What Consulting Teams Can Learn

The Amazon model has three specific implications for consulting practice. First, the pre-read matters more than most teams treat it: a pre-read that is not read by all participants is equivalent to no pre-read. Teams that send pre-reads should consider whether the pre-read is compelling and concise enough that all participants will actually read it — not a sixty-slide deck but a four-to-six-page executive summary. Second, meeting time should be reserved for discussion and decision — not for information transfer. Any meeting that begins with the facilitator presenting information that participants could have reviewed in advance is wasting the most expensive meeting time: the first twenty minutes. Third, the quality of the preparation determines the quality of the decision. A decision made in a meeting where all participants are fully informed is categorically better than one made where only the presenting team has complete information.
