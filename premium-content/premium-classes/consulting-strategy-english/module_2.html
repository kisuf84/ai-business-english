Framework Vocabulary

Study these 21 essential strategy framework terms — the vocabulary of SWOT, PESTLE, Porter's Five Forces, competitive advantage, and strategic analysis.

Module Progress

📚 Key Legal Terms
🔲
SWOT Analysis
A structured framework that organises an organisation's internal and external strategic position into four categories: Strengths (internal capabilities and advantages), Weaknesses (internal limitations and gaps), Opportunities (external conditions that could be exploited), and Threats (external conditions that could cause harm). SWOT is the most widely used strategy framework because of its simplicity and versatility — applicable to organisations, business units, product lines, or individual strategic decisions. Its primary value is not the categorisation itself but the conversation it structures: SWOT forces a team to be explicit about what is true about the organisation now (internal) and what is true about the environment now (external).
🌍
PESTLE Analysis
A framework for scanning the macro-environmental factors that affect an organisation's strategic context — standing for Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, and Environmental factors. PESTLE is most useful at the beginning of a strategy engagement, before the organisation-level analysis: it establishes what external forces are shaping the landscape in which the company must compete. Political factors include regulatory change, trade policy, and political stability. Economic factors include interest rates, inflation, exchange rates, and GDP growth. Social factors include demographic shifts, consumer values, and lifestyle trends. Technological factors include disruptive innovations, digitalisation, and automation. Legal factors include employment law, competition law, and sector-specific regulation. Environmental factors include climate risk, sustainability standards, and ESG requirements.
🔵
Porter's Five Forces
A framework developed by Michael Porter (Harvard Business School, 1979) for analysing the competitive dynamics of an industry — specifically, the forces that determine the profitability potential of a market. The five forces are: (1) Competitive Rivalry — how intense is competition among existing players; (2) Threat of New Entrants — how easily can new competitors enter the market; (3) Threat of Substitution — how easily can customers switch to alternative products or services; (4) Bargaining Power of Buyers — how much leverage do customers have to push prices down; and (5) Bargaining Power of Suppliers — how much leverage do input providers have to push costs up. Markets with high competitive rivalry, easy entry, strong substitution threats, and powerful buyers and suppliers tend to be structurally unattractive.
🎯
The 3Cs Framework
A market analysis framework that structures the strategic landscape around three dimensions: Company (the internal capabilities, strengths, and strategic position of the organisation), Customer (the needs, behaviours, segments, and decision criteria of the target market), and Competitor (the strategies, strengths, weaknesses, and positioning of rival organisations). The 3Cs were developed by business strategist Kenichi Ohmae and provide a useful shorthand for ensuring that any strategy analysis covers the most important perspectives. A good strategy must make sense from all three angles simultaneously: it must leverage the Company's strengths, match what Customers value, and be differentiated from what Competitors offer.
💪
Strength
In SWOT analysis, an internal attribute or capability that gives the organisation an advantage relative to competitors. A genuine strength is specific, demonstrable, and competitively meaningful — not a generic statement like 'experienced team' or 'good culture'. Strong strengths in SWOT analysis are stated in comparative terms: 'the lowest cost logistics platform in the UK SME market', 'a proprietary contract management algorithm with 94% customer retention', or 'a 12-year exclusive partnership with the largest enterprise legal database provider'. The test of a genuine strength: would a competitor acknowledge it as an advantage? If not, it may be a neutral attribute rather than a true competitive strength.
⚠️
Weakness
In SWOT analysis, an internal limitation or gap that puts the organisation at a disadvantage relative to competitors or prevents it from pursuing available opportunities. Weaknesses must be internal to the organisation — factors within its control or resulting from its own decisions. The most common mistake in identifying weaknesses is describing external threats as weaknesses, or describing the absence of an opportunity as a weakness. Genuine weaknesses include: 'no dedicated customer success function in a market where competitors offer 24/7 enterprise support', 'single-founder dependency with no identified successor', or 'legacy technology infrastructure that cannot support real-time data processing'.
🚀
Opportunity
In SWOT analysis, an external condition or trend in the environment that the organisation could exploit to its advantage. Opportunities are external — they exist in the market, the regulatory environment, the technology landscape, or the competitive situation — and they are not yet realised advantages. The most common mistake in identifying opportunities is describing strengths as opportunities: 'our experienced team' is not an opportunity; 'increasing demand for specialised legal technology from in-house lawyers at high-growth SaaS companies' is an opportunity. A genuine opportunity exists regardless of whether the company currently has the capability to exploit it — the SWOT analysis then asks whether the company's strengths are well-positioned to capture it.
🔴
Threat
In SWOT analysis, an external condition or trend that could harm the organisation's performance or strategic position. Threats, like opportunities, are external — they arise from the environment, not from the organisation's own limitations. Common categories of threat include: new competitive entrants, regulatory changes that restrict the business model, technological disruption that makes the product obsolete, shifts in customer preference away from the current value proposition, and macroeconomic headwinds that reduce the addressable market. The strategic value of identifying threats is not to produce a list of risks but to assess which threats are material — likely to occur, significant in impact, and near-term enough to require a strategic response.
🏭
Competitive Rivalry
One of Porter's Five Forces — the intensity of competition among existing players in a market. High competitive rivalry is characterised by: many competitors of similar size and capability, low product differentiation (products are easily substitutable), high fixed costs that drive competitors to fill capacity regardless of price, and low switching costs for customers. Highly competitive markets tend to have lower average profitability because participants compete away the available returns. Factors that reduce competitive rivalry — and therefore increase market attractiveness — include high product differentiation, high customer loyalty, and regulated pricing that limits destructive price competition.
🚪
Threat of New Entrants
One of Porter's Five Forces — the degree to which new competitors can enter a market and challenge existing players. Markets with low barriers to entry are threatened by new entrants who increase supply and intensify competition. Barriers to entry include: capital requirements (high upfront investment deters entry), proprietary technology or intellectual property, regulatory licences or certifications, established brand loyalty that requires sustained marketing investment to overcome, and network effects that give incumbents structural advantages (a marketplace with more users is more valuable, making it harder for new entrants to match). High barriers to entry are a key source of sustainable competitive advantage for established players.
🔄
Threat of Substitution
One of Porter's Five Forces — the degree to which customers can switch from a product or service to a different type of solution that meets the same underlying need. Substitution threats come not from direct competitors but from different categories of solution: the threat to a legal technology platform might come not from a competing platform but from a law firm that offers the same services at a fixed monthly fee. The threat of substitution is highest when the substitute is cheaper, more convenient, or perceived as higher quality than the existing solution — and when switching costs for customers are low.
🛒
Buyer Power
One of Porter's Five Forces — the degree to which customers can influence price, quality, or terms in their favour. Buyer power is high when: customers are large relative to the supplier (a few large customers buying from many small suppliers), products are undifferentiated and customers can easily switch, customers have complete price and quality information, and customers could plausibly produce the product themselves ('backward integration'). High buyer power compresses supplier margins and forces investment in quality and service improvements. Consulting engagements frequently include an assessment of buyer power when evaluating whether a market is attractive to enter or whether a client's pricing strategy is sustainable.
⚙️
Supplier Power
One of Porter's Five Forces — the degree to which input providers can influence the prices or terms they charge. Supplier power is high when: there are few suppliers of a critical input, the input is unique or difficult to substitute, the supplier's product is an important component of the buyer's final product, and the supplier could plausibly sell directly to the buyer's customers ('forward integration'). For a logistics company, supplier power might be assessed in terms of fuel prices (high — oil markets are global), vehicle manufacturers (moderate — several viable alternatives), and specialist software providers (potentially high — if the software is deeply integrated and difficult to replace).
🌟
Competitive Advantage
The attribute or set of attributes that allows an organisation to outperform competitors — either by delivering greater value to customers (differentiation advantage) or by delivering equivalent value at lower cost (cost advantage). Competitive advantage is sustainable when it is rooted in resources or capabilities that are valuable, rare, difficult to imitate, and supported by the organisation's structure (the VRIO framework). Competitive advantage is temporary when it is based on factors that competitors can replicate quickly: a price reduction is not a competitive advantage; a proprietary technology that enables a structural cost reduction is. Identifying the source of a client's competitive advantage — or the absence of it — is one of the most common tasks in a strategy engagement.
🔬
Core Competency
A distinctive capability that gives an organisation a competitive advantage and cannot easily be replicated by competitors — originally described by C.K. Prahalad and Gary Hamel in their 1990 Harvard Business Review article 'The Core Competence of the Corporation'. A core competency meets three criteria: it provides access to a wide variety of markets, it makes a significant contribution to the perceived value of the end product, and it is difficult for competitors to imitate. Examples include Honda's core competency in engine design (applied across motorcycles, cars, boats, and power equipment) and Sony's core competency in miniaturisation. In consulting, identifying a client's core competencies — and assessing whether the proposed strategy builds on them — is a common strategic assessment task.
📊
Environmental Scan
A systematic review of the external factors — macro-environmental, industry-level, and competitive — that shape the strategic context of an organisation. An environmental scan typically begins with PESTLE (macro-level factors) and then narrows to Porter's Five Forces (industry-level dynamics) before moving to competitor and customer analysis. The environmental scan is the starting point for most strategy engagements: it establishes what the world looks like before asking what the organisation should do. A well-structured environmental scan surfaces the most strategically material external factors — those with the highest combination of likelihood and impact — rather than producing an exhaustive list of every possible external consideration.
🗺️
Strategic Implication
The 'so what' of a strategic analysis — the conclusion that follows from a set of analytical findings and has direct relevance to the organisation's strategic decisions. Identifying strategic implications is one of the most important and most frequently underdeveloped skills in consulting: it is the step between 'here is what we found' and 'here is what you should do about it'. A strategic implication is specific, actionable, and causal: 'Because regulatory approval timelines in Germany are 40% longer than in France (finding), entering Germany first would delay revenue generation by 8-12 months relative to a France-first strategy (implication), suggesting France is the lower-risk first-entry market (recommendation).'
📉
Market Attractiveness
An assessment of how profitable and strategically desirable a market is to enter or compete in. Market attractiveness is typically assessed across several dimensions: market size and growth rate, competitive intensity (Porter's Five Forces), regulatory environment, customer accessibility and willingness to pay, and strategic fit with the entering organisation's capabilities. A market can be large and growing but highly unattractive if competitive intensity is extreme and entry barriers are low. Conversely, a small but growing niche market with high barriers to entry, low competitive intensity, and good fit with the firm's capabilities might be highly attractive despite its smaller headline size.
🔭
Scenario Analysis
A structured approach to strategic planning under uncertainty — constructing a small number of distinct, internally consistent pictures of possible future environments and assessing the organisation's strategic position and options under each. Scenarios are not predictions and not sensitivity analyses: they are plausible futures that differ on the most uncertain and consequential dimensions. A scenario analysis for a logistics company entering Europe might construct three scenarios: a Base Case (regulatory and economic conditions as currently expected), a Headwinds Scenario (regulatory tightening and economic slowdown), and a Tailwinds Scenario (accelerated digital infrastructure investment and favourable trade policy). The organisation then assesses which strategies perform well across all three scenarios.
⛓️
Value Chain
A framework developed by Michael Porter for identifying where competitive advantage is created in an organisation's operations — by mapping all the primary and support activities that contribute to producing and delivering the end product. Primary activities include: inbound logistics (receiving and storing inputs), operations (transforming inputs into products), outbound logistics (delivering products to customers), marketing and sales, and after-sales service. Support activities include: firm infrastructure, human resource management, technology development, and procurement. Value chain analysis asks: in which activities does the organisation perform better or at lower cost than competitors? The activities where the organisation outperforms are the sources of its competitive advantage.
🎲
Strategic Fit
The alignment between an organisation's strategy and its internal capabilities, resources, and external environment — the degree to which the chosen strategy is well-matched to what the organisation is capable of executing and what the external environment rewards. A strategy that requires capabilities the organisation does not have (poor internal fit) or that targets a market that does not reward the organisation's strengths (poor external fit) is unlikely to succeed. Strategic fit assessment is a standard step in strategy consulting: before recommending that a client enters a new market or pursues a new strategy, the consulting team assesses whether the client's capabilities are adequate and whether the market environment is conducive to the proposed approach.

Text 1: How Strategy Frameworks Are Used in Consulting Practice — Selection, Application, and Limits

Strategy frameworks are tools, not answers. SWOT, PESTLE, Porter's Five Forces, the 3Cs, the Value Chain — these are analytical lenses that organise information in useful ways, not algorithms that produce correct recommendations from inputs. The most common mistake made by new entrants to consulting is treating frameworks as processes: 'step one, do SWOT; step two, do PESTLE; step three, produce recommendation.' This approach produces mechanical analysis that is comprehensive without being useful — covering all the boxes without illuminating the most strategically consequential issues. Expert framework use, by contrast, begins with the question: what is the actual strategic decision this analysis needs to inform? The framework is then selected or adapted to make that specific decision as tractable as possible.

Framework selection is itself a strategic skill. SWOT is most useful when the central question is about the organisation's strategic position — what should we do given what we are and what the market looks like? PESTLE is most useful when the central question concerns the external environment — what macro forces are shaping the landscape we must compete in? Porter's Five Forces is most useful when the central question is about industry attractiveness — is this a good market to be in? The 3Cs are most useful when the central question is about market strategy — given what we are, what customers want, and what competitors offer, what is our best position? Selecting the wrong framework — applying PESTLE to a question that requires a competitor analysis — produces analysis that is rigorous within its frame but irrelevant to the decision at hand.

The most sophisticated use of frameworks in consulting practice is not applying them in sequence but using them as diagnostic tools to identify where the most strategically material analysis lies. A PESTLE scan might reveal that regulatory change is the dominant environmental factor — suggesting that deeper regulatory analysis, rather than more PESTLE boxes, is what the engagement needs. A Porter's analysis might reveal that buyer power is the critical force — suggesting that customer research, rather than competitive benchmarking, is the most important next step. Good framework use is not a destination — it is a starting point for identifying where the real analytical work needs to be done.

Text 2: SWOT and PESTLE in Practice — What Good Analysis Looks Like and Why Most Falls Short

SWOT analysis is one of the most frequently used and most frequently poorly executed tools in strategic analysis. The typical failure mode is what might be called the 'generic SWOT': each box is filled with plausible-sounding but strategically undifferentiated statements — 'experienced team', 'strong brand', 'growing market', 'competitive pressure'. A generic SWOT does not help a management team make a decision because it could apply equally to any company in any sector. The standard of rigour required for a consulting-quality SWOT is that every item in every box should pass the specificity test: it should be verifiable, comparative, and meaningful in the context of the specific strategic decision the SWOT is designed to inform.

The specificity test has three components. First, verifiable: a strength should be demonstrable with evidence, not asserted. 'Experienced team' is an assertion; '78% of the leadership team has more than 10 years of logistics sector experience, compared to a competitor average of 45%' is evidence. Second, comparative: a strength is only a strength relative to something — either to competitors or to what the market requires. 'Large customer base' is not a strength unless the customer base is large relative to competitors or relative to what makes the business model viable. Third, meaningful: the factor should be relevant to the strategic decision being made. An extensive UK distribution network is a significant strength if the strategy is to expand within the UK; it is an irrelevant strength if the strategy is to enter a new market in Germany.

PESTLE analysis has a different typical failure mode: exhaustiveness without prioritisation. A comprehensive PESTLE might identify 30-40 factors across the six categories — each accurate, each relevant, each individually interesting. But 30-40 factors is not a strategic analysis; it is a research compendium. The value of a PESTLE analysis is in the prioritisation that follows: of all the macro-environmental factors identified, which are the three to five most strategically consequential? The factors most worth acting on are those with the highest combination of impact (if this factor plays out, how much does it affect our strategic position?) and certainty (how confident are we that this factor will materialise?). A PESTLE that surfaces everything but highlights nothing produces the illusion of strategic analysis without its substance.

Grammar Point 1: Language for Describing Strategic Factors and Their Interactions
Categorising Factors Precisely — Internal vs External, Positive vs Negative

Strategy framework language requires explicit categorisation of every factor into the correct box. The most common error is confusing internal with external, or confusing what is true now with what might be true in future.

Internal positive (Strength): 'Meridian's proprietary route optimisation algorithm reduces delivery costs by 12% relative to competitors — a verifiable, comparative, and competitively meaningful advantage.'
External positive (Opportunity): 'Increasing demand from German B2B manufacturers for technology-led logistics solutions creates an addressable opportunity of approximately €2.8 billion in Meridian's core product category.'
Internal negative (Weakness): 'Meridian has no European brand recognition — confirmed by a survey of 200 German enterprise buyers, 94% of whom could not identify Meridian as a logistics provider.'
External negative (Threat): 'The proposed EU Freight Transport Sustainability Directive would require fleet electrification by 2028 — a regulatory change external to Meridian that threatens the financial case for a capital-light market entry.'
Language for Comparing Factors Across Frameworks

Strategy analysis frequently requires comparison across frameworks — between what PESTLE reveals (macro) and what Porter reveals (industry), or between what SWOT reveals (current state) and what scenario analysis reveals (future state).

'While the PESTLE analysis identifies regulatory change as the dominant macro factor, the Porter's analysis reveals that buyer power — not regulation — is the most immediate threat to margin in the German market.'
'The SWOT analysis confirms Meridian's internal position is strong; the Porter's analysis reveals that the market it is entering is structurally challenging — suggesting the strategy must leverage the company's strengths to overcome structural headwinds.'
'The PESTLE scan surfaces a significant technological opportunity (AI-driven route optimisation adoption); the 3Cs analysis reveals that Meridian is uniquely positioned to exploit it — with the technology already deployed at scale in the UK.'
'The base case scenario assumes macro conditions as per the PESTLE analysis; the headwinds scenario assumes two of the identified PESTLE threats materialise simultaneously — regulatory tightening and economic slowdown.'
Describing the Significance of a Strategic Factor

Not all SWOT or PESTLE factors are equally important. Language that signals significance — 'the most material', 'the critical', 'the single most consequential' — helps the reader prioritise attention.

'Of the 38 PESTLE factors identified, the regulatory dimension is the most strategically consequential — high impact and high certainty of materialisation.'
'The critical force in the Porter's analysis is buyer power — not because it is the most extreme force, but because it directly constrains Meridian's ability to command a technology premium.'
'The single most important strategic implication of the competitive analysis is the 18-24 month window before a well-resourced incumbent can replicate Meridian's technology advantage.'
'While all five Porter's forces are present in the German market, competitive rivalry and buyer power interact to create a particularly challenging pricing environment for new entrants.'
📋
Grammar Point 2: Causal Language for Strategic Implications
Connecting Analysis to Implication — The Core Linguistic Structures

The most important linguistic step in any consulting output is connecting the analytical finding to its strategic consequence. Without this connection, the analysis is descriptive rather than prescriptive.

'Because [finding], [implication].' — 'Because buyer power is high and product differentiation is low in the retail express segment, margins in that sub-segment are structurally compressed below Meridian's required return threshold.'
'This suggests that [strategic action].' — 'This suggests that Meridian should avoid the retail express segment and concentrate the entry strategy on the B2B corporate freight market.'
'The consequence of this is [implication].' — 'The consequence of the 18-24 month technology advantage window is that the market entry timeline is not flexible — delay beyond 24 months risks entering against a re-equipped incumbent.'
'Given this, we recommend [specific action].' — 'Given the joint venture partner's existing relationships with 5 of Meridian's top 10 target accounts, we recommend leading the entry strategy with co-branded proposals through the partner's commercial team.'
Distinguishing Finding from Implication — The Two-Sentence Structure

The most reliable structure for communicating strategic implications in consulting is the two-sentence pattern: sentence one states the finding; sentence two states the implication. This structure prevents the findings and implications from blurring together.

'The German B2B logistics market is growing at 4.2% annually with below-average competitive concentration [finding]. This creates a window for Meridian to establish a premium technical position before the market consolidates [implication].'
'Meridian's route optimisation platform delivers a 12% cost advantage over incumbent scheduling systems [finding]. This means the go-to-market strategy should lead with a quantifiable ROI proposition rather than a brand proposition [implication].'
'The proposed EU Sustainability Directive will require fleet electrification by 2028 [finding]. Entering the German market through a partner with an existing electric fleet resolves this compliance risk without requiring upfront capital investment [implication].'
'Buyer power is high in the retail express segment [finding]. Meridian cannot sustain a price premium in that sub-segment and should avoid it in the initial market entry phase [implication].'
Conditional and Contingent Implication Language

Many strategic implications are conditional — they hold only if certain assumptions are met. Conditional implication language preserves the analytical honesty of a conditional recommendation.

'If the Sustainability Directive timeline is brought forward by more than 18 months, the financial case for a standalone entry collapses — making the joint venture the only viable approach.'
'The premium pricing strategy is sustainable provided Meridian can maintain the technology advantage for at least 18 months — which requires continued R&D investment at current levels.'
'The recommended entry sequence — Munich first, then Hamburg — holds provided customer research in Hamburg confirms the same willingness-to-pay pattern as Munich. If it does not, a Frankfurt-first sequence may be preferable.'
'Should the competitive response be more aggressive than the base case assumes — price cuts exceeding 20% — the projected payback period extends from 24 to 36 months, which remains within the board's acceptable threshold.'
📋
Grammar Point 3: Precision and Hedging in Framework-Based Analysis
Expressing the Right Level of Confidence — Certain, Probable, Possible

Strategy consulting operates under uncertainty. Language must accurately reflect the confidence level of each claim — neither overclaiming certainty (which damages credibility when wrong) nor hedging so heavily that the recommendation loses conviction.

High confidence: 'The competitive analysis confirms that Meridian's technology advantage is unique among the four operators assessed — no incumbent offers real-time route optimisation at a comparable performance level.'
Moderate confidence: 'The customer research suggests a willingness to pay a 15% technology premium — though stated preferences may overstate actual purchase behaviour by 20-30%.'
Lower confidence: 'The regulatory timeline is uncertain — we have modelled a 2028 implementation date consistent with current EU legislative track, but an earlier implementation is plausible given recent political developments.'
Distinguishing: 'We are confident in the direction of the recommendation — Germany via joint venture — and more cautious about the specific financial projections, which carry assumptions with 20-30% uncertainty.'
Hedging Without Losing the Recommendation

Hedging language should acknowledge uncertainty while preserving the clarity and actionability of the recommendation. Over-hedging (every claim qualified to the point of meaninglessness) is as damaging as under-hedging.

'The recommendation to enter Germany via joint venture holds under all three modelled scenarios — including the headwinds case — which gives us confidence that the recommendation is robust to the identified uncertainties.'
'While we cannot predict competitive response with precision, the joint venture recommendation is more resilient to aggressive response than standalone entry — because the partner's established relationships reduce dependence on Meridian's brand.'
'The financial projections carry meaningful uncertainty, particularly in year two and three; however, the directional conclusion — that entry is financially viable at the conservative end of the range — holds under all reasonable assumptions.'
'We would revisit the Germany-first recommendation if the regulatory timeline changes materially — this is the single assumption that, if wrong, would most significantly change the strategic priority order.'
Precision Language for SWOT and PESTLE Items

SWOT and PESTLE items must pass the specificity test: verifiable, comparative, and meaningful. The language used to state each item signals whether it is genuine analysis or box-filling.

Strong SWOT strength: 'Proprietary real-time route optimisation — verified by external benchmarking — reduces per-delivery cost by 12% relative to the four incumbent German operators' scheduling systems.'
Weak SWOT strength (avoid): 'Strong technology platform that is well-regarded by customers.'
Strong PESTLE factor: 'EU Freight Transport Sustainability Directive: High certainty of implementation by 2028; high impact on fleet capital requirements; requires a fleet electrification investment of approximately €8M for Meridian's projected German operational scale.'
Weak PESTLE factor (avoid): 'Regulatory environment in Germany is important and may affect Meridian's operations.'

Case Study 1: Michael Porter and the Development of Competitive Strategy — How Five Forces Changed Strategic Analysis
How a Harvard Business School professor's 1979 article transformed how companies and consultants think about industry structure and competitive advantage

Background

Michael Porter's 'How Competitive Forces Shape Strategy', published in the Harvard Business Review in 1979, is one of the most influential business articles ever written. Porter, then an assistant professor at Harvard Business School, argued that the profitability of a company was determined not primarily by how well-managed it was but by the structural characteristics of the industry it competed in. This was a radical claim: it shifted the focus of strategic analysis from company-level competence to industry-level structure, and it gave strategists and consultants a specific analytical framework — the Five Forces — for assessing that structure. The framework was subsequently developed in full in Porter's 1980 book Competitive Strategy, which became one of the best-selling business books in history.

The Key Insight: Industry Structure Determines Profitability

Porter's central insight was that five structural forces — competitive rivalry, threat of new entrants, threat of substitution, buyer power, and supplier power — collectively determine how much profit an industry generates for its participants, regardless of how efficiently any individual company operates. An airline industry characterised by intense rivalry, low barriers to entry (new airlines can and do launch), high threat of substitution (passengers can drive, take trains, or use video conferencing), powerful buyers (large corporate travel buyers), and significant supplier power (Boeing and Airbus, fuel suppliers, airport authorities) will structurally generate lower average profitability than a pharmaceutical company with patent-protected drugs, high barriers to entry (clinical trial requirements), low substitution threats, and fragmented, price-insensitive buyers. The framework made it possible to explain and predict industry profitability differences systematically rather than attributing them to managerial skill alone.

How Porter's Framework Is Used in Consulting Practice

Porter's Five Forces framework became one of the most widely used analytical tools in strategy consulting. In an M&A engagement, the framework helps assess whether a target industry is structurally attractive before committing to an acquisition. In a market entry engagement, it identifies which forces are most likely to constrain the entrant's profitability. In a competitive strategy engagement, it identifies which forces can be addressed through strategic action — for example, reducing buyer power by differentiating the product, or reducing the threat of new entrants by building switching costs. The framework's enduring value is not that it provides answers but that it ensures the strategic conversation covers the most important structural dimensions of an industry before arriving at conclusions.

Case Study 2: The SWOT Analysis — Origins, Evolution, and Why It Remains the Most Useful Framework in the Strategy Toolbox
The history of SWOT, its most common failure modes, and the discipline required to make it genuinely useful rather than a bureaucratic exercise

Background

The SWOT analysis — Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats — is simultaneously the most widely used strategy framework in the world and the most frequently poorly applied. Its origins are attributed to Albert Humphrey, a management consultant at the Stanford Research Institute who led a research project in the 1960s studying why corporate planning failed. Humphrey's original framework was called SOFT — Satisfactory, Opportunity, Fault, Threat — and evolved into SWOT through subsequent development. By the 1980s, SWOT had become the standard opening tool in strategic planning processes at large corporations, consulting firms, and business schools worldwide. Its appeal is its accessibility: unlike Porter's Five Forces or a discounted cash flow analysis, a SWOT can be completed by any manager with knowledge of their business and its environment.

The Common Failure Mode: The Generic SWOT

The most common failure of SWOT analysis in practice is what might be called the 'strategy theatre' problem. SWOT is run as a workshop exercise, each box is filled with plausible-sounding items, and the output is filed in a strategy document that nobody uses. The items are generic — 'strong leadership team', 'competitive market', 'new technology trends' — and the analysis generates no insights that could not have been generated without a framework at all. The failure mode has a structural cause: SWOT is an open-ended tool that reveals exactly as much as the rigour of the analyst brings to it. Applied carelessly, it produces generic output. Applied rigorously — with the specificity test applied to every item, with genuine comparative analysis backing every strength, with strategic implications derived from every finding — it is one of the most powerful strategic planning tools available.

What Makes SWOT Genuinely Useful

SWOT analysis produces genuine strategic value when it is used to answer a specific strategic question (not 'assess our position' but 'should we enter Germany and if so how?'), when every item passes the specificity test (verifiable, comparative, meaningful), and when the SO, ST, WO, and WT combinations are used to derive strategic options. The SO analysis (how can we use our strengths to exploit our opportunities?) directly generates strategic recommendations. The WT analysis (what are the threats that exploit our weaknesses?) generates risk mitigation priorities. The best SWOT analyses end not with four lists but with a set of strategic recommendations that are demonstrably derived from the interaction of the four quadrants. In the words of one senior McKinsey partner: 'A SWOT that doesn't generate at least three specific strategic recommendations should be done again.'
