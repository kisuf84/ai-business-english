Module Overview

Welcome to Module 12: Ethics and Corporate Governance. In this module you will learn essential vocabulary and language for discussing ethical behaviour in business, the structures that govern corporations, and the responsibilities companies have to their stakeholders. By the end of this module, you will be able to discuss corporate scandals, argue for ethical business practices, and explain governance structures using accurate grammar and professional vocabulary.

This module is suitable for professionals at B1–B2 level working in management, compliance, HR, finance, or any role requiring communication about corporate responsibility, ESG standards, whistleblowing, and regulatory frameworks.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Define key concepts such as corporate governance, ethical conduct, and stakeholder theory using precise business vocabulary.
2
Discuss the roles and responsibilities of boards of directors, shareholders, and senior management in maintaining ethical standards.
3
Use participle clauses correctly to describe corporate events, policies, and governance procedures in formal English.
4
Apply cause and result linkers (therefore, consequently, as a result of, owing to) in professional writing and speaking contexts.
5
Identify and use phrasal verbs commonly found in discussions of corporate governance, compliance, and business ethics.
6
Analyse real-world corporate governance cases and evaluate the ethical decisions made by companies and their leadership.
7
Write a formal report and a persuasive essay on topics related to corporate ethics, CSR, and whistleblowing policies.
8
Listen to professional discussions and presentations on ethics and extract specific information, arguments, and recommendations.

Text 1: The Importance of Corporate Governance

An overview of governance principles and their business impact

Corporate governance refers to the structures and processes through which companies are directed, controlled, and held accountable. At its core, it defines the relationships between a company's management, its board of directors, shareholders, and other stakeholders. Good governance ensures that companies operate transparently, ethically, and in accordance with the law, thereby protecting the interests of everyone involved. In recent decades, high-profile corporate scandals — from Enron's accounting fraud to the Volkswagen emissions scandal — have underscored the consequences of weak governance frameworks.

One of the foundational principles of effective corporate governance is accountability. Executives and board members must be answerable for their decisions, and companies must establish clear mechanisms for oversight and reporting. This is often achieved through audit committees, independent non-executive directors, and regular financial disclosures. Transparency is equally important: when companies provide accurate, complete, and timely information, they build trust with investors, regulators, and the general public. Without these safeguards, organisations become vulnerable to fraud, mismanagement, and reputational damage.

The concept of stakeholder theory has significantly influenced modern governance practices. Rather than focusing exclusively on shareholder returns, companies are now expected to consider the impact of their operations on employees, customers, communities, and the environment. This broader view of corporate responsibility is reflected in the rise of ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) investing, where capital is increasingly directed towards companies that demonstrate high ethical and sustainability standards. Businesses that embed governance principles into their strategy often outperform their peers over the long term, as they are better positioned to manage risk, attract talent, and maintain regulatory compliance.

Text 2: Whistleblowing and Ethical Culture in Organisations

Exploring the role of individuals in promoting corporate integrity

Whistleblowing — the act of an employee disclosing information about illegal or unethical practices within their organisation — plays a critical role in corporate accountability. Notable cases, such as that of Sherron Watkins at Enron or Bradley Birkenfeld at UBS, demonstrate how individuals can expose systemic misconduct that internal management has either failed to address or actively concealed. However, whistleblowers often face significant personal and professional risks, including retaliation, dismissal, and reputational damage. This has prompted many governments to introduce legal protections for whistleblowers, recognising their vital contribution to transparency and the rule of law.

Building a genuine culture of ethics requires more than a code of conduct on paper. It demands consistent leadership by example, open channels for raising concerns, and real consequences for misconduct at all levels of the organisation. Research consistently shows that employees are more likely to report wrongdoing when they trust that their concerns will be taken seriously and that they will be protected from retaliation. Conversely, in organisations with a culture of fear or excessive hierarchy, unethical behaviour tends to go unreported, allowing small problems to escalate into major crises.

The relationship between ethics and business performance is increasingly well-documented. Companies with strong ethical cultures tend to attract better talent, retain employees longer, and enjoy stronger brand loyalty from customers. Moreover, they face fewer regulatory penalties and are less likely to suffer the catastrophic reputational damage that accompanies public scandals. Investors, too, are paying closer attention to governance and ethics as predictors of long-term value. In this sense, ethics is not just a moral obligation — it is a strategic business advantage that progressive organisations are learning to leverage effectively.

🔍 GRAMMAR REVIEW

Grammar Reference

Detailed grammar reference boxes for all three grammar points in this module.

Grammar Reference 1: Participle Clauses

What are participle clauses?

Participle clauses are subordinate clauses that use a participle (present or past) instead of a full finite verb. They are common in formal and academic English, especially in business writing and reports.

Present Participle Clauses (-ing)

Use the present participle to show a simultaneous action or one that immediately precedes the main verb. They can replace subject + verb constructions.

  • Recognising the risks, the board postponed the merger.
  • Having reviewed the accounts, the auditors submitted their report.

Past Participle Clauses (-ed/-en)

Past participle clauses are typically passive in meaning and often describe the subject of the main clause.

  • Accused of misconduct, the executive was asked to resign.
  • The policy, introduced in 2020, has improved compliance rates.

Perfect Participle Clauses (Having + past participle)

Use the perfect participle to indicate an action completed before the main clause action.

  • Having implemented the new framework, the company saw improved results.
  • Having hesitated for months, the whistleblower finally came forward.
Grammar Reference 2: Cause & Result Linkers

Expressing Cause

These linkers introduce the reason for something. They may begin a clause or modify a noun phrase.

  • Due to poor risk management, the company lost millions.
  • Owing to the new regulations, all firms must disclose their ESG impact.
  • As a result of the scandal, the CEO resigned.

Expressing Result

These linkers introduce the consequence or outcome of an event or action.

  • The board failed to act; consequently, the company faced heavy fines.
  • Profits fell sharply; as a result, several executives were dismissed.
  • The governance framework was weak; therefore, fraud went undetected.

Concession vs. Contrast

Be careful not to confuse cause/result linkers with contrast words. Despite and although show contrast, not cause.

  • Despite the new regulations, some companies continued to behave unethically. (contrast)
  • Due to the new regulations, companies had to change their behaviour. (cause)

Formal Alternatives

In formal writing, vary your use of linkers. Avoid repeating 'so' or 'because' — use more sophisticated alternatives:

  • Thereby (= as a result of this): She implemented new controls, thereby reducing the risk of fraud.
  • Following (= as a result of): Following the scandal, several directors resigned.
Grammar Reference 3: Phrasal Verbs in Business & Governance Contexts

What are phrasal verbs?

Phrasal verbs combine a verb with a preposition or adverb to create a new meaning. They are common in business English, particularly in informal reports and discussions.

Phrasal Verbs for Governance & Compliance

These are commonly used when discussing investigations, policies, and corporate decisions:

  • draw up (a policy/report) = to create or write officially
  • call off (a deal/investigation) = to cancel
  • carry out (an audit/review) = to conduct or perform
  • look into (a matter) = to investigate

Phrasal Verbs for Corporate Actions

Use these when discussing how companies and executives respond to challenges:

  • take on (responsibility) = to accept
  • clamp down on (misconduct) = to take strict action against
  • come into effect (regulations) = to become active/operative
  • bring in (experts/changes) = to introduce

Separable vs. Inseparable Phrasal Verbs

Some phrasal verbs can be separated by an object; others cannot.

  • SEPARABLE: draw up a plan / draw a plan up
  • INSEPARABLE: look into the matter (NOT: look the matter into)
  • TIP: When using a pronoun, always put it between the verb and particle: call it off, not call off it.

Good afternoon, everyone. Today we're going to contrast two foundational theories in business ethics: shareholder theory and stakeholder theory. Shareholder theory, most famously associated with Milton Friedman, argues that the sole responsibility of a business is to increase its profits — within the constraints of the law. Friedman believed that when executives spend corporate money on social or environmental causes, they are essentially spending someone else's money without consent, which is a form of theft from shareholders. Now, this view was dominant for much of the twentieth century. However, stakeholder theory, developed by Edward Freeman in the 1980s, offers a fundamentally different perspective. Freeman argued that a business creates value not just for shareholders, but for all parties that affect or are affected by the organisation — employees, customers, suppliers, communities, and even the environment. The debate between these two theories has intensified in recent years. In 2019, the Business Roundtable — a group of nearly 200 CEOs of major US corporations — issued a statement explicitly abandoning the primacy of shareholder value and committing to the interests of all stakeholders. This was a landmark shift. But critics argue it was largely symbolic. The question for today is: can a corporation genuinely serve all stakeholders simultaneously, or does doing so inevitably require trade-offs that ultimately harm profitability?

Case Study 1
The Wirecard Scandal: A Governance Failure
Germany · FinTech · 2020  ·  One of the largest corporate fraud cases in European history
Background

Wirecard AG was a German payment processing and financial services company that was once celebrated as a European technology success story. Founded in 1999, the company grew rapidly, processing payments for thousands of merchants globally and securing a coveted spot in the prestigious DAX index — Germany's top 30 listed companies. At its peak, Wirecard was valued at over €24 billion, and its CEO Jan Marsalek was celebrated as a visionary tech entrepreneur.

The Fraud

In June 2020, Wirecard admitted that €1.9 billion — approximately a quarter of its stated assets — simply did not exist. The funds were allegedly held in trust accounts in the Philippines, but Philippine banks confirmed no such accounts were held. Auditors from Ernst & Young, who had signed off on Wirecard's accounts for over a decade, came under intense scrutiny for failing to identify what turned out to be a systematic, multi-year fraud. Shortly after the admission, Wirecard filed for insolvency, and Jan Marsalek fled Germany — his whereabouts remained unknown for years.

Governance Failures

The Wirecard case exposed multiple governance failures simultaneously. Analysts and journalists had raised questions about the company's accounts as early as 2015, but regulators — including Germany's BaFin — initially failed to act and even investigated the short-sellers raising concerns rather than the company itself. The supervisory board did not have sufficient independence or technical expertise. Internal controls were inadequate, and the auditors allowed relationships with management to compromise their independence. Germany subsequently introduced significant reforms to its audit and governance oversight frameworks.

Case Study 2
Patagonia's Ethical Business Model: A Governance Success
USA · Outdoor Apparel · Ongoing  ·  How one company embedded ethics and sustainability into its core governance
Background

Patagonia is an American outdoor clothing company founded by Yvon Chouinard in 1973. From its early days, Patagonia positioned itself as a company with a strong environmental conscience, donating 1% of its annual sales to environmental organisations through its self-imposed 'Earth Tax.' The company has consistently rejected the idea that profit and ethics are incompatible, instead arguing that strong environmental and social values are fundamental to long-term business success. Its mission statement — 'We're in business to save our home planet' — is reflected throughout its operations and governance.

Governance and Stakeholder Commitment

In 2022, Patagonia's founder Yvon Chouinard made an extraordinary governance decision: he transferred ownership of the company to a specially created trust and a non-profit organisation, rather than selling it or taking it public. This ensured that all future profits — estimated at approximately $100 million per year — would be used to fight climate change and protect undeveloped land. The decision was widely praised as a landmark moment in corporate governance, demonstrating that it is possible to structure a company in a way that explicitly prioritises the planet over personal gain.

Lessons for Business

Patagonia's approach offers several governance lessons. First, that mission-driven governance creates long-term resilience — Patagonia's loyal customer base has been built on trust and shared values, not just product quality. Second, that supply chain transparency matters: Patagonia publishes its full supply chain online, including factory names and locations, which holds the company accountable for its ethical commitments. Third, that governance structures can and should reflect company values — the transfer of ownership was a structural decision with ethical implications. Critics argue the model is not easily replicable for all businesses, but its influence on the broader conversation about corporate purpose is undeniable.
