Module Overview

Welcome to Module 11: Business Presentations. In this module you will develop the language and skills needed to design, structure, and deliver professional presentations in English. You will learn how to open with impact, organise your content logically, use signposting language to guide your audience, handle questions confidently, and close with a memorable conclusion.

This module is ideal for professionals at B1–B2 level who need to present to clients, colleagues, senior management, or at conferences. You will practise both formal and semi-formal presentation styles, focusing on clarity, persuasion, and audience engagement.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1
Plan and structure a business presentation using an effective introduction, body, and conclusion framework.
2
Use a wide range of signposting language to guide your audience through the stages of a presentation clearly and confidently.
3
Apply modal verbs correctly to make recommendations, express possibility, and convey certainty or uncertainty during presentations.
4
Use passive voice constructions to describe data, findings, and processes in a formal, objective presentation style.
5
Identify and use phrasal verbs and collocations commonly found in professional presentations and business communication.
6
Respond to audience questions professionally using hedging language, clarification strategies, and polite deflection phrases.
7
Write a presentation script and a post-presentation follow-up email using appropriate register and professional vocabulary.
8
Analyse effective and ineffective presentations to identify key features of delivery, content, and audience engagement.

Text 1: The Art of Structuring a Business Presentation

How to organise content for maximum clarity and impact

A well-structured business presentation is the foundation of effective communication in professional environments. Regardless of the topic — whether launching a new product, presenting quarterly results, or proposing a strategic change — the basic framework remains consistent: a strong introduction, a clearly organised body, and a memorable conclusion. The introduction serves three essential functions: it captures the audience's attention, establishes the presenter's credibility, and sets out a clear agenda for what will follow. Experienced presenters often open with a surprising statistic, a rhetorical question, or a brief anecdote to create an immediate connection with their audience.

The body of the presentation is where the substantive content is delivered. To maintain clarity and help the audience follow the logic of the argument, skilled presenters use signposting language throughout — phrases such as 'Moving on to our second point,' 'As I mentioned earlier,' and 'This brings me to the key question.' These linguistic markers act as signposts on a road, preventing the audience from losing their way. It is generally recommended that a business presentation contain no more than three to five main points, as audiences struggle to retain more information than this during a single session. Each point should be supported by evidence: data, case studies, testimonials, or demonstrations.

The conclusion is arguably the most important part of the presentation, as it is what the audience will remember most clearly. A strong conclusion restates the key message, summarises the main points briefly, and ends with a clear call to action — a specific request or next step for the audience. Presenters should avoid introducing new information in the conclusion, as this can confuse the audience and dilute the impact of the closing. Closing with a memorable phrase, a return to the opening hook, or a moment of audience interaction can significantly increase the lasting impression of a presentation.

Text 2: Delivering with Confidence — Managing Nerves and Engaging Your Audience

Practical techniques for professional presentation delivery

Presentation anxiety — the fear of speaking in front of an audience — affects a significant proportion of professionals, regardless of their level of experience or seniority. Research consistently shows that public speaking is among the most commonly cited fears in workplace surveys. However, anxiety is not necessarily a barrier to effective delivery; in fact, a moderate level of nervous energy can sharpen focus and improve performance. The key lies in channelling anxiety productively through thorough preparation, rehearsal, and the development of coping strategies such as controlled breathing, positive visualisation, and physical warm-up exercises before presenting.

Audience engagement is one of the most critical skills a presenter can develop. A passive audience that is merely receiving information will retain far less than one that is actively involved. Effective engagement techniques include asking rhetorical questions ('Have you ever wondered why…?'), pausing strategically to let key points sink in, varying pace and tone to maintain interest, and incorporating interactive elements such as polls, short activities, or moments for audience reflection. Research from cognitive science suggests that attention naturally dips after approximately ten minutes of continuous listening; skilled presenters account for this by introducing a change of format or activity at regular intervals.

Handling questions is an area where many otherwise competent presenters struggle. The Q&A session can feel unpredictable and expose gaps in knowledge. Professional presenters use a set of reliable strategies to manage this: restating the question to ensure they have understood it correctly and to give themselves thinking time, using hedging language ('That's a very interesting point — I'd suggest that…') when uncertain, and confidently deferring questions they cannot answer immediately ('That falls slightly outside today's scope, but I'd be happy to follow up with you directly'). The ability to manage a Q&A session with grace and confidence is widely regarded as the mark of a truly skilled communicator.

🔍 GRAMMAR REVIEW

Grammar Reference

Detailed reference boxes covering all three grammar points in this module.

Grammar Reference 1: Modal Verbs in Business Presentations

What are modal verbs?

Modal verbs (can, could, will, would, may, might, should, must, shall) are auxiliary verbs used to express ability, possibility, certainty, advice, or obligation. In presentations, they help you sound measured and professional.

Expressing Probability and Possibility

Use modal verbs to present predictions and findings with the appropriate level of certainty:

  • will → certain: 'This will reduce costs by 20%.'
  • should → fairly certain: 'Results should improve in Q3.'
  • may / might → possible but uncertain: 'This may affect our projections.'
  • could → one of several possibilities: 'This could be the key factor.'

Making Recommendations

Use should, could, and would to propose next steps professionally:

  • We should consider expanding into new markets.
  • I would recommend a phased approach to implementation.
  • The board could review this proposal at the next meeting.

Polite Presentation Language

Modal verbs are essential for polite, formal presentation language:

  • I would like to draw your attention to… (polite focus)
  • You may notice that… (inviting attention)
  • I can't answer that in full right now, but… (polite deferral)
Grammar Reference 2: Passive Voice in Formal Presentations

Why use the passive voice?

The passive voice is preferred in formal presentations when the action is more important than the person who performed it, or when you want to sound objective and professional.

Forming the Passive

Form: subject + be (conjugated) + past participle

  • Active: We collected the data from 500 companies.
  • Passive: The data was collected from 500 companies.
  • Active: The team has completed the analysis.
  • Passive: The analysis has been completed.

Common Tenses in Presentation Passive

Different tenses are used in different parts of a presentation:

  • Past simple passive: 'The research was conducted last year.'
  • Present simple passive: 'All figures are expressed in euros.'
  • Present perfect passive: 'Three recommendations have been identified.'
  • Future passive: 'Further details will be provided in the report.'

Impersonal Passive Structures

These structures allow you to report findings without attributing them to a specific person — very common in formal presentations:

  • It is estimated that… / It is expected that…
  • It has been reported that… / It has been shown that…
  • It is widely believed that… / It was found that…
Grammar Reference 3: Signposting Language in Presentations

What is signposting?

Signposting language consists of phrases that tell the audience where you are in the presentation and what is coming next. It acts as a roadmap, improving clarity and comprehension significantly.

Opening the Presentation

Use these phrases to start clearly and professionally:

  • Today, I'm going to talk about… / The aim of today's presentation is…
  • I'd like to begin by… / Let me start by giving you an overview of…
  • This presentation is divided into three parts…

Moving Through the Presentation

Signal transitions between sections and ideas:

  • Moving on to… / Turning to our next point…
  • This brings me to… / Let me now focus on…
  • As I mentioned earlier… / Returning to the point I made about…
  • I'd like to emphasise that… / I want to draw your attention to…

Closing and Summarising

Bring the presentation to a clear, professional close:

  • To summarise… / In conclusion… / To wrap up…
  • The key takeaway from today is… / The main point I'd like to leave you with is…
  • I'd like to close by… / Thank you for your attention — I'm now happy to take any questions.

Case Study 1
Steve Jobs and the Art of the Product Launch Presentation
USA · Apple Inc. · Technology · Iconic Presentations  ·  What made Jobs' presentations legendary — and what business presenters can learn from them
Background

Steve Jobs, co-founder and longtime CEO of Apple Inc., is widely regarded as one of the greatest business presenters of his generation. His product launch events — particularly the introduction of the Macintosh in 1984, the iPod in 2001, and the iPhone in 2007 — became cultural events watched by millions worldwide. Jobs' presentations were not simply product demonstrations; they were carefully crafted theatrical performances that blended storytelling, showmanship, and strategic communication to create anticipation, desire, and loyalty among both consumers and investors alike.

Presentation Techniques

Jobs employed a distinctive set of techniques that communication experts have since studied and codified. He opened every major presentation with a bold, memorable statement — the iPhone launch, for example, began with: 'Every once in a while, a revolutionary product comes along that changes everything.' He structured presentations around a simple narrative arc: identify a problem, present the solution dramatically, and demonstrate it live. His slides were famously minimalist — often just a single word or image — forcing the audience to focus entirely on his spoken words. He rehearsed obsessively, reportedly practising entire keynote presentations for weeks in advance, despite his reputation for effortless spontaneity.

Lessons for Business Presenters

The lessons from Jobs' approach translate directly to everyday business presentations. First, simplicity is not weakness — stripping content to its essentials forces clarity of thinking and makes the message more memorable. Second, narrative structure engages the audience far more effectively than a list of features or facts; framing a presentation as a story with a problem and a solution is a universally effective technique. Third, the power of the 'one more thing' — Jobs' famous technique of appearing to conclude before surprising the audience with an additional reveal — demonstrates that controlling the audience's emotional journey is as important as the content itself. Finally, Jobs treated every presentation, regardless of audience size, as a performance worth practising to perfection.

Case Study 2
Death by PowerPoint: A Case Study in Poor Presentation Design
Global · Corporate Settings · Communication Failures  ·  How poorly designed presentations damage communication, decisions, and organisational culture
The Problem

The phrase 'Death by PowerPoint' has entered corporate vocabulary to describe presentations that are so dense, confusing, and poorly structured that they actively prevent effective communication. The phenomenon is remarkably widespread: surveys suggest that the average professional sits through more than 9,000 hours of presentations in their career, and a significant proportion describe the majority of these as either unhelpful or counterproductive. The core problem is the misuse of presentation software as a document-writing tool rather than a visual communication aid. The result is slides packed with bullet points, multi-paragraph text, and complex tables that are impossible to read and understand simultaneously while listening to a speaker.

Real-World Consequences

The consequences of poor presentations extend beyond boredom. In a widely cited case, the US space agency NASA came under criticism following the Columbia Space Shuttle disaster in 2003, when investigators found that critical safety information had been buried in complex PowerPoint slides during engineering briefings. The information existed, but the format in which it was presented made it effectively invisible to decision-makers. In corporate settings, poor presentations have been linked to missed investment opportunities, failed change management programmes, and senior executives making decisions based on misunderstood data. The cost of unclear communication is estimated by some researchers to run into hundreds of billions of dollars annually across global businesses.

Solutions and Best Practices

The antidote to Death by PowerPoint begins with a fundamental shift in thinking: the presenter is the presentation, and the slides are merely a supporting tool. Practitioners recommend what is sometimes called the 'SOS' approach: Simplify the slides, Organise the narrative, and Speak to — not from — the content. Organisations such as McKinsey and Bain are known for their discipline in creating clear, single-message slides with one visual per page and a clear 'so what?' headline that tells the audience the key takeaway before they have even processed the detail. Training professionals in effective visual communication and storytelling is increasingly seen not as a soft skill, but as a core business competency with measurable impact on organisational performance.
