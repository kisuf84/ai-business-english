Module Overview
B1–B2 · Decision Making and Problem Solving

Welcome to Module 6: Decision Making and Problem Solving. This module develops the professional English vocabulary and language skills required to analyse complex business problems, evaluate competing options, and communicate decisions with clarity, precision, and confidence. You will study the frameworks that underpin structured decision-making in professional contexts, practise the grammar of analysis and argumentation, and build the listening and writing skills needed to contribute effectively to decision-making processes at every organisational level.

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Use precise vocabulary to describe problem-solving frameworks, decision criteria, and analytical processes.
  • Discuss and evaluate options using structured, professional English language.
  • Apply grammar structures — including conditional sentences, modal verbs of deduction, and cause-and-effect language — in business decision-making contexts.
  • Write a professional problem analysis memo and a structured decision brief.
  • Listen critically to business discussions involving complex decisions and identify key arguments and conclusions.

Text 1 — The Science and Practice of Business Decision Making

Decision making is the central activity of management. Every day, managers at every level face choices that range from the routine and reversible — which vendor to approve, how to schedule a team meeting — to the strategic and consequential: whether to enter a new market, acquire a competitor, or restructure an entire division. What distinguishes effective decision-makers from ineffective ones is not, as popular mythology would suggest, raw intelligence or superior information. Research in cognitive psychology and behavioural economics has demonstrated repeatedly that even highly intelligent, well-informed individuals make systematically poor decisions when operating under conditions of uncertainty, time pressure, or emotional intensity. The most reliable predictor of decision quality is not cognitive horsepower, but the quality of the process through which the decision is made — specifically, whether that process includes structured problem definition, deliberate evidence evaluation, and disciplined consideration of alternatives before any commitment is made.

The primary obstacle to effective decision-making in organisations is cognitive bias — the deeply embedded mental shortcuts that the human brain uses to reduce the effort required for judgment. Among the most dangerous are confirmation bias (the tendency to seek out and overvalue evidence that supports a conclusion already intuitively reached), anchoring (the disproportionate influence of the first number or data point encountered on all subsequent estimates), and groupthink (the suppression of individual dissent in favour of group consensus, which in practice often produces the worst of both worlds — a decision that carries the authority of consensus while reflecting the analysis of none). These biases are not primarily the result of poor character or laziness; they are intrinsic features of the cognitive architecture that in other contexts is a powerful adaptive advantage. The practical implication is that awareness of bias, while necessary, is insufficient — structural safeguards such as devil's advocacy, pre-mortem analysis, and diverse decision-making teams are required to counter their effects reliably.

Effective organisations have responded to the challenge of cognitive bias by building decision frameworks — structured processes that guide decision-makers through a consistent set of steps regardless of the nature or urgency of the problem at hand. The most widely adopted frameworks share several features: a clear definition of the problem separate from its proposed solution; an explicit articulation of the decision criteria against which options will be evaluated; a disciplined generation of alternatives rather than defaulting to the first viable option identified; a structured risk assessment for each alternative; and a transparent record of the reasoning behind the final choice, which enables post-decision review and organisational learning. When these steps are followed consistently, organisations not only make better individual decisions but develop a cumulative institutional capability for high-quality judgment that becomes one of their most durable competitive advantages.

Text 2 — Problem Solving in Practice: From Diagnosis to Implementation

In a business context, a problem is not simply an undesirable situation — it is a gap between the current state and a desired state, where the path from one to the other is not immediately obvious. This distinction matters because it reframes the task: the manager's job is not merely to make the bad thing go away, but to understand precisely why the gap exists and to determine which actions will most reliably close it. The most common failure in organisational problem-solving is not a failure of action, but a failure of diagnosis — the tendency to implement solutions before the root cause of the problem has been identified. When this happens, organisations find themselves repeatedly addressing the same issue in different guises: a recurring customer complaint that is resolved operationally but never at source; a team performance problem that is treated as an individual performance issue when its actual driver is structural. The 5 Whys technique, developed within the Toyota Production System, addresses this failure directly: by repeatedly asking "why" in response to each identified cause, the analyst is forced past surface symptoms to the systemic origin of the problem.

Once the root cause has been identified, the problem-solving process moves to solution generation — a phase that is critically undermined when organisations move too quickly to the first viable option. Research in design thinking and decision science consistently shows that the quality of a final solution is strongly correlated with the breadth of the option set considered before any solution is selected. Brainstorming and its more structured relatives — lateral thinking exercises, analogical reasoning, and "how might we?" framing — are powerful tools precisely because they force the generation of options that would never emerge from linear problem analysis alone. The critical discipline is to separate solution generation from solution evaluation: options should be developed without immediate critique, and only once a sufficiently broad set has been created should evaluation criteria be applied. This separation prevents premature convergence on a solution that is merely familiar or politically comfortable, rather than genuinely optimal.

The final and most frequently neglected phase of organisational problem-solving is implementation and review. A solution that is not implemented consistently and completely does not solve the problem — it creates the illusion of having solved it while the underlying issue persists. Effective implementation requires clear ownership (a single named individual accountable for each action), a realistic timeline with defined milestones, and a monitoring mechanism that provides early warning if the solution is not producing the intended results. The post-decision review — a structured assessment conducted after implementation — is the tool that transforms individual problem-solving into organisational learning: it asks not only whether the problem was solved, but whether the diagnosis was accurate, whether the solution selected was the best available, and what the process itself revealed about the organisation's broader decision-making capabilities. Organisations that conduct post-decision reviews systematically are, in effect, building a proprietary database of managerial wisdom that compounds in value over time.

3 Detailed Reference Boxes · All Grammar Points from Module 6

1. Conditional Sentences in Decision-Making English

Conditionals are essential in decision-making language — for evaluating options, discussing consequences, and reflecting on past choices. Mastering all four types is critical for professional discussions about strategy, risk, and outcomes.

  • Zero conditional — General truths and reliable patterns: If + present simple, present simple — "If managers combine data with intuition, decisions tend to be more robust."
  • First conditional — Real, likely future scenarios: If + present simple, will + base verb — "If the board takes scenario planning seriously, it will be better prepared."
  • Second conditional — Hypothetical present/future (unreal): If + past simple, would + base verb — "If the organisation built a post-decision review culture, it would accumulate more institutional wisdom."
  • Third conditional — Unreal past situations and their past consequences: If + past perfect, would have + pp — "If the team had completed the stakeholder analysis earlier, they would have anticipated the objection."
  • Mixed conditional — Unreal past condition with present consequence: If + past perfect, would + base verb — "If the leadership team had scrutinised the proposal more carefully, they would not now be dealing with the reputational consequences."
  • Unless = "if…not": "Unless decision-makers establish clear criteria, they risk anchoring bias."
✔ Zero: "If decision frameworks are used consistently, outcomes improve." (general truth)
✔ 1st: "If the company accelerates due diligence now, it will be ready by month end." (real future)
✔ 2nd: "If they built a review culture, they would accumulate more wisdom." (hypothetical present)
✔ 3rd: "If they had conducted RCA, the problem would not have recurred." (unreal past)
✔ Mixed: "If they had scrutinised it, they would not now be in this position." (past cause → present result)

2. Modal Verbs of Deduction and Probability

In professional decision-making contexts, modal verbs are essential for expressing the degree of certainty behind an analysis, conclusion, or deduction — enabling precise, calibrated communication about what is known, inferred, or possible.

  • must / must have — Strong logical deduction (near certainty): "There must be an error." / "They must have known about the risk."
  • can't / couldn't have — Near impossibility / strong disbelief: "That can't be the root cause." / "The decision couldn't have been made without approval."
  • might / could / may — Genuine possibility (uncertainty): "This might resolve the issue." / "The new strategy could generate revenue."
  • should have — Past criticism or regret: "The team should have acted more quickly." / "Management should have consulted the risk committee."
  • could have — Past unrealised possibility: "They could have reached a better decision with diverse input."
  • might have — Past speculation (uncertain): "There might have been other contributing factors."
✔ "There must be a fundamental problem with the onboarding experience — the data is conclusive." (strong deduction)
✔ "The supplier must have been aware — three QA reports were signed off." (past deduction)
✔ "The decision couldn't have been made without consulting governance." (past impossibility)
✔ "The team should have acted more quickly — the delay cost three weeks." (past criticism)
✔ "They could have reached a better outcome with a dissenting voice in the room." (past unrealised possibility)

3. Cause-and-Effect Language in Problem Analysis

Cause-and-effect language is fundamental in professional problem analysis and decision communication. It enables precise description of the relationships between problems, their origins, and their consequences.

  • Expressing cause (noun phrase): "Due to…" / "As a result of…" / "Because of…" / "Owing to…" / "In light of…"
  • Expressing cause (clause): "Because…" / "Since…" / "Given that…" / "As…"
  • Tracing the source of a problem: "stems from" / "arises from" / "is triggered by" / "is attributed to" / "can be traced back to"
  • Expressing result/consequence: "As a result, …" / "Consequently, …" / "Therefore, …" / "This led to…" / "resulted in" / "which meant that"
  • Expressing purpose: "in order to" / "so as to" / "so that" / "with the aim of"
  • Concession (contrast despite cause): "Although…" / "Even though…" / "Despite…" / "In spite of…" / "Nevertheless…"
✔ "Due to the absence of a decision framework, outcomes were inconsistent." (cause: noun phrase)
✔ "The failure to conduct due diligence resulted in significant legal liabilities." (result: verb)
✔ "The problem stems from a faulty supplier component, not a process error." (source tracing)
✔ "The team implemented cost reductions in order to preserve the operating margin." (purpose)
✔ "Although the decision was made under pressure, a risk assessment was still completed." (concession)

✍️ Writing Task 1 — Problem Analysis Memo

Welcome back. Today I want to talk about something that I genuinely believe is the most underappreciated challenge in organisational leadership: the systematic ways in which our own minds work against us when we make decisions. We call these cognitive biases, and the research on them is, frankly, humbling. The most dangerous one — because it is the most invisible — is what we call confirmation bias. This is the tendency to seek out, weight, and remember information that confirms what we already believe, while unconsciously filtering out or discounting information that contradicts it. And the really uncomfortable truth is that intelligence makes you more susceptible to this, not less. Intelligent people are better at constructing sophisticated justifications for conclusions they reached intuitively. They are better at the rationalisation. The second bias I want to discuss is anchoring — the disproportionate influence of the first data point you encounter on all your subsequent estimates. If I tell you a company is worth 10 million and then ask you to value it, your estimate will be higher than if I had said 2 million — even if you know the anchor was arbitrary. In business, this shows up constantly: in salary negotiations, pricing decisions, and acquisition valuations. The single most effective countermeasure for both of these biases is what researchers call a "pre-mortem." Before you commit to a decision, you ask your team to assume the decision has already failed — catastrophically, completely — and to explain why. This forces the generation of negative information that confirmation bias would otherwise suppress, and it breaks the anchor by reframing the starting point. It costs about forty-five minutes and has been shown to reduce decision failure rates by up to thirty percent in controlled studies.

Interviewer: Professor, in your research you argue that decision-making capability is the most critical and most underdeveloped organisational competence. Can you explain what you mean by that? Professor: Yes — thank you. What I find consistently, across industries and geographies, is that organisations invest heavily in the technical skills of their people — financial modelling, strategic analysis, project management — and invest almost nothing in teaching those people how to think clearly under uncertainty. And yet the ability to make sound decisions in ambiguous, high-stakes situations is precisely what separates the leaders who create long-term organisational value from those who don't. The paradox is that most organisations believe they are good at decision-making because they are busy making decisions. But activity is not the same as quality. Interviewer: So what should organisations actually do? Professor: Three things. First, invest in structured decision processes — not as bureaucratic requirements, but as cognitive scaffolding that protects against the worst effects of bias. Second — and this is the one most organisations resist — create genuine psychological safety around dissent. The most dangerous thing in any decision-making group is the absence of a voice willing to say "I think we are wrong." If people fear the consequences of challenging a decision, you will consistently get decisions that reflect the preferences of the most powerful person in the room, not the best analysis of the group. Third — review your decisions systematically. Not to assign blame, but to learn. Most organisations treat post-decision review as an optional extra. It should be mandatory. It is the cheapest and most powerful source of decision-making improvement that exists.

Case Study 1 · Consumer Technology · United States

Novaris Tech: The Cost of Deciding Without a Framework

Novaris Tech is a consumer electronics company based in Austin, Texas, with approximately 1,800 employees and annual revenues of $620 million. In 2021, the company's executive team made a strategic decision to discontinue its best-selling tablet product line and redirect all resources to a new category of smart home devices, based on the CEO's conviction that the smart home market represented a transformative opportunity. The decision was made over four weeks at the executive level, without a structured feasibility report, without a formal competitive analysis, and without any consultation with the sales and customer insights teams who held the richest data on customer preferences and buying behaviour. When two members of the product strategy team raised concerns about the pace of the decision and the absence of supporting data, they were told that the leadership team had made its call and that the focus should now be on execution.

The financial consequences became apparent within twelve months. The smart home product range launched six months behind schedule and significantly over budget, due to supply chain complexity that had not been assessed in advance. Meanwhile, the discontinued tablet line — whose loyal customer base had not been consulted about its discontinuation — generated approximately $47 million in lost recurring revenues in its first year of absence. A competitor that had been trailing Novaris by a significant margin in the tablet category accelerated its own product development and captured the majority of the displaced customers within nine months. The executive team, by contrast, had allocated all available engineering and sales capacity to a category in which Novaris had no brand heritage, no established distribution partnerships, and no tested customer insights.

A retrospective analysis commissioned by the new CFO — appointed following the resignation of her predecessor — identified four critical decision-making failures: the absence of any formal decision framework; the complete exclusion of frontline knowledge from the analysis; the suppression of dissenting voices that constituted a textbook case of groupthink at the leadership level; and the failure to model even basic opportunity-cost implications of exiting the tablet category. The retrospective also noted that the decision had been based primarily on the CEO's intuition about market trends — intuition that, while not unreasonable, had never been tested against any structured scenario or risk assessment. The board subsequently approved a mandatory Decision Framework Protocol for all decisions with a revenue impact exceeding $5 million.

Case Study 2 · Healthcare / Public Sector · Colombia

Clínica del Valle: Transforming Decision-Making with Structured Process

Clínica del Valle is a private hospital group operating four facilities in the Valle del Cauca region of Colombia, with a combined annual patient volume of approximately 280,000. In 2022, the group's executive leadership identified a persistent and costly operational problem: average patient wait times in the emergency departments had increased from 48 minutes to 91 minutes over an 18-month period, despite two previous intervention attempts that had each produced temporary improvements before the problem returned. The CEO, Dr Elena Vargas, recognised that the organisation's previous approach — identifying a visible symptom and implementing a tactical fix without structured diagnosis — had repeatedly produced surface-level solutions that did not address the root cause. She commissioned a formal problem-solving programme, managed jointly by the Operations Director and the Head of Quality Improvement, with clear terms of reference and a defined eight-week timeline.

The root cause analysis, conducted using a combination of process mapping, staff interviews, and patient flow data, identified two primary drivers. First, the triage process contained a significant bottleneck at the initial patient registration stage, where a single administrative system was shared across all intake categories, causing a queue that delayed the clinical triage itself. Second, the discharge process was consistently delayed by a communication breakdown between the ward teams and the pharmacy — medications were often not ready when patients were clinically cleared for discharge, forcing patients to wait for up to 90 minutes before they could leave. Each of these was a process failure, not a staffing or resource failure — a distinction that previous interventions had missed entirely because they had prioritised visible activity over diagnostic rigour.

With the root causes clearly identified, the operations team used a structured scenario planning exercise to evaluate three intervention options, each modelled against four decision criteria: speed of impact, cost, operational disruption during implementation, and durability of the improvement. The highest-scoring option was a dual intervention: separating the registration pathway for urgent and non-urgent patients using a dedicated intake terminal, and introducing a real-time pharmacy notification protocol tied to the clinical discharge system. The intervention was piloted in the largest facility for six weeks before being rolled out across all four sites. A post-decision review conducted twelve months after full implementation recorded an average wait time of 44 minutes — below the original 48-minute baseline — and a 28% reduction in complaint volumes. Dr Vargas attributed the success not to the specific solutions chosen, but to the quality of the diagnostic and decision-making process that had preceded them.
