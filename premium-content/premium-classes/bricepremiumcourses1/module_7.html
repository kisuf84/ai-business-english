Module Overview
B1–B2 · Risk Management and Planning

Welcome to Module 7: Risk Management and Planning. This module builds the professional English vocabulary, reading comprehension, and communication skills required to identify, assess, and manage business risks — and to develop and present contingency plans with clarity and confidence. Risk management is a core competence at every level of business leadership, from operational project planning to board-level strategic governance. You will develop the language to discuss risk frameworks, probability and impact, mitigation strategies, and crisis response in professional English.

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Use precise vocabulary to identify, classify, and describe business risks and their potential impact.
  • Discuss risk probability, likelihood, and severity using appropriate hedging and modal language.
  • Apply grammar structures — including future forms, passive constructions, and reported speech — in risk management contexts.
  • Write a professional risk register entry and a structured contingency plan.
  • Listen critically to professional conversations about risk assessment and crisis management.

Text 1 — The Architecture of Enterprise Risk Management

Risk is an unavoidable feature of all business activity. Every strategic decision, every operational process, and every external relationship carries some degree of uncertainty — the possibility that outcomes will deviate from what was planned. What distinguishes high-performing organisations from those that are repeatedly caught off guard by adverse events is not the absence of risk, but the quality of their approach to identifying, assessing, and managing it. Enterprise Risk Management, or ERM, is the discipline through which organisations take a structured, comprehensive view of all the risks they face — financial, operational, reputational, legal, and strategic — and develop the frameworks, processes, and cultures needed to manage those risks in alignment with their appetite for uncertainty. The foundational insight of ERM is that risks rarely operate in isolation: a supply chain disruption can trigger a financial impact, which triggers a reputational consequence, which affects customer retention. Managing risks as isolated events rather than as an interconnected system is one of the most common and costly failures of organisational risk governance.

The centrepiece of any risk management system is the risk register — a living document that records every identified risk, its probability of occurrence, its potential impact if it materialises, the name of the individual accountable for managing it, and the specific mitigation actions that have been agreed. Effective risk registers distinguish between inherent risk — the exposure that exists before any controls are applied — and residual risk, the level of exposure that remains after mitigation measures are in place. This distinction is important because it forces the organisation to be honest about how much of its risk has actually been reduced by its controls, rather than assuming that the existence of a policy or a process has removed the exposure entirely. Risk registers should also include early warning indicators: measurable signals that suggest a risk may be increasing in likelihood or severity before it fully materialises, giving the organisation time to activate its contingency plan proactively rather than reactively.

At the governance level, risk management is most effective when it is embedded in the organisation's culture, not treated as a compliance exercise confined to a specialist department. Research consistently shows that the organisations with the strongest risk outcomes are those in which risk awareness is a shared responsibility — where frontline employees feel empowered to escalate emerging risks, where middle managers integrate risk thinking into operational planning, and where the board has a clear, current, and honest view of the organisation's risk landscape. The risk heat map — a visual tool that plots risks by probability and impact across a matrix — is one of the most effective instruments for achieving board-level clarity: it enables non-specialist directors to see at a glance which risks require immediate attention, which can be monitored at regular intervals, and which are currently within the organisation's stated risk tolerance. Organisations that treat risk management as a strategic capability, rather than an administrative burden, consistently outperform their peers in periods of disruption.

Text 2 — Contingency Planning: From Risk Identification to Crisis Response

A contingency plan is a pre-designed response to a risk event that has been identified in advance. Unlike reactive crisis management — which improvises a response after an unexpected event has occurred — contingency planning assumes that certain risk events are foreseeable and worth planning for before they happen. The quality of a contingency plan is determined by three factors: the accuracy of the underlying risk analysis (if the wrong risks have been identified, the plan will be misaligned with the actual threats the organisation faces); the specificity of the planned response (a plan that says "contact the relevant stakeholders" without naming them, specifying the communication channel, or setting a timeline is not a plan — it is an aspiration); and the organisation's readiness to execute the plan under pressure, which depends on whether the plan has been rehearsed, communicated, and assigned to named individuals with the authority to act.

Contingency plans are most effective when they are built around risk triggers — clearly defined thresholds or events that indicate a risk is beginning to materialise. A trigger-based plan avoids two common failure modes. The first is under-reaction: without a defined trigger, organisations tend to delay activating their response plan while debating whether the situation is "bad enough" to warrant it — a debate that wastes precisely the time that early intervention would have saved. The second is over-reaction: without a calibrated trigger, organisations sometimes activate their full crisis response to a minor disruption, expending resources and causing unnecessary alarm. A well-designed trigger system ensures that the right level of response is matched to the severity of the situation. For example, a retailer might define three escalation tiers: a yellow alert triggered when supplier delivery delays exceed 48 hours; an amber alert when a key supplier confirms they cannot fulfil an order; and a red alert when the delay is projected to affect the customer fulfilment rate below a defined threshold.

Business continuity planning extends the logic of contingency planning to the organisation's most critical functions — those whose disruption would threaten the survival of the organisation itself. Where a contingency plan addresses a specific risk, a business continuity plan asks: if our core systems, facilities, or capabilities were unavailable for an extended period, how would we continue to deliver our most essential services? Effective business continuity plans identify the minimum viable operations the organisation must maintain, specify the alternative resources or processes that would be activated, define the maximum tolerable downtime for each critical function, and assign clear ownership for each element of the response. The test of a business continuity plan is not its sophistication on paper, but whether the people who would need to execute it could do so accurately under the stress of a real crisis — which is why regular simulation exercises are a non-negotiable element of any serious continuity planning programme.

Grammar Review
3 Detailed Reference Boxes · All Grammar Points from Module 7

1. Future Forms in Risk Planning English

Risk management language uses a range of future forms to describe predictions, plans, scheduled events, and ongoing future activities. Selecting the right form signals the degree of certainty and the nature of the future event.

  • will + base verb — Spontaneous decisions or neutral predictions: "I will contact the risk owner now." / "The situation will escalate if controls are not implemented."
  • be going to + base verb — Predictions based on present evidence / pre-made plans: "Based on these trends, we are going to face a significant shortfall." / "We are going to review the register next week."
  • Present continuous — Fixed future arrangements already scheduled: "We are running the simulation in March." / "The board is reviewing the heat map on Thursday."
  • will be + -ing (future continuous) — Ongoing future activity across a period: "The team will be monitoring exposure levels throughout the quarter."
  • will have + past participle (future perfect) — Action completed before a future point: "By the time the audit starts, we will have completed all mitigation actions."
  • will have been + -ing (future perfect continuous) — Duration up to a future point: "By Q4, we will have been running the framework for two years."
✔ "Based on current trends, we are going to face a funding gap." (evidence-based prediction)
✔ "I will contact the risk owner immediately." (spontaneous decision)
✔ "The team is presenting the risk heat map on Thursday." (fixed arrangement)
✔ "By year end, all risks will have been reassessed." (future perfect passive)
✔ "The team will be monitoring three triggers throughout the quarter." (future continuous)

2. Passive Voice in Risk Management Documents

Passive constructions are the standard grammar of formal risk documents — registers, reports, and policies. They create an impersonal, authoritative tone and shift focus from who is acting to what is being done or what has been decided.

  • Present passive (are/is + pp) — Standing procedures: "All risks are reviewed quarterly." / "Each action is assigned to a named owner."
  • Present perfect passive (has/have been + pp) — Recent changes still relevant now: "The risk has been reclassified as high." / "The framework has been updated."
  • Past passive (was/were + pp) — Completed past actions: "The simulation was conducted last quarter." / "The trigger was breached twice."
  • Future passive (will be + pp) — Planned future actions: "The plan will be updated before year end." / "All controls will be tested."
  • Impersonal "It" passive — Formal observations and findings: "It was found that…" / "It is noted that…" / "It is recommended that…"
✔ "All mitigation actions are assigned to a named risk owner with a defined deadline." (present passive)
✔ "It was found that the trigger had been breached without escalation." (past passive + impersonal)
✔ "The contingency plan will be tested in a simulation exercise next quarter." (future passive)
✔ "The risk has been reclassified following the competitor incident." (present perfect passive)
✔ "It is recommended that the risk appetite statement be reviewed by the board." (formal recommendation)

3. Reported Speech in Risk Briefings and Committee Minutes

Reported speech (also called indirect speech) is essential for meeting minutes, risk briefings, and board papers — converting what was said directly into formal written summary. The main rule is backshift: tenses typically move one step back in time.

  • Present → Past: "The risk is high" → reported that the risk was high
  • Present continuous → Past continuous: "We are monitoring it" → reported that they were monitoring it
  • Present perfect → Past perfect: "We have updated the register" → reported that they had updated the register
  • Simple past → Past perfect: "The simulation took place last month" → reported that it had taken place the previous month
  • will → would: "We will activate the plan" → confirmed that they would activate the plan
  • can → could: "Can you identify the triggers?" → asked whether they could identify the triggers
  • must → had to: "You must review the BCP" → told them that they had to review the BCP
  • Imperative negative → not + to infinitive: "Don't approve it yet" → warned them not to approve it
✔ The Risk Director stated that the residual risk was still above tolerance. (present → past)
✔ She confirmed they would activate the plan if the trigger were breached. (will/is → would/were)
✔ He reported that the simulation had been conducted twice. (present perfect → past perfect)
✔ The CFO warned the board not to approve the investment until due diligence was complete.

Risk Register180–240 words

What they are actually saying is that they cannot afford to invest in understanding the threats to their business model. That strikes me as a very dangerous position. The organisations I have seen manage crises most effectively are always those that invested in risk management before the crisis occurred — not as a regulatory requirement, but because their leadership genuinely understood that managing uncertainty is the core work of management.

Interviewer: You argue that risk management culture is more important than any risk framework. Can you explain what you mean? Expert: Absolutely. The best risk register in the world is worth nothing if the people who work in your organisation do not feel safe raising risks. And by safe, I mean psychologically safe — safe from the career consequences of being the person who flags a problem that turns out to be smaller than anticipated, or who slows down a project by identifying a risk that senior management would prefer not to hear about. The single greatest predictor of risk management failure that I have observed — across many organisations — is a culture in which bad news does not travel upward. When frontline employees, project managers, and middle managers routinely filter out uncomfortable information before it reaches the board, the board is effectively flying blind. They believe they have a clear picture of the organisation's risk landscape because the risk register looks manageable. But the register only reflects what people were willing to document. Interviewer: So how do you build a risk-aware culture? Expert: Three things, primarily. First, leadership modelling: leaders must visibly reward the escalation of risks, even — especially — when those risks turn out to be smaller than feared. If a manager raises a significant concern and the response is frustration rather than thanks, that sends a powerful message to everyone else. Second, simplicity: risk reporting systems must be simple enough that a busy line manager can actually use them without significant administrative burden. Complexity is the enemy of compliance. Third, accountability without blame: the post-incident review must be genuinely focused on learning rather than attribution. If people believe the purpose of a review is to find someone to blame, they will conceal information rather than share it.

Case Study 1 · Retail / Supply Chain · United Kingdom

MeridianRetail: How a Missing Contingency Plan Cost £4.2 Million

MeridianRetail is a UK-based home furnishings retailer with 48 stores and a rapidly growing e-commerce operation. In 2021, the company was highly dependent on a single third-party logistics provider — FastTrack Distribution — which handled 78% of its last-mile delivery volume. The commercial relationship with FastTrack had developed organically over eight years and had never been subject to a formal risk assessment. FastTrack was listed in the company's risk register simply as "key supplier — to be monitored," without a probability rating, a defined impact assessment, a named risk owner, or any associated contingency plan. In June 2021, FastTrack informed MeridianRetail at six weeks' notice that it was entering administration following the collapse of its parent company. The logistics provider was unable to guarantee continuity of service beyond the notice period.

MeridianRetail's response was improvised entirely in real time. Over the following three weeks, the operations team contacted fourteen alternative logistics providers, of which only two had sufficient capacity for the volumes required — and neither had experience with the company's specific home furnishings delivery requirements, which included two-person room-of-choice delivery for large items. Customer-facing delivery times increased from 3–5 days to 14–21 days during the transition period. Approximately 18,000 orders were significantly delayed, generating over 6,000 customer complaints, 1,200 refund requests, and extensive negative coverage across social media and consumer review platforms. The company's Trustpilot rating fell from 4.2 to 2.8 over a six-week period. The total financial impact — including expedited logistics costs, refunds, customer retention programmes, and reputational management — was estimated at £4.2 million.

A post-incident review commissioned by the board identified four specific failures: the risk had been listed on the register without a meaningful risk assessment; no contingency plan had been developed; no early warning indicators had been set up to monitor FastTrack's financial health; and no alternative suppliers had been qualified in advance. Following the review, the board mandated a full risk assessment for all suppliers representing more than 15% of the company's operational capacity, with a requirement for a documented contingency plan, a named risk owner, and quarterly review for each.
