Module Overview
B1–B2 · Conducting and Participating in Meetings

Welcome to Module 4: Conducting and Participating in Meetings. This module develops the professional English language skills you need to perform confidently in the full range of business meeting contexts — from one-to-one catch-ups to formal board presentations. You will build vocabulary for meeting structure and facilitation, practise the grammar of discussion and negotiation, and develop the listening and writing skills required to document and follow up on meetings effectively.

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Use precise vocabulary to describe meeting roles, procedures, and outcomes.
  • Open, facilitate, and close meetings in clear, professional English.
  • Interrupt politely, clarify points, and manage disagreement in meetings.
  • Write professional meeting agendas, minutes, and action point summaries.
  • Apply grammar structures — including passive voice, discourse markers, and indirect questions — in authentic meeting contexts.

Text 1 — The Anatomy of an Effective Business Meeting

A business meeting is one of the most powerful — and most frequently wasted — resources in any organisation. Research consistently estimates that professionals spend between a quarter and a third of their working week in meetings, yet surveys repeatedly show that the majority of participants consider a significant proportion of those meetings to be unnecessary, unproductive, or poorly managed. The most common complaints are familiar: no clear purpose was stated, the discussion drifted far from the original agenda, and the meeting ended without anyone being certain what had been decided or what action was expected of them. The root of this dysfunction is almost always inadequate preparation. An effective meeting begins not when participants enter the room, but when the chairperson first asks: what is the specific outcome we need to achieve, and is a meeting genuinely the best way to achieve it?

The practical architecture of an effective meeting rests on three interconnected pillars: structure, participation, and documentation. Structure begins with a well-constructed agenda — a document that goes far beyond a simple list of topics. A professionally written agenda includes a clear statement of the meeting's purpose, the name of the item owner for each point, an estimated time allocation for each item, and an indication of whether the item requires a decision, discussion, or simply an update. Participation is equally critical: the most productive meetings are those in which the chairperson actively manages the distribution of contributions, drawing out quieter participants, politely redirecting those who dominate, and ensuring that all relevant perspectives are heard before any decision is made. Documentation closes the loop — a meeting whose outcomes are not accurately recorded in formal minutes is a meeting that exists only in the imperfect and diverging memories of those who attended.

Technology has introduced both opportunities and complications for business meetings in the modern workplace. Video conferencing tools have made it possible for globally distributed teams to meet instantaneously regardless of geography, significantly reducing travel costs and enabling faster decision-making across time zones. However, virtual meetings present their own distinct facilitation challenges: maintaining engagement across a screen is significantly harder than in a physical room, the informal social dynamics that build trust and rapport in face-to-face interactions are largely absent, and the risk of technical disruption adds an additional layer of uncertainty. Hybrid meetings — in which some participants attend in person while others join remotely — are widely regarded as the most challenging format of all, as they require the chairperson to simultaneously manage two different participation environments. Organisations that invest in training their meeting leaders to navigate these complexities consistently report higher meeting satisfaction scores and more durable decision-making outcomes.

Text 2 — The Language of Meetings: Participation, Diplomacy, and Decision-Making

Professional competence in business meetings extends far beyond subject-matter expertise. The language used in meetings — how participants open discussions, challenge ideas, signal agreement, manage disagreement, and seek clarification — determines not only whether communication is effective but also how a professional is perceived and respected by their peers and superiors. Research in business communication consistently shows that professionals who speak with precision and diplomatic confidence in meetings are disproportionately likely to be judged as capable, authoritative, and promotable — regardless of the technical content of what they say. This observation underlines the critical importance of developing a sophisticated repertoire of meeting language: the specific phrases, discourse markers, and interpersonal strategies that allow a professional to contribute purposefully and persuasively without alienating colleagues or overstepping the boundaries of the meeting's formal procedure.

Several key language functions are particularly important in meeting contexts. Interrupting politely — a skill that many non-native speakers find especially challenging — requires specific softening phrases such as "If I could just come in here…" or "Could I add something at this point?" that signal the intention to speak without being abrupt or dismissive. Challenging or disagreeing with a colleague's view requires equally careful formulation: direct contradiction ("That's wrong.") is almost never appropriate in a professional setting, whereas hedged disagreement ("I take your point, but I would argue that…") allows for robust intellectual exchange while preserving the relationship. Seeking clarification, when done using appropriate language such as "Could you expand on what you mean by…?" or "Just to make sure I've understood correctly…", signals engagement and intellectual rigour rather than confusion. All of these language functions share a common principle: they must be precise enough to communicate clearly and courteous enough to maintain the collaborative atmosphere that effective meetings depend upon.

The decision-making phase of a meeting is frequently where professional language is under the greatest scrutiny. When a chairperson summarises a discussion and moves toward a resolution, precise language is essential to avoid ambiguity about what has actually been agreed. Phrases such as "So the consensus appears to be…", "Could we confirm that we are all in agreement that…", and "The motion is carried — this will be recorded in the minutes as…" give clarity and finality to what might otherwise be fuzzy or contested conclusions. Similarly, when a decision cannot be reached, the language used to defer or adjourn must be professional and forward-looking: "I suggest we table this item and return to it at the next meeting with additional data" is significantly more constructive than expressing frustration at the lack of agreement. The most effective meeting participants understand that every word they use in a meeting is, in some sense, a professional self-presentation — and they choose those words accordingly.

Grammar Review
Summary of all grammar points covered in Module 4 exercises

1. Passive Voice in Meeting and Business Communication Contexts

The passive voice is used extensively in formal meeting minutes, business reports, and official communications because it is impersonal, objective, and places emphasis on the action or decision rather than on who performed it.

  • Simple past passive: Subject + was/were + past participle — "A resolution was passed."
  • Present perfect passive: Subject + has/have been + past participle — "The proposal has been supported."
  • Modal passive: Subject + modal + be + past participle — "The agenda should be circulated."
  • Impersonal "It" passive: "It was agreed that…" / "It has been noted that…" / "It is recommended that…" — extremely common in formal minutes
✔ A formal resolution was passed at the end of the meeting. (simple past passive)
✔ It was agreed that the revised budget would be circulated by Friday. (impersonal passive)
✔ The following action points were identified during the meeting. (past passive)
✔ The agenda should be circulated at least five days in advance. (modal passive)

2. Discourse Markers for Professional Meeting Communication

Discourse markers are words and phrases that connect ideas, structure spoken and written communication, and signal the relationship between different parts of an argument or discussion. They are essential for clear, professional meeting facilitation.

  • Sequencing/ordering: First and foremost / First of all / Secondly / Finally / To begin with
  • Adding information: In addition / Furthermore / Moreover / Also
  • Contrast and concession: However / That said / Nevertheless / On the other hand / Despite this
  • Cause and effect: As a result / Therefore / Consequently / For this reason
  • Transitioning to a new point: Moving on / Turning to / Shall we turn to…
  • Summarising and closing: To sum up / To wrap up / In conclusion / To summarise
✔ "First and foremost, I'd like to acknowledge the team's progress." (prioritising)
✔ "That said, we still need to address the timeline concerns." (concessive contrast)
✔ "As a result, we need to accelerate our go-to-market strategy." (cause and effect)
✔ "To wrap up, could we confirm the action points and next meeting date?" (closing)

3. Indirect Questions in Professional Meeting Contexts

Indirect questions are considerably more polite and diplomatic than direct questions, making them the preferred form in formal business and meeting environments. They are introduced by a polite phrase and use statement (not question) word order.

  • Structure: Polite opener + question word / whether/if + subject + verb (statement order)
  • Common polite openers: "Could you tell us…" / "I'd like to know…" / "Could you clarify…" / "I wonder…" / "Could someone confirm…"
  • Yes/No indirect questions: Use whether or if — "I'd like to know whether the budget has been approved." (NOT: …if has the budget been approved)
  • Wh- indirect questions: Use the question word + statement order — "Could you clarify how this affects current contracts?" (NOT: …how does this affect)
  • Key rule: Never use inversion (auxiliary before subject) inside an indirect question
✔ "Could you tell us when the new system will be ready?" (NOT: when will the system be ready)
✔ "I'd like to know whether the committee has reached a decision." (NOT: if has the committee)
✔ "Could you clarify how this proposal affects the timeline?" (NOT: how does this affect)

4. Hedging Language: Expressing Uncertainty and Caution in Meetings

Hedging language is used to soften claims, express uncertainty, and avoid making absolute or overconfident statements — a critical skill in professional and diplomatic business communication.

  • Modal verbs: might / could / may / would — "This might resolve the issue."
  • Adverbs: arguably / possibly / perhaps / conceivably / potentially
  • Introductory phrases: "It would seem that…" / "It appears that…" / "It is conceivable that…" / "There is a possibility that…"
  • Tentative verbs: "appear to" / "tend to" / "seem to" / "would suggest"
  • Hedged personal opinion: "I would tend to think…" / "I'm not entirely convinced that…" / "I'm inclined to believe…"
  • Hedged data interpretation: "The figures would tend to indicate…" / "The data appears to suggest…"
✔ "The data appears to suggest an improvement, though we await the full figures." (tentative hedge)
✔ "It would seem that the budget constraint was a factor, but there may be other issues." (tentative + possibility)
✔ "The proposal might resolve the short-term issue." (modal hedge)
✔ "I'm not entirely convinced the client will accept this at this stage." (hedged negative opinion)

Meridian Consulting: When Too Many Meetings Kill Productivity

Meridian Consulting is a 280-person management consultancy based in London with offices in Manchester and Edinburgh. In 2023, the firm's managing partner, Caroline Ashworth, commissioned an internal productivity audit after a significant number of senior consultants flagged "excessive and unproductive meeting culture" as a primary contributor to both burnout and declining client output quality. The audit findings were striking: on average, senior consultants were spending 62% of their working week in internal meetings, leaving less than 40% of billable time for actual client work. Many of these meetings had no agenda, no named decision-maker, and no follow-up documentation. A recurring complaint was the presence of between twelve and twenty participants in meetings that involved decisions that could have been made by two or three people. The firm's billable hour rate had declined by 18% year-on-year, and three high-performing consultants had resigned in the previous six months, all citing meeting culture in their exit interviews.

Caroline Ashworth brought in an external facilitation specialist, Dr Priya Mehta, to design and implement a firm-wide meeting reform programme. The programme introduced four non-negotiable rules: every meeting required a written agenda stating its single decision objective, circulated at least 24 hours in advance; no meeting could have more than six participants without the managing partner's explicit approval; all meetings were to be time-boxed with a maximum default length of forty-five minutes; and all meetings required a follow-up action log distributed within 24 hours. Dr Mehta also trained fifteen internal "meeting champions" — senior staff who were empowered to challenge poorly structured meetings and model the new practices publicly. The firm adopted a "no agenda, no meeting" rule that became its most visible cultural marker.

Within nine months of the programme launch, the results were measurable and significant. The average number of weekly meetings per senior consultant had fallen from fourteen to six. Billable hours had increased by 22%, and client satisfaction scores — which had been declining — reversed their downward trend. Three of the five most recent new hires cited Meridian's reformed meeting culture as a specific reason they had chosen to join the firm. Caroline Ashworth noted in her quarterly report that the single highest-return intervention of the entire programme had been the introduction of the mandatory written agenda — a simple, zero-cost change that transformed the seriousness with which every meeting was approached from the moment it was scheduled.

Fintera: Bridging the Cross-Cultural Meeting Divide

Fintera is a financial technology company founded in Medellín, Colombia, which expanded into the European market by acquiring a Madrid-based payments startup in early 2022. The integration of the two teams — 85 staff from Medellín and 62 from Madrid — created significant operational challenges, but none more persistent and damaging than the cultural differences in how the two teams conducted and experienced business meetings. The Colombian team, led by Operations Director Santiago Reyes, had a highly relational meeting culture: meetings began with extended informal conversation, agendas were treated as flexible starting points rather than binding schedules, and decision-making was typically deferred to the most senior person in the room. The Madrid team, led by General Manager Isabel Fontaine, came from a professional culture where punctuality was non-negotiable, agendas were precise and adhered to rigorously, and it was expected that any participant — regardless of seniority — could raise objections and influence decisions.

The clashes were immediate and significant. Madrid team members found the Colombian meetings frustrating and inefficient, feeling that time was wasted before substantive discussion began and that decisions were never clearly made or recorded. Santiago's team, by contrast, experienced the Madrid-run meetings as cold, transactional, and dismissive of relationship-building — a value they considered essential to effective professional collaboration. Two significant project decisions were delayed by over six weeks as a result of meetings that concluded without any clear resolution, because neither team's expectations of how a decision should be reached or communicated had been made explicit. The situation was compounded by language dynamics: while both teams conducted meetings in English as a common language, differing competency levels created additional power imbalances in discussions.

The company engaged an intercultural communication consultant, who facilitated a joint workshop attended by both leadership teams. The workshop identified five agreed principles for all Fintera meetings going forward: a brief, structured "check-in" at the beginning of every meeting (meeting the Colombian team's relationship needs without consuming agenda time); a standardised agenda template including a decision field for every item; a clear meeting role allocation system identifying the chairperson, minute-taker, and timekeeper in advance; a multilingual glossary of key meeting phrases available to all participants; and a post-meeting action log reviewed at the next meeting's opening. Within four months, cross-team project completion rates had improved by 31%, and both Santiago and Isabel described the revised meeting structure as one of the most effective cross-cultural integration tools they had experienced in their careers.
