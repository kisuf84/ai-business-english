Module Overview
B1–B2 · Leading and Managing Teams

Welcome to Module 3: Leading and Managing Teams. In this module you will develop the professional English vocabulary and language structures needed to lead, motivate, and manage people effectively in a business environment. You will explore key themes including leadership styles, team dynamics, delegation, performance management, conflict resolution, and professional communication.

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Use specialised vocabulary to describe leadership styles and team roles.
  • Discuss delegation, motivation, and performance management using accurate, professional language.
  • Write formal emails and reports related to team management contexts.
  • Understand spoken English in meetings, briefings, and managerial conversations.
  • Apply grammar structures — including conditionals, modals, and reported speech — in authentic business contexts.

Text 1 — The Art of Leadership: Styles, Trust, and Team Performance

Leadership is far more than issuing instructions and monitoring results. At its core, effective leadership requires a deep understanding of human motivation, behaviour, and the complex dynamics that emerge whenever a group of people works toward a shared goal. Researchers have identified several distinct leadership styles, each with its own strengths and limitations depending on the organisational context. Autocratic leaders retain centralised decision-making authority and expect compliance from team members; this approach can be effective in high-pressure, time-critical situations where rapid, unambiguous direction is needed. Democratic leaders, by contrast, actively involve their teams in decision-making, which fosters a greater sense of ownership, improves morale, and often produces more creative and well-rounded solutions. Transformational leaders go further still — they inspire team members through a compelling shared vision, encouraging them to exceed their own expectations and grow both professionally and personally.

The foundation of every high-performing team is trust — specifically, what researchers call "psychological safety." A psychologically safe team environment is one in which all members feel genuinely comfortable speaking up, challenging existing ideas, admitting mistakes openly, and taking calculated risks without fear of embarrassment or professional punishment. Google's celebrated Project Aristotle, a landmark study of hundreds of internal teams, identified psychological safety as the single most important factor in determining team effectiveness — ranking above individual talent, technical skills, and even team composition. Leaders who actively cultivate this culture do so by modelling vulnerability themselves, explicitly inviting dissenting views, responding to mistakes with curiosity rather than blame, and consistently demonstrating that every team member's voice is not only heard but genuinely valued. When psychological safety is absent, team members default to self-protective silence, innovation stagnates, and critical information rarely reaches the people who need it most.

Effective leaders also recognise that motivation is not a static, one-size-fits-all resource. Different team members are driven by entirely different factors — some primarily by financial rewards and recognition, others by meaningful challenges, professional growth, a sense of belonging, or the autonomy to direct their own work. Psychologist Frederick Herzberg distinguished between "hygiene factors" — baseline conditions such as salary and job security whose absence causes dissatisfaction but whose presence alone does not produce engagement — and "motivators" such as achievement, recognition, and development, which genuinely inspire sustained high performance. A skilled manager, therefore, takes time to understand what truly drives each individual team member and tailors their management approach accordingly. They use structured one-to-one meetings, development planning conversations, and regular constructive feedback not as bureaucratic obligations, but as powerful opportunities to connect, inspire, and grow the unique human talent within their team.

Text 2 — Delegation, Conflict, and the Performance Management Cycle

Delegation is one of the most essential — and most frequently misused — tools in any manager's repertoire. Effective delegation involves far more than simply handing off tasks to free up a manager's calendar. When done well, delegation is a powerful act of trust and professional development: it signals confidence in an employee's capabilities, stretches their skills into new territory, and cultivates the kind of ownership and engagement that no amount of direct supervision can manufacture. However, the delegation process can fail at several critical junctures. Over-delegation — assigning tasks without adequate context, resources, or support — leaves employees feeling overwhelmed and exposed to failure. Under-delegation, or micromanagement, conveys distrust, stifles initiative, and creates an unsustainable bottleneck at the managerial level. The most effective managers master the art of contextual delegation: matching the complexity of a task to the capability and confidence level of the individual, providing clear objectives and decision-making boundaries, and remaining available as a resource without becoming an obstacle.

Conflict within teams, when managed well, can be a powerful catalyst for innovation and better decision-making. Research consistently shows that teams that engage in healthy, respectful task-related conflict — disagreements about ideas, approaches, and solutions — produce significantly better outcomes than those that avoid all friction in the name of harmony. However, when conflict becomes personal, emotional, or entrenched, it rapidly becomes destructive. Effective managers intervene early, before positions harden and trust is irreparably damaged. Key conflict resolution techniques include active listening, perspective-taking, reframing the disagreement in terms of shared goals, and establishing clear behavioural expectations going forward. In some cases, structural solutions — such as clarifying role boundaries, redistributing responsibilities, or restructuring team dynamics — are required to prevent the same conflicts from recurring. A manager who is skilled at navigating conflict does not seek to eliminate all disagreement, but to channel it productively toward the team's collective objectives.

A robust performance management cycle is the operational backbone of team leadership. This cycle typically consists of four interconnected phases: goal-setting, ongoing monitoring and coaching, formal review and feedback, and development planning. The goal-setting phase is where clarity is established — objectives should align with organisational priorities and be specific, measurable, and time-bound. The monitoring phase is where many managers fail: effective monitoring requires regular, substantive check-ins rather than the passive assumption that no news is good news. When performance falls below expectations, the manager's response should be timely, honest, and constructive — focusing on specific behaviours and measurable outcomes rather than personal characteristics. The development planning phase closes the loop by identifying skill gaps, training needs, and growth opportunities that will equip each team member to perform more effectively in the future. When all four phases are executed consistently and with genuine care for each individual's development, performance management transforms from a compliance exercise into a genuine engine of team growth.

Grammar Review
Summary of all grammar points covered in Module 3 exercises

1. Conditional Sentences in Management English

Conditionals are used constantly in management discussions to explore cause-and-effect relationships, scenarios, and consequences of decisions.

  • Zero conditional — General truths and facts: If + present simple, present simple
  • First conditional — Real, likely future scenarios: If + present simple, will + base verb
  • Second conditional — Hypothetical present/future: If + past simple, would + base verb
  • Third conditional — Unreal past situations and their consequences: If + past perfect, would have + pp
  • Mixed conditional — Past condition with present result: If + past perfect, would + base verb
  • Unless = "if not" — used with 1st and 2nd conditionals to express conditions
✔ If leaders hold regular 1:1s, they catch performance issues early. (zero — fact)
✔ If you delegate this project, she will develop her skills significantly. (1st — real future)
✔ If the manager took emotional intelligence more seriously, morale would be much higher. (2nd — hypothetical)
✔ If they had addressed the conflict early, trust would not have broken down. (3rd — unreal past)

2. Modal Verbs for Professional Communication

Modal verbs express key professional meanings such as obligation, advice, possibility, and criticism — essential for precise management communication.

  • must / have to — obligation, necessity, strict rules
  • must not — prohibition (do not do this)
  • should / ought to — recommendation, expectation, strong advice
  • should not — strong advice against an action
  • could / might / may — possibility, uncertainty, tentative suggestion
  • should have + past participle — criticism or regret about a past action
  • must have + past participle — logical deduction about a past situation
  • could have + past participle — an unrealised past possibility
✔ All managers must complete the training. (obligation)
✔ You should address the performance issue this week, not next month. (recommendation)
✔ She should have delegated that task earlier — now she's overloaded. (past regret)
✔ The team's low morale might improve with better recognition. (possibility)

3. Reported Speech in Business and Management Contexts

Reported speech is used in meeting minutes, management reports, emails, and briefings when summarising what someone said or instructed.

  • Tense backshift: present simple → past simple | "will" → "would" | present perfect → past perfect | present continuous → past continuous
  • Time/reference shifts: "now" → "at that time" | "today" → "that day" | "next week" → "the following week"
  • Reported imperatives: Direct: "Delegate more." → Reported: She told him to delegate more. / She told him not to micromanage.
  • Indirect questions: Yes/No: "Did you complete it?" → He asked whether/if I had completed it. (statement word order, no inversion)
  • Formal written register: Use impersonal passives: "It was agreed that...", "It has been noted that...", "It is recommended that..."
✔ Direct: "I will redesign the onboarding process." → Reported: She said she would redesign the onboarding process.
✔ Direct: "Don't skip the one-to-ones." → Reported: He told her not to skip the one-to-ones.
✔ Direct: "Are you using 360-degree feedback?" → Reported: She asked whether they were using 360-degree feedback.

4. Gerunds and Infinitives in Management Language

Gerunds (verb + -ing) and infinitives (to + verb) follow specific verbs and patterns — mastering them is essential for fluent professional communication.

  • Verbs followed by gerund: suggest, recommend, avoid, consider, enjoy, practise, finish, keep, risk, mind, involve, admit, deny, postpone
  • Verbs followed by infinitive: agree, decide, refuse, promise, plan, manage, fail, hope, want, need, choose, expect, afford, offer
  • Verbs followed by either (different meaning): remember, forget, stop, try, regret + gerund vs. infinitive can change the meaning significantly
  • Gerunds as subjects: "Delivering effective feedback is one of the hardest managerial skills." — gerund as grammatical subject
  • Struggle / tend / manage + to-infinitive: "Many leaders struggle to delegate effectively in their first management role."
✔ She suggested holding a team retrospective at the end of each sprint. (suggest + gerund)
✔ The board agreed to review the succession plan before the next quarter. (agree + infinitive)
✔ Managing performance effectively requires both skill and emotional intelligence. (gerund as subject)
✔ He avoided addressing the underperformance, which made the situation worse. (avoid + gerund)

Case Study 2 · Financial Services · Colombia

BancoVerde: Transforming Team Culture Through Psychological Safety

BancoVerde is a regional bank operating in Colombia with over 2,400 employees. In 2022, the bank's Risk Management division — a team of 34 analysts, compliance specialists, and senior advisors — was identified as having a critically low employee engagement score of 38%, compared to a company-wide average of 64%. Exit interviews conducted over the previous two years had revealed a consistent and troubling pattern: multiple departing employees had cited an atmosphere of fear, blame, and hierarchical rigidity as their primary reasons for leaving. In a division whose core function required accurate and timely communication of potential risks to senior stakeholders, the absence of open communication was not merely a management problem — it was a direct operational and regulatory risk. The bank's Chief Operating Officer, Dr Valentina Moreno, was tasked with leading a comprehensive cultural transformation programme for the division.

Dr Moreno began with a diagnostic phase, conducting structured listening sessions with every member of the team, including junior analysts who had never before been formally consulted about their experience. The picture that emerged was unambiguous: a small number of senior managers had consistently responded to bad news, honest concerns, and differing professional opinions with dismissal, mockery, or overt anger. Over time, this had trained the entire team to withhold information, soften assessments, and self-censor rather than risk a negative reaction. The psychological contract between the team and its leadership had been severely damaged. Dr Moreno identified this as a textbook case of absent psychological safety — and made its restoration the central pillar of her intervention strategy.

The transformation programme she designed included: mandatory training in psychological safety and inclusive leadership behaviours for all managers in the division; the introduction of anonymised team feedback surveys conducted every six weeks; the explicit reframing of mistakes as learning opportunities in all team communications; and a formal revision of the performance management framework to reward collaborative behaviour and transparent communication alongside individual technical output. The two managers most responsible for the toxic dynamics were moved to non-managerial specialist roles as part of a broader restructuring. Within fourteen months, team engagement scores had risen to 71% — above the company average — attrition had fallen by 40%, and the team had successfully identified and flagged three regulatory compliance risks that had previously gone undetected. Dr Moreno attributed the risk improvements directly to the restored culture of open, honest communication.
