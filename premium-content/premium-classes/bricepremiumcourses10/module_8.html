Professional Register
The level of formality, vocabulary, tone, and style appropriate for a specific professional communication context — determined by the relationship between writer and reader, the purpose of the communication, and the platform or format in which it appears. In hospitality writing, professional register is a particularly critical concept because the same hotel may need to communicate in formal register with a corporate client requesting a conference proposal, in semi-formal register with a leisure guest following up on a booking, in warm and personal register with a returning VIP guest, and in engaging, brand-appropriate register with social media followers. The ability to read the context and select the right register — adjusting vocabulary, sentence length, salutation, sign-off, and tone accordingly — is a core professional writing competency. Using the wrong register (too formal with a casual leisure guest, too informal with a corporate lawyer) signals a failure to understand the relationship and undermines the credibility of the communication.

Subject Line
The brief text visible in the recipient's inbox before an email is opened — the single most important element of a business email in terms of determining whether the email is opened, when it is opened, and what expectation the reader brings to the content. A professional hospitality subject line is: specific (identifying exactly what the email contains rather than 'Following up' or 'Re: your enquiry'); concise (typically under 50 characters to avoid truncation on mobile devices); and appropriately branded (reflecting the hotel's tone of voice). Subject lines for guest-facing emails should lead with what matters to the guest: 'Your Reservation Confirmation — The Ashgrove, 14 March' is far more useful than 'Confirmation'. For internal emails, subject lines serve an archiving and retrieval function — a team member searching their inbox six months later should be able to identify the email's content from the subject line alone.

Dish Description
The written text accompanying a menu item — describing the dish's key components, preparation method, primary flavour experience, and any notable ingredients or provenance — in language that creates appetite and communicates the restaurant's identity. A professional dish description typically contains: the name of the dish (which may itself be descriptive or evocative); the primary protein or principal ingredient; two or three key supporting components; a preparation or cooking method detail; and a provenance reference where appropriate. Effective dish descriptions use precise, sensory, and evocative language ('slow-braised', 'char-grilled', 'hand-picked', 'smoked over applewood') rather than generic filler ('served with vegetables' or 'accompanied by sauce'). Descriptions that are too long overwhelm the reader; those that are too sparse fail to create appetite. The optimal length for a fine dining dish description is typically two to three lines; for a bistro-style menu, one line may be sufficient.

Provenance
The origin and traceability of food ingredients — particularly used in menu writing and restaurant marketing to communicate that ingredients are sourced from specific farms, producers, regions, or suppliers whose identity, ethos, and quality standards are known and valued. Provenance language in menus ('Hereford beef from [Farm Name]', 'Cornish dayboat fish', 'herbs from our kitchen garden', 'aged in a local dairy') communicates several things simultaneously: ingredient quality (specific sourcing implies the chef has chosen the best, rather than using commodity ingredients); ethical commitment (local, traceable sourcing aligns with sustainability values); and a connection to place and season that many guests find meaningful. Provenance language is more powerful when it is specific and genuine rather than vague and aspirational: 'locally sourced where possible' communicates very little, while 'lamb from Ryland Farm, three miles from this kitchen' communicates a great deal.

Call to Action
A specific, direct instruction in a written communication that tells the reader what to do next — 'Book now', 'Reserve your table', 'Reply to this email to confirm', 'Call us on [number]'. In professional hospitality writing, a call to action closes the communication loop: a guest who receives a spa package proposal without a clear call to action must decide for themselves how to proceed, which creates friction and reduces conversion. An effective hospitality call to action is: specific (telling the reader exactly what to do); easy to execute (providing a link, button, phone number, or email address that makes the action simple); and appropriately urgent without being pressuring ('We have limited availability at this rate — we would love to confirm your booking by Friday' conveys useful information without feeling aggressive). Every guest-facing email should end with a clear call to action that tells the reader what the writer needs them to do.

Tone of Voice
The personality and character of a brand as expressed through its written communication — the consistent set of qualities that distinguish one hotel's emails, menu copy, website text, and social media posts from another's, even when the content is similar. A hotel's tone of voice is typically defined in a brand communications guide and covers: the vocabulary register (formal, semi-formal, warm and conversational); the personality characteristics the writing should communicate (confident and authoritative, warm and personal, witty and playful, understated and sophisticated); what the writing should never do (use jargon, be condescending, use aggressive sales language); and specific examples of on-brand and off-brand phrasing. Maintaining a consistent tone of voice across all written touchpoints — from a booking confirmation email to a room information card to a sign in the gym — is a powerful brand signal that communicates professionalism, coherence, and attention to detail.

Active Voice
A grammatical structure in which the subject of a sentence performs the action — 'The chef prepares all sauces fresh each morning' — as opposed to passive voice, in which the subject receives the action: 'All sauces are prepared fresh each morning by the chef'. Active voice is almost universally preferred in professional hospitality writing because it is more direct, more energetic, and clearer about who is doing what. Passive voice, while grammatically correct, can create a sense of distance, bureaucracy, or evasion — qualities that are the opposite of what hospitality communication should convey. Compare: 'We will personally escort you to your room' (active, warm, personal) versus 'You will be escorted to your room' (passive, impersonal, institutional). In menu writing, active voice creates appetite: 'We smoke our own salmon over Scottish whisky barrels' is more engaging than 'Our salmon is smoked over Scottish whisky barrels'.

Standard Template
A pre-written email or document structure that contains fixed content (the hotel's address, logo, standard greeting and sign-off, and any legally required information) alongside variable sections that the writer completes for each specific communication. Standard templates are a practical necessity in hotel operations — they ensure consistency of format and brand presentation across different team members and departments, guarantee that legally required information (such as allergen notices or booking terms) is never omitted, and reduce the time required to produce routine communications. The risk of over-reliance on templates is that communications feel impersonal, formulaic, and insufficiently responsive to the specific situation — a guest who receives a template response to a detailed and specific enquiry will notice that their enquiry has not been carefully read. Best practice combines structural templates (consistent format, reliable inclusion of key information) with genuine personalisation of the content (referencing specific details of the guest's enquiry or situation).

Plain English
A writing philosophy and set of practical techniques that prioritise clarity, concision, and accessibility over complexity, jargon, and institutional language. The core principles of plain English writing include: using short sentences rather than complex, multi-clause constructions; choosing familiar words over their Latinate equivalents ('use' rather than 'utilise', 'start' rather than 'commence', 'end' rather than 'terminate'); avoiding jargon that the reader may not understand; using active voice; and structuring information so that the most important point comes first. In hospitality writing, plain English is the default standard for all guest-facing communication — it ensures that guests of varying language backgrounds and varying literacy levels can understand what is being communicated without needing to re-read or seek clarification. Plain English does not mean simple or banal writing — it is entirely compatible with warmth, personality, and sophisticated vocabulary choices, provided those choices serve clarity rather than obscuring it.

Proofreading
The careful review of a completed piece of writing to identify and correct errors in spelling, punctuation, grammar, and factual accuracy before the document is sent, published, or printed. In a professional hospitality context, proofreading is a non-negotiable standard for all guest-facing written communication: a spelling error in a menu, a grammatical mistake in a guest email, or a factual error in a booking confirmation undermines the credibility of the hotel's entire service proposition. Proofreading is distinct from editing (which addresses the clarity, structure, and effectiveness of the content) and should ideally be done by a different person from the writer — familiarity with the text makes it harder to spot errors that are obvious to a fresh reader. Effective proofreading strategies include: reading the text aloud (which catches errors that the eye skips over); reading from bottom to top one sentence at a time (which prevents the brain from autocorrecting); and using spellcheck as a starting point but not as a substitute for human review.

Upselling Copy
Persuasive written content designed to encourage guests to purchase additional or upgraded products and services — such as a room upgrade, a spa package, an afternoon tea, or an early check-in. Effective upselling copy in hospitality creates desire rather than pressure: it describes the experience the guest will have rather than simply listing the product and its price, communicates the value rather than just the cost, and makes the ask feel natural rather than commercially aggressive. Techniques include: leading with the guest's benefit ('Wake up to uninterrupted countryside views from our Garden Suite — and enjoy a complimentary champagne breakfast before a full day of exploring'); using sensory and evocative language; creating gentle urgency where genuinely appropriate ('This rate is available until Saturday and we have limited availability'); and always making the path to booking simple and immediate. Upselling copy that sounds desperate, pushy, or irrelevant to the guest's specific situation is actively counterproductive.

Seasonal Menu
A restaurant or hotel menu that changes regularly — monthly, quarterly, or with the seasons — to reflect what ingredients are at their peak quality and availability at a given time of year. Seasonal menus are both a culinary philosophy (cooking with ingredients at their best) and a commercial strategy (creating reasons for guests to return throughout the year to experience something new). In a writing context, communicating a seasonal menu change requires producing engaging content across multiple channels: the printed or digital menu itself; emails or posts announcing the change to guests and followers; social media content featuring specific dishes; and briefing notes for the service team so they can describe the new menu accurately and enthusiastically. Effective seasonal menu communication emphasises freshness, craft, and the connection to the current moment ('This autumn, head chef Sofia Reyes is celebrating the harvest...') rather than simply announcing that the menu has been updated.

Allergen Information
Written disclosure of the fourteen allergens identified by EU and UK food labelling regulations as the most commonly and seriously allergenic food substances — including cereals containing gluten, crustaceans, eggs, fish, peanuts, soybeans, milk, nuts, celery, mustard, sesame, sulphur dioxide and sulphites, lupin, and molluscs. In the UK, food businesses are legally required to provide accurate allergen information for all dishes sold, whether on a printed menu, verbally on request, or both. For hospitality writing, allergen information must be: accurate (incorrect information can cause a life-threatening reaction); complete (covering all fourteen regulated allergens); accessible (easy to find and understand for guests with allergies); and consistently maintained when menus change. Best practice in hotel and restaurant menu writing places allergen information on a clearly accessible reference document, highlights the option to ask a member of staff for detailed allergen advice, and uses clear, internationally understood symbols where appropriate.

Promotional Copy
Written content created to promote a hotel's products, services, or experiences — including website landing pages, email marketing campaigns, social media posts, printed brochures, in-room promotional materials, and advertising. Effective promotional copy in hospitality is benefit-led (describing what the guest will experience rather than listing product features), emotionally resonant (connecting the product to a feeling, occasion, or aspiration rather than simply stating facts), and always clear about the offer (price, availability, how to book). The most common failures of hotel promotional copy include: excessive use of superlatives without substance ('the most stunning venue', 'unparalleled luxury'); vague aspirational language that applies to any hotel ('where memories are made', 'your home away from home'); ignoring the specific guest segment being addressed; and burying the key offer information under layers of purple prose. The best hospitality promotional copy is specific, sensory, honest, and ends with a clear call to action.

Internal Briefing Note
A short, structured written document produced for internal distribution within a hotel or hospitality organisation — providing team members with the information they need to understand a situation, prepare for an event or arrival, or implement an operational decision. Internal briefing notes differ from guest-facing communications in register (typically more direct and less formal), in purpose (operational rather than promotional or relational), and in structure (typically using bullet points, numbered lists, and clear headings for quick scanning rather than prose paragraphs). A well-written internal briefing note covers: the context (what is happening and why the team needs to know); the key details (specific names, dates, times, numbers, and requirements); and the required actions (what each team member or department needs to do and by when). Briefing notes that are too long, too vague, or too conversational fail in their primary purpose of communicating operational information efficiently and reliably.

Guest Correspondence
Written communication between a hotel and an individual guest — encompassing booking confirmations, pre-arrival communications, welcome letters, response to enquiries, complaint responses, post-stay follow-up, and any other personalised written exchange. Guest correspondence is distinct from mass marketing communication in that it is directed at a specific named individual and addresses their particular situation, booking, or concern. The quality of guest correspondence is a significant indicator of a hotel's overall service standards: a guest who receives a warm, personalised, and error-free booking confirmation before arrival begins the relationship with confidence; one who receives a generic, template-heavy, or error-riddled email arrives with reduced expectations. Best practice in guest correspondence requires: using the guest's name; referencing their specific booking or situation; writing in the hotel's established tone of voice; being complete (covering everything the guest needs to know); and always ending with a clear offer of further assistance.

Menu Pricing Language
The conventions and techniques used to present prices on a menu in a way that is clear, legally compliant, and consistent with the restaurant's positioning and atmosphere. Menu pricing language varies significantly by restaurant type and positioning: a fine dining restaurant may present prices without pound signs (simply '24', '32') to create a sophisticated atmosphere and reduce the psychological friction of seeing explicit currency symbols; a casual dining restaurant typically uses clear, prominent pricing with £ signs; and a set menu format presents a single price for a defined number of courses. In the UK, all menu prices for food and drink must include VAT. A service charge — if applied — must be clearly stated on the menu, along with information about whether it is discretionary or mandatory. In writing a menu, pricing language should be consistent throughout: mixing formats (some dishes with £ signs, some without; some with 'pp', some not) creates a disorganised impression regardless of the food quality.

Email Sign-Off
The closing formality of a professional email — comprising the valediction ('Yours sincerely', 'Kind regards', 'Warm regards', 'Best wishes'), the sender's full name, their job title, and typically the hotel's name, address, and contact details. In hospitality writing, the email sign-off is a small but significant element of brand presentation: it communicates the formality of the relationship, the professionalism of the sender, and — through the inclusion of the hotel's branding and contact details — reinforces the hotel's identity at the close of every communication. The choice of valediction should match the register of the email: 'Yours sincerely' is appropriate for formal initial correspondence; 'Kind regards' or 'Warm regards' for semi-formal ongoing correspondence; and 'With warmest wishes' or 'Looking forward to welcoming you' for personal correspondence with a known and valued guest. A sign-off that is too casual ('Cheers', 'Thanks') or too formal ('I remain, yours faithfully') for the context signals a misreading of the relationship.

Event Proposal
A structured document — sent by a hotel's sales or events team to a client who has made an enquiry about hosting an event — presenting the hotel's proposed solutions for the client's requirements, including venue details, capacity, catering options, technical facilities, pricing, and terms and conditions. A professional event proposal is one of the most commercially significant pieces of writing a hospitality team produces: it is often the document that determines whether a corporate client chooses one venue over another, and it must therefore combine factual completeness (covering everything the client needs to know to make a decision) with persuasive quality (communicating the hotel's distinctiveness, experience, and commitment to making the event exceptional). Event proposals should be: structured logically (venue overview → specific spaces → catering → technical → pricing → terms → next steps); personalised to the client's specific requirements; professionally formatted and presented; and followed up with a proactive call or email within 48 hours.

Follow-Up Email
A subsequent email sent after an initial communication — typically to check whether the recipient has received and considered a proposal, to provide additional information they have requested, to confirm a decision or next step, or to maintain a relationship after a specific interaction. In hospitality sales and event management, follow-up emails are a critical part of the booking conversion process: research consistently shows that a significant proportion of bookings are lost not because the client chose a competitor but because no one followed up and the enquiry went cold. A professional hospitality follow-up email: references the original communication specifically ('I am following up on the event proposal I sent you on Tuesday for your June conference'); adds value rather than simply asking whether the client has made a decision ('I wanted to mention that we do have limited availability in June — and I have also checked on the outdoor terrace option you asked about'); and ends with a clear next step.

Booking Confirmation
A formal written communication sent to a guest after a reservation has been made — confirming all the material details of the booking and providing a reference number for the guest's records. A professional booking confirmation for a hotel reservation contains: the guest's name and contact details; the reservation reference number; the hotel's name and address; the check-in and check-out dates; the room type and rate; the inclusions (breakfast, parking); the cancellation policy; the payment method and any pre-payment made; special requests noted; and the hotel's contact details for pre-arrival queries. A booking confirmation for an event or group reservation may also include: the event date and duration; the venue spaces booked; the catering arrangements; the technical requirements confirmed; and the payment schedule and final balance due date. The booking confirmation is a legally significant document and must therefore be accurate, complete, and clearly presented.

Text 1: Professional Hospitality Email Communication — Principles, Structures, and Standards
How to write emails that are clear, professional, and appropriately branded across every hospitality context

Email is the single most frequently produced piece of writing in most hotel operations — and the single most frequently underestimated. Hotel teams that invest in training, systems, and standards for their verbal guest interactions will often leave email communication entirely to individual judgment, with the result that the same hotel sends, in a single week, emails ranging from impeccably professional to grammatically uncertain, from warmly personal to robotically templated, from detailed and useful to vague and evasive. This inconsistency is invisible only until a guest or client reads two different emails from the same hotel and notices the gap. At that point, it signals not just a writing problem but an operational one: a hotel that does not manage its written communication manages nothing consistently.

Professional hospitality email communication is built on five foundations. The first is register — selecting the right level of formality for the specific relationship and purpose: formal correspondence with a new corporate client, semi-formal with a returning leisure guest, warm and personal with a VIP whose preferences you know. The second is structure — organizing the email so that the most important information comes first, supporting detail follows, and the email ends with a clear call to action rather than trailing off into vagueness. The third is completeness — ensuring that the email answers the question that was asked and anticipates the questions that will follow, rather than requiring a back-and-forth that frustrates both parties. The fourth is accuracy — proofreading for errors in spelling, grammar, punctuation, and factual content before sending. The fifth is voice — ensuring that the email sounds like the hotel, not like an anonymous bureaucratic operation.

The most commercially significant type of hospitality email is the response to an event or group enquiry. A corporate client who has sent an enquiry to three hotels will make their initial shortlisting decision — at least in part — based on the quality of the responses they receive. A response that is prompt (within four hours for a serious commercial enquiry), complete (addressing every requirement the client specified), personal (demonstrating that the specific enquiry has been read and understood), and professionally presented will consistently outperform a response that arrives two days later, addresses only some of the requirements, uses a generic template, and contains three spelling errors. The email is the hotel's first piece of hospitality — and it should be delivered with the same care as the check-in.

Text 2: The Craft of Menu Writing — How Language Shapes the Dining Experience
How professional menu language creates appetite, communicates identity, and guides the guest experience

A menu is far more than a list of dishes and prices. It is a brand communication document, a sales tool, a legal document (in the context of allergen information), and a piece of writing that sets the guest's expectations before a single dish arrives at the table. Research consistently shows that well-written menus — those that use evocative, specific, and accurate language to describe dishes — generate higher average spend per cover, higher satisfaction scores, and higher rates of return compared to menus with identical food but weaker descriptions. The words on the menu do not change what the chef cooks, but they profoundly influence how the guest feels about what they are about to eat, and how they remember it afterwards.

The principles of effective menu writing begin with specificity. 'Served with seasonal vegetables' communicates almost nothing — virtually every dish at virtually every restaurant could make this claim. 'Served with honey-roasted heritage carrots and a wilted watercress and walnut salad' communicates a particular kitchen's sensibility, its attention to detail, and the specific pleasure the dish will provide. This principle of specificity extends to every element of the description: the cut of meat ('slow-braised ox cheek' rather than 'braised beef'), the preparation method ('stone-baked' rather than 'baked'), the provenance ('Cornish dayboat mackerel' rather than 'fresh mackerel'), and the primary flavour experience ('a clean, mineral finish' rather than 'a delicious flavour'). Each specific detail creates a mental image that generic language cannot produce.

Equally important is what menu writing should avoid. The most damaging failure is overclaiming — describing every dish as 'exquisite', every ingredient as 'world-class', every preparation as 'artisan'. Superlatives in menu writing are self-defeating: when everything is exceptional, nothing is. A guest who reads that the chicken is 'succulent and tender' and the steak is 'meltingly tender and deeply flavourful' and the fish is 'perfectly fresh and expertly prepared' learns nothing about any dish — only that the menu was written by someone with a thesaurus and no discipline. The best menu writers are restrained with praise, saving evocative language for the specific qualities that genuinely distinguish a dish, and trusting that precision and specificity are more persuasive than superlatives.

Exercise 2 — Grammar 2: Menu Writing — Specific, Sensory, and Evocative Dish Descriptions

Effective menu descriptions use specificity (exact ingredient names, provenance, preparation methods), sensory language (texture, temperature, flavour contrasts), and restraint (no superlatives — let the ingredients speak).

Before and after — the principle in action:

  • BEFORE: 'Roast chicken with vegetables and gravy'
  • AFTER: 'Hartley Farm free-range chicken, slow-roasted on the crown, heritage root vegetables, roasting jus, garden watercress'

Structural pattern for a dish description:

  1. Principal ingredient (with provenance if available)
  2. Preparation method
  3. Key supporting components
  4. A flavour or texture note (optional but powerful)

What to avoid:

Exercise 3 — Grammar 3: Promotional and Upselling Copy — Benefit-Led, Specific, and Brand-Appropriate

Effective promotional copy leads with the guest's benefit and emotion, uses specific details (what exactly is included, the price, the dates), and avoids empty superlatives. The call to action should be clear and easy to act on.

Before (generic, superlative-heavy):

  • 'Experience unparalleled luxury in our award-winning spa. Book the ultimate retreat and create memories that will last a lifetime.'

After (specific, benefit-led, honest):

  • 'The leaves are turning and the days are shortening. This is the season for long mornings, warm oils, and the particular pleasure of having nowhere to be. The Ashgrove Autumn Restoration includes two nights, our signature herbal massage, and a morning in the thermal suite. From £320. October and November only.'

Key techniques:

  • Lead with the emotion or occasion → then the specific product → then the price and availability → then the call to action.
  • One superlative maximum per piece of copy — spend it wisely.

How a hotel's investment in email writing standards converted an uncertain enquiry into a recurring annual contract
This case study is drawn from a composite of real sales email scenarios documented in UK hospitality management training programmes. This case study documents a representative scenario from a UK hotel restaurant that was performing below its potential despite consistently high food quality. Guest feedback consistently rated the food highly (average 8.4 out of 10 in post-stay surveys) but average spend per cover at dinner was tracking 15% below the hotel's competitive set benchmark — an anomaly that the restaurant manager, on reviewing the data carefully, attributed to a specific cause: a disproportionate number of guests were choosing the cheapest dishes on the menu.

An analysis of the menu revealed the issue. The most expensive dishes — a heritage beef Wellington and a pan-roasted halibut — had descriptions that were technically accurate but commercially inert. The Wellington was described as 'Beef Wellington with mushroom duxelles, wrapped in puff pastry, served with green beans and a red wine jus'. The halibut was described as 'Pan-roasted halibut fillet with seasonal vegetables and a lemon beurre blanc.'

Both descriptions told guests what the dishes were but gave them no reason to want them specifically — no sense of the sourcing, the craft, or the flavour experience that distinguished them from alternatives at a lower price point. A guest weighing up a £32 Wellington against a £22 chicken dish had no textual reason to choose the more expensive option, because the more expensive option was written as if it were equally unremarkable.

The restaurant manager worked with the head chef to rewrite the descriptions with the specificity the dishes deserved. The Wellington became: 'Dry-aged Hereford beef tenderloin from Hartley Farm, wrapped in house-made mushroom and tarragon duxelles and golden puff pastry, slow-roasted and rested, with charred tenderstem and a ten-hour Madeira jus.' The halibut became: 'Cornish dayboat halibut, line-caught and landed yesterday, pan-roasted to a golden crust, with a salad of Isle of Wight tomatoes, dill oil, and a cold-pressed lemon butter.'

In the first month following the rewrite, average spend per cover at dinner increased by 9%. The Wellington became the most ordered main course. The halibut became the most ordered fish dish. The menu itself — the food, the prices, the chef, the kitchen — had not changed. Only the words had changed.

The lesson applies beyond the restaurant context to every piece of hospitality writing: if your writing does not make people want what you are selling, it is not doing its job — regardless of how good the product is.
