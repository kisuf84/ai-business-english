Treatment
A specific therapy booked individually at the hotel spa — such as a massage, facial, body wrap, or hydrotherapy session. Each treatment has a specified duration, price, and description highlighting therapeutic benefits. Staff should use benefit-focused language and make personalised recommendations based on the guest's needs.

Spa Package
A pre-designed combination of spa treatments, dining, and sometimes accommodation at an inclusive price — typically saving money compared to booking elements individually. Formats include half-day (two to three treatments with lunch), full-day (three to four treatments with lunch and afternoon tea), and residential packages. Popular for birthdays and anniversaries. Staff should emphasise value, the complete experience, and occasion-specific enhancements.

A La Carte
A dining format in which each dish is individually priced and guests choose and pay for their selections separately — as opposed to a fixed-price set menu. A la carte gives guests the greatest flexibility: one, two, or three courses, paying only for what they choose. It contrasts with table d'hote (fixed-price), tasting menu (chef-designed multiple courses at a fixed price), and prix fixe (fixed price for a set number of courses). Staff should indicate approximately what a typical meal costs.

Sommelier
A trained wine professional responsible for the hotel restaurant's wine programme — advising on selection, recommending food and wine pairings, managing the cellar, and serving wine at table. The sommelier translates preferences (dry or sweet, light or full-bodied, budget) into a specific recommendation. Front desk and concierge staff should be able to briefly describe the wine programme and direct guests to the sommelier for specialist advice.

Leisure Facilities
The physical amenities available to guests for exercise, relaxation, and recreation — typically including an indoor pool, gym, sauna, steam room, Jacuzzi, and outdoor spaces. Among the most frequently enquired-about hotel amenities. Key information guests want: opening hours; towel availability; pool temperature and depth; whether children are permitted; and whether advance booking is required for specific facilities.

Afternoon Tea
A formal British hospitality tradition — finger sandwiches, scones with clotted cream and jam, and miniature pastries served with fine teas between 2pm and 5pm. Hotels offer enhanced versions including champagne afternoon tea, seasonal themed teas, and dietary-adapted versions. When describing to international guests, staff should explain what is included, the price, and that a reservation is usually required.

Butler Service
A personalised service from a dedicated team member managing all of the guest's needs throughout their stay — including unpacking luggage, pressing clothes, drawing baths, in-room dining service, managing requests, and anticipating personal preferences. Butler service transforms the guest experience from transactional to personal. Describing it requires a tone communicating personal attentiveness without formality or intimidation.

Bar Programme
The totality of a hotel bar's offering — the cocktail menu, spirits and wine selection, beer range, non-alcoholic options, snack menu, and any themed events. A well-described bar encourages guests to spend their evening at the hotel rather than going out. Key elements to communicate: the bar's speciality; signature cocktails; hours of service; food available; and the atmosphere.

Wellness
A broad concept encompassing physical, mental, and emotional health — and in hospitality, a growing category of services supporting guests' holistic wellbeing. Wellness services range from spa treatments and gym access to yoga classes, meditation, guided breathwork, nutrition consultations, sleep programmes, and nature-based activities. Hotels leading in wellness describe their offering as a philosophy and experience — communicating how the guest will feel rather than listing what is available.

Fitness Centre
The hotel's dedicated exercise facility — equipped with cardiovascular machines (treadmills, bikes, ellipticals), resistance machines, free weights, and stretching areas. Many hotel fitness centres also offer personal training and group classes. Business travellers are frequent users. Key information guests want: opening hours (24-hour access is a significant selling point); equipment range and quality; whether towels and water are provided; and whether classes or personal training are available.

Valet Parking
A hotel service in which a team member takes the guest's vehicle at the entrance, parks it, and returns it on request — removing the inconvenience of finding a car park, especially valuable in city-centre hotels. Hotels charge a daily rate higher than self-parking; the premium reflects the convenience. Key information: the daily rate; how to request the vehicle; typical retrieval time; and service hours.

Complimentary
Provided to the guest at no additional charge — included in the room rate or offered as a gesture of goodwill. Complimentary amenities are a key differentiator: guests who feel they receive unexpected value review more positively and return. Standard complimentary services at a four-star hotel typically include: high-speed Wi-Fi; use of the gym and pool; welcome fruit in the room; and access to the business centre.

Laundry Service
A hotel service through which guests can have clothing laundered, dry-cleaned, ironed, or pressed during their stay. Most full-service hotels offer same-day service (collected by 9am, returned by 6pm) and an express option. Particularly important for business guests with meetings. Key information: collection and return times; services available; pricing per item; and express service surcharge.

Business Centre
A dedicated space supporting guests with work-related needs — typically including computers, printing and scanning, secure Wi-Fi, and sometimes private meeting pods. Modern hotel business centres focus on high-speed Wi-Fi throughout the property, in-room desk conditions, and printing access rather than shared terminals. When describing, staff should focus on what is most relevant to the specific guest's needs.

A private lounge exclusively for guests in club-level rooms or suites — offering complimentary breakfast, afternoon snacks, evening cocktails and canapes, all-day tea and coffee, daily newspapers, and personalised service. When communicating club lounge benefits to newly upgraded guests, staff should mention service times, what is included, and how the guest accesses the lounge.

In-Room Technology
The digital and electronic amenities available in a hotel room — including smart TVs with streaming, digital room controls (lighting, temperature, blinds), in-room tablets for ordering services, charging pads, USB ports, high-speed Wi-Fi, and Bluetooth speakers. When describing to a less tech-savvy guest, focus on the benefit, offer to demonstrate, and mention that reception is available 24 hours for any technical questions.

Recreation Facilities
The broader range of sporting and entertainment facilities beyond the standard gym and pool — which may include tennis courts, golf, cycling routes, guided walks, a games room, or a cinema. Recreation varies widely by hotel type: a countryside resort might offer fishing, horse riding, and archery; a city hotel might offer jogging maps and nearby sports partnerships. Ask about the guest's interests before listing everything — a personalised suggestion is far more effective.

A spa treatment unique to the hotel — developed specifically for the property, typically incorporating local ingredients, cultural traditions, or the expertise of the spa's lead therapist. Signature treatments are a powerful differentiator: they cannot be replicated by competitors and tell a story about the hotel's identity. When describing, staff should emphasise what makes it unique, the sensory experience, and why it is particularly appropriate for the individual guest.

Room Service
A hotel service allowing guests to order food and beverages delivered directly to their room — from a dedicated menu that may be a subset of the restaurant menu or a purpose-designed selection. Important for guests wanting privacy, those with unusual schedules, or those who prefer the comfort of their room. Key information guests want: hours; full or limited menu; how to order; delivery time; any service charge; and minimum order value.

Events and Entertainment
Programmed activities, performances, and special occasions organised by the hotel — including live music, themed dining, wine tastings, cinema nights, and seasonal celebrations. A strong events programme distinguishes hotels that actively invest in guest experience. Staff should highlight events happening during the guest's specific stay, make a personal recommendation, and offer to add the guest's name to a reservation list where booking is required.

Hydrotherapy
Water-based spa treatments and facilities using the therapeutic properties of water — temperature, pressure, and mineral content — to promote relaxation, improve circulation, ease muscle tension, and support wellbeing. Hotel spa hydrotherapy facilities typically include a heated pool, Jacuzzi, cold plunge pool, hydrotherapy pool with jets, and thermal suite (sauna and steam room). When describing, staff should reassure guests that no experience is necessary and guide them through the typical sequence of facilities.

Text 1: The Hotel Spa — How to Describe Wellness Facilities with Professional Confidence
The language and techniques a hospitality professional needs to describe spa services engagingly

The hotel spa has become one of the most commercially important amenities in the modern full-service hotel. Spa and wellness consistently appear in the top three factors influencing leisure hotel selection — alongside room quality and location. Yet many hotel staff find it difficult to describe the spa convincingly. They either recite a generic list ('we have a pool, sauna, and steam room') or redirect all enquiries to the spa reception. Both represent a missed opportunity: every spa enquiry is a chance to generate a booking and demonstrate the hotel's commitment to a holistic experience.

Describing a spa effectively requires three things: knowledge, language, and personalisation. Knowledge means understanding what is offered — the treatments, packages, opening hours, and booking procedure. Language means using sensory, benefit-focused vocabulary: not 'we have a hot stone massage' but 'the hot stone is our most popular treatment — guests tell us it is deeply releasing for the back and shoulders, and most people fall asleep on the table'. Personalisation means tailoring the description to the specific guest: a solo business traveller after a stressful day needs to hear something different from a couple celebrating an anniversary.

The most effective spa descriptions follow a simple structure: open with an evocative atmosphere sentence; highlight two or three distinctive offerings rather than listing everything; mention key practical details; and close with a personalised suggestion or offer to book. This structure — atmosphere, highlights, practicalities, personalised close — works consistently across all spa and wellness descriptions.

Text 2: Hotel Food and Beverage — Presenting Dining Options with Expertise and Enthusiasm
How to describe a hotel's F&B offering in a way that creates appetite and generates revenue

Food and beverage is the largest revenue department in most full-service hotels after rooms — and the one where verbal description most directly drives guest spending. A guest told 'the restaurant is just through there' will make a different spending decision from one told: 'Our restaurant is led by head chef Marcus Osei — he works with local suppliers and changes the menu seasonally. Tonight's centrepiece is a slow-cooked Hereford beef short rib. The bar team does excellent cocktails — the signature Old Fashioned uses locally distilled whisky. Would you like me to reserve a table?' The second description creates appetite, communicates quality, and ends with a booking action.

Describing the hotel's F&B confidently requires knowing four things: the concept (what kind of food, what atmosphere, who it is for); the provenance (where key ingredients come from); the current highlights (what the chef is excited about this week); and the practical details (opening times, reservation requirement, price range). A guest who receives a response referencing specific dishes and the chef's approach will feel they have been given insider information — a powerful ingredient in the guest experience.

Beyond the main restaurant, most full-service hotels have F&B options guests may not think to ask about: the bar (with food); room service; afternoon tea (a premium bookable experience with cultural resonance for international visitors); private dining; and seasonal events. Staff who know all these options and recommend them in context — matching the offering to the guest's occasion, group size, and preferences — are far more effective revenue generators than those who know only the main restaurant.

Exercise 1 — Grammar 1: Describing Facilities — Sensory Language and Benefit-Focused Vocabulary

Effective facility descriptions use sensory language, atmosphere words, and benefit-focused vocabulary — focusing on what the guest gains rather than just what the facility is.

Atmosphere: 'The spa is a genuinely peaceful space — the moment you walk in, you feel completely removed from the outside world.'

Benefit not feature:

  • NOT 'We have a hot stone massage' → BUT 'The hot stone is deeply releasing — most guests fall asleep.'
  • NOT 'The gym is on the second floor' → BUT 'The fitness centre is open 24 hours — so you can train at 6am before meetings.'

Exercise 3 — Grammar 3: Explaining Inclusions, Costs, and Practical Details — Clarity Without Confusion

Positive inclusion:

Case Study 1: The Description That Filled the Restaurant
How one hotel transformed restaurant occupancy by investing in frontline F&B description skills
This case examines a four-star countryside hotel whose restaurant — with an excellent chef and strong menu — was consistently under-utilised by hotel guests who chose to drive to a nearby market town for dinner. Exit surveys revealed the problem: guests did not know what made the restaurant special. The most common responses were 'I did not know what kind of food it was' and 'nobody really told me about it.'

Management identified a clear root cause: front desk staff were not equipped to describe the restaurant in a way that created appetite. When guests asked 'what is the restaurant like?', responses were 'it is very nice — they do a varied menu' or 'it is just to the left.' Neither gave guests a reason to stay in.

The hotel invested in a structured F&B description training programme, giving every front desk and concierge team member a weekly updated card with: the chef's key ingredient of the week; two or three specific dishes the chef was proud of; and a sentence describing the atmosphere. The team was also trained to end every description with: 'Would you like me to reserve a table?'

Within eight weeks, in-hotel restaurant covers from hotel guests increased by 34%. The training cost was recovered within the first fortnight. The lesson: restaurant revenue is not just a function of food quality — it is equally a function of the quality of description given to guests before they decide where to eat.

Case Study 2: The Spa That Spoke for Itself
How improving front-of-house spa communication doubled treatment bookings without increasing marketing spend
A four-star city hotel had a well-equipped spa generating revenue well below its potential — with 12 treatment rooms, a full thermal suite, and 28 treatments, but utilisation running at approximately 40% weekdays and 65% weekends. A mystery guest audit eventually revealed the problem: the spa was almost invisible to hotel guests.

When the auditor asked three separate front desk team members about the spa, responses were: 'it is through there on the lower ground floor'; 'I think it is quite nice — I have not been myself'; and 'you can pick up a spa menu at the desk.' None mentioned a treatment, described the atmosphere, personalised their response, or offered to book. The spa might as well not have existed.

The intervention was simple. The spa manager ran a 90-minute workshop for all front desk and concierge staff — taking them through the spa physically, having each experience the thermal suite and a 15-minute taster treatment, and training them in a four-step framework: atmosphere, signature treatment, practical details, booking offer. Each team member was given a personal favourite treatment they had experienced and could describe authentically.

Within six months, weekly treatment bookings had doubled. Weekend utilisation rose from 65% to 94%. Annual revenue increase exceeded £180,000. Cost: therapist time for tasters and one afternoon of the manager's time. The principle: people describe best what they know. The moment front desk staff knew the spa from personal experience, they talked about it differently — and guests booked.
