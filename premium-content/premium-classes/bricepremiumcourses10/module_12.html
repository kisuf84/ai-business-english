Fire Evacuation
The controlled movement of guests, staff, and visitors from a building or area when a fire alarm sounds or a fire is confirmed — following a pre-established procedure that directs everyone to designated muster points via defined escape routes. A professional fire evacuation in a hotel context requires: the front desk or duty manager to confirm the alarm and initiate evacuation; staff to immediately begin directing guests out of rooms, corridors, and public areas toward the nearest fire exit; lifts to be taken out of use (they are not to be used during fire evacuations as the lift shaft can act as a chimney); the fire doors to be closed (not locked) to prevent the spread of smoke and fire; the muster point to be communicated to all guests and staff clearly; a roll call or sweep check to confirm that all guests have evacuated; and the fire register (a daily record of which rooms are occupied) to be used to verify that no one has been left in the building. The hotel's duty manager is typically responsible for coordinating the evacuation and meeting the fire service on arrival.

Duty of Care
The legal and ethical obligation of a hospitality business to take reasonable steps to protect the safety, health, and wellbeing of all guests, staff, contractors, and visitors on its premises. In UK law, the duty of care in hospitality is governed primarily by the Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 (for employees) and the Occupiers' Liability Acts 1957 and 1984 (for guests and visitors). In practice, the duty of care requires: maintaining the premises in a reasonably safe condition; identifying and mitigating foreseeable risks through regular risk assessments; providing adequate training for all staff in health and safety procedures; having appropriate emergency procedures in place and regularly practised; and responding promptly and effectively when an incident occurs. Failure to discharge the duty of care can result in civil liability for personal injury claims, enforcement action by the Health and Safety Executive, and criminal prosecution in serious cases.

Incident Report
A formal written record documenting any accident, injury, near miss, or safety incident that occurs on the hotel's premises — capturing the facts of what happened, who was involved, where and when it occurred, what immediate action was taken, and what follow-up is planned. Incident reports are a legal requirement under UK health and safety legislation: the Reporting of Injuries, Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences Regulations 2013 (RIDDOR) require hotels to report certain categories of workplace injury to the Health and Safety Executive. Beyond legal compliance, incident reports serve an operational learning function: a hotel that analyses its incident reports regularly can identify patterns of recurring risk (a specific staircase that generates multiple slip incidents, a storage procedure that causes back injuries) and take corrective action before a more serious incident occurs. Incident reports must be completed as soon as possible after the event while memory is fresh, and must be accurate, factual, and objective — not speculative or apologetic.

Muster Point
A designated, pre-identified location outside a building to which all guests, staff, and visitors should proceed during an evacuation — clearly marked on fire exit plans, communicated to guests at check-in (or on the information card in the room), and briefed to all staff as part of their induction. Muster points must be: at a safe distance from the building (to prevent obstruction of emergency services and to remove evacuees from risk of falling debris, smoke, or fire spread); accessible to guests with mobility impairments; clearly marked with appropriate signage; and known to all team members so they can direct guests confidently and without hesitation. Communicating the muster point clearly during an emergency — 'Please proceed immediately to the car park at the front of the hotel — that is our muster point' — is one of the most important and most frequently practised elements of hotel emergency communication.

💊
First Aid
Immediate, basic care given to a person who has been injured or has experienced a sudden medical episode — before the arrival of professional medical assistance. In a hotel context, first aid may be required for a wide range of situations: a guest who has slipped and fallen; a guest experiencing chest pain or a suspected heart attack; a guest who is choking; a child who has been injured in the pool area; a staff member who has sustained a kitchen burn. UK law requires hotels to have adequate first aid provision for both staff and guests — including trained first aiders, a stocked first aid kit, and clearly communicated procedures for obtaining first aid assistance. First aiders in a hotel should be trained in CPR and the use of an AED, the management of bleeding wounds, the recovery position, basic fracture management, and the recognition and initial management of anaphylaxis. All staff — not only trained first aiders — should know who the first aiders are, where the first aid kit is, and how to call for emergency services.

Risk Assessment
A systematic process of identifying hazards in the workplace or in a specific activity, evaluating the likelihood and potential severity of harm, and implementing control measures to reduce or eliminate the risk. In the hospitality sector, risk assessments are legally required for all work activities that could harm employees (under the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999) and are also best practice for activities that could harm guests. Common hospitality risk assessments include: slips, trips, and falls (particularly in wet or polished floor areas, on staircases, and in swimming pool areas); manual handling (kitchen, housekeeping, and maintenance operations); food safety and allergen management; fire risk; swimming pool safety; and external grounds maintenance. Risk assessments must be recorded in writing (for organisations with five or more employees), reviewed regularly, and updated whenever significant changes occur in the workplace. The responsibility for risk assessment typically sits with the health and safety manager, department managers, and the employer.

RIDDOR
Reporting of Injuries, Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences Regulations 2013 — UK legislation that requires employers to report certain workplace injuries, occupational diseases, and dangerous occurrences to the Health and Safety Executive (HSE) within defined timeframes. In a hospitality context, RIDDOR reportable incidents include: any work-related death; any specified injury to a worker (including fractures other than to fingers, thumbs, and toes; amputations; injuries leading to loss of consciousness; injuries requiring resuscitation; and others); any over-7-day incapacitating injury to a worker; any non-fatal injuries to non-workers (guests or visitors) that require them to be taken from the site to hospital; and dangerous occurrences that could have caused death or serious injury. RIDDOR reports must be submitted online to the HSE using the appropriate form and within the specified timeframe (immediately for deaths and specified injuries; within 10 days for over-7-day injuries). Failure to report is a criminal offence.

Lockdown
A security procedure initiated when there is a credible threat to the safety of people in a building — requiring all persons to remain in a secure location within the building, all external entry points to be secured, and normal operations to be suspended until the threat has been assessed and resolved by the appropriate authorities. In a hotel context, lockdown may be required in response to: a violent or threatening individual on or approaching the premises; a security alert or police operation in the surrounding area; a bomb threat; or a major civil disturbance. The lockdown procedure should specify: who has the authority to initiate a lockdown; how guests and staff are to be notified and directed to safe areas; how the hotel's external doors are to be secured; how communication with emergency services is to be maintained during the lockdown; and how the lockdown is to be lifted and guests and staff safely released. A hotel that has not practised its lockdown procedure before it is needed will find it significantly more difficult to execute effectively under the pressure of an actual emergency.

AED
Automated External Defibrillator — a portable electronic device that can analyse a person's heart rhythm and, if required, deliver an electric shock (defibrillation) to restore normal cardiac rhythm in a person experiencing a cardiac arrest. AEDs are designed to be used by anyone — including people with no medical training — and provide step-by-step audio and visual instructions throughout the process. UK guidelines recommend that AEDs should be accessible within three minutes of a cardiac arrest — making them an important life-saving resource in hotels, conference centres, leisure facilities, and other hospitality venues where large numbers of people gather. When a guest collapses and is unresponsive: call 999 immediately; begin CPR if trained; send someone to retrieve the AED; and use it as soon as it arrives, following its instructions. The survival rate from cardiac arrest increases significantly with every minute of delay — access to an AED on site and a team member who knows where to find it can be the difference between life and death.

Fire Marshal
A trained member of staff with specific responsibilities for fire safety in a defined area of the hotel — typically responsible for sweeping their designated area during a fire evacuation to ensure all persons have evacuated, directing guests and colleagues to the nearest fire exit, checking that fire doors are closed (not locked), and reporting to the assembly officer at the muster point with a confirmation that their area is clear. Fire marshals are a legal requirement in UK workplaces under the Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005 — hotels must appoint a sufficient number of trained fire marshals to cover all areas of the building at all times, including during busy event periods when additional guests are present. Fire marshal training typically covers: the causes and spread of fire; the hotel's fire evacuation procedure; the location and use of fire extinguishers; the location of all fire exits and muster points; and the specific sweep route for their designated area. Fire marshals should wear hi-visibility vests during an evacuation to make them identifiable to guests and emergency services.

🚑
Emergency Services
The public services responsible for responding to emergencies — in the UK, comprising the police (999 for emergency, 101 for non-emergency), the fire service (999), and the ambulance service (999 or 112). In a hospitality emergency, calling emergency services promptly and providing clear, accurate information is one of the most critical actions a hospitality professional can take. Key information to provide when calling 999: the nature of the emergency (fire, medical emergency, security incident, chemical spill); the exact address and, where relevant, the specific location within the building (room number, floor, area of the hotel); the number of people involved and any specific information about their condition; the name and contact number of the person calling; and whether the hotel has an AED on site (if the emergency is cardiac-related). When emergency services arrive, the duty manager or designated liaison should meet them at the hotel entrance, provide any relevant information about the building (access routes, floor plans, location of the incident), and assist them as required.

Near Miss
An incident that had the potential to cause harm but did not — either because no one happened to be in the wrong place at the wrong time, or because someone noticed and intervened before an injury occurred. Common hospitality near misses include: a wet floor without a warning sign that a guest nearly slipped on; a heavy storage shelf that nearly fell; a frayed electrical cable that nearly caused a shock; or a glass that shattered in a kitchen without injuring anyone. Near-miss reporting is as important as incident reporting — and in some ways more so — because a near miss is an early warning signal of a hazard that, if not addressed, may cause a serious injury in the future. UK health and safety law encourages near-miss reporting as part of a positive safety culture; organisations that cultivate a culture of near-miss reporting consistently have lower rates of serious injuries than those that do not. Hotels should make near-miss reporting easy, anonymous if necessary, and clearly connected to visible corrective action — so that staff believe their reports make a difference.

COSHH
Control of Substances Hazardous to Health — UK regulations (COSHH Regulations 2002) that require employers to identify substances used in the workplace that could be hazardous to health, assess the risks they pose, implement appropriate control measures, and ensure that employees are trained in the safe handling and storage of hazardous substances. In a hotel context, COSHH is particularly relevant for: housekeeping teams using cleaning chemicals (bleach, descaling agents, disinfectants); kitchen teams using industrial cleaning and food preparation chemicals; maintenance teams using paints, solvents, and industrial lubricants; and laundry operations using detergents and fabric treatment chemicals. COSHH control measures typically include: providing appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE) such as gloves, goggles, and protective clothing; ensuring adequate ventilation; storing chemicals in clearly labelled containers; providing data sheets (COSHH assessments) for each substance; training staff in the safe use, storage, and disposal of each chemical; and knowing what to do in case of accidental exposure, spillage, or ingestion.

PEEP
Personal Emergency Evacuation Plan — an individually designed plan for a guest or staff member who may need assistance to evacuate the building in an emergency due to a mobility impairment, sensory impairment, cognitive condition, or any other reason that means they cannot use the standard evacuation route independently. PEEPs are a legal requirement in UK workplaces under the Equality Act 2010 and the Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005. In a hotel context, PEEPs should be proactively offered to: guests with mobility impairments or who use wheelchairs; guests with visual impairments; guests with hearing impairments who may not hear a fire alarm; and guests who have disclosed any relevant health condition at check-in. A PEEP specifies: the guest's name and room number; the specific evacuation route to be used (which may avoid stairs); the name of the team member responsible for assisting the guest; the location of any 'refuge areas' where the guest can wait safely if immediate evacuation is not possible; and the communication plan for informing the fire service of any guests awaiting assistance.

🌊
Pool Safety
The procedures, equipment, and supervision arrangements that a hotel must maintain to ensure the safety of guests using its swimming pool facilities — a specific area of hospitality risk management governed by multiple legal standards and industry guidelines. UK law requires hotel pools to: be supervised by a qualified lifeguard (NPLQ-certified) during all hours of public use, or to implement an effective alternative supervision arrangement where a lifeguard is not present; display clear rules (no diving, no running, maximum capacity, children under a specified age must be accompanied by an adult); have appropriate depth markers and a safety rope separating the shallow and deep ends; have emergency equipment accessible at poolside (reaching pole, throw ring, first aid kit, AED); and conduct regular water quality testing. Communicating pool safety rules to guests — particularly in a multi-language environment — requires clear, unambiguous signage and, ideally, a verbal briefing by the poolside attendant or the check-in team for guests with young children.

Personal Protective Equipment
Protective clothing, equipment, or devices worn or used by staff to protect against identified health and safety risks in the workplace — abbreviated as PPE. Under the Personal Protective Equipment at Work Regulations 2022, employers are required to provide PPE free of charge where a risk assessment identifies that it is necessary for a work activity. In a hotel context, PPE is relevant across multiple departments: kitchen staff should wear appropriate footwear (slip-resistant, closed-toe), oven gloves, and cut-resistant gloves when handling sharp equipment; housekeeping staff should use chemical-resistant gloves, eye protection, and appropriate footwear when using cleaning chemicals; maintenance staff may require safety helmets, harnesses, hi-visibility clothing, and eye protection depending on the task; pool attendants should have access to appropriate floatation and rescue equipment. PPE is the last resort in the hierarchy of risk controls — risks should first be eliminated or reduced at source, with PPE providing additional protection where residual risk remains.

Safety Inspection
A regular, systematic check of the hotel's premises, equipment, and procedures to identify hazards, assess whether existing safety controls are effective, and ensure that legal compliance standards are being maintained. Safety inspections in a hotel context typically cover: fire exits (unobstructed, properly marked, doors operational); first aid equipment (kits stocked and in date); AED (battery charged and pads in date); wet floor signs (available and in use where needed); pool area (equipment present, depth markers visible, rules displayed); food storage (temperatures correct, date labels present); COSHH chemicals (correctly stored and labelled); and general housekeeping (trip hazards removed, equipment stored safely). Safety inspections should be conducted on a defined schedule (daily checks for critical items such as fire exits, weekly checks for a wider range of items, monthly or quarterly for more detailed reviews), recorded in writing, and any identified deficiencies reported and resolved promptly. The inspection record forms part of the hotel's legal compliance documentation.

Evacuation Communication
The language, tone, and method used to direct guests and staff toward the correct exits during a building evacuation — requiring a combination of clear verbal instruction, physical direction, and reassurance. Professional evacuation communication in a hotel context is: calm (a panicked instruction increases guest anxiety and reduces compliance); clear (specific, unambiguous directions — 'Please leave your room now and use the staircase at the end of the corridor to your left to go down to the ground floor and out through the main entrance'); authoritative (guests need to believe that the person directing them knows what they are doing and that they should follow the instruction without hesitation); and inclusive (identifying and assisting any guests who appear confused, distressed, or physically unable to evacuate independently). Evacuation communication must be practised: a team member who has only read the fire procedure and never spoken the evacuation instruction aloud will perform significantly less effectively in a real emergency than one who has drilled it.

Allergen Emergency
A medical emergency arising from a severe allergic reaction — most commonly anaphylaxis — triggered by contact with or ingestion of a substance to which a guest is allergic. Anaphylaxis is a life-threatening allergic reaction that requires immediate treatment with an epinephrine (adrenaline) auto-injector (such as an EpiPen) and emergency medical assistance. Symptoms of anaphylaxis include: swelling of the throat, tongue, or lips; severe difficulty breathing; a rapid fall in blood pressure; loss of consciousness; severe skin reactions (hives, swelling, widespread redness); and vomiting or diarrhoea. The hospitality professional's response to a suspected anaphylaxis: call 999 immediately — do not wait to see if symptoms improve; ask the guest if they have an EpiPen and assist them to use it if they do; lay the person flat and raise their legs (unless they are having breathing difficulties, in which case keep them upright); do not give food or drink; stay with the guest until the ambulance arrives; and inform the kitchen immediately of the specific allergen so that no further exposure can occur.

Refuge Area
A designated safe space within a building — typically a fire-protected lobby, a corridor with fire-resistant walls and a self-closing fire door, or a stairwell landing — where a person who cannot use the standard evacuation route can wait safely during an evacuation until they can be assisted by the fire service or another evacuee. Refuge areas are a critical element of evacuation planning for guests with mobility impairments: if a hotel's fire evacuation plan assumes that all guests can use the staircase independently, it is not compliant with the duty of care for guests who cannot. Refuge areas should be: clearly marked with appropriate signage; equipped with a two-way communication system (an intercom or phone) so the person can alert the fire service to their location; included in the hotel's fire register so that the fire service knows to check them; and specified in any relevant guest PEEP. A member of the fire marshal team should be responsible for checking all refuge areas during an evacuation and reporting any persons present to the fire service on arrival.

Legionella
A type of bacteria that can grow in water systems — particularly in water stored or distributed at temperatures between 20°C and 45°C, in stagnant or slow-moving water, and in the presence of nutrients such as rust, sludge, or other organic matter. Legionella bacteria cause Legionnaires' disease, a serious and potentially fatal form of pneumonia contracted by inhaling contaminated water droplets. In a hotel context, Legionella is a significant health and safety risk in: hot and cold water systems; cooling towers (if present); spa pools and whirlpools; swimming pool systems; and any water features that generate aerosols. UK law requires hotels to conduct a Legionella risk assessment, implement a water safety management plan (typically including regular water temperature monitoring, flushing of infrequently used outlets, and periodic cleaning and disinfection of the water system), and maintain records of all monitoring and maintenance activities. Hotels that fail to manage their Legionella risk have faced prosecutions following outbreaks that have resulted in guest illness or death.

Text 1: Legal Duties and Safety Obligations — What the Law Requires of Hospitality Businesses
The principal UK health and safety legislation relevant to hotels and hospitality operations

The hospitality sector operates within a comprehensive framework of UK health and safety legislation — and the professionals who work in it are legally expected to understand, not merely comply with, the requirements that apply to them. The Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 is the foundational legislation: it places a duty on all employers to ensure, so far as is reasonably practicable, the health, safety, and welfare of all employees at work; and a duty on employees to take reasonable care of their own health and safety and that of others who may be affected by their actions. The phrase 'so far as is reasonably practicable' is significant: it does not require employers to eliminate all conceivable risk — an impossible standard — but to take all reasonable steps whose cost and effort are proportionate to the risk involved. A hotel that knows its kitchen floor becomes dangerously slippery when wet and takes no preventive action has not met this standard; a hotel that provides anti-slip matting, requires appropriate footwear, and posts wet floor signs has taken reasonable steps.

In addition to the general duty under the Health and Safety at Work Act, hospitality businesses are subject to a range of specific regulations covering particular risk areas. The Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 require employers to conduct risk assessments for all work activities with the potential to cause harm and to implement appropriate controls. The Reporting of Injuries, Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences Regulations 2013 (RIDDOR) require employers to report specified categories of workplace injury to the Health and Safety Executive within defined timeframes. The Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 (COSHH) govern the safe management of hazardous chemicals in the workplace. The Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005 requires hotels to conduct a fire risk assessment, implement appropriate fire safety measures, and appoint a responsible person for fire safety. And the Occupiers' Liability Acts 1957 and 1984 govern the duty owed to visitors and trespassers on the hotel's premises.

The consequences of failing to meet legal safety obligations in hospitality are significant. Civil liability for personal injury claims can result in compensation awards of hundreds of thousands of pounds — the costs of defending and losing a claim often far exceeding the cost of the preventive measure that would have eliminated the risk. Enforcement action by the Health and Safety Executive can result in improvement notices, prohibition notices that close parts of the business, and unlimited fines following criminal prosecution. In the most serious cases — where a death has occurred and the failure to meet legal obligations was egregious — individuals (including directors and managers, not just the corporate employer) can face prosecution for gross negligence manslaughter. The investment in a rigorous health and safety culture in a hospitality business is not optional — it is commercially necessary as well as legally required.

Text 2: Emergency Communication in Hospitality — Speaking Calmly, Clearly, and Authoritatively Under Pressure
The principles and language of effective emergency communication with guests, staff, and emergency services

The moment a fire alarm sounds, a guest collapses, or a security alert is received, a hotel professional's ability to communicate — clearly, calmly, and with authority — becomes a life-safety skill as important as any physical first aid technique. Research on human behaviour in emergencies consistently finds that people in unfamiliar environments look to figures of apparent authority for direction: a guest who hears a fire alarm in a hotel they have never visited before does not know whether it is a real fire, a test, or a false alarm. They do not know the layout of the building, the location of the fire exits, or the distance to the muster point. They will look, instinctively, for someone who appears to know what to do — and the front desk agent, the housekeeper, or the restaurant server who projects calm authority in that moment will be followed. The one who appears uncertain or panicked will not.

Effective emergency communication shares three qualities. The first is calmness: a calm voice, even under extreme pressure, signals competence and reduces the panic that is itself one of the most dangerous elements of any emergency. Calmness is not the absence of feeling — it is the discipline of choosing not to communicate anxiety in a moment when the people around you need to borrow confidence they do not have. The second quality is clarity: in an emergency, communication must be stripped of ambiguity, qualification, and politeness conventions that would normally soften an instruction. 'Could I perhaps suggest that you might want to think about using the exit?' is not an evacuation instruction — 'Please leave your room now and follow me to the exit' is. The third quality is specificity: guests cannot follow vague instructions in an unfamiliar building — 'This way' is less useful than 'Turn left out of your room and use the staircase directly ahead of you — do not use the lift.'

When communicating with emergency services, the same principles apply but the context is different. Emergency dispatchers are trained professionals who will ask specific questions in a specific order — the caller's most important job is to answer them accurately and completely. The hotel's address and postcode should be available to all staff from their first day — a caller who cannot provide a precise address under pressure may significantly delay emergency response. The nature of the emergency should be stated first: 'We have a fire in the hotel kitchen' or 'A guest has collapsed and is unresponsive' communicates the most critical information before the dispatcher begins their structured questioning. After calling emergency services, the duty manager should meet the emergency services at the hotel entrance, have the hotel floor plan available, and be prepared to provide access and information throughout the response. A hotel that prepares its team for these conversations — through training, role play, and regular review — will communicate far more effectively in a real emergency than one that has never practised.

Exercise 1 — Grammar 1: Emergency Communication — Giving Clear, Calm, and Authoritative Instructions

Effective emergency communication is calm (signals competence), clear (no ambiguity or unnecessary politeness conventions), and specific (gives guests actionable, directional information in an unfamiliar building).

Evacuation instructions — the key principles:

  • State what guests must do, not what they might want to consider doing
  • Give the specific route: 'Turn left, staircase on the right, do not use the lift'
  • Include the reason: 'This is not a drill — there is a confirmed fire'
  • Name the muster point: 'Proceed to the car park at the front of the hotel'

Compare — ineffective vs effective:

  • INEFFECTIVE: 'Could I perhaps ask you to begin making your way towards the exit?'
  • EFFECTIVE: 'Please leave your room now and follow me to the fire exit.'

For guests who need special assistance:

  • 'There's a refuge area right here — please wait and I will radio the fire team. They will come to you. You will be safe here.'

Case Study 1: The Evacuation That Saved Lives — How Training and Communication Made the Difference
How a well-trained hotel team's calm and professional evacuation prevented casualties in a serious kitchen fire
This case study is drawn from a representative composite of hotel fire incident scenarios documented in UK fire service and hospitality safety literature. It illustrates how systematic safety training and clear emergency communication can prevent a serious incident from becoming a fatal one.

A 120-room city-centre hotel experienced a serious kitchen fire at 11.30pm on a Thursday evening when the hotel was at 87% occupancy. The fire started in the kitchen's deep fat fryer when a member of the kitchen team left a fryer unattended during a busy dinner service rush. The fryer overheated and ignited. The kitchen team activated the manual call point immediately and began evacuating the kitchen before the alarm reached the front desk.

The duty manager received the alarm signal at 11.32pm. She confirmed with the kitchen that the alarm was real, activated the evacuation procedure, made a public address announcement, and called 999 simultaneously — the address and postcode were pinned to the back of the front desk telephone because the duty manager had insisted on this practice. Within four minutes, the front desk team had made two PA announcements, posted fire marshals to all three guest bedroom floors, and begun checking rooms.

The fire marshals — all trained within the previous six months — swept their floors systematically, knocking on every door and directing guests to the fire exits. Two guests were identified as having mobility impairments: both had PEEPs in place and were directed to the nearest refuge areas and communicated to the fire service on arrival. One hundred and four guests were at the muster point within seven minutes of the alarm being activated. The fire service arrived four minutes later and confirmed that the building was fully evacuated.

The kitchen sustained significant damage, but there were no injuries. The fire service incident commander specifically noted the speed of the evacuation, the quality of the muster point register (which confirmed the building was clear), and the immediate and accurate information provided to the fire service on arrival.

The lesson: the evacuation succeeded not because of any individual heroism but because of systematic preparation — regular training, a clear procedure, a PIN-held address, pre-prepared PEEPs for vulnerable guests, and a team that had practised what to do before the emergency arrived.

Case Study 2: The Incident That Should Have Been Prevented — How Near-Miss Reporting Failure Led to a Serious Injury
How the absence of a near-miss reporting culture allowed a preventable hazard to injure a guest
This case study documents a composite scenario representative of slip, trip, and fall incidents commonly reported in UK hospitality health and safety investigations. It illustrates the commercial, legal, and human consequences of a failure to act on near-miss information.

A four-star hotel in a UK coastal town had been experiencing periodic reports from housekeeping staff about a specific section of polished corridor on the second floor near the lift lobby. After heavy rainfall, this section of floor became noticeably damp — the result of a small gap in the window seal above it that allowed moisture to enter. Housekeeping had mentioned this informally to two different supervisors on three separate occasions over a six-month period. No formal near-miss reports had been filed. No corrective action had been taken.

In October, following a period of sustained heavy rain, a guest slipped on the damp corridor and sustained a fractured hip. She required emergency surgery, was hospitalised for six days, and was unable to work for fourteen weeks. The incident was investigated by the hotel, the guest's solicitors, and the Health and Safety Executive.

The investigation revealed: the gap in the window seal had been known about for at least six months; housekeeping staff had reported it informally on three occasions; no near-miss report had been filed and no corrective action had been taken; and no wet floor sign had been placed in the corridor on the day of the incident despite recent rain.

The hotel's legal position was extremely weak. The personal injury claim was settled for £175,000. The HSE issued an improvement notice requiring the hotel to implement a formal near-miss reporting system, conduct a comprehensive review of all outstanding maintenance issues, and provide additional training for all supervisors. The total cost of the incident — legal, medical contribution, operational disruption, staff time, and reputational damage — was estimated at over £200,000.

The corrective action required to have prevented this outcome: a near-miss report filed on the first occasion the damp corridor was identified, triggering a maintenance inspection and repair. Estimated cost: £300 for the window seal repair. The ratio of prevention cost to consequence cost: approximately 1:670.
