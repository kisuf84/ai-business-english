Online Review
A public evaluation of a hospitality property, service, or experience submitted by a guest through a third-party review platform — such as TripAdvisor, Google Reviews, Booking.com, Expedia, or Trustpilot — and visible to all users of that platform. Online reviews have transformed the hospitality industry since the mid-2000s: research consistently shows that the majority of travellers consult online reviews before booking a hotel or attraction, and that review score and recency are among the most influential factors in booking decision-making. For hotels, online reviews are simultaneously a reputational asset (a strong review score attracts bookings and justifies a premium price), an operational intelligence tool (reviews reveal patterns of guest experience that internal measurement may miss), and a reputation management challenge (negative reviews require professional, prompt response to limit reputational damage). The management of a hotel's online review profile has become a dedicated professional discipline within hospitality operations.

Guest Satisfaction Survey
A structured questionnaire sent to guests — typically by email after check-out — asking them to rate and comment on specific aspects of their experience. Guest satisfaction surveys are the primary tool for gathering systematic, comparable feedback across a hotel's operations: unlike online reviews (which reflect self-selected guests with strong opinions), surveys reach a broader cross-section of guests including those who were satisfied but not vocal. Most hotel chains and independent properties use a standardised survey structure — covering overall satisfaction, room quality, cleanliness, F&B, service, value for money, and likelihood to return and recommend — with both numerical rating scales and optional free-text comment boxes. Survey results are typically compiled into monthly and annual satisfaction reports and benchmarked against competitive set performance. The response rate to guest satisfaction surveys varies widely: industry averages range from 5% to 20%, and a low response rate can skew results toward strong opinions at either end of the satisfaction spectrum.

Net Promoter Score
A widely used customer loyalty metric that asks a single question: 'On a scale of 0 to 10, how likely are you to recommend [hotel / restaurant / experience] to a friend or colleague?' Respondents are categorised as Promoters (9–10), who are enthusiastic advocates likely to recommend and return; Passives (7–8), who are satisfied but not enthusiastic; and Detractors (0–6), who are dissatisfied and may actively discourage others. The NPS is calculated by subtracting the percentage of Detractors from the percentage of Promoters: a score of +50 or above is generally considered excellent in hospitality; a score of 0 or below indicates significant satisfaction challenges. NPS is valued for its simplicity and comparability — the same question produces directly comparable results across different properties, markets, and time periods — and for its commercial predictive power: high NPS correlates strongly with repeat bookings and word-of-mouth referrals.
▼ Show definition
💬
Review Response
A public reply posted by the hotel management to a guest's online review — visible to all users of the review platform and therefore functioning as a communication to potential future guests as much as to the specific reviewer. A professional review response to a negative review typically: thanks the guest for their feedback; acknowledges the specific issues raised without dismissiveness; apologises sincerely for the experience; provides relevant context (but does not make excuses); describes what action has been taken or will be taken; and invites the guest to make direct contact for further discussion. A professional response to a positive review: thanks the reviewer specifically and warmly; references specific details of their review to demonstrate that it was genuinely read; and reinforces a key brand message. Review responses that are generic, defensive, copy-pasted, or grammatically poor communicate as much to potential guests as the review itself — and almost always communicate the wrong things.

📈
Reputation Score
A composite metric — typically generated by a reputation management platform such as ReviewPro, TrustYou, or Revinate — that aggregates a hotel's ratings across multiple review platforms and internal surveys into a single, trackable performance indicator. Reputation scores allow hotel managers to monitor overall reputation performance across time and to benchmark against competitors without having to manually check multiple platforms separately. Most reputation management platforms also segment scores by department or category (rooms, F&B, service, cleanliness, value, location) — allowing managers to identify specific areas of strength and weakness that may not be visible from the overall average. Reputation scores have become a key performance indicator in hotel management contracts, with some hotel chains setting minimum reputation score requirements as a contractual obligation for property owners and operators.
▼ Show definition
🔍
Sentiment Analysis
An automated text analysis technique — typically powered by artificial intelligence — that scans large volumes of written guest feedback and categorises the emotional tone as positive, negative, or neutral, and identifies the specific topics and themes most frequently mentioned. Sentiment analysis allows hotel management teams to identify patterns in guest feedback at a scale that would be impossible through manual reading — for example, detecting that the word 'noisy' appears in 34% of negative reviews in a specific month, or that positive reviews mentioning the word 'breakfast' are 40% more likely to also mention the word 'Marcus', identifying a high-performing team member. Reputation management platforms integrate sentiment analysis tools that automatically tag and categorise review content by department, sub-department, and sentiment — generating heat maps, trend charts, and alert systems that allow managers to identify and respond to emerging issues quickly.
▼ Show definition
🔗
Review Platform
A third-party website or app on which guests can submit and read reviews of hospitality properties — including TripAdvisor, Google Maps / Google Business Profile, Booking.com, Expedia, Hotels.com, Airbnb, Yelp, and OpenTable (for restaurants). Different platforms serve different audiences and carry different commercial implications: TripAdvisor and Google are primarily discovery and research platforms used before booking; Booking.com and Expedia are booking platforms whose reviews are verified (only guests who booked through the platform can leave a review); and Airbnb hosts a review ecosystem specific to its own marketplace. For hotels, the most commercially significant platforms are typically Google (which affects search visibility), TripAdvisor (which maintains a city-level ranking that directly influences booking intent), and Booking.com or Expedia (whose scores affect placement in search results on those platforms). Hotels must monitor and respond to reviews across all relevant platforms simultaneously.
▼ Show definition
📣
Review Solicitation
The practice of proactively encouraging guests to submit reviews — through post-stay emails, in-room cards, verbal requests from staff, or prompts in the hotel's digital communication. Review solicitation is a legitimate and widely practised element of hotel reputation management: since satisfied guests are less likely than dissatisfied ones to spontaneously submit a review, proactive encouragement helps to generate a more representative review volume and score. The key constraint is that review platform terms of service typically prohibit: asking guests to write only positive reviews; offering incentives (discounts, gifts, or loyalty points) in exchange for reviews; and 'review gating' (screening guests before asking for a review, and only directing satisfied guests to the review platform). Legitimate review solicitation simply asks all guests to share their experience honestly — typically with language such as: 'If you enjoyed your stay, we would love to hear from you on TripAdvisor — your feedback means a great deal to our team.'

TripAdvisor Ranking
A hotel's position in TripAdvisor's 'Popularity Ranking' for its city or destination — calculated using a proprietary algorithm that considers the quality, recency, and volume of reviews. A hotel's TripAdvisor ranking directly affects its visibility in city search results and is one of the factors most heavily weighted by independent travellers when choosing accommodation. Being ranked in the top ten of a competitive city can generate significant incremental bookings; falling in ranking due to a decline in reviews or a cluster of negative feedback can have measurable commercial consequences. The ranking algorithm rewards hotels that generate a steady stream of recent, high-quality reviews: a hotel that received excellent reviews three years ago but has had few recent submissions will rank below a hotel with more recent review activity, even if the older reviews averaged a higher score.
▼ Show definition
📧
Post-Stay Email
An automated email sent to guests after check-out — typically 24 to 48 hours after departure — thanking them for their stay and inviting them to complete a satisfaction survey and/or leave an online review. The post-stay email is the most widely used and most effective review solicitation tool in hotel operations: it reaches guests at the right moment (recent enough that the experience is fresh; delayed enough that the guest has had time to arrive home and settle), it is personal and branded, and it can direct guests to the specific review platform most relevant to the hotel's commercial strategy. Effective post-stay emails: use the guest's name; reference something specific about their stay (room type, length of stay) rather than being entirely generic; lead with gratitude; make the review request simple and specific (including a direct link to the review platform); and are sent from a named member of the management team rather than an anonymous corporate address.
▼ Show definition
📉
Negative Review
A review that reflects a guest's dissatisfaction with one or more aspects of their experience — typically scoring 1 to 3 stars on a 5-star scale, or 1 to 5 on a 10-point scale. Negative reviews are one of the most valuable sources of operational intelligence available to a hospitality manager: they identify specific service failures, operational weaknesses, and guest experience gaps that may not be visible through internal quality processes. A hotel that treats negative reviews as attacks to be defended against misses this intelligence entirely; a hotel that reads every negative review as a data point for operational improvement gains a significant competitive advantage. Negative reviews also require prompt, professional public response: research shows that potential guests are almost as influenced by how a hotel responds to negative reviews as by the negative reviews themselves — a hotel that responds thoughtfully and constructively to criticism demonstrates operational credibility and a genuine commitment to guest experience.
▼ Show definition
✅
Verified Review
A review submitted by a guest whose stay or visit has been independently confirmed by the review platform — as opposed to an unverified review that could theoretically be submitted by anyone. Booking.com, Expedia, and Hotels.com only allow guests who booked through their platforms to submit reviews — making all reviews on those platforms verified. TripAdvisor allows any user to submit a review, though it uses fraud detection algorithms to identify and remove suspicious submissions. Google Reviews allows any Google account holder to submit a review. Verified reviews are commercially significant because they cannot (in principle) be submitted by hotel employees, competitors, or individuals with no direct experience of the property. For hospitality businesses, verified platforms tend to generate lower average scores but higher credibility — a 4.2 on Booking.com may be worth more commercially than a 4.5 on a platform that allows unverified submissions.

🔄
Response Rate
The proportion of reviews on a given platform to which the hotel has posted a management response — typically expressed as a percentage. A high response rate (responding to 90%+ of reviews) communicates to potential guests that the hotel actively monitors its reputation, takes guest feedback seriously, and is transparent about both its successes and its areas for improvement. Review platforms including TripAdvisor and Google actively display the management response rate and average response time — making these metrics visible to all users. Industry best practice in hotel review response sets a target of responding to 100% of negative reviews within 24 to 48 hours, and to 100% of all reviews within 7 days. A low response rate — particularly a pattern of responding to positive reviews but ignoring negative ones — communicates the opposite of what hotels intend, suggesting that criticism is unwelcome or that the hotel does not have the operational capacity or confidence to engage with it publicly.

💡
Actionable Feedback
Guest feedback — from any source, including reviews, surveys, verbal comments, and complaint records — that contains specific, concrete information about the guest experience that the hotel can use to make an operational improvement. Not all feedback is actionable: a review that simply says 'wonderful stay — highly recommend' provides no specific intelligence; a review that says 'the shower pressure in room 412 is very weak and the WiFi disconnected twice during our stay' provides two specific, actionable data points. The distinction between actionable and non-actionable feedback has important implications for how hotel managers read and analyse feedback data: the goal is to identify patterns of specific, addressable issues — the recurring mention of slow breakfast service, the cluster of complaints about a specific room corridor, the repeated reference to a team member's exceptional warmth — and to translate these patterns into operational decisions. Actionable feedback loops are a critical component of a continuous quality improvement culture.

Review Gating
The prohibited practice of screening guests before inviting them to submit a review — directing only guests who indicate they had a positive experience to the public review platform, while routing guests who indicate dissatisfaction to a private feedback form or complaint process instead. Review gating inflates a hotel's public review score by filtering out negative reviews before they can appear on the platform, producing a misleading picture of the overall guest experience. TripAdvisor, Google, and most major review platforms explicitly prohibit review gating in their terms of service — violations can result in the removal of all reviews, a penalty notice on the hotel's listing, or suspension from the platform. The test is straightforward: if a hotel's review solicitation process would result in a satisfied guest being directed to TripAdvisor and a dissatisfied guest being directed to a private email, the process is gating and is prohibited. Ethical review solicitation invites all guests to share their experience honestly.

📆
Review Velocity
The rate at which new reviews are being submitted for a hospitality property — the speed and recency of review generation as distinct from the total volume or average score. Review velocity matters because most review platform algorithms weight recent reviews more heavily than older ones: a hotel that generated excellent reviews two years ago but has seen a decline in review submission will rank below a hotel with more recent activity, regardless of the historical score differential. For hotel reputation managers, maintaining review velocity means: maintaining a consistent review solicitation programme throughout the year rather than in bursts; ensuring that post-stay email timing and messaging remain optimised for response rates; and monitoring velocity data alongside score data to detect early warning signals — a sudden decline in review velocity may indicate a decline in guest volume, a failure in the post-stay email system, or guests who are opting to complain through other channels rather than submitting reviews.

🏅
Competitive Benchmarking
The process of comparing a hotel's review scores, reputation metrics, and guest satisfaction data against those of a defined set of direct competitors — typically properties of similar type, category, location, and price point. Competitive benchmarking provides context for a hotel's own performance data that internal analysis alone cannot provide: a hotel that achieves a 7.8 average review score on a 10-point scale may not know whether this is excellent or average without knowing that the competitive set average is 8.2, or 7.2. Reputation management platforms such as ReviewPro and Revinate provide competitive benchmarking reports that show a hotel's rank and score relative to its defined competitive set across multiple platforms and categories. Competitive benchmarking data is particularly useful for identifying relative strengths and weaknesses: a hotel that scores lower than its competitive set on 'value for money' but significantly higher on 'cleanliness' can make strategic decisions about where to invest improvement effort and where to leverage existing advantages in its marketing.

Qualitative Feedback
Guest feedback in written or verbal narrative form — including the free-text comments in satisfaction surveys, the written content of online reviews, and the verbal feedback shared directly with staff. Qualitative feedback provides the context, specificity, and nuance that numerical ratings alone cannot capture: a guest who rates the hotel 7 out of 10 overall but writes 'the room was beautifully appointed but the noise from the bar on Friday evening made it impossible to sleep' has provided both a specific actionable issue (bar noise reaching upper rooms on Friday nights) and a context that explains why an otherwise satisfied guest scored lower than the property's typical range. Qualitative feedback analysis — whether conducted manually (reading through comments to identify themes) or through automated sentiment analysis tools — is one of the most powerful operational intelligence methods available to hotel management. The challenge is volume: a busy hotel may generate hundreds of written comments per month, requiring either dedicated analytical resource or technology to manage effectively.

🌡️
In-Stay Feedback
Feedback gathered from guests during their stay — before check-out — through mechanisms including: a mid-stay satisfaction call from the duty manager; an in-stay survey sent via the hotel app or email after the first night; a verbal check-in from a room service delivery or housekeeping visit; or a comment made directly at the front desk. In-stay feedback is the most operationally valuable form of guest feedback because it arrives in time for the hotel to address issues before the guest departs — converting what would otherwise be a post-stay negative review into a service recovery opportunity. The challenge of in-stay feedback is that guests are often reluctant to raise concerns during their stay (they do not want to seem difficult, they assume the problem cannot be fixed, or they are too busy to engage with a formal feedback process) — which is why the manner and timing of the in-stay check-in are so important. A warm, informal, specific enquiry ('Is your room comfortable? Is there anything we can do to make your stay even better?') typically generates more honest feedback than a formal survey request.

Feedback Loop
The complete cycle through which guest feedback is collected, analysed, communicated to the relevant operational teams, acted upon, and then measured again in subsequent feedback to determine whether the action has produced the intended improvement. A closed feedback loop — in which every stage from collection to operational change to re-measurement is completed — is the hallmark of a genuinely learning hospitality organisation. Many hotels have the collection and analysis stages in place but fail to close the loop: feedback is gathered, reports are produced, and then the data sits in an inbox rather than driving specific operational changes with clear ownership and timelines. A culture in which the feedback loop is genuinely closed requires: clear communication of feedback insights to front-line teams (not just managers); specific, assigned, time-bound improvement actions; and follow-up measurement to determine whether the change has produced the intended improvement in the feedback data.
▼ Show definition
🧩
Feedback Culture
The organisational values, behaviours, and systems that determine how a hospitality property or chain approaches the collection, analysis, sharing, and use of guest feedback — and the degree to which every team member, at every level, understands and contributes to the feedback process. A strong feedback culture is characterised by: leaders who actively share and discuss feedback data with their teams; front-line team members who feel empowered to share guest comments they receive verbally; a genuine curiosity about what guests experience rather than a defensive response to criticism; and a clear connection between feedback data and operational decisions and investments. A weak feedback culture is characterised by: feedback data that is reviewed only by senior managers; front-line teams who are either unaware of or disengaged from the hotel's review score; a tendency to dismiss negative reviews as unreasonable rather than investigating them; and operational decisions driven by internal preference rather than external evidence. Feedback culture is ultimately a leadership choice — it is modelled by managers and replicated by teams.

Text 1: Online Reviews and Reputation Management — Why Every Review Matters and How to Respond
The commercial significance of online reviews and the principles of professional review response

The transformation of the hospitality industry by online reviews is one of the most significant shifts in the history of travel marketing. Before the rise of TripAdvisor, Booking.com, and Google Reviews in the mid-2000s, a hotel's reputation was built primarily through word-of-mouth, travel agent recommendations, and printed guidebooks. Today, the first place most travellers go when choosing a hotel is not a travel agent — it is a review platform. Research conducted across multiple markets consistently finds that more than 80% of travellers read online reviews before booking a hotel, and that the review score is among the most influential variables in the final booking decision. A one-point improvement in a hotel's average review score on a 5-point scale has been associated with an average revenue per available room increase of between 5% and 9% in multiple academic studies. These are not marginal statistics — they represent the commercial logic that drives the enormous investment hotels now make in review management.

The most important and most frequently misunderstood element of review management is the response. Many hotel managers still treat review responses as an optional extra — something that gets done when there is time, or as a damage-limitation exercise directed exclusively at negative reviews. Both approaches miss the point. A review response is not a private communication between the hotel and the reviewer — it is a public broadcast to every potential future guest who reads that page. Research conducted by Cornell University's Centre for Hospitality Research found that potential guests are almost as influenced by how a hotel responds to reviews as by the reviews themselves: a hotel that responds thoughtfully and professionally to a critical review demonstrates operational competence and genuine guest focus in a way that a page of uniformly glowing reviews cannot. The response to a negative review is, paradoxically, often the most effective piece of marketing a hotel can produce.

Effective review responses share several structural characteristics regardless of whether they address positive or negative feedback. They are timely — posted within 24 to 48 hours for negative reviews and within 7 days for all reviews. They are personalised — referencing specific details of the review rather than using a generic template. They are proportionate — matching the tone and length of the response to the nature of the review (a glowing five-star review merits a warm, enthusiastic response; a detailed complaint merits a thorough, earnest one). They are signed by a named individual — ideally a manager or the General Manager — rather than 'The Management Team'. And they are honest — acknowledging genuine failures without making excuses, and providing accurate context where inaccuracies in the review need to be addressed. A response that ticks all of these boxes will do more to protect and enhance a hotel's reputation than any marketing campaign.

Text 2: Internal Feedback Systems — Using Guest Satisfaction Data to Drive Operational Excellence
How hotels collect, analyse, and act on feedback data to create continuous improvement and competitive advantage

While online reviews are the most visible form of guest feedback, they represent only a fraction of the feedback intelligence available to a hospitality operator. The most operationally sophisticated hotels build systematic feedback collection processes that extend far beyond public review platforms: post-stay satisfaction surveys that reach all departing guests rather than only those with strong opinions; in-stay feedback mechanisms that catch issues in time to address them before check-out; verbal feedback capture systems that translate comments made to front-line team members into logged operational data; and mystery guest programmes that provide structured evaluation of the guest experience from an external, objective perspective. Together, these sources create a comprehensive picture of the guest experience that is far richer and more representative than public reviews alone.

The analysis of feedback data — whether from surveys, reviews, or other sources — becomes genuinely valuable when it moves beyond the aggregate score to identify specific, actionable patterns. A hotel whose overall satisfaction score is 7.8 out of 10 has one piece of information; a hotel that knows its 7.8 overall hides a 9.1 for service and a 6.2 for room quality, that the room quality score has declined by 0.4 points over the past three months, and that the most frequently cited issue in room quality comments is 'tired decor and outdated bathroom fittings' has operational intelligence that can drive a specific investment decision. The movement from score to story — from the number to the operational narrative that explains it — is the analytical skill that distinguishes hotels that genuinely improve from those that simply monitor their decline.

Translating feedback insights into operational action requires one more step that many hotels neglect: closing the loop. The feedback loop is closed when the insight from the data produces a specific action, the action produces a change in the operation, and the change is reflected in subsequent feedback data. In practice, this means: assigning specific responsibility for identified issues to named team members or managers; setting clear timelines for corrective action; briefing front-line teams on what the feedback says and what is being done about it (a team member who knows that guests have been commenting on slow breakfast service is far more motivated to improve their performance than one who is simply told to speed up); and measuring whether the next round of feedback data shows improvement in the identified area. The feedback loop is the engine of continuous improvement — and the hotels that run it most effectively are the ones that stay ahead of both their guests' evolving expectations and their competitive set.

Exercise 1 — Grammar 1: Responding to Online Reviews — Professional, Personalised, and Brand-Appropriate Language

A professional review response — positive or negative — is a public communication to all future guests. It must be timely, personalised (referencing specific details), proportionate, signed by a named individual, and honest.

Responding to a negative review:

  • Thank for feedback → Acknowledge specific issues → Apologise sincerely → Provide honest context (not excuses) → Describe action taken → Invite direct contact
  • 'Thank you for taking the time to share your experience, [Name]. I was very sorry to read about the breakfast service delays you experienced — a 25-minute wait is simply not acceptable and I completely understand your frustration...'

Responding to a positive review:

  • Thank warmly → Reference specific detail → Reinforce a key brand message → Invite return
  • 'Thank you so much for this wonderful review, [Name] — reading that [specific detail] genuinely made the whole team's day...'

What to avoid:

  • Generic openers ('We are sorry you were not satisfied'); defensive language ('we would like to point out'); corporate boilerplate ('we take all feedback seriously')

Exercise 2 — Grammar 2: Requesting and Encouraging Feedback — Ethical, Warm, and Effective Language

Encouraging guests to leave reviews must be ethical (inviting all guests honestly, not just satisfied ones), specific (telling them where and how to leave a review), and warm (framing the request as a genuine expression of the hotel's care for feedback rather than a commercial ask).

In-stay feedback request:

  • 'Is your room comfortable? Is there anything we can do to make your stay even better?' [Not: 'Are you satisfied?' — too yes/no]

Post-stay review solicitation (verbal):

  • 'If you've enjoyed your stay, we would genuinely love to hear from you on TripAdvisor — your feedback means a great deal to our team and helps us improve for every guest.'

Post-stay email language:

  • 'Thank you for staying with us, [Name]. If you would be willing to share your experience, a few minutes on [platform] would mean a great deal — here is the link: [link].'

What to avoid:

  • Offering incentives in exchange for reviews; directing only satisfied guests to the platform (review gating); pressuring guests who seem dissatisfied to leave a review

Exercise 3 — Grammar 3: Presenting and Acting on Feedback Data — Analytical and Operational Language

Presenting feedback data to a team or in a report requires moving from numbers to narrative — explaining what the scores mean, what patterns emerge from qualitative feedback, and what specific operational actions should follow.

Presenting scores in context:

  • 'Our overall reputation score is 8.2 — stable compared to last month, but 0.3 below the competitive set average of 8.5. That gap is worth understanding.'

Identifying patterns in qualitative feedback:

  • 'The F&B sub-score has dropped from 7.8 to 7.3. Looking at the qualitative data, two themes are clustering: breakfast speed (18 mentions) and coffee temperature (9 mentions).'

Assigning actionable next steps:

  • 'Tom — the staffing issue is causing the speed issue. Can you have a rota proposal ready by Friday?'

Case Study 1: The Review That Changed Everything — How One Hotel Turned Negative Feedback Into Its Greatest Competitive Advantage
How a hotel's strategic response to a cluster of negative reviews transformed its operations, its reputation, and its revenue
This case study is drawn from a representative composite of transformation scenarios documented across UK hospitality management literature. It illustrates how a sustained, professional, and operationally serious response to negative guest feedback can become a source of genuine competitive advantage.

In 2019, a well-regarded four-star hotel in a competitive UK city market noticed a pattern in its TripAdvisor reviews that had not been visible in its internal satisfaction surveys: a cluster of reviews specifically mentioning that breakfast service was slow and the dining room felt chaotic. The hotel's internal survey data showed breakfast scoring 7.2 out of 10 — below average but not alarming. The reviews, however, were more pointed: 'Waited 40 minutes for eggs'; 'Staff seemed overwhelmed and disorganised'; 'Beautiful hotel but the breakfast let it down completely.'

The operations manager made a decision that was simple in principle but significant in practice: she would read every review mentioning breakfast that had been submitted in the previous twelve months, extract specific, actionable feedback, and use it to redesign the breakfast operation. She identified four recurring themes: wait time (mentioned in 67% of relevant reviews), the absence of a dedicated host at the restaurant entrance (mentioned in 31%), temperature of hot dishes (23%), and a perceived lack of communication between the kitchen and the floor team (19%).

The redesign was surgical: a host was appointed to greet and seat all breakfast guests; kitchen-to-floor communication was restructured using a ticket system; hot holding equipment was upgraded; and a promise of fifteen-minute service for hot dishes was introduced (and, critically, publicised to guests at the table by the host).

Within six months, breakfast reviews had transformed. The phrase that had defined the negative feedback cluster — 'beautiful hotel but the breakfast' — disappeared entirely from the review data. The breakfast sub-score rose from 7.2 to 8.8. Overall TripAdvisor ranking improved by fourteen positions in the city. The hotel's occupancy in the following year's first quarter was up 8% year-on-year.

The case illustrates a principle that appears repeatedly in hospitality management research: operationally specific negative feedback, acted upon with precision and urgency, produces improvements that are disproportionate to the cost of the intervention.

Case Study 2: The Response That Did More Damage Than the Review — What Not to Do When Replying to a Negative Review
How a defensive, dismissive, and poorly crafted review response amplified reputational damage and drove guests to competitors
This case study documents a representative scenario that captures a pattern of review response failure documented across multiple hospitality management publications. It illustrates how a poorly executed review response can do significantly more reputational damage than the original negative review.

A three-star hotel in a UK city received a detailed negative review from a guest who had experienced what they described as a noisy room, a slow check-in process, and a dismissive response from a front desk team member when they raised concerns. The review was specific, articulate, and gave the hotel three out of five stars. It attracted 47 helpful votes within a week — indicating that other readers found it credible and useful.

The hotel's response, posted three days later by an unnamed 'Hotel Management Team', read as follows: 'We are sorry to hear that you were not satisfied with your stay. We take all feedback seriously. However, we would like to point out that the room you occupied is in a quiet location on the third floor and that we have never previously received noise complaints from that area. We also note that our check-in process is highly efficient and our team members are trained to the highest standards. We hope you will give us another chance in the future.'

The response did several things simultaneously, all of them wrong. It was not personalised — 'we are sorry to hear that you were not satisfied' does not acknowledge the specific issues raised. It was defensive — three of the four sentences pushed back against the guest's account rather than acknowledging it. It implicitly called the reviewer dishonest — suggesting that the noise complaint was unfounded because no one else had complained from that room. And it was templated — phrases like 'we take all feedback seriously' and 'trained to the highest standards' are immediately recognisable as corporate boilerplate.

The response received fourteen replies from other TripAdvisor users — including three who stated that the hotel's response had convinced them not to book. One response from another guest said: 'I was planning to stay here next month but this response has made me reconsider.' The original review's credibility was amplified, not diminished, by the hotel's response.

The lesson: a poorly crafted review response is not neutral — it is actively harmful. The review that started as a three-star complaint became, through the hotel's response, a five-star indictment of the hotel's approach to guest experience and accountability.
