Distribution Channel
Any route through which a hotel sells its rooms to guests — including the hotel's own website (direct), telephone reservations, travel agents (physical and online), Global Distribution Systems (GDS), Online Travel Agencies (OTAs), tour operators, corporate booking tools, and group sales. Hotels typically distribute through multiple channels simultaneously, each with different commercial terms, commission structures, and guest profiles. The mix of channels a hotel uses — and the proportion of bookings generated through each — is known as the channel mix. Managing channel mix is a strategic commercial function: direct bookings (through the hotel's own website or phone line) generate no commission and the highest net revenue per booking; OTA bookings generate commission of typically 15–25%; GDS bookings generate both a commission and a GDS access fee. Understanding how channels interact, how to optimise the channel mix, and how to use pricing strategy to shift bookings toward higher-net-revenue channels is a core competency of modern hotel commercial management.

GDS
Global Distribution System — a worldwide electronic network that connects hotel, airline, car hire, and other travel inventory to travel agents, corporate booking tools, and other distribution partners. The three dominant GDS platforms are Sabre, Amadeus, and Travelport (which includes Galileo and Worldspan). Hotels load their room availability, rates, and conditions onto the GDS, and travel agents or corporate booking tools access this information in real time to make bookings on behalf of their clients. GDS bookings are particularly significant in the corporate travel sector — many large companies require their employees to book through a corporate booking tool that connects to the GDS, where the company's negotiated hotel rates are stored. GDS bookings typically attract a per-booking transaction fee as well as agent commission, making them one of the more expensive distribution channels on a cost-per-booking basis — but access to the GDS is non-negotiable for hotels that want to participate in the managed corporate travel market.
▼ Show definition
📱
OTA
Online Travel Agency — a third-party website on which consumers can search, compare, and book hotel rooms (and often flights, car hire, and holiday packages) at properties worldwide. The major OTA platforms relevant to UK hotels include Booking.com, Expedia, Hotels.com, Airbnb, Agoda, and TripAdvisor (through its hotel booking function). OTAs charge hotels a commission on each booking, typically in the range of 15–25% depending on the platform and the hotel's commission tier. In exchange for the commission, the OTA provides access to its very large customer base, sophisticated search and ranking algorithms, and a trusted consumer-facing brand that many travellers use as their primary hotel discovery and booking tool. The tension between hotels and OTAs has intensified over the past decade as OTA market power has grown: in many markets, OTAs now account for a majority of hotel bookings, giving them significant commercial leverage over hotels in commission negotiations and ranking decisions.
▼ Show definition
💰
Commission
The percentage of a booking's value paid by the hotel to the booking agent or platform that originated the reservation. Commission is the primary commercial model for travel agencies and OTAs: rather than charging guests a booking fee, they charge the hotel for the business they deliver. Commission rates vary significantly by channel and by negotiated agreement: traditional leisure travel agents typically earn 8–12% commission on hotel bookings; OTAs typically charge 15–25%; specialist niche platforms may charge lower rates; and corporate travel management companies typically operate on a lower commission or fixed fee model depending on the arrangement. Commission is calculated on the net room rate (excluding VAT and any local taxes in most markets). Understanding the true cost of distribution — factoring in commission, GDS fees, marketing contributions, and any other channel costs — is essential for accurate revenue management and commercial decision-making.
▼ Show definition
🏨
Rack Rate
The published, undiscounted list price for a hotel room — the maximum rate at which a room can be sold, from which all discounts, negotiated rates, and promotional pricing are derived. The rack rate is the baseline against which all other rates are positioned: a corporate rate is a percentage discount from rack; a leisure package rate may be positioned as 'X% below rack'; and OTA promotional rates are typically a further discount on the hotel's already-discounted standard rate. In practice, very few hotels sell rooms at rack rate — most rooms are sold at some form of discounted or negotiated rate, and the rack rate functions primarily as a commercial anchor point rather than a price that many guests actually pay. The rack rate is particularly significant in the luxury and upper-upscale segments, where a high published rack rate communicates brand positioning even when the effective achieved rate is considerably lower.
▼ Show definition
📋
Corporate Rate
A negotiated, discounted hotel room rate agreed between a hotel and a specific corporate client or travel management company — offering a consistent discount on the hotel's standard rate in exchange for a commitment of a defined volume of room nights per year. Corporate rates are stored in the hotel's PMS and in the relevant GDS under the company's account code, allowing authorised company employees to book at the negotiated rate either through the hotel's own booking tools or through the company's corporate booking tool. The corporate rate is typically accompanied by a set of additional conditions — last room availability (the hotel must honour the rate even when only the last room remains); blackout dates (peak dates on which the corporate rate is not available); and an annual minimum volume commitment (below which the rate may be renegotiated). Corporate accounts are a significant source of predictable, high-quality volume for city-centre hotels serving the business travel market.
▼ Show definition
🔒
Rate Parity
The requirement, historically enforced by OTA contract terms, that a hotel must offer the same room rate on all public booking channels — ensuring that the OTA's displayed rate is never higher than the rate available on the hotel's own website or through any other public channel. Rate parity clauses were designed to prevent hotels from undercutting the OTA by offering cheaper direct rates, and their widespread adoption significantly constrained hotels' ability to incentivise direct booking with lower prices. In recent years, rate parity requirements have been challenged by competition authorities in several European markets, including the UK, France, Germany, and Italy, leading to the removal of most so-called 'wide parity' clauses from OTA contracts. Many OTA contracts now include only 'narrow parity' — requiring price parity with other OTAs but permitting lower rates for the hotel's own direct booking channels. This shift has enabled hotels to offer 'direct booking benefits' — exclusive rates or added value (complimentary breakfast, room upgrades, loyalty points) available only when booking directly.
▼ Show definition
🎟️
Allotment
A block of rooms reserved by a hotel for a specific tour operator, travel agency, or group organiser — set aside from general availability and held at a pre-agreed rate for a defined period, typically until a release date at which unbooked rooms are returned to general sale. Allotment agreements are common between hotels and tour operators: the operator contracts for a number of rooms per night over a defined period (often a summer or winter season), agrees a rate, and commits to selling those rooms to their customers. Rooms within the allotment are available to the operator to sell freely within the allotment size; rooms are 'released' at a pre-agreed cut-off date if they have not been sold, returning to the hotel's general inventory. Allotment agreements reduce the hotel's market risk (a guaranteed volume of bookings) but also reduce its yield management flexibility (rooms allocated to allotment cannot be sold at higher market rates when demand is strong).
▼ Show definition
📡
Channel Manager
Software that connects a hotel's property management system (PMS) to all its external booking channels — including OTAs, GDS, the hotel's own website booking engine, and any other distribution platforms — and updates room availability and rates across all channels simultaneously and in real time. Channel management software is essential for any hotel distributing across multiple channels: without it, managing availability manually across each platform separately creates the risk of overbooking (the same room sold twice through different channels) and rate inconsistency. When a room is booked through any connected channel, the channel manager instantly reduces the available inventory across all other channels — preventing double bookings. Major channel management software providers include SiteMinder, RateGain, and Cloudbeds. The quality and reliability of the channel manager is one of the most operationally critical technology choices a hotel makes.
▼ Show definition
🤝
Travel Management Company
A specialist corporate travel agency — known as a TMC — that manages the travel booking, policy compliance, expense management, and duty of care for a corporate client's business travel programme. TMCs such as American Express Global Business Travel (Amex GBT), BCD Travel, and CWT (Carlson Wagonlit Travel) are major players in the managed corporate travel market and hold negotiated hotel rates on behalf of their corporate clients, stored in the GDS under client-specific codes. For hotels, the TMC is a critical channel partner: corporate travel managed through a TMC represents a significant source of high-occupancy, high-rate business travel, particularly in city-centre properties near major business hubs. Building productive relationships with key TMC account managers, ensuring the hotel's rates are correctly loaded in the GDS, and maintaining preferred status on TMC programmes are important commercial activities for hotels competing in the corporate travel segment.
▼ Show definition
📊
Yield Management
The practice of adjusting hotel room rates dynamically based on real-time supply and demand — charging higher rates when demand is strong and supply is constrained, and lower rates when demand is weak and inventory would otherwise go unsold. Yield management (also referred to as revenue management) uses historical booking data, forward-looking demand signals (events, competitor pricing, booking pace), and pricing algorithms to determine the optimal rate for each room type on each future date. A hotel practising yield management may charge £250 per night on a Friday in September when a major event fills the city, and £95 per night on a quiet Tuesday in February when occupancy is projected to be low. Yield management creates tension with corporate clients who prefer price certainty — which is why corporate rate agreements typically include last room availability clauses and defined blackout dates rather than applying across all market conditions.
▼ Show definition
🏷️
Best Available Rate
The lowest publicly available rate offered by a hotel on a specific date through its own booking channels — the hotel's real-time market rate that fluctuates with demand. BAR is distinct from rack rate (the maximum published price) and from negotiated rates (the discounted rates agreed with specific corporate clients or tour operators). BAR is typically the rate shown to consumers booking directly through the hotel's website and is adjusted daily or even hourly by the revenue management team in response to changing demand conditions. Knowing the BAR and how it compares to negotiated rates is important for corporate clients managing their travel budgets: if a corporate rate is £120 and the BAR on a specific date is £85, a well-designed corporate rate agreement should include a 'BAR fallback' provision that allows the booker to access the lower rate. Hotels use BAR management as a core yield tool — raising BAR on high-demand dates to maximise revenue and lowering it on low-demand dates to stimulate bookings.

Last Room Availability
A clause in a corporate rate or preferred supplier agreement that guarantees the negotiated rate will be available for booking even if only the last available room in that category remains in the hotel's inventory. Last room availability (LRA) is a significant commercial concession by the hotel — without it, a hotel's yield management system may close availability to corporate rates on high-demand dates in favour of higher-rate open-market bookings. With LRA, the hotel commits to honouring the corporate rate regardless of how constrained availability becomes, up to the final room. Corporate clients and TMCs typically prioritise LRA in rate negotiations: a corporate rate that is unavailable on the high-demand dates when business travel typically occurs is of limited practical value. Hotels that offer LRA to corporate accounts effectively accept a yield cost in exchange for the volume certainty and relationship value of a preferred corporate account.

🚫
Blackout Date
A date on which a negotiated corporate rate, promotional rate, or allotment agreement is not available — typically corresponding to periods of peak demand when the hotel has sufficient open-market demand to fill its rooms at higher rates without relying on discounted contracted rates. Blackout dates are specified in corporate rate agreements and allotment contracts and are typically associated with: major local events (conferences, sporting events, festivals) that generate strong demand at premium rates; school holiday periods in leisure-oriented properties; and the hotel's own peak booking windows where contracted rates would create a yield management conflict. Blackout dates must be communicated clearly to corporate clients and travel agents when a rate agreement is established — a client who attempts to use a negotiated rate on a blackout date and is told it is unavailable has a poor experience, particularly if they are a high-volume account. Best practice requires proactively alerting corporate accounts to upcoming blackout dates.

📬
Direct Booking
A reservation made directly with the hotel — through the hotel's own website, by telephone, by email, or in person — without the involvement of a third-party intermediary such as an OTA, GDS, or travel agent. Direct bookings are the most commercially attractive type of reservation for hotels because no commission is paid to an intermediary, the hotel retains the full room revenue (minus credit card processing fees), and the hotel maintains direct control of the guest relationship. Encouraging direct booking over OTA booking is a central element of most hotel commercial strategies: hotels use loyalty programme benefits, exclusive direct-booking rates (made possible by the relaxation of strict OTA rate parity requirements), and direct booking perks (complimentary breakfast, room upgrade, early check-in) to incentivise guests to bypass OTAs and book directly. The 'book direct' movement among hotel groups represents one of the most significant commercial shifts in hotel distribution over the past decade.
▼ Show definition
🌍
Tour Operator
A company that designs, packages, and sells holiday programmes — combining accommodation, transport, and often activities and meals into a complete product sold to consumers either directly or through travel agents. Tour operators contract with hotels for room allotments at negotiated wholesale rates, typically significantly below the hotel's standard retail rate, in exchange for volume commitment and advance booking. The UK tour operator market includes large integrated operators (such as TUI and Jet2holidays), specialist operators (focussing on specific destinations, activities, or guest segments), and incoming tour operators (who manage arrangements for international visitors coming to the UK). For hotels in tourist destinations, tour operator business can provide a reliable, high-volume base of bookings — but the low wholesale rate and volume risk (if the operator does not fill its allotment) require careful management and realistic yield expectations.
▼ Show definition
📝
Booking Terms
The conditions under which a hotel reservation is made and confirmed — specifying the guest's or agent's obligations, the hotel's obligations, and the consequences of changes or cancellations on either side. Core booking terms include: the cancellation policy (when cancellation is free of charge, and what charges apply within specified timeframes); the no-show policy (what the hotel will charge if a confirmed guest does not arrive and does not cancel); the payment terms (when payment is taken, whether a credit card guarantee is required, and whether the rate is pre-paid or pay-on-arrival); the amendment policy (whether changes to the booking can be made and at what cost); and any specific terms applicable to group or allotment bookings (minimum stay requirements, rooming list deadlines, attrition clauses). Booking terms must be communicated clearly to all parties at the time of reservation and are typically included in the booking confirmation document.

🔑
Preferred Supplier
A hotel or hotel chain that has been formally selected by a travel management company, corporate client, or travel agency as a recommended or exclusively promoted supplier — typically in exchange for a negotiated rate, a minimum volume commitment, and often additional marketing or promotional support. Preferred supplier status confers significant commercial benefits: a hotel that is on a TMC's preferred supplier list will be promoted as the recommended option when a TMC client makes a booking, giving it a structural booking advantage over non-preferred competitors in the same market. Achieving and maintaining preferred status requires competitive rates, consistent product quality, reliable service, and strong account management relationships with the TMC. In large managed corporate travel programmes, the TMC's preferred supplier list may be the single most important commercial document determining where a hotel's corporate volume comes from.

Room Night
A standard unit of measurement in hotel bookings and commercial analysis — representing one room occupied for one night. Room nights are the primary volume metric in hotel commercial operations: total room nights booked, room nights per account, room nights per channel, and room nights per rate type are all key data points in commercial reporting and forecasting. When a travel management company or corporate client discusses their annual volume commitment to a hotel, they will typically express this as a room night target (for example, 500 room nights per year). The value of a corporate account is typically evaluated in terms of the room nights it delivers, the average rate achieved per room night, and the consistency of delivery against volume commitments. Room night data, combined with average rate data, produces the hotel's total room revenue — the primary financial metric in hotel commercial performance.

Release Date
The deadline by which a tour operator, travel agent, or group organiser must either confirm their use of a contracted allotment or release unbooked rooms back to the hotel's general inventory. The release date is a critical term in any allotment or group booking agreement: it determines when the hotel regains the commercial freedom to sell its remaining inventory on the open market. A release date that is too close to the arrival date (for example, 14 days before) may leave the hotel with unsold rooms at a point when competitive rates have been filled by other channels. A release date that is too distant (for example, 90 days before) forces the hotel to hold inventory from open-market sale for a longer period. Negotiating appropriate release dates is an important yield management function: for popular summer periods, hotels will push for longer release dates to maximise their market flexibility; for quieter periods, they may be more generous to encourage operators to contract.
▼ Show definition
🗓️
Minimum Stay
A booking restriction applied by a hotel that requires guests to book a specified minimum number of consecutive nights in order to access a particular room or rate — most commonly applied during periods of high demand to prevent short-stay bookings that would block longer, higher-value reservations. For example, during a major event weekend, a hotel may apply a two- or three-night minimum stay to prevent guests from booking only the highest-demand night and leaving adjacent nights unsold. Minimum stay restrictions are a yield management tool: used judiciously, they increase total revenue by ensuring that peak nights are not sold in isolation; used excessively, they reduce bookings by constraining guest flexibility. Minimum stay requirements must be communicated clearly at the point of search and booking — guests who discover a minimum stay restriction only when they attempt to complete a booking have a poor experience that can damage the hotel's reputation on review platforms.

Text 1: Hotel Distribution Economics — Understanding How Channels Work and What They Cost
The commercial logic of hotel distribution channels and why channel mix strategy matters

A hotel room is a perishable product: unlike a manufactured good, an unsold room generates no revenue and the opportunity to sell it disappears at midnight each night. This fundamental characteristic of hotel inventory drives a distribution logic that is unique in the commercial world — and that shapes every decision a hotel commercial team makes about how, where, and at what price to sell its rooms. The goal is not simply to sell every room, but to sell every room at the highest achievable net revenue, through the most cost-effective channel, to the most valuable guest. Getting this balance right across the arc of a year, across multiple room types, and across a complex landscape of competing distribution channels is the discipline of revenue and distribution management.

The economics of hotel distribution are determined primarily by the cost of the channel through which a booking arrives. A direct booking — made through the hotel's own website, by phone, or by walk-in — incurs no commission and only minimal processing costs (credit card fees of approximately 1.5–2%); it is the highest net revenue booking the hotel can receive. A booking through a leisure travel agent generates a commission of typically 8–12%, reducing the net revenue by that proportion. A booking through a major OTA generates a commission of 15–25%, further reducing net revenue. A booking through the GDS adds both an OTA-level commission and a GDS transaction fee, making it the most expensive channel on a pure cost-per-booking basis. The difference between a direct booking and an OTA booking on a £150 room rate is between £22.50 and £37.50 in commission — the equivalent of a complimentary breakfast or more.

This commission differential explains why hotel chains have invested so heavily in 'book direct' marketing campaigns over the past decade, why loyalty programmes have been enhanced to reward direct bookers with exclusive rates and benefits, and why the relaxation of strict rate parity requirements by OTAs has been commercially significant. It also explains why channel mix — the proportion of total bookings arriving through each channel — is one of the most closely monitored commercial metrics in hotel operations. A hotel that shifts 10% of its bookings from OTA to direct in a year of 20,000 room nights at an average rate of £150, saving an average of £25 in commission per booking, generates an additional £50,000 in net revenue without changing a single rate or selling a single additional room. The channel is the margin.

Text 2: Travel Agents, TMCs, and the Booking Ecosystem — Working Effectively with Distribution Partners
How travel agents, corporate booking tools, and TMCs work — and how hotels can build productive distribution partnerships

The transformation of the travel booking landscape by the internet has not eliminated the travel agent — it has changed the travel agent's role from information provider to specialist curator and problem-solver. In an era when any consumer can search and book a hotel room independently in minutes, the leisure travel agent who survives and thrives is the one whose value proposition is not access to information but the ability to curate extraordinary experiences, navigate complexity, and provide trusted personal service that a website cannot replicate. This specialisation has driven the leisure travel agency market toward a higher-value, higher-margin segment — adventure travel, luxury tourism, destination weddings, accessible travel, multi-generational family holidays — where the complexity, personalisation requirements, and emotional significance of the booking justify the agent's fee or commission.

In the corporate travel market, the transformation has taken a different form. The Travel Management Company (TMC) has grown in importance not despite the internet but because of it: managing a large company's travel policy compliance, duty of care obligations, cost reporting, and carbon footprint data across dozens of simultaneous bookings from hundreds of employees is a task that requires specialist systems, expertise, and relationships — none of which an individual booking their own travel online can replicate. The TMC's value to a corporate client is measured in policy compliance rates, managed travel cost savings, duty of care capability, and the quality of the management information it provides. For hotels, the TMC is both a key channel partner and a powerful commercial gatekeeper: the preferred supplier list a TMC curates for its corporate clients can direct tens of thousands of room nights per year toward or away from a hotel depending on whether the hotel has secured preferred status.

For hotel reservations and commercial teams, working effectively with distribution partners — whether leisure travel agents, tour operators, TMCs, or OTA account managers — requires a specific set of competencies beyond operational hotel management. These include: understanding the partner's commercial model and what they need from the relationship (a leisure travel agent needs a competitive commission, accurate product information, and a responsive reservations team; a TMC needs correctly loaded GDS rates, last room availability, and reliable account management communication); communicating rate and availability information accurately and promptly; managing allotment and release date agreements professionally; and resolving booking discrepancies or disputes quickly and constructively. Hotels that invest in partner relationship management consistently report better channel performance, higher preferred status with key TMCs, and stronger loyalty from the travel agent community — all of which translates directly into higher booking volumes and better commercial outcomes.

Case Study 1: The Channel Mix Transformation — How One Hotel Shifted 18% of Bookings from OTA to Direct in Two Years
How a deliberate direct booking strategy reduced commission costs and increased net revenue without changing a single rate
This case study documents a representative scenario from UK hotel commercial management literature, illustrating how a focused channel mix strategy can produce significant commercial results without requiring changes to the hotel's pricing, product, or operational model.

In 2021, a four-star city-centre hotel reviewed its channel mix data and identified a concerning pattern: OTA bookings had grown to represent 58% of total room nights, with direct bookings (website, phone, and walk-in) accounting for only 27% — down from 41% three years earlier. Given an average room rate of £160 and an average OTA commission of 18%, the cost of OTA dependence was approximately £16.70 per room night in commission. Across 15,000 annual OTA room nights, this represented approximately £250,000 in commission paid to OTA partners each year.

The commercial team set a two-year objective: to reduce OTA share to below 45% and increase direct bookings to above 40% of total room nights, without reducing total occupancy or average rate. The interventions were deliberate and multi-layered: a loyalty programme was launched offering exclusive direct-only rates and complimentary breakfast for direct bookers; the hotel's direct booking website was redesigned for usability and conversion; a post-stay email programme was introduced directing satisfied guests back to the direct booking channel for future stays; the hotel's TripAdvisor listing was updated with a clear 'book direct and save' message; and the reservations team received training in direct booking conversion language.

By the end of year two, OTA share had fallen to 43% and direct bookings had risen to 41%. Total occupancy had remained stable at 83%. The commission saving from the channel shift — approximately 15,000 additional direct room nights at an average commission saving of £16.70 — represented approximately £250,000 in recovered net revenue. The full cost of the two-year programme (loyalty programme launch, website redesign, staff training, and marketing materials) was approximately £45,000.

The return on investment was approximately 5.5:1. The lesson: channel mix management, executed with discipline and sustained investment, produces commercial results that rate management alone cannot achieve.

Case Study 2: The Preferred Supplier That Lost Its Status — What Happens When Hotels Neglect TMC Relationships
How a hotel's failure to manage its TMC relationship cost it preferred supplier status and the volume that came with it
This case study documents a composite scenario representative of preferred supplier relationship failures documented in UK corporate travel management literature. It illustrates how quickly a hotel can lose significant corporate volume through a failure to invest in the relationship and service quality required to maintain preferred status with a key TMC partner.

A four-star London hotel had held preferred supplier status with a major TMC for three consecutive years. The arrangement generated approximately 600 room nights per year at a corporate rate of £195 — representing approximately £117,000 in annual revenue. The hotel's account management for the TMC relationship was handled informally: there was no dedicated account manager, no quarterly review process, and no proactive communication from the hotel to the TMC when issues arose.

In the second year of the arrangement, three issues accumulated without resolution. First, the hotel's GDS rates were not updated following an annual rate review — TMC agents were booking at the previous year's rate for three months before the error was identified. Second, the hotel's last room availability guarantee was found to have been honoured incorrectly on two occasions — the TMC's clients had been quoted the corporate rate but found it unavailable when booking. Third, when the TMC's account manager raised both issues by email, the responses from the hotel took between five and eight business days — significantly beyond the 48-hour response time the hotel had committed to in its preferred supplier agreement.

At the annual TMC preferred supplier review in April, the hotel was formally removed from preferred status. The TMC's stated reasons: GDS rate inaccuracy, LRA failures, and inadequate account management responsiveness. The replacement property — a competitor hotel that had invested in a dedicated TMC account manager and a quarterly rate and performance review process — was added to the preferred list in the same review.

The loss of preferred status reduced the hotel's corporate volume from 600 room nights to approximately 80 over the following year — a reduction of approximately £101,000 in annual revenue. The cost of adequate account management to have prevented this outcome: approximately £8,000 in a part-time account manager allocation.

The lesson: preferred supplier status is not a commercial outcome to be achieved and then maintained passively. It requires active, consistent, and accountable relationship management — and the cost of losing it is rarely recovered quickly.
