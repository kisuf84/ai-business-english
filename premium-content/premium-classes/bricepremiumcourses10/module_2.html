Guaranteed Reservation
A hotel booking secured by a valid credit or debit card, ensuring the room is held for the guest until a specified time (typically midnight) and allowing the hotel to charge for a no-show. A guaranteed reservation provides certainty for both parties: the guest knows their room will be held regardless of arrival time; the hotel has financial protection against no-shows. In most modern systems, all online reservations are automatically guaranteed through card pre-authorisation. Reservations not guaranteed by card may be released if the guest has not arrived by a stipulated time, typically 6pm.

❌
Cancellation Policy
The terms under which a guest may cancel a reservation without financial penalty, and the charges that apply after the stated deadline. Flexible or refundable rates allow cancellation 24 to 48 hours before arrival with no charge; non-refundable rates charge the full amount at booking. Group booking cancellation policies are more complex, typically including a group cancellation deadline, a rooming list deadline, and a final attrition clause. Staff must be able to explain cancellation policy clearly and professionally to guests who call to cancel.
▼ Show definition
💳
Pre-Authorisation
A temporary hold placed on a guest's credit or debit card at check-in, reserving a specified amount for potential incidental charges without actually charging it. The pre-authorisation is released when the guest checks out and the actual charges are settled. Pre-authorisation protects the hotel against non-payment and allows guests to charge services during their stay. Debit card holds can take 3 to 5 business days to release; credit card holds typically clear within 24 to 48 hours.
▼ Show definition
🔢
Average Daily Rate
The average revenue earned per occupied room per day, calculated as Total Room Revenue divided by Number of Rooms Sold. ADR measures the hotel's pricing power: a high ADR relative to the competitive set indicates premium pricing; a low ADR suggests underperformance. Revenue managers increase ADR through upselling, yield management, and reducing discounted bookings. ADR is distinct from RevPAR: a hotel can achieve a high ADR on a low-occupancy night, but RevPAR will still be low.
▼ Show definition
🌐
Channel Management
The centralised control and synchronisation of a hotel's room inventory, rates, and availability across all booking channels, including the hotel's own website, OTAs, and GDS platforms. Channel management is essential for preventing overbooking and maintaining rate parity. A channel manager connects the hotel's PMS to all external platforms, updating availability in real time as reservations are made or cancelled. Without effective channel management, a hotel risks overbooking, underbooking, and rate inconsistency.
▼ Show definition
🏷️
Rate Plan
A defined pricing structure within the hotel's reservation system, specifying the room rate, inclusions (room only, bed and breakfast, half board), and conditions (cancellation policy, minimum stay, advance purchase requirement). Hotels maintain multiple rate plans: Best Available Rate (BAR); non-refundable advance purchase; corporate rates; group rates; OTA rates; and package rates. Front desk staff must understand the conditions of the rate plan on any reservation they handle, particularly regarding cancellation and amendment charges.
▼ Show definition
🔄
Modification
A change made to an existing reservation, including changes to arrival or departure dates, room type, number of guests, or meal plan. Handling modifications professionally requires: checking availability for the requested change; confirming whether the new dates are available at the original or a new rate; advising the guest clearly of any policy changes; and updating the PMS with a notation. Not all modifications are possible at the original rate, particularly for non-refundable advance purchase bookings.
▼ Show definition
👥
Group Booking
A reservation for a minimum number of rooms (typically 10 or more) for a group travelling together, including conference delegates, wedding parties, sports teams, and tour groups. Group bookings are handled by the sales team with a group rate, a room block, and group-specific terms including rooming list deadline, cancellation policy, and attrition clause. Group check-in requires pre-assigned rooms, a group check-in area, master folio arrangements, pre-prepared key packets, and a staff briefing.
▼ Show definition
📋
Rooming List
A document provided by the group organiser before arrival, listing the names of all group members, their room preferences, special requirements, and billing instructions. The rooming list allows the hotel to pre-assign rooms and prepare key packets, making check-in significantly more efficient. Most hotels require the rooming list 7 to 14 days before arrival. A rooming list arriving late or incomplete creates operational pressure on the front desk.
▼ Show definition
🗺️
Room Assignment
The process of allocating a specific physical room to a guest reservation, matching the booking (room type, floor preference, accessibility requirement, special request) to an available room. Effective room assignment honours special requests, recognises loyalty programme benefits, places VIP guests in premium positions, manages housekeeping flow, and spreads guests efficiently across the property. Failure to honour documented special requests is one of the most common guest complaint triggers.
▼ Show definition
🔑
Key Card
The electronic access card or mobile phone app that gives a guest access to their room and other secured hotel areas during their stay. Most modern hotels use RFID or magnetic stripe cards programmed at check-in with the room number, floor access, and check-out date. Mobile keys allow guests to bypass the front desk entirely if pre-check-in is completed online. Agents must explain which areas the key provides access to and remind guests that it deactivates after the checkout date.
▼ Show definition
📝
Folio
The individual itemised account maintained for each guest during their stay, recording all charges (room rate, meals, spa, bar, laundry, parking) and any credits. The folio is presented to the guest at check-out as their final bill. Disputes about charges are common at check-out and must be handled efficiently. Corporate guests may request folios in a specific format for expenses compliance. Most hotels allow guests to review their folio via the TV or hotel app during the stay.
▼ Show definition
🏨
Walk-In Guest
A guest who arrives at the hotel without a prior reservation and requests a room on the spot. Walk-in guests can fill rooms that would otherwise go unsold but create pressure during busy periods. Hotels typically apply a walk-in rate, often close to the rack rate. Agents handling walk-in guests must quickly assess availability, quote the applicable rate, take ID and payment, and complete check-in. Walk-in guests who cannot be accommodated should receive a warm response and a recommendation for nearby alternatives.
▼ Show definition
🔓
Early Check-In
A request by a guest to access their room before the hotel's standard check-in time, typically 2pm or 3pm. Options for handling early check-in include: assigning a room already clean from the previous night's departure; offering complimentary or chargeable early check-in subject to availability; providing access to facilities while the room is prepared; and storing luggage securely. Staff should never promise early check-in without confirming availability. High-tier loyalty members may be entitled to early check-in as a standard benefit.
▼ Show definition
🌙
Late Check-Out
A request by a guest to retain their room beyond the standard checkout time, typically 11am or 12 noon. The hotel's ability to accommodate late check-out depends on the day's occupancy and the room's next arrival booking. Options include complimentary late checkout to noon or 1pm for loyalty members; chargeable late checkout for others; or advising that late check-out is not possible with an offer to store luggage. On high-occupancy arrival days, late check-out should require duty manager approval.

📞
Reservation Call
A telephone enquiry or booking made by a potential guest who wishes to reserve a room directly with the hotel. A professional reservation call follows a consistent structure: greeting and identification; understanding the enquiry (dates, guests, room preference, occasion); checking availability; presenting options (room types, rates, inclusions, cancellation policy); taking the booking; and confirming all details before closing. The telephone reservation call is an opportunity to make the first positive impression of the hotel's service.
▼ Show definition
✉️
Booking Confirmation
A written communication sent to the guest after a reservation has been made, confirming all booking details and providing a reference number. A complete booking confirmation contains: guest name and contact details; reservation reference; hotel name and address; check-in and check-out dates; room type and rate; inclusions; cancellation policy; payment method; special requests noted; and hotel contact details. Confirmations should be sent immediately or within a few hours of the reservation being made.
▼ Show definition
🔒
No-Show
A guest with a guaranteed reservation who fails to arrive and does not cancel in advance. No-shows represent lost revenue as the room was held and unavailable to other guests. Under most guaranteed reservation policies, the hotel charges one night's accommodation on the card provided. The no-show charge policy must be clearly communicated at booking, in the confirmation, and in reminders before arrival. Hotels may at their discretion waive charges in genuine emergency circumstances. No-show rates are tracked as an indicator of booking quality.
▼ Show definition
🏆
VIP Guest
A guest who receives enhanced attention, personalised recognition, and additional amenities due to high-tier loyalty status, regular repeat visits, a special occasion, or high profile. VIP designation triggers a pre-arrival checklist: personal room inspection by a manager; welcome amenity placement (flowers, chocolates, champagne, personalised note); action on known preferences; and front desk briefing. VIP handling requires discretion, personalisation, and flawless execution.
▼ Show definition
🌟
Special Request
A guest preference or requirement noted on a reservation that the hotel is asked to accommodate, from operational requests (high floor, quiet room, connecting rooms) to personal needs (hypoallergenic room, accessibility requirements, cot for infant) to special occasion requests (flowers for birthday, champagne for anniversary). Special requests must be clearly flagged in the PMS so relevant departments can act before arrival. Failing to act on a documented special request is one of the most common guest complaint triggers.
▼ Show definition
📊
Occupancy Forecast
A projection of how many rooms will be occupied on each future date, used by revenue managers, operations managers, and department heads to plan staffing, pricing, and capacity. The forecast is generated by the PMS or revenue management system and updated daily as reservations are made and cancelled. It shows current bookings, pick-up trends, and expected final occupancy. The forecast drives pricing decisions, staffing levels, and operational planning across all departments.

Text 1: The Hotel Reservation Process — From Enquiry to Confirmed Booking
How modern hotels manage the reservation process across multiple channels

The hotel reservation process has been transformed over the past two decades by the rise of online booking platforms, global distribution systems, and revenue management technology. Where once a guest would call the hotel directly or visit a travel agent, today they are equally likely to book through Booking.com, Expedia, or the hotel's own website — often comparing prices across multiple channels simultaneously. This transformation has made reservations simultaneously more complex (managing rates across dozens of channels) and more transparent (guests have unprecedented access to pricing and peer reviews). For the hospitality professional, mastering modern hotel reservations means understanding both the human interaction skills of the reservation call and the operational infrastructure that supports it.

At its core, the reservation process follows a consistent logic regardless of channel. A potential guest has a need (accommodation on specific dates); the hotel has supply (rooms at a price); the reservation matches the two. What makes hotel reservations complex is the revenue management overlay: prices vary dynamically by date, length of stay, booking lead time, and the balance of supply and demand. A room costing £150 on a quiet Tuesday may cost £350 on a busy Saturday. A guest booking six weeks in advance may access an advance purchase rate unavailable to a guest booking the day before. This dynamic pricing model — familiar from airlines — requires reservations staff to be clear and confident in communicating rates, rate conditions, and availability to guests who may not understand why prices have changed.

The booking confirmation is the most important communication in the reservation process — the document that transforms an enquiry into a contractual commitment. A confirmation that is clear, complete, and professionally presented builds guest confidence; one that is vague or delayed creates doubt and generates unnecessary pre-arrival enquiries. Best practice includes: sending the confirmation within minutes of booking (automated where possible); including all material booking details; stating the cancellation policy prominently; providing a direct contact point for pre-arrival queries; and including practical arrival information to reduce uncertainty. The confirmation is also the hotel's first opportunity to cross-sell — suggesting restaurant reservations, spa bookings, or local experience packages.

Text 2: Professional Check-In and Check-Out — The Procedures, Language, and Moments That Matter
How to conduct complete and professional hotel check-in and check-out

Check-in and check-out are the two operational bookends of every hotel stay — the moments when the hotel's brand promise is most directly tested by frontline service. Both procedures have a defined operational structure (steps completed in sequence for compliance, security, and operational reasons) and a service dimension (the warmth, personalisation, and efficiency that shapes the guest's emotional impression). Mastering both simultaneously — completing the operational checklist without losing the human connection — is the defining skill of an excellent front desk professional.

The check-in procedure follows a consistent sequence. Greeting: the agent acknowledges the approaching guest immediately and delivers a warm welcome by name if the reservation is visible. Identity verification: politely requesting a passport or driving licence and verifying against the reservation. Booking confirmation: confirming room type, rate, inclusions, and departure date. Payment and pre-authorisation: taking the credit card and explaining the hold amount and release timing. Special requests: confirming what has been arranged — or apologising proactively if not possible. Room key and orientation: providing the key card(s) and briefly orientating the guest to key facilities. Closing: wishing the guest a genuinely warm welcome and reminding them how to reach reception.

Check-out is briefer but no less important. The agent greets the departing guest warmly, retrieves and prints the folio for review, answers any charge queries efficiently, processes payment, returns any pre-authorisation hold, and closes with a warm farewell and invitation to return. The check-out moment is particularly valuable for service recovery: a guest who experienced a problem may raise it at check-out — the agent's response at this final moment can rescue an otherwise negative experience. A check-out that is efficient, friendly, and leaves the guest feeling appreciated is the experience they will describe to friends and in online reviews. The difference between a guest who writes 'great check-out' and one who does not mention the front desk at all is often a single genuine human moment — a remembered name, a sincere farewell, a small act of care.

Exercise 1 — Grammar 1: Taking Reservations — Questions, Confirmations, and Telephone Language

Reservation calls require a specific sequence of professional questions, confirmation structures, and polite closing language.

Opening a reservation call:

  • 'Good afternoon, [Hotel Name] reservations, [Name] speaking. How may I help you?'

Gathering information:

  • 'Could I start with the dates you're looking at?'
  • 'How many guests will be staying?' / 'Do you have a preference for room type?'

Presenting options and confirming:

  • 'We have two options that might suit you...'
  • 'To confirm: [room type], [dates], [rate], [inclusions], [cancellation policy].'

Exercise 2 — Grammar 2: Handling Modifications, Cancellations, and Difficult Requests

When guests request changes or cancellations, use language that is clear and honest about what is possible, empathetic, and solution-focused.

Acknowledging a request:

  • 'Of course — let me check availability for the new dates.'
  • 'I'd be happy to look into adding breakfast for you.'

Explaining a constraint:

  • 'I'm afraid the rate you booked does not permit date changes without rebooking at the current rate.'
  • 'Our cancellation policy requires 48 hours' notice — a charge of one night will apply.'

Offering a solution:

  • 'What I can do is...' / 'The best option would be...'

Case Study 1: The Overbooking Crisis — What Happens When a Hotel Sells More Rooms Than It Has
How channel management failures lead to overbooking, and what exceptional hotels do when they cannot accommodate a confirmed guest
Hotel overbooking — selling more rooms than are physically available — is one of the most serious operational failures in hospitality. Unlike airlines, which routinely oversell flights as a revenue strategy, hotels are generally expected to honour confirmed reservations. When a hotel finds itself overbooked — typically from a channel management error, system failure, or forecasting mistake — the consequences are severe: a guest arriving after a long journey to find no room is one of the most negative experiences in the entire guest journey.

The root cause of most overbooking is inadequate channel management. If a hotel sells rooms through its own website, Booking.com, Expedia, and Hotels.com simultaneously without real-time synchronisation, a surge in bookings can result in the same room sold through two channels. Modern channel management software eliminates this risk by connecting all platforms to the hotel's central PMS and updating availability within seconds. Hotels using manual systems — updating each platform separately — are significantly exposed to overbooking risk.

When overbooking occurs, the industry term is 'walking' a guest — finding alternative accommodation at a comparable nearby hotel. Professional guest walking involves: immediately sourcing a comparable or superior room at a nearby hotel, at the overbooked hotel's expense; arranging and paying for transport; personally escorting or arranging the escort; providing a sincere and unconditional apology; and making a substantial goodwill gesture. The handling of a walking situation is a true test of the hotel's service values — the difference between a guest who becomes a vocal critic and one who eventually becomes a forgiving advocate is almost entirely determined by how the walking is managed.

Case Study 2: The Perfect Reservation — How One Telephone Call Created a Loyal Guest for Life
How a single reservation call handled with skill and warmth created an exceptional first impression and a long-term guest relationship
In an era dominated by online booking, the telephone reservation call might seem like a declining art. UK data suggests direct telephone bookings now represent fewer than 15% of full-service hotel reservations. Yet the telephone reservation call remains disproportionately valuable: guests who call directly prefer personal interaction, often have more complex requirements, and — if handled well — are significantly more likely to book directly in future, bypassing OTA commission and giving the hotel a direct guest relationship.

Consider the following case from UK luxury hotel training material. A guest called to enquire about a three-night stay the following month. She mentioned, unprompted, that the visit was to celebrate her mother's 70th birthday — the family was gathering from across the country. The reservations agent did three things beyond standard procedure: asked for the mother's name; made a note of the occasion and family context; and mentioned the team would love to do something small to mark it — a birthday card and a small cake in the room on the first evening.

The caller was surprised and delighted. The booking was confirmed. What followed was the real case study: the agent passed the information to Guest Relations, who arranged a birthday card signed by the team, a small cake, and a General Manager's note. On arrival, the family were greeted by name, the mother personally welcomed by the duty manager, and the occasion acknowledged throughout. The family — six people — returned for the same celebration the following year, and annually for five consecutive years. Total booking value over five years: approximately £18,000. Cost of the initial gesture: approximately £35. Return on investment: over 500 to 1. The lesson: a single reservation call handled with exceptional skill and genuine human warmth can create a loyal guest relationship worth thousands.
