Event Brief
A comprehensive document capturing all the client's requirements, expectations, and constraints for an event — gathered during the initial planning meeting and used as the foundation for all subsequent planning, quotation, and coordination. A professional event brief covers: the event type and purpose (conference, product launch, gala dinner, training day, awards ceremony); the date, time, and duration; the expected number of guests or delegates; the room configuration and layout preferences; catering requirements and style; audio-visual and technical requirements; branding and theming preferences; dietary and accessibility requirements; the client's budget range; and any specific preferences, sensitivities, or previous event experiences the client wants to build on or avoid. The event brief is the most important document in the planning process: an incomplete or inaccurate brief leads to mismatched expectations, rework, and — if not caught before the event — a delivered product that does not meet the client's needs. Best practice is to confirm the brief in writing within 24 hours of the initial meeting.

Function Sheet
A detailed operational document — also known as a Banquet Event Order (BEO) — that translates the client's event brief into specific instructions for every department involved in the event's delivery. The function sheet is the single most important internal communication document in event management: it tells the kitchen the menu, timing, and service style; it tells the AV team the setup, equipment list, and sound check schedule; it tells the housekeeping team the room configuration, setup time, and linen requirements; it tells the front desk or reception team about expected arrivals and any special requirements; and it tells the food and beverage team the service flow and delegate refresh timing. A function sheet contains no ambiguity — it is specific, numbered, timed, and named. An event that is perfectly planned in the coordinator's head but not communicated in writing through a function sheet is a professional liability: things change, people change shifts, and the function sheet is the document that ensures consistent delivery regardless of who is on duty.

Room Layout
The physical configuration of chairs, tables, and equipment within an event space — chosen to support the specific objectives and atmosphere of the event. The most common room layouts in hotel event management are: theatre (rows of chairs facing a stage or screen — maximum capacity, formal, suitable for presentations to large audiences); classroom (rows of tables with chairs, like a lecture room — smaller than theatre, more individual workspace, suitable for training); boardroom (a single large rectangular or oval table with chairs around it — intimate, collaborative, suitable for senior meetings of up to 20); cabaret (round tables with chairs on three sides, leaving the fourth side facing the stage — combines dining with presentation, suitable for awards and interactive events); reception (standing only, chairs at the perimeter — maximum mingling, suitable for drinks receptions and networking events); and banquet (round tables with chairs all the way around — full dining configuration, suitable for gala dinners and celebration events).

AV Requirements
The audio-visual equipment and technical services required for an event — including microphones (handheld, lapel, podium), speakers, mixing desk, projectors or LED screens, presentation laptops and connections, lighting rigs, video conferencing systems, and any specialist production elements. AV requirements are one of the most technically complex elements of event planning: the right AV setup for a 30-person boardroom meeting (a single large screen and a conferencing microphone) is completely different from the requirements for a 400-person gala dinner with a live band, an awards ceremony, and a live-streamed keynote. Hotels typically operate an in-house basic AV capability (projector, screen, microphones) and partner with specialist AV contractors for more complex requirements. The function sheet must specify every AV element precisely, including: what equipment is needed; who is supplying it; when it needs to be set up and tested; which technician is responsible; and what the backup plan is if a specific piece of equipment fails.
▼ Show definition

Delegate Rate
A packaged pricing model for conference and corporate event catering — combining the meeting room hire, standard AV, and a defined catering package into a single per-delegate price. The full-day delegate rate (DDR) typically includes: meeting room hire; basic AV (screen, projector, flipchart); arrival refreshments (tea, coffee, pastries); a working lunch (typically a buffet); mid-morning and mid-afternoon refreshment breaks (tea, coffee, biscuits or fruit); and unlimited water, mints, and stationery. The half-day delegate rate (HDR) typically includes the same but with only one catering break and either no lunch or a lighter option. Delegate rates allow corporate clients to budget predictably for events without itemising every element separately — and they simplify the operational process for the hotel by standardising the catering delivery against a known delegate count. Most hotels offer delegate rates for events of a minimum size, typically 10 or more delegates.

Event Timeline
A detailed, chronological schedule for an event — specifying the precise time at which each element of the programme is to begin, how long each element lasts, who is responsible for each element, and what the immediate next action is. The event timeline is an internal operational tool: it tells the events team and every department involved exactly when each thing needs to happen and who is responsible for making it happen. A professional event timeline for a full-day conference might contain 30 to 50 separate timed actions — from 'AV technician on site to begin setup' and 'Kitchen to lay refreshments in Break Room 1' through 'First delegate arrivals — registration desk staffed by [names]' and 'Chair to open programme from podium' to 'Networking lunch service begins in Hartley Suite' and 'Post-event clear: all furniture to store by 7pm'. The timeline is the instrument of accountability: when things go wrong, the timeline shows what was supposed to happen and when, creating a basis for understanding what changed and why.

Run of Show
A document that describes the sequence and content of the event programme from the perspective of the audience experience — typically used for events with a stage, a programme, or a significant production element (awards ceremonies, gala dinners, conferences with multiple speakers). The run of show differs from the event timeline in purpose and audience: the event timeline is an operational document for the hotel team; the run of show is a programme-level document shared with the client, the speakers, the MC, the AV team, and any performers. It typically specifies: the order of speakers and topics; the timing of video content, award presentations, or entertainment; the MC's script cues; the lighting and sound changes at specific programme points; and the timing of catering service around the programme. A run of show that is distributed and reviewed by all parties before the event eliminates many of the most common sources of confusion and delay — no one should be uncertain about what happens next.
▼ Show definition
📢
MC
Master of Ceremonies — the individual who introduces speakers, manages the flow of the event programme, makes key announcements, keeps the event on time, and maintains the energy and atmosphere of the room between programme elements. The MC (also called a compere in informal UK event terminology) is one of the most important roles in any formal event: a skilled MC can recover from technical difficulties, manage an over-running speaker without causing offence, warm a cold room, and create a sense of occasion that elevates the entire event. Hotels may provide an MC from their in-house events team, or the client may supply their own MC (often a senior company figure or a professionally hired external presenter). In either case, the hotel's event coordinator should brief the MC thoroughly before the event: on the programme, the run of show, the client's key people and their titles, any sensitivities, and the protocols for managing specific situations (what to do if a speaker over-runs, how to handle a technical failure).

Attrition Clause
A contractual provision in a hotel event agreement that obliges the client to pay a defined penalty if the actual number of attendees or room nights falls below a specified minimum threshold. Attrition clauses protect the hotel against the financial risk of a client booking event capacity on the basis of a projected delegate count that subsequently falls short — leaving the hotel with unused space, unsold rooms, and catering prepared for a larger group. A typical attrition clause might specify that the client guarantees a minimum of 80% of the contracted delegate number, with a defined charge per delegate for any shortfall below that threshold. Clients should be made aware of attrition clause terms at the time of contracting — a client who discovers an attrition penalty after falling short will be dissatisfied; a client who understood the clause when they signed will accept it as a fair commercial provision. Most professional corporate clients are familiar with attrition clauses and will negotiate the threshold and penalty amount as part of the contract discussion.

Minimum Spend
A contractual requirement that the total spend on food, beverage, and other services at a specific event or venue reaches a defined minimum value — typically set in relation to the venue's capacity and the commercial needs of the hotel. Minimum spend provisions are common in hotel event and private dining contracts: a hotel that provides exclusive use of a function room for an event incurs fixed costs (staffing, setup, opportunity cost of not booking the room to other clients) regardless of how much the client spends on food and beverage. The minimum spend threshold ensures that the hotel receives sufficient commercial return to justify the room's exclusivity. When explaining minimum spend to a client, it is important to be clear, positive, and transparent: 'The minimum spend for exclusive use of the Hartley Suite is £3,500 — this means we need the food, beverage, and any other services to total at least that amount. Based on the dinner you've described with forty guests, I would expect your spend to be comfortably above that threshold.

Technical Rehearsal
A pre-event run-through of all technical elements — typically the day before or on the morning of an event — in which the AV setup, lighting, sound, and any presentation technology is tested and refined before the event begins. Technical rehearsals (also called tech checks or tech runs) are particularly important for events with complex production elements: multiple speakers using different presentation software or screen formats; live streaming or recording; live music or entertainment that requires specific sound mixing; video content that needs to be cued correctly in the run of show; and lighting states that change at specific programme points. A thorough technical rehearsal identifies and resolves problems that cannot be fixed mid-event without disruption: a projector that cannot display the correct aspect ratio, a microphone with an unexpected interference issue, a presentation that requires a different connection adaptor, or a video clip that plays without sound. The investment of 60 to 90 minutes in a technical rehearsal consistently produces smoother, more professional event delivery.

Contingency Plan
A pre-prepared alternative arrangement for every event element that could fail or be disrupted — including AV equipment failures, catering delays, speaker cancellations, weather-dependent elements, and room configuration changes. Every professional event plan should include a contingency column: if the main projector fails, what is the backup? If the keynote speaker cancels at 48 hours' notice, what alternatives does the hotel or client have? If the outdoor drinks reception is rained out, where does it move inside and who communicates the change to arriving guests? If the evening's main band cannot perform, what happens to the entertainment schedule? Contingency planning is not pessimism — it is professionalism. A hotel events team that has thought through contingencies in advance and communicated them to the client is in a fundamentally stronger position when the unexpected occurs than one that has to improvise under pressure in front of the client and their guests.

Catering Style
The format and service model chosen for food delivery at a hotel event — a fundamental decision that affects the event's atmosphere, logistics, cost, staffing, and timing. The main catering styles in hotel event management are: sit-down dinner (formal, plated courses served to seated guests — maximum control of timing and presentation, highest staffing requirement, creates a formal atmosphere); buffet (self-service from a central display — more informal, allows flexible dining timing, suits a wide range of dishes simultaneously); fork buffet (standing, with food served from chafing dishes — combines elements of a buffet and reception, allows eating while standing and networking); canapé reception (bite-sized food passed by servers — ideal for networking events, no tables required, allows the maximum number of guests in a given space); and grazing table (an abundant display of food arranged for guests to help themselves throughout the event, often without clear serving structure — a relaxed, theatrical style suitable for creative or celebratory events).

Breakout Room
A smaller meeting space adjacent to or separate from the main conference room — used for small-group working sessions, syndicate discussions, breakout workshops, interview stations, or private meetings during a larger event. Breakout rooms are a fundamental requirement of any conference or training event involving group exercises, and their availability and configuration is an important factor in venue selection for complex events. When briefing a client on breakout room options, key information includes: how many breakout rooms are available and their capacity; whether they are separate rooms or can be created within the main space using partitions; whether they have their own AV (screen, flip chart, writing materials); how close they are to the main room; and whether refreshments can be served directly to breakout rooms. The flow of delegates between the main room and breakout rooms requires careful management in the event timeline — transitions between plenary and breakout sessions are a common source of timing overruns.

Event Signage
Printed, digital, or handwritten directional and informational displays placed throughout the hotel to guide guests to the correct event spaces, communicate the event programme, welcome delegates, and reinforce the client's branding. Event signage is a critical but often overlooked element of the guest experience: delegates who cannot find their meeting room, who are uncertain whether they have arrived at the correct venue, or who cannot see the programme at a glance have a poor first impression before the event has even begun. Professional event signage typically includes: directional signs from the hotel entrance and reception area to the event space; a welcome board at the event entrance with the client's branding; a programme board showing the day's schedule; name cards and table plans for dinners; and any themed or branded elements specified by the client. The hotel's events coordinator is responsible for ensuring that signage is in place, accurate, and well-positioned well in advance of delegate arrival.

Post-Event Report
A formal written document produced by the hotel's events team after an event — summarising the event's delivery against the plan, recording attendance figures, noting any deviations from the agreed programme, flagging operational issues and how they were resolved, and including financial reconciliation. Post-event reports serve two purposes: internally, they provide a record for quality review, operational learning, and future event planning; externally, when shared with the client, they demonstrate the hotel's professionalism, accountability, and commitment to continuous improvement. A professional post-event report for a significant corporate event might include: a summary of the event as delivered; attendance versus contracted figures; catering service summary; AV performance notes; any incidents or service failures and their resolution; delegate feedback if collected; financial summary; and recommendations for the next event. Post-event reports that highlight what went well, acknowledge honestly what could have been better, and propose specific improvements for the next event are the most commercially valuable — they demonstrate that the hotel is a professional partner, not just a space provider.

Event Coordinator
The hotel team member responsible for planning and managing the logistics of a specific event — working with the client during the planning phase to confirm all requirements, producing the function sheet and event timeline, briefing all departments, and being the primary point of contact during the event itself. The event coordinator role exists on a spectrum from a junior team member managing straightforward meeting packages through to an experienced professional managing complex multi-day conferences and gala dinners. In most hotel operations, the event coordinator is the client's single point of contact throughout the planning process, reducing the client's need to communicate with multiple departments independently. The coordinator's success is measured by the smoothness of the event delivery — a well-coordinated event feels effortless to the client and delegates even when the operational complexity behind it is significant.

Lead Time
The time between the initial client enquiry or booking confirmation and the event date — a critical variable in event planning that determines how much planning time is available and therefore how complex an event can realistically be. Lead times in hotel event management vary enormously: a straightforward meeting room booking may have a lead time of 48 hours; a medium-complexity one-day conference may have a lead time of four to eight weeks; a large-scale gala dinner or multi-day conference typically requires six to twelve months of lead time to source entertainment, produce printed materials, confirm guest travel arrangements, and manage all the dependencies involved. For hotel event coordinators, managing lead time is a critical professional skill: knowing at the point of enquiry how much time is available and whether it is sufficient to deliver what the client is requesting, and communicating any constraints clearly at that stage rather than discovering them under time pressure closer to the event.

Site Visit
A guided tour of the hotel's event spaces given to a prospective client who is considering the hotel as a venue for their event — typically conducted by the event coordinator or a senior member of the events team. The site visit is one of the most commercially important activities in hotel event sales: a client who has seen the spaces, met the team, experienced the hotel's atmosphere, and had their specific questions answered in person is significantly more likely to book than one who has only received a written proposal. A professional site visit involves: a warm welcome and introduction; a tour of all relevant event spaces in the configuration they would be used for the client's event; a demonstration of AV and technical capabilities; a visit to the proposed catering areas; an opportunity to taste sample menus or canapés if relevant; and a clear conversation about commercial terms. The site visit is also a diagnostic opportunity: the questions a client asks during a site visit reveal what they care most about and allow the events team to tailor the proposal accordingly.

On-the-Day Management
The coordination of all operational elements of an event on the day itself — typically managed by a named event coordinator or event manager who is present throughout the event, available to the client at all times, and responsible for ensuring that every element of the function sheet and event timeline is delivered as planned. On-the-day management distinguishes between the planning function and the execution function: an event can be planned to the highest standard but delivered poorly without effective on-the-day management. Key on-the-day responsibilities include: confirming setup is complete and correct before delegate arrival; managing the registration and welcome process; monitoring the programme against the timeline and managing any overruns; liaising with catering, AV, and housekeeping throughout the day; being the single point of contact for the client; managing any unexpected incidents; and ensuring that the post-event clear is initiated and completed within the agreed timeframe. The on-the-day event coordinator should have no other responsibilities during the event — they must be fully focused on the delivery.

Post-Event Follow-Up
The communication sent by the hotel to the client in the days following an event — typically including a thank-you message, a summary of the event as delivered, any financial reconciliation, a request for feedback, and — where appropriate — a proactive proposal for the next event. Post-event follow-up is both a service standard and a commercial tool: it closes the service loop on the current event and opens the conversation for the next. A professional post-event follow-up email from the events coordinator is personalised to the specific event, acknowledges specific highlights or challenges, thanks the client for their business, and — if the event went well — makes a specific, timely proposal for a repeat event while the positive experience is fresh in the client's mind. Hotels that manage post-event follow-up systematically — treating it as a planned activity rather than an afterthought — significantly improve their repeat event conversion rate. A corporate client who books an annual event is worth many times the value of a single booking.

Text 1: The Architecture of a Hotel Event — Planning Principles for Professionals
How professional event planners approach the design, communication, and coordination of complex hotel events

Planning a hotel event is an exercise in managed complexity. At its simplest — a ten-person boardroom meeting with tea, coffee, and a projector — it is a straightforward operational task. At its most complex — a three-day residential conference for two hundred delegates, with gala dinner, awards ceremony, breakout sessions, exhibition stands, live entertainment, and international keynote speakers — it is a significant project management challenge that touches every department in the hotel and requires months of detailed preparation. What distinguishes the hotels that consistently deliver excellent events from those that merely deliver adequate ones is not the quality of their spaces or the size of their menus: it is the quality of their planning systems, their internal communication, and the skill of their event coordinators.

The function sheet is the single most important document in hotel event management. It is the bridge between the client's vision — captured in the event brief — and the operational reality of what happens on the day. A function sheet that is incomplete, ambiguous, or inaccurate creates operational failures that are entirely preventable: the kitchen prepares the wrong number of covers because the confirmed delegate count was not updated after the client's final numbers arrived; the AV team sets up the wrong room layout because the client changed from theatre to cabaret three days before the event and no one updated the document; the catering team serves lunch an hour early because the function sheet shows an outdated programme timeline. None of these failures require exceptional skill or knowledge to prevent — they require only a complete, accurate, and current function sheet distributed to all relevant departments.

The best event coordinators operate according to a principle that might be called 'proactive communication' — they do not wait for problems to emerge and then respond; they anticipate where problems are most likely to occur and communicate ahead of them. In event management, the highest-risk moments are: the transition from planning to execution (when the responsibility for the event passes from the coordinator's desk to the live operational team); the delegate arrival window (when everything happens at once and any gap in preparation is immediately visible); and the first fifteen minutes of the programme (when technical failures, timing overruns, or unexpected client requests create the impression that the event is not under control). An event coordinator who has thought through each of these moments in advance, prepared specific actions for each high-risk window, and briefed their team thoroughly will deliver an event that feels effortless to the client — regardless of the operational complexity that lies beneath.

Text 2: Managing the Day — Client Communication, Incident Response, and Post-Event Excellence
How professional event managers communicate with clients, handle the unexpected, and turn events into long-term partnerships

The event coordinator who is excellent at planning but poor at day-of communication has only half the skill set. The day itself — when the client is present, the delegates are arriving, and every decision is visible in real time — requires a different set of competencies from the planning phase. Chief among them is the ability to communicate with the client in a way that maintains their confidence throughout, even when things are not going entirely to plan. This does not mean deceiving the client about what is happening — it means choosing the right moment, the right language, and the right level of detail to inform them without creating unnecessary anxiety or distracting them from their role as the event's host.

When something goes wrong during an event — and in events of any complexity, something usually does — the event coordinator's response follows a consistent pattern in the hands of a professional: acknowledge the issue internally and immediately; assess whether it requires the client to know right now or whether it can be resolved without involving them; if the client needs to know, communicate promptly and with a solution rather than a problem ('the projector has developed a fault — our AV team is switching to the backup unit, which will be live in three minutes, and we suggest continuing the welcome reception for slightly longer while we complete the switch'); deploy the pre-planned contingency; and follow up after resolution to ensure the client is satisfied with the outcome. This pattern — acknowledge, assess, communicate with solution, deploy contingency, follow up — is the event manager's incident response framework, and it applies whether the issue is a technical failure, a catering delay, a speaker who has not arrived, or an unexpected change in the number of delegates.

The post-event phase is where excellent event management creates long-term commercial relationships. A corporate client who books an annual gala dinner is worth, over five years, many times the value of a single event — and the decision about whether to rebook is made not only by the quality of the event itself but by the quality of the relationship the hotel's events team has built with the client during and after the planning process. Post-event follow-up is therefore a commercial activity as much as a service one: calling or emailing the client within 24 hours to check that they are satisfied, sharing the post-event report within a week, and — when the event has gone well — making a specific and timely proposal for the next event while the positive experience is fresh. Hotels that treat post-event follow-up as a systematic commercial activity, rather than an afterthought, significantly improve their repeat event conversion rate.

Exercise 1 — Grammar 1: Event Client Communication — Briefing, Clarifying, and Confirming

Exercise 3 — Grammar 3: Post-Event Communication — Debriefing, Reporting, and Proposing the Next Event

Post-event communication has two purposes: service closure (confirming what was delivered, acknowledging what could have been better) and commercial development (proposing the next event while the positive experience is fresh).

  • 'I wanted to reach out today specifically — I want to hear your thoughts while the evening is still fresh.'

Asking for critical feedback:

  • 'May I ask — was there anything that didn't quite hit the mark? Even a small detail?'

Responding to feedback without being defensive:

Case Study 1: The Event That Built a Five-Year Partnership — How Excellent Coordination Created a Recurring Annual Client
How a hotel's investment in event planning excellence turned a one-off corporate dinner into a five-year annual contract
This case study documents a representative composite of corporate event relationship development scenarios from UK hotel management literature. It illustrates how the quality of event delivery — and particularly the quality of post-event communication — creates long-term commercial relationships that are significantly more valuable than individual bookings.

A regional law firm hosted its annual client appreciation dinner at a four-star hotel for the first time in 2018. The event was 60 guests, a sit-down dinner with three awards, and a budget of £6,000. The firm had previously held the event at a private dining club and moved to the hotel primarily on the strength of the site visit — the events coordinator, who was thorough, enthusiastic, and had clearly done her homework on the firm before the meeting, made a strong first impression.

The event itself delivered above expectations. The function sheet was precise, all dietary requirements were handled correctly, the awards presentation ran exactly to the run of show, and the coordinator was visibly present throughout — available to the client without hovering. The post-event experience was equally professional: the coordinator called the following morning, sent a post-event report within four days, and — crucially — sent a personalised proposal for the following year's event within three weeks, incorporating specific improvements she had noted in her post-event review.

The firm re-booked. Then again. Then again. Over five years, the annual event grew from 60 to 120 guests and from £6,000 to £18,500 per event. The lead partner at the firm mentioned the hotel in a profile interview in a legal industry publication, which generated three new enquiries from other law firms. The hotel estimates that the total commercial value of the relationship — including the direct event revenue and the indirect referral value — exceeded £120,000 over the five-year period.

The cost of the coordinator's post-event communication programme — the calls, the reports, the follow-up proposals — was approximately four hours of staff time per event. The lesson: systematic post-event follow-up is not an administrative nicety. It is one of the highest-return commercial activities an events team can perform.

Case Study 2: The Event That Should Have Been Cancelled — Lessons from Poor Contingency Planning
How the absence of contingency planning turned a manageable problem into a significant reputational incident
This case study documents a composite scenario representative of event management failures documented across UK hospitality publications. It illustrates the commercial and reputational consequences of inadequate contingency planning and poor incident communication.

A hotel hosted a 200-person product launch event for a consumer goods company. The event required a complex AV setup: a live-streamed keynote presentation from the CEO, projected across four screens simultaneously, with a pre-recorded video product reveal timed to begin at the exact moment the CEO said a specific trigger phrase. The AV was being supplied by an external contractor who had assured the hotel's events team that all equipment had been tested and would be operational.

On the morning of the event, the external AV contractor arrived two hours late — a staffing issue within the contractor's team that the contractor had not communicated in advance. The tech check that was scheduled for 10am could not begin until noon. The event was due to start at 1pm. The coordinator decided not to inform the client immediately, hoping the situation could be resolved.

At 1pm, the tech check was still incomplete. The live-stream connection was unstable. The CEO stepped onto the stage at 1.15pm — fifteen minutes late — and the product reveal video failed to trigger correctly, playing three seconds before the trigger phrase and creating an awkward, confusing moment in front of 200 guests and an unknown number of live-stream viewers.

The client confronted the hotel's events manager after the event. Their view was simple: they had not been informed of the AV contractor's late arrival; they had not been given the opportunity to make contingency decisions; and the event's headline moment had been irreparably damaged in front of their most important trade customers.

The hotel apologised and offered a significant financial remedy. The client declined to rebook. The incident was shared within the client's industry peer network and the hotel received no further enquiries from that sector for over two years.

The lessons are threefold: external contractors must be contractually required to communicate disruptions immediately; clients must be informed of significant issues as soon as they are identified, not after the hotel has attempted to resolve them quietly; and a tech check that is not complete 90 minutes before an event of this complexity should trigger a client conversation about whether to proceed, delay, or implement a contingency format.
