Gross Development Value
The total market value of a completed development — calculated by multiplying the number of units (or the total floor area) by the assumed end sale price per unit (or per square metre). Gross Development Value (GDV) is the starting point of every development appraisal and the figure from which all other appraisal components are derived. For a residential scheme: GDV = Number of units × Average sale price per unit. For a commercial development: GDV = Net lettable area (sq m) × Market rent per sq m ÷ Yield. The GDV assumption is the most sensitive variable in any development appraisal — a 5% change in assumed sale prices or rents typically produces a change of 20–40% in residual land value. This sensitivity analysis is why developers conduct detailed market research before committing to a scheme and why they hedge their GDV assumptions with sales rate and pricing contingency allowances.

Residual Land Value
The maximum price a developer can afford to pay for a site, calculated as: Residual Land Value = Gross Development Value − Total Development Cost − Developer's Profit. Total development cost includes: construction costs; professional fees (architects, structural engineers, quantity surveyors — typically 8–12% of construction costs); finance costs (interest on construction debt throughout the build programme); marketing and sales costs; and any planning and s106 contributions required by the local authority. Developer's profit is typically expressed as a percentage of GDV (commonly 15–20% of GDV for residential, 15–25% for commercial) or as a percentage of total cost (20–25% on cost for speculative development). Residual land value analysis allows a developer to determine their maximum bid for a site and to test whether a site is viable at a given asking price — or to negotiate the land price down if the scheme's viability is marginal.

Development Profit
The financial return achieved by a developer after deducting all development costs (land, construction, fees, finance, and sales costs) from the gross development value. Development profit is the reward for the developer's risk-taking, expertise, and capital commitment. It is typically expressed in two ways: as a percentage of GDV (profit on GDV) and as a percentage of total cost (profit on cost). A profit on GDV of 20% is equivalent to a profit on cost of 25% (because cost is 80% of GDV). Minimum required profit levels depend on the risk profile of the scheme: a speculative development (with no pre-sales or pre-lets) carries more risk than a fully pre-sold or pre-let development and therefore requires a higher profit margin. Viability assessments submitted to local authorities as part of planning applications must demonstrate that the proposed development can generate sufficient profit to justify the development — including the delivery of any affordable housing contributions.

Capitalisation Rate
The rate at which the net operating income of a commercial property is capitalised to produce a capital value — equivalent in practice to the net initial yield used in UK commercial property investment. Capitalisation Rate = Net Operating Income ÷ Capital Value (expressed as a percentage). In the US market, 'cap rate' is the standard metric; in the UK market, 'net initial yield' is the equivalent term. The calculation is identical: a property generating £100,000 net operating income valued at a 5% cap rate has a capital value of £2,000,000. The cap rate reflects the market's assessment of the risk and growth potential associated with a particular property type, location, and income profile. Lower cap rates (implying higher capital values relative to income) reflect properties perceived as lower risk and higher quality; higher cap rates reflect secondary or riskier assets. Cap rate compression (falling cap rates) drives capital value growth; cap rate expansion (rising cap rates) drives capital value falls.

Return on Equity
The annual return generated on the equity capital invested in a property — calculated as: Net Annual Cash Flow ÷ Equity Invested × 100. Return on equity (ROE) takes into account the use of leverage: because debt allows the investor to acquire a more valuable asset than their equity alone would permit, the ROE is typically higher than the property's net yield when leverage is positive (i.e., when the net yield exceeds the cost of debt). Example: a property generating a net yield of 5.5% purchased with 70% LTV financing at 4.5% interest. The equity return exceeds the property yield because the investor earns 5.5% on the whole asset but only pays 4.5% on the 70% financed by debt — the 1% spread on the borrowed capital is earned by the equity investor. When debt costs exceed the property yield (negative leverage), ROE falls below the net yield.

Break-Even Analysis
A financial calculation that identifies the minimum level of income, occupancy, or rent needed for a property investment or development to cover all its costs — the point at which revenue equals total expenditure, producing neither profit nor loss. In property investment, break-even analysis is used in two main ways: to calculate the minimum occupancy rate needed to cover all costs in a multi-let commercial property (break-even occupancy = Total fixed costs ÷ Gross rent at full occupancy); and to calculate the minimum rent achievable at renewal or review that keeps the investment cash flow neutral after financing and operating costs. Break-even analysis is also central to development appraisal — developers calculate the minimum GDV (or minimum sales price per unit) at which the scheme generates the required minimum profit, allowing them to assess the margin of safety in their scheme.

Effective Rent
The true annual rent cost to a tenant after accounting for any rent-free periods, stepped rents, or capital contributions from the landlord — used to compare leases that have different incentive structures on a consistent basis. Headline rent (the rent stated in the lease) may significantly overstate the cost to the tenant during the early years if a rent-free period has been granted. Effective rent is calculated as: (Headline Rent × Rent-Paying Months) ÷ Total Lease Months. Example: a 10-year lease at £100,000 per annum with a 12-month rent-free period has an effective rent of (£100,000 × 9 years) ÷ 10 years = £90,000 per annum. For landlords comparing competing offers from tenants, effective rent is the correct metric — a higher headline rent with a long rent-free period may produce a lower effective rent than a lower headline rent with no incentive. For valuers, the agreed effective rent is the basis for comparable evidence in subsequent rent reviews.

Debt Service Coverage Ratio
The ratio of a property's net operating income to its total annual debt service (interest plus principal repayment) — a key metric used by commercial property lenders to assess the adequacy of a property's income to service its debt. DSCR = Net Operating Income ÷ Annual Debt Service. Most commercial property lenders require a minimum DSCR of 1.25 to 1.35 — meaning the property must generate £1.25 to £1.35 in net income for every £1 of debt service. Unlike the residential buy-to-let ICR (which is calculated on interest only), the commercial DSCR typically includes principal repayment as well as interest — reflecting the expectation that commercial mortgages are amortising rather than interest-only. A DSCR below 1.0 means the property's income is insufficient to cover its debt service — the borrower must fund the shortfall from other sources or risk default.

Price Per Square Metre
A standardised metric expressing the value or cost of a property per unit of floor area — used to compare properties of different sizes on a consistent basis. In commercial property, both capital values and rental values are almost universally expressed per square metre (or per square foot in traditional UK practice). This allows direct comparison: a 500 sq m office generating £80 per sq m rent produces £40,000 per annum; a 1,000 sq m office generating £75 per sq m produces £75,000 per annum — the second generates more total rent but at a lower rate per square metre. In residential property, price per square metre is increasingly used by valuers and analysts — particularly in London and major cities — to compare properties across different sizes and types. The metric is calculated as: Total Price ÷ Net Internal Area (sq m). Net internal area excludes structural walls, shared circulation space, and other non-lettable areas.

Payback Period
The time required to recover the initial capital investment in a property from its net cash flows — expressed in years. Payback Period = Initial Investment ÷ Annual Net Cash Flow. Example: a buy-to-let property purchased for £200,000 with a £50,000 cash equity investment (after 75% LTV mortgage), generating a net annual cash flow of £2,500 after mortgage payments and costs, has a payback period of 50,000 ÷ 2,500 = 20 years. The payback period is a simple, intuitive measure of investment risk — the shorter the payback period, the sooner the investor recovers their capital and the lower their risk exposure. However, it is limited as a metric because it ignores cash flows received after the payback date (and therefore does not capture the total return), does not account for the time value of money, and is insensitive to the magnitude of returns beyond the payback point. It is useful as a quick screening tool but should be used alongside IRR and NPV for full investment analysis.

Stamp Duty Land Tax Calculation
Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT) in England is calculated on a tiered basis — different percentage rates apply to different portions of the purchase price. For residential property (standard rates): 0% on the first £125,000; 2% on £125,001 to £250,000; 5% on £250,001 to £925,000; 10% on £925,001 to £1,500,000; 12% above £1,500,000. For second homes and investment properties, an additional 3% surcharge applies to every band. First-time buyers are eligible for relief on properties up to £500,000 (0% on first £425,000; 5% on £425,001 to £500,000). SDLT is one of the most significant transaction costs in UK property — on a £500,000 investment property purchase, the standard SDLT would be £12,500 plus the 3% surcharge of £15,000 = £27,500. SDLT must be paid within 14 days of completion and is typically handled by the buyer's solicitor.

Capital Gains Tax Calculation
Capital Gains Tax (CGT) is payable by individuals on the profit made from selling residential investment property — calculated as the sale price minus the original purchase price minus allowable costs (solicitor's fees, SDLT, costs of capital improvements, agent's fees on sale). The taxable gain is then reduced by the Annual Exempt Amount (£6,000 in 2023/24; reduced from previous years as part of the government's CGT reform programme). CGT on residential property is charged at 18% for basic rate taxpayers and 24% for higher and additional rate taxpayers (following changes from April 2024). CGT must be reported and paid within 60 days of completion of the sale. Limited companies pay corporation tax (25%) on chargeable gains rather than CGT — the difference is a key consideration when structuring a property investment portfolio. Business Asset Disposal Relief (formerly Entrepreneurs' Relief) does not typically apply to residential property investment.

Mortgage Affordability Calculation
The process by which a lender assesses whether a borrower can afford to service a mortgage — either on a personal income basis (residential mortgages) or on a rental income basis (buy-to-let mortgages). For residential mortgages: affordability is calculated as a multiple of the borrower's gross annual income (typically 4 to 4.5 times income for standard residential mortgages), subject to an affordability assessment that considers monthly outgoings, existing debt, and the impact of potential interest rate rises. For buy-to-let mortgages: affordability is calculated using the ICR — the annual rent must cover the annual interest at a stressed rate (typically 5.5%) by a minimum of 125% to 145%. Example: a £150,000 buy-to-let mortgage at 5.5% = £8,250 annual interest. At 145% ICR, the minimum annual rent required is £8,250 × 1.45 = £11,963. If the actual rent is £12,000, the property just passes the affordability test.

Rent Review Calculation
The process of determining the new rent payable under a commercial lease at the contractual rent review date — typically conducted by comparing the current rent with the current open market rent for comparable properties, supported by comparable evidence of recent lettings. Most UK commercial leases include upward-only rent reviews every five years — meaning the rent can increase at review but cannot fall below the current level. The review is conducted by the landlord's and tenant's surveyors, who gather comparable evidence (recent lettings of similar properties in similar locations on similar lease terms) and negotiate the new rent. If agreement cannot be reached, the lease typically provides for the appointment of an independent surveyor to determine the new rent — either as an expert or as an arbitrator. The new rent is backdated to the review date, so any outstanding rent must be paid with interest from that date.

Net Operating Income
The annual rental income from a commercial property after deducting all operating expenses paid by the landlord — used as the primary input to commercial property valuation and investment analysis. NOI = Gross Rental Income − Landlord's Operating Expenses. Landlord's operating expenses typically include: property management fees; insurance (building insurance); maintenance and repair costs (unless borne by the tenant under an FRI lease); void costs (when the property is vacant); and any service charge deficit (where the service charge collected from tenants is insufficient to cover the actual service charge expenditure). Under a full repairing and insuring (FRI) lease, the tenant bears all or most operating costs — so the NOI is close to the headline rent. Under a gross lease (where the landlord pays operating costs), the NOI may be significantly below the headline rent. NOI is the numerator in the cap rate formula and the key input to DCF modelling.

Finance Cost Calculation
In development appraisal, the calculation of the interest cost on the money borrowed to fund the construction programme — one of the most significant and variable costs in any development budget. Finance costs are calculated as: Average Debt × Interest Rate × Build Period. Because construction draws down progressively (the developer does not borrow all the money on day one — it is drawn as construction proceeds), the average debt is typically approximately half the peak debt over the build programme. A developer who borrows £10 million to fund a two-year build at 7% interest would have finance costs of approximately: (£10,000,000 ÷ 2) × 7% × 2 years = £700,000. Finance costs also include the arrangement fee and any monitoring fees charged by the lender. In a sensitivity analysis, finance costs are a key variable — a 1% increase in the interest rate during a two-year build programme on a £10 million loan adds approximately £100,000 to the development cost, reducing residual land value by the same amount.

Equivalent Yield
A blended yield that reflects the expected return from a commercial property investment over time — taking into account both the current (initial) income and the anticipated reversionary income at future rent reviews or lease renewals. Unlike the net initial yield (which is based on the current passing rent) or the reversionary yield (which is based on the estimated rental value), the equivalent yield averages the two — weighted by the timing of the expected income changes. Equivalent yield is particularly useful for comparing properties where the current rent differs significantly from the market rent: a property that is over-rented (passing rent above market) has a high initial yield but the equivalent yield (which incorporates the expected fall in rent at review) will be lower. Conversely, an under-rented property (passing rent below market) has a low initial yield but a higher equivalent yield. The equivalent yield is calculated using a discounted cash flow model and is the yield metric most commonly used by valuers in formal commercial property valuations.

Blended Rate Calculation
A calculation that produces a single weighted average interest rate across a property portfolio — used to assess the overall cost of debt when different loans carry different interest rates and are of different sizes. Blended Rate = Σ (Loan Amount × Interest Rate) ÷ Total Debt. Example: Loan 1: £500,000 at 5.5%. Loan 2: £750,000 at 6.0%. Loan 3: £250,000 at 6.5%. Total debt: £1,500,000. Weighted interest: (500,000 × 5.5%) + (750,000 × 6.0%) + (250,000 × 6.5%) = 27,500 + 45,000 + 16,250 = £88,750. Blended rate = £88,750 ÷ £1,500,000 = 5.92%. The blended rate allows a portfolio investor to compare their overall cost of debt against the portfolio's blended net yield — quickly identifying whether the portfolio is positively or negatively geared at the portfolio level.

Uplift Calculation
A calculation used in development and value-add investment appraisal to quantify the increase in value achieved through an improvement — expressed as a percentage of the cost of the improvement or as a percentage of the opening value. In residential property: if a landlord spends £20,000 refurbishing a flat that was valued at £180,000 (unrefurbished) and that is subsequently valued at £215,000 (refurbished), the uplift in value is £35,000 against a spend of £20,000 — a return on cost of 175%. In commercial property: if an investor acquires a vacant building for £1.5 million, spends £500,000 on refurbishment, and lets it at a rent that produces a GDV of £2.8 million, the total profit is £2.8 million − £1.5 million − £0.5 million − other costs of say £150,000 = £650,000. Uplift calculations are used by value-add investors to assess whether the cost of an improvement is justified by the expected increase in value — and at what risk.

Sensitivity Analysis Calculation
A financial modelling technique that tests the impact of changing one or more key assumptions on the output of an appraisal — revealing which variables have the greatest impact on the result and what level of adverse movement the investment can withstand before becoming unviable. In a development appraisal, the most common sensitivity tests are: a reduction in GDV (e.g., what happens if sales prices are 5% or 10% lower than assumed?); an increase in build costs (e.g., what happens if construction costs are 10% higher?); and an extension of the build period (increasing finance costs). In an investment appraisal, sensitivity tests typically include: an increase in the exit yield (reducing the exit capital value); a reduction in rental income (e.g., from a void period or a rent-free period granted to a new tenant); and an increase in the cost of debt. A well-presented sensitivity analysis shows the impact of each variable independently and in combination — allowing decision-makers to understand the full range of outcomes.

Comparable Analysis
The systematic process of gathering, adjusting, and applying evidence from transactions in similar properties to support a valuation, a price negotiation, or a rent review. Comparable analysis requires: identifying transactions that are genuinely comparable (similar type, size, location, specification, and tenure); adjusting for differences between the comparable and the subject property (time elapsed since the transaction; differences in size, condition, or floor level); and applying the adjusted evidence to the subject property in a structured, supportable way. In residential valuation, comparables are typically expressed as price per square metre — allowing comparison across properties of different sizes. In commercial valuation, comparables are expressed as rent per square metre and yield (for investment transactions). A well-constructed comparable analysis presents the best three to five transactions in a table, shows the adjustments made, and demonstrates how the subject property's value has been derived. It is the primary evidence base for both formal valuations and informal negotiations.

Text 1: Residential Property Financial Appraisal — The Calculations Every Property Professional Must Know
The core financial calculations used in residential investment analysis, mortgage assessment, and property valuation

Financial literacy is a non-negotiable requirement for any property professional advising clients on residential investment or purchase decisions. Whether calculating the gross yield on a buy-to-let property, assessing the stamp duty cost on a second home purchase, or explaining the impact of leverage on an investor's equity return, the ability to perform and clearly explain these calculations in English — fluently, accurately, and accessibly — distinguishes the trusted adviser from the merely knowledgeable estate agent. This text sets out the core calculations that every residential property professional should be able to perform and explain.

The yield calculation family begins with gross yield — annual rent divided by purchase price, multiplied by 100. A property purchased for £250,000 generating £13,500 per annum has a gross yield of 5.4%. Net yield subtracts annual costs from the gross rent before dividing by the purchase price: if management costs are £1,350 (10%), maintenance £800, insurance £300, and void allowance £1,125 (one month), the net income is £13,500 − £3,575 = £9,925, giving a net yield of 3.97%. Neither of these figures accounts for financing. Cash-on-cash return incorporates the mortgage: if the investor uses 70% LTV financing at 5.5% on £175,000 (interest = £9,625 per annum), the net cash flow is £9,925 − £9,625 = £300 per annum on £75,000 equity — a cash-on-cash return of just 0.4%. This thin return illustrates the challenge of financing residential investment at high LTV in a high interest rate environment.

The stamp duty calculation is essential for any property professional advising clients on acquisition costs. For an investment property purchased at £350,000, the calculation is: 0% on £125,000 = £0; 2% on £125,000 (£125,001–£250,000) = £2,500; 5% on £100,000 (£250,001–£350,000) = £5,000; plus the 3% additional rate surcharge on the full £350,000 = £10,500. Total SDLT: £0 + £2,500 + £5,000 + £10,500 = £18,000. Expressed as a percentage of the purchase price: 5.14%. This acquisition cost is often underestimated by first-time investors and significantly affects the total investment deployed. A complete acquisition cost calculation adds solicitor's fees (approximately £1,200–£2,000), survey (£400–£800), and any initial refurbishment, to arrive at the total capital required — the correct denominator for cash-on-cash return analysis.

Text 2: Commercial Property Development Appraisal — How Developers Assess Viability and Land Value
The mechanics of development appraisal and why residual land value analysis is central to property development economics

Development appraisal is the financial analysis that determines whether a proposed development scheme is economically viable and, if so, what price a developer can afford to pay for the land on which it will be built. Every development project — whether a residential housing scheme, a commercial office building, or a mixed-use regeneration project — is underpinned by a development appraisal that works backwards from the expected value of the completed development (the GDV) to derive the maximum land value. Understanding development appraisal is essential for any property professional involved in development, planning, or land and development agency.

The structure of a development appraisal follows a consistent logic. Starting with the GDV: for a residential scheme of 20 units with an assumed average sale price of £350,000 per unit, the GDV is £7,000,000. From this, the developer deducts all development costs: construction costs at £1,800 per square metre for 1,500 square metres of total floor area = £2,700,000; professional fees at 12% of construction = £324,000; finance costs (assuming the average debt is half the total cost and the build period is 18 months at 7%) = approximately £283,000; marketing and sales costs at 3% of GDV = £210,000; and s106/CIL planning contributions of £200,000. Total development costs: £3,717,000. Developer's profit at 18% of GDV: £1,260,000. Residual land value = GDV − Total Costs − Profit = £7,000,000 − £3,717,000 − £1,260,000 = £2,023,000. This is the maximum the developer can pay for the land and still achieve their required profit.

The most important insight in development appraisal is the sensitivity of residual land value to changes in the GDV and construction cost assumptions. If the average sale price falls by 5% to £332,500 per unit, the GDV falls to £6,650,000 — a reduction of £350,000. Residual land value falls from £2,023,000 to £1,673,000 — a fall of 17.3% from a 5% reduction in GDV. This disproportionate impact is because land is the residual — after all fixed costs (construction, fees, profit requirement) are met, the reduction in GDV flows almost entirely through to the land value. Conversely, if construction costs increase by 10%, the residual land value falls by approximately 13%. The combination of a modest fall in GDV and a modest increase in costs can render a scheme completely unviable — making the land worthless. This is why land values are so volatile at the margin of development viability, and why developers insist on significant contingency allowances in their appraisals.

True or False — click your answer
1. Gross Development Value (GDV) is the starting point for a development appraisal — calculated as the total value of the completed scheme from which all costs and profit are deducted to derive the residual land value.
2. Development profit is always expressed as a percentage of the land value — a 20% profit on land is the standard requirement for speculative residential development.
3. Effective rent is lower than headline rent when a rent-free period has been granted — it represents the true cost to the tenant averaged across the full lease term.
4. The Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR) is calculated the same way as the Interest Coverage Ratio (ICR) — both use annual interest only as the denominator.
5. A capitalisation rate of 5% applied to a net operating income of £100,000 produces a capital value of £2,000,000 — calculated as NOI ÷ cap rate.
6. In a development appraisal, a 5% fall in GDV produces approximately a 5% fall in residual land value — the impact is proportionate because land value is one of the fixed costs.
7. The equivalent yield in commercial property is always lower than the net initial yield when a property is over-rented — because the equivalent yield incorporates the expected fall in income at review.
8. Break-even analysis in a multi-let property identifies the occupancy rate at which gross rent equals total fixed costs — below this level the investment generates a loss.
9. A blended rate calculation produces a single average interest rate weighted by the size of each loan — used to assess whether the portfolio is positively or negatively geared at the portfolio level.
10. In UK practice, price per square metre is only used for commercial property — residential property is always compared using price per bedroom.

Case Study 1: The Viability Challenge — When Development Economics and Affordable Housing Obligations Collide
How development appraisal is used to negotiate planning obligations and why the residual model is both a planning tool and a negotiating instrument
One of the most practically significant applications of development appraisal in modern property practice is not in determining whether a scheme is profitable for the developer, but in negotiating with local planning authorities over affordable housing obligations and planning contributions. Under the UK planning system, local authorities are entitled to require developers to provide a proportion of new residential development as affordable housing — typically 25% to 40% of units. Where a developer argues that this level of affordable housing makes a scheme unviable, they are required to submit a development viability assessment to the planning authority, supported by a detailed development appraisal.

The viability assessment follows the same residual model described in this module: GDV minus development costs minus developer's profit equals residual land value. If the residual land value is below the benchmark land value (the value at which a reasonable landowner would be willing to sell — typically assessed as the existing use value plus a premium), the development is considered unviable at the required affordable housing level, and the planning authority and developer negotiate a reduced obligation.

This process has created a significant body of professional practice and dispute. Developers and planning authorities often produce dramatically different viability assessments using the same underlying residual methodology — because the assumptions made in the appraisal (GDV, construction costs, finance costs, and required profit) are each subject to professional judgement and the parties have directly opposing interests. A developer seeking to reduce an affordable housing obligation has an incentive to use pessimistic GDV assumptions and high cost assumptions; a planning authority seeking to maximise affordable housing has an incentive to challenge those assumptions. The resolution of these disputes typically involves expert evidence from surveyors on both sides, site inspections, analysis of comparable evidence, and — ultimately — a negotiated compromise. The ability to read, construct, and critically appraise a development appraisal is therefore a core skill for any surveyor or planning consultant working in this field.

Case Study 2: The Comparable Evidence Battle — How Rent Review Disputes Are Won and Lost on the Quality of Evidence
Why the quality, recency, and comparability of transaction evidence is more important than the sophistication of the adjustment methodology
Commercial rent review disputes are won or lost on evidence — not on the elegance of the adjustment methodology. A surveyor who has three genuinely comparable recent lettings at a rent that supports their position will almost always prevail over one who has a sophisticated adjustment methodology applied to weak or dated comparables. This case study examines a representative rent review dispute and illustrates the central role of comparable evidence.

A landlord's surveyor was seeking to increase the rent on a 1,200 sq m office suite from £90,000 per annum to £108,000 — a 20% increase. The tenant's surveyor argued that the market was flat and that the passing rent already reflected the market level. The landlord's surveyor had two comparables: a 600 sq m suite in the same building let 22 months ago at £95 per sq m; and a 1,500 sq m suite in a neighbouring building let 6 months ago at £88 per sq m. The tenant's surveyor had three comparables: the two used by the landlord, plus a 1,100 sq m suite in the same building let 8 months ago at £85 per sq m — which the landlord's surveyor had not produced.

The key dispute centred on the 22-month-old comparable. The landlord applied a 15% time adjustment — arguing the market had risen 15% over 22 months, supported by a general market report. The tenant disputed this entirely, noting that the specific sub-market showed no such growth and that the report covered a broader area. The independent expert appointed under the lease agreed with the tenant: in the absence of specific evidence of rental growth in this sub-market, no time adjustment was warranted. The determination was based primarily on the 8-month-old comparable at £85 per sq m, adjusted upward by 5% for the smaller size of the subject unit (1,200 vs 1,100 sq m). Determined rent: £85 × 1.05 × 1,200 = £107,100 — rounded to £107,000. Below the landlord's opening position, above the tenant's. The lesson: the surveyor who found and produced the more recent comparable won the dispute.
