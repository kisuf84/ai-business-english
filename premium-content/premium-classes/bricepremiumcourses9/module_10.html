Reactive Maintenance
Repair and maintenance work carried out in response to a reported defect, failure, or emergency — as distinct from planned preventive maintenance, which is scheduled in advance. Reactive maintenance is typically more expensive than planned maintenance because it involves call-out fees, emergency rates, and the cost of temporary measures taken while a permanent repair is arranged. It also creates more disruption for occupiers and greater risk of consequential damage: a roof leak left unaddressed for weeks causes far more damage than one attended to promptly. Property managers who rely entirely on reactive maintenance typically find their managed properties deteriorate faster and cost more to maintain over time than those managed with a balance of planned and reactive approaches.

Planned Preventive Maintenance
A proactive approach to property maintenance in which routine inspections, servicing, and minor repairs are scheduled in advance — based on the known lifespans of building components and systems — rather than waiting for failures to occur. Planned preventive maintenance (PPM) programmes typically cover: annual boiler and gas appliance servicing; periodic electrical testing (EICR — required every five years for rental properties); fire safety equipment testing; external decoration cycles (typically every five to seven years); roof and gutter inspections (typically annually or after severe weather); and damp-proof course checking. A well-designed PPM programme reduces the total cost of maintenance over time, extends the working life of building components, reduces the risk of regulatory non-compliance, and demonstrates professional management to landlord clients.
▼ Show definition
🔍
Property Inspection
A structured visit to a managed property to assess its condition, identify any maintenance requirements, check that the tenant is complying with their tenancy obligations, and provide a formal written report to the landlord. Property inspections are a core service provided by managing agents and are typically carried out quarterly for residential managed properties, with a more comprehensive annual inspection that includes external areas, roof condition, and services. The inspection report should: record the overall condition of the property; note any specific defects or maintenance requirements identified; photograph any areas of concern; identify any tenant obligation issues; and make recommendations for action.
▼ Show definition
📋
Condition Report
A formal written document recording the condition of a property at a specific point in time — used as evidence of the property's state at the start and end of a tenancy (check-in and check-out), after a maintenance intervention, or at the point of instruction of a management mandate. A comprehensive condition report describes the condition of every room, every significant surface, every fixture and fitting, and all services and appliances — typically using standardised condition ratings (excellent, good, fair, poor, requires attention) supported by dated photographs. Condition reports are the primary evidence in deposit disputes and dilapidations claims.

Defect
A fault, failure, or shortcoming in a building element that requires repair, replacement, or remediation. Defects in property maintenance are classified by urgency: emergency defects (immediate risk to safety — a burst pipe, a faulty boiler, a broken door lock); urgent defects (significant inconvenience or risk of consequential damage — a leaking roof, a broken heating system in winter); and routine defects (no immediate risk — a dripping tap, a loose tile, a worn door seal). By cause: structural defects (arising from the building's fabric); services defects (failure of heating, plumbing, or electrical systems); wear and deterioration (natural degradation); and damage (deliberate or negligent harm caused by an occupier).
▼ Show definition
📝
Specification of Works
A written document describing in precise detail the scope, standard, and method of the repair or maintenance work to be carried out — used as the basis for obtaining contractor quotations and for verifying that work has been completed to the required standard. A good specification includes: the location of the works; the nature of the defect or requirement; the materials and components to be used; the method of repair; the standard to which work is to be completed (all works to be completed to a good and workmanlike standard and in accordance with current building regulations); any programme requirements; and any special requirements such as access arrangements or occupied property protocols.
▼ Show definition
🚨
Emergency Repair
A maintenance issue that requires immediate attention — typically within 24 hours — because it poses a risk to the safety of occupiers, causes ongoing significant damage to the property, or makes the property uninhabitable. Emergency repairs in residential lettings typically include: total heating failure in cold weather; major water ingress (burst pipe, serious roof leak); complete loss of hot water; failure of a gas appliance where there is a suspected gas leak; security failures following a break-in; and loss of electricity. Letting agents are typically authorised by their landlord clients to spend up to a pre-agreed limit (often £250 to £500) on emergency repairs without prior landlord approval, to avoid delays that could worsen the situation or endanger the tenant.
▼ Show definition
🏠
Void Property
A property that is temporarily unoccupied — between tenancies, during refurbishment, or as a result of an extended marketing period. Void properties require active management: they are at greater risk of vandalism, water damage (from undetected leaks or burst pipes in winter), and deterioration than occupied properties. Standard void property checks typically include: weekly visual inspections; ensuring utilities are maintained (mains water should be isolated and the system drained in frost risk); checking that all locks are secure; and maintaining adequate insurance cover (many standard policies require notification if a property is unoccupied for more than 30 days).

🔩
Snagging List
A list of defects, incomplete works, or minor finishing issues identified at the end of a construction or refurbishment project — typically produced by the client or their surveyor during a final inspection before practical completion is certified. The snagging list is presented to the contractor, who is required to address all items before the retention money (typically 2.5 to 5% of the contract sum) is released. In a new-build property context, the developer is obliged to address all items under the new-build warranty. Snagging lists typically include: areas where decoration is incomplete or poor; fittings that are not properly fixed; appliances that have not been tested; and doors and windows that do not close or seal properly.

💧
Damp Report
A professional assessment of the cause, extent, and severity of damp in a property — typically produced by a specialist surveyor or damp-proofing contractor. Damp in residential properties has several distinct causes: rising damp (moisture travelling up through masonry from ground level — caused by failure of the damp-proof course); penetrating damp (moisture entering through external walls, roof, or around windows — caused by defective pointing, failed renders, or blocked gutters); and condensation damp (moisture from cooking, bathing, and breathing condensing on cold surfaces — a function of heating, ventilation, and insulation). A damp report should identify the specific type and cause of damp, assess the extent of damage, and recommend a proportionate remediation programme.
▼ Show definition
⚡
EICR
Electrical Installation Condition Report — a formal inspection and test of a property's fixed electrical installation carried out by a qualified electrician, producing a written report on the safety and condition of the installation. EICRs are legally required for all rental properties in England: since April 2021, landlords must ensure the electrical installation is inspected at least every five years, that a copy of the EICR is provided to the tenant, and that any C1 (danger present) or C2 (potentially dangerous) defects are remediated within 28 days. Failure to comply with the EICR regulations is a criminal offence and can result in a local authority civil penalty of up to £30,000.

🔥
Gas Safety Certificate
A document produced by a Gas Safe registered engineer following the annual safety inspection and service of gas appliances in a residential property — confirming that all gas appliances, flues, and pipework have been inspected and are safe for use. Landlords are legally required under the Gas Safety (Installation and Use) Regulations 1998 to: ensure all gas appliances and flues are inspected annually; obtain a Gas Safety Certificate (CP12) at each inspection; provide a copy to the tenant within 28 days of the inspection (or before the start of a new tenancy); and retain the previous two years' records. Failure to comply is a criminal offence.

Dilapidations Schedule
A formal document prepared by a landlord's surveyor at the end of a commercial lease — listing all items of disrepair and deterioration for which the tenant is liable under their repairing obligations, together with the estimated cost of remediation. The dilapidations schedule is typically served on the tenant before or at the expiry of the lease, giving the tenant the opportunity to dispute the items, carry out some or all of the repairs themselves, or negotiate a financial settlement. Commercial leases are typically full repairing and insuring (FRI), making the tenant responsible for all repairs during the term.
▼ Show definition
🔑
Landlord Obligations
The statutory and contractual repair and maintenance duties owed by a landlord to their tenant — including the implied repair obligations under the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 (structural integrity, exterior, services), the safety obligations imposed by the Gas Safety Regulations, the EICR Regulations, smoke and carbon monoxide alarm requirements, and the fitness for habitation standard under the Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018. Landlord obligations are not negotiable — a clause in a tenancy agreement that purports to transfer the landlord's statutory repair obligations to the tenant is void. Letting agents who manage properties typically assume responsibility for ensuring that these obligations are met.

🌡️
Thermal Comfort
The condition in which a property is maintained at a temperature adequate for the health and reasonable comfort of the occupants — a component of the fitness for human habitation standard that landlords are legally required to meet. Thermal comfort is affected by the property's insulation, glazing, heating system, and draughtproofing. The Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS) identifies excess cold as one of the most serious Category 1 hazards in the private rented sector. Landlords who fail to address thermal comfort issues promptly risk enforcement action by the local authority housing team.

Maintenance Budget
A financial plan allocating funds for the repair, maintenance, and improvement of a property or portfolio over a defined period — typically one year for routine maintenance and five to ten years for major capital expenditure. A property maintenance budget typically includes: a reactive maintenance allowance (typically 1 to 2% of the property's value per annum for an older property); planned preventive maintenance costs; planned capital expenditure (boiler replacement, kitchen and bathroom renewal); and a void property maintenance allowance. Presenting a clear and realistic maintenance budget to landlord clients is an important part of professional property management.

🏢
Contractor Management
The process of selecting, instructing, supervising, and evaluating contractors who carry out maintenance and repair work on managed properties. Effective contractor management involves: maintaining an approved contractor list of vetted, insured, and appropriately qualified contractors; obtaining competitive quotations for non-emergency works above a defined value threshold; issuing clear written instructions; supervising the works or arranging a post-works inspection; verifying invoices against agreed prices; and monitoring contractor performance over time. Managing agents must ensure that contractors carry appropriate insurance, and that tradespeople carrying out regulated work (gas, electrical) hold the required qualifications.

📐
Schedule of Condition
A document — typically accompanied by photographs — recording the physical condition of a property at a specific point in time, used to establish a baseline against which future deterioration can be assessed. In the context of property maintenance, schedules of condition are used: at the start of a commercial lease to limit the tenant's repairing obligations to maintaining the property in no worse condition than at the start; at the point of instruction of a management mandate; and as part of a pre-purchase survey. A well-prepared schedule with comprehensive photographs is significantly more evidentially valuable than a vague narrative description.

🔐
Compliance Inspection
A formal inspection of a property to verify that it meets all applicable safety, legal, and regulatory requirements — including smoke and carbon monoxide alarms (legal requirement in England since 2022 for all tenancies); gas safety (annual CP12 certificate); electrical safety (five-yearly EICR); legionella risk assessment; and fire safety requirements. Compliance inspections are typically carried out at the start of each new tenancy as part of the move-in process, and at regular intervals during managed tenancies. Letting agents who manage properties typically take responsibility for maintaining a compliance calendar that tracks the expiry dates of all certificates.

Works Order
A formal written instruction issued by a property manager to a contractor, authorising the contractor to carry out specified maintenance or repair work at a specific property — and forming the contractual basis for the engagement. A well-drafted works order includes: the property address; the date of issue; a description of the works to be carried out; the agreed price (or estimated budget for day-rate works); the start and completion dates; access arrangements; any special requirements; and the agent's authorisation signature. The works order protects all parties by confirming the scope of authorised works, the price, and the programme — reducing the risk of disputed invoices and scope creep.

Structural Defect
A fault in the load-bearing elements of a building — foundations, walls, floors, roof structure, or the connections between them — that affects the structural integrity of the building. Structural defects are the most serious category of building defect because they can threaten the safety of occupiers and may be very costly to remediate. Common structural defects include: subsidence (downward movement of the foundations caused by shrinkage of soil); settlement (gradual downward movement as the building's weight compresses the soil); heave (upward movement caused by expansion of clay soil); and structural movement caused by defective construction. Structural defects require investigation by a structural engineer.

Text 1: Planned Preventive Maintenance — Why Professional Property Managers Schedule Maintenance in Advance
The business case for planned maintenance, the key maintenance cycles, and the obligations of managing agents

The distinction between reactive and planned preventive maintenance is one of the most important concepts in professional property management. Reactive maintenance — fixing things when they break — is the default approach for many landlords, particularly those managing their own properties without professional assistance. It feels economical: why spend money on servicing a boiler that appears to be working? The problem with this approach becomes apparent over time. A boiler that receives no annual service is more likely to fail suddenly, typically at the worst possible time — on the coldest day of the year, when an emergency call-out costs three times the price of a routine service and a replacement is needed at short notice. A roof that is never inspected gradually develops minor defects that allow water ingress, which causes damage to ceilings and walls that costs far more to repair than the periodic gutter-clearing and re-pointing that would have prevented it.

Professional managing agents typically implement a planned preventive maintenance (PPM) programme covering all properties in their portfolio. The core elements of a PPM programme for a residential rental property are: annual gas safety inspection and service by a Gas Safe registered engineer (a legal requirement); Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR) every five years by a qualified electrician (a legal requirement since April 2021); smoke alarm testing at the start of each tenancy and periodic checks during managed tenancies; carbon monoxide alarm testing where there are gas or solid fuel appliances; legionella risk assessment; annual boiler service beyond the legal gas safety check; periodic external decoration and timber treatment (typically every five to seven years); annual gutter inspection and clearance; and periodic roof inspection annually or after severe weather events.

Managing agents who implement a PPM programme demonstrate professional management to their landlord clients, reduce the total cost of maintenance over the property's life, maintain regulatory compliance, and significantly reduce the risk of emergency repairs. They also build a comprehensive maintenance history for each property — a record that is valuable when properties change hands, when disputes arise, and when insurance claims are made. The cost of a PPM programme is not an additional expense — it is an investment that pays for itself many times over in reduced reactive maintenance costs, extended component lifespans, and avoided regulatory penalties.

Text 2: Reporting Defects and Managing Contractors — The Language and Practice of Property Maintenance
How to describe defects precisely, specify repair requirements clearly, and manage contractors professionally

Effective property maintenance depends on precise communication at every stage — from the moment a defect is identified or reported to the completion and verification of the remedial work. Imprecise communication is one of the most common causes of maintenance failures: a contractor who receives a vague instruction (the boiler is not working) will attend, assess, and likely return for a second visit with the correct parts; a contractor who receives a precise instruction (the combination boiler, Vaillant EcoTec Plus 830, is failing to ignite — the pilot light comes on but the main burner does not fire; the fault code displayed is F75) will attend with a much higher probability of resolving the issue on the first visit.

The standard framework for reporting a maintenance defect follows a consistent structure. Location: where in the property is the defect? Nature: what is the defect? Extent: how large or widespread is the defect? History: when was the defect first noticed, and has it changed since? Urgency: what level of response is required? A defect report that follows this structure gives the contractor, the landlord, and the property manager all the information they need to respond appropriately. For example: the kitchen ceiling at first-floor level shows a water stain approximately 600mm in diameter immediately below the bathroom; the stain was first noticed by the tenant approximately three weeks ago and has grown in the past week suggesting an active leak; response required within 48 hours.

Managing contractors effectively requires clear instructions, realistic expectations, and consistent follow-up. The works order — the formal written instruction to the contractor — should specify the scope of work, the agreed price, the completion date, and the access arrangements. After the work is completed, the property manager or their representative should carry out a post-works inspection to verify that the work has been completed to the specified standard before authorising payment. Good contractor relationships are built on fair treatment, consistent communication, and honest feedback — both positive and negative. A portfolio manager who maintains a panel of reliable, fairly-treated contractors is in a significantly better position to manage emergency repairs and difficult situations than one who constantly switches contractors based purely on price.

Exercise 1 — Grammar 1: Describing Defects — Location, Nature, Extent, and Urgency

Maintenance reporting requires precise, structured language to describe defects so that contractors and landlords can respond appropriately. Every defect report should cover location, nature, extent, and urgency.

Location language:

  • 'Located in the master bedroom, north-facing wall, approximately 300mm from the ceiling.'
  • 'On the south elevation at first-floor level, above the bay window.'

Nature and extent language:

  • 'A water stain approximately 600mm in diameter with active dripping.'
  • 'Cracked render extending approximately 1 metre from the window reveal.'

Urgency language:

  • 'Emergency — immediate action required.' / 'Urgent — action within 48 hours.'
  • 'Routine — schedule at next available opportunity.'

Exercise 2 — Grammar 2: Commissioning Contractors — Instruction Language and Works Orders

Works orders and contractor instructions use formal directive language, specification structures, and clear conditions to ensure the contractor knows exactly what to do, to what standard, by when, and for how much.

Instructing the works:

  • 'You are hereby instructed to...' / 'Please attend and carry out the following works...'
  • 'The scope of works is as follows...'

Specifying standard and materials:

  • 'All works to be completed to a good and workmanlike standard.'
  • 'Materials to match existing as closely as possible.'

Conditions and authority:

  • 'Your authority for this instruction is £250 including VAT.'
  • 'Please contact us before proceeding if the cost is likely to exceed this amount.'

Case Study 1: The £800 Repair That Became a £45,000 Claim — How Deferred Maintenance Creates Catastrophic Liability
A representative case study illustrating the financial and legal consequences of failing to address reported maintenance issues promptly
This case study follows a fictional but representative sequence of events illustrating how a simple maintenance failure, compounded by poor communication and failure to act, can result in catastrophic financial and legal consequences.

A residential tenant reported a damp patch on the bedroom wall at the start of winter. The managing agent passed the report to the landlord, who said he would have a look at it over Christmas. The Christmas visit did not happen. By January the damp patch had spread significantly and mould was forming. The tenant reported this to the managing agent again. The agent sent a reminder to the landlord. The landlord visited in February and said the damp looked like condensation — the tenant's problem, not his. No contractor was instructed.

By March the tenant had been sleeping in the living room because the bedroom mould was so extensive she felt unable to sleep there. She formally complained to the managing agent and was advised to contact the local authority. The local authority housing team visited, identified a Category 1 hazard under the Housing Health and Safety Rating System (damp and mould — excess cold), and served an improvement notice on the landlord requiring remediation within 28 days.

The landlord then instructed a contractor who identified penetrating damp from a failed external render and a blocked downpipe — a repair that would have cost approximately £800 to £1,200 at the time of the first report. By March, the extent of the damage raised the repair cost to £6,500. The tenant instructed a solicitor and sued for £45,000 under the Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018 — covering personal belongings damaged by mould, a rent reduction for the period the bedroom was unusable, the tenant's legal costs, and general damages for distress. The case settled for £32,000. The managing agent also faced an Ombudsman investigation for failing to escalate the matter when the landlord refused to act.

Case Study 2: The Well-Maintained Portfolio — How a Professional PPM Programme Protects Landlords and Managers
A case study illustrating the commercial and protective value of a professionally maintained maintenance programme
This example illustrates how a professional planned preventive maintenance programme protects landlords, protects managing agents, and creates tangible commercial value.

A property management company took on a portfolio of twelve residential properties from a new landlord client. At instruction, they carried out a full compliance audit of all twelve properties.
