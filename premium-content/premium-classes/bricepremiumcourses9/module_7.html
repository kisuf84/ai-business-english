Client Care Letter
A formal letter sent by a solicitor, estate agent, or letting agent to a new client at the outset of a professional relationship — confirming the instructions received, the services to be provided, the fees agreed, the complaints procedure, and the regulatory information the client needs to know. Client care letters are a regulatory requirement for solicitors under the Solicitors Regulation Authority (SRA) Code of Conduct and a professional standard for estate agents under the Property Ombudsman Code of Practice. The letter protects both parties: the client has a clear written record of what has been agreed, and the professional has evidence that the terms were communicated. For estate agents, the client care letter typically includes the instruction (the right to market the property), the agency type (sole or multi-agency), the commission rate and how it is calculated, the marketing plan, and the complaints procedure.

Terms of Business
The standard conditions under which a professional provides their services — setting out the rights and obligations of both the professional and the client in the event of different scenarios. Estate agents' terms of business typically cover: the type of agency (sole agency, sole selling rights, or multi-agency) and the commission implications of each; the commission rate and how it is triggered (on completion, on introduction, or on exchange); the marketing budget and how it is allocated; the circumstances in which commission is payable even if the sale does not complete (for example, if the agent introduces a buyer and the seller then sells privately to that buyer); withdrawal rights; and the complaints procedure. Clients should read terms of business carefully before signing — the conditions triggering commission liability are particularly important and can create unexpected obligations if not understood.

Instruction
The formal appointment of an estate agent or other property professional to act on a client's behalf — for example, to market a property for sale or let, to manage a property, or to find a buyer or tenant. An instruction creates a contractual relationship between the agent and the client. The instruction is typically confirmed in writing — in the client care letter or the terms of business — and specifies the property, the price, the type of agency, the commission rate, and the duration of the instruction. For an estate agent, receiving an instruction to market a property is the starting point of the commercial relationship. Agents compete for instructions — the valuation meeting at which the agent meets the vendor, assesses the property, and proposes an asking price and marketing strategy is the key opportunity to win the instruction.

Sole Agency
An instruction under which only one estate agent is appointed to market a property — as opposed to multi-agency, in which two or more agents are appointed simultaneously. Under sole agency, the appointed agent is entitled to commission if the property is sold during the agency period regardless of who introduces the buyer — provided the buyer was not introduced by the vendor directly or through another agent before the instruction. Sole agency is generally recommended for vendors because agents tend to invest more in marketing a sole agency property; it typically results in a better buyer experience because communication is centralised through one agent; and the commission rate is typically lower than for multi-agency. Under multi-agency, each agent knows they may not receive commission even if they invest in marketing — which can result in less commitment to individual properties.

Status Update
A regular communication from a property professional to a client summarising the current position of a property transaction, a marketing campaign, or a management matter — providing factual information, noting any changes since the last update, and advising on next steps. Regular status updates are a key element of professional client care: they demonstrate that the professional is actively managing the client's matter, they pre-empt questions and calls from clients who would otherwise chase for information, and they create a written record of events and decisions that is useful if disputes arise. For estate agents marketing a property for sale, a weekly status update to the vendor typically includes: number of enquiries and viewings arranged that week; feedback from each viewing; portal statistics (number of times the listing was viewed); any offers received or outstanding; and a recommendation on whether any adjustment to the price or marketing is required.

Client Complaint
A formal expression of dissatisfaction by a client with a service provided — communicated verbally or in writing. In property, client complaints are common and the way they are handled is critically important — both for the professional relationship and for regulatory compliance. The Property Ombudsman Code of Practice requires member agents to have a written complaints procedure, to respond to complaints in writing within a specified timeframe, to attempt internal resolution before referring the client to the Ombudsman, and to cooperate fully with any Ombudsman investigation. Complaints that are handled promptly, professionally, and with genuine empathy are far less likely to escalate to the Ombudsman than those that are dismissed, delayed, or handled defensively. The Property Ombudsman can award compensation to complainants of up to £25,000 for significant maladministration.

Cross-Cultural Communication
The adaptation of communication style, language, and approach when working with clients from different cultural backgrounds — acknowledging that professional norms, communication preferences, relationship expectations, and attitudes to formality, directness, and negotiation vary significantly across cultures. In the UK property market, agents regularly work with buyers, sellers, landlords, and tenants from many different cultural backgrounds. Effective cross-cultural communication requires: awareness of different communication styles (high-context versus low-context cultures); sensitivity to cultural norms around directness, hierarchy, and decision-making; patience and clarity in written communication; and an avoidance of cultural assumptions. A client from a culture where indirect communication is the norm may not signal disagreement explicitly; an agent who assumes that the absence of objection means agreement may face a fall-through that could have been prevented by more careful, inclusive communication.

Onboarding
The process of welcoming, registering, and setting up a new client at the start of a professional relationship — providing them with the information they need, completing required due diligence (identity verification, anti-money laundering checks), and establishing the communication framework for the relationship. Effective onboarding in property sets the tone for the entire client relationship: a smooth, professional, well-organised onboarding process builds client confidence and reduces the risk of misunderstanding. Key elements of property onboarding include: confirming the instruction in writing; conducting anti-money laundering and identity checks; explaining the marketing plan, timeline, and next steps; explaining the communication process (how and when the client can expect updates); and answering any initial questions the client has. A client who leaves the onboarding process with clear expectations and confidence in their agent is significantly more likely to remain satisfied throughout.

Proactive Communication
Reaching out to a client with relevant information before they ask for it — in contrast to reactive communication, which responds to client requests and queries. Proactive communication is one of the most effective tools for building client satisfaction and reducing complaints in property. A client who receives regular, informative updates — viewing feedback, portal statistics, market commentary, progress on conveyancing — feels informed and valued; a client who hears nothing between milestones is likely to feel anxious and under-served, and may make unnecessary calls, become dissatisfied, or ultimately lose confidence in their agent. Proactive communication does not require elaborate systems: a brief email after every viewing, a weekly summary during the marketing period, and a prompt call after any significant development are the foundation of excellent client service in property.

Formal Email
A written communication in a professional, structured format — using an appropriate greeting, a clear subject line, formal language, a logical structure, and a professional closing. In property, formal emails are used for: confirming instructions and terms of business; communicating offers and counter-offers; providing status updates; responding to complaints; and communicating with solicitors, surveyors, and other professionals. The conventions of formal email in property include: using 'Dear [Name]' rather than 'Hi'; using complete sentences with correct grammar and punctuation; avoiding abbreviations and emojis; including a clear subject line that describes the content; and closing with 'Yours sincerely' (when the recipient's name is known) or 'Yours faithfully' (when the recipient is unknown). Even in a sector where WhatsApp and informal messaging are common, formal email remains the standard for all significant communications in a property transaction.

Digital Communication
The use of electronic channels — email, messaging apps (WhatsApp, SMS), video calls (Zoom, Microsoft Teams), property portals, and client management platforms — to communicate with clients and other parties in a property transaction. Digital communication has transformed the pace and format of property communication: buyers expect instant responses to portal enquiries; vendors track their listing statistics in real time; landlords and tenants manage maintenance requests through apps; and solicitors exchange documents electronically. Property professionals must manage digital communication effectively: responding promptly to portal enquiries (within hours, not days); maintaining professional tone in WhatsApp messages; using video calls for meetings that would previously have required a physical visit; and ensuring that all significant communication is confirmed in writing, even if initially agreed verbally or by message.

Active Listening
A communication technique in which the listener gives their full attention to the speaker — not merely waiting for their turn to speak, but genuinely processing what is being said, reflecting it back to confirm understanding, and responding to the emotional as well as informational content of the communication. In property, active listening is essential when a client is frustrated, anxious, or confused: an agent who interrupts, dismisses concerns, or jumps to solutions before the client has finished speaking typically escalates the situation. Active listening involves: maintaining eye contact (or, on a call, giving verbal signals like 'I understand' and 'go on'); avoiding interruptions; summarising what the client has said to confirm understanding ('so what I'm hearing is that you're concerned about the impact of the survey result on the buyer's position — is that right?'); and acknowledging the emotional content before moving to solutions.

Difficult Conversation
A professional interaction in which the subject matter is sensitive, potentially unwelcome, or likely to produce an emotional response — such as delivering a low offer to a vendor, telling a buyer they have been gazumped, advising a landlord that their property needs significant work before it can be let, or raising a complaint with a client or colleague. Difficult conversations are a regular feature of property work and handling them well is a core professional skill. Effective difficult conversation technique involves: preparing thoroughly before the call or meeting; delivering the key message clearly and early (not burying it in context); using empathetic but honest language; allowing the client space to respond; avoiding defensiveness; and following up in writing to confirm what was discussed and agreed. A difficult conversation handled with skill typically strengthens the client relationship; one handled poorly can destroy it.

Plain English
Writing that communicates clearly and directly — using simple vocabulary, short sentences, active voice, and a logical structure — making it accessible to readers regardless of their specialist knowledge or language background. Plain English is particularly important in property communications with clients who are not legal or property professionals, and with clients for whom English is not their first language. Industry jargon (stamp duty, exchange, dilapidations, FRI) should either be avoided or explained when it is used; sentence structures should be simple and direct ('we need you to sign the contract' rather than 'it would be appreciated if the contractual documentation could be signed at your earliest convenience'); and the purpose of each paragraph should be immediately clear. The Plain English Campaign produces guidance that is widely used in professional services.

Client Handover
The formal transfer of a client relationship from one professional to another — for example, when a property manager leaves an agency and their portfolio of managed properties is handed to a colleague, or when a client's file is transferred from one department to another. A professional client handover involves: notifying the client in advance of the change; introducing the new contact clearly and warmly; providing the new contact with a comprehensive briefing on the client's history, property, preferences, and any outstanding matters; and following up to confirm the client is satisfied with the new arrangement. Poor handovers — where clients are not informed, or the new contact has insufficient information — are a significant source of client dissatisfaction and complaints in property management.

Marketing Report
A written summary provided by an estate agent to a vendor, reporting on the performance of the property's marketing campaign — typically covering: the number of portal views and enquiries; the number of viewings arranged and conducted; feedback from viewers; any offers received; a comparison with the agent's performance targets; and a recommendation on whether any change to the price, the marketing material, or the strategy is required. Marketing reports are a key tool for maintaining the vendor's confidence and managing their expectations during the marketing period. A vendor who receives a detailed, honest marketing report each week understands what the market is saying about their property; one who receives vague updates ('we're getting some interest') may lose confidence in their agent — particularly if the property is not selling as quickly as expected.

Complaint Response
A formal written reply from a property professional to a client who has raised a complaint — acknowledging the complaint, summarising the client's concerns, providing the professional's response and explanation, setting out any remedy offered, and explaining the next steps if the client remains dissatisfied. An effective complaint response: is prompt (the Property Ombudsman requires a substantive response within a defined timeframe); is empathetic in tone (acknowledging the client's frustration without necessarily admitting liability); is factually accurate (if challenged, every statement in the response must be supportable); and is clear about the outcome (what, if anything, the agent is offering to do differently or to compensate). The complaint response is a legally significant document — it may be referred to in any subsequent Ombudsman investigation and should be drafted with care.

Confidentiality
The professional and legal obligation to protect information provided by a client from disclosure to third parties — without the client's consent or a legal obligation to disclose. In property, confidentiality is particularly important in several situations: an estate agent must not disclose a vendor's minimum acceptable price to a buyer; a letting agent must not share a tenant's personal financial information with the landlord beyond what is necessary; a solicitor must not disclose the details of their client's instructions to the other side's solicitor without authority. Breaching client confidentiality is both a professional misconduct issue and potentially a criminal offence under data protection legislation. Property professionals handle significant volumes of personal and financial data — names, addresses, financial information, identity documents — all of which must be managed in compliance with the UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018.

Appointment Confirmation
A written communication sent in advance of a meeting, viewing, or call — confirming the date, time, location (or call link), and purpose, and providing any information the recipient needs to prepare. Appointment confirmations are a professional standard in property: confirming viewings to both the buyer and the vendor; confirming valuation appointments; confirming meetings with solicitors or surveyors; and confirming completion day arrangements. A well-drafted appointment confirmation reduces the risk of no-shows, ensures both parties arrive prepared, and demonstrates organisational professionalism. Digital calendar invitations (sent via Outlook or Google Calendar) are now commonly used alongside or instead of written confirmation emails — they automatically send reminders and allow recipients to add the appointment to their own calendar.

Client Satisfaction
The degree to which a client's expectations of a property service have been met or exceeded — measured through feedback, reviews, repeat business, and referrals. Client satisfaction is the ultimate measure of professional performance in property, and the most successful agencies are those that consistently achieve high satisfaction ratings. The drivers of client satisfaction in property are well-researched: communication quality (regular, honest, proactive updates) is consistently identified as the most important factor, ahead of the final price achieved or the speed of sale. Clients who receive poor communication — even when the outcome (sale price, rental achieved) is good — frequently give poor reviews; clients who receive excellent communication — even when the outcome is disappointing — are more likely to give positive reviews and to recommend the agent. Property review platforms such as Trustpilot, Google Reviews, and AllAgents provide public feedback on individual agents and agencies.

CRM System
Customer Relationship Management system — software used by estate agents and letting agents to manage their database of clients, properties, enquiries, viewings, offers, and communications in one centralised platform. CRM systems are essential for professional property management at any scale: they store contact details and communication history for every buyer, seller, landlord, and tenant; track the status of every instruction, viewing, and transaction; generate automatic reminders for follow-up calls, review appointments, and compliance deadlines; and produce marketing reports and management information for agency principals. Modern property CRM systems include portal integration (automatically capturing enquiries from Rightmove and Zoopla), email automation (sending templated status updates and follow-up emails), and performance dashboards. An agent who manages client relationships through memory and a notebook will miss follow-up calls, lose enquiries, and ultimately provide a lower standard of service than one who uses a well-configured CRM system to ensure every client receives consistent, timely attention.

Text 1: Written Communication in Property — How to Write Emails and Letters That Build Client Confidence
The principles of effective professional written communication and the standards expected in real estate client correspondence

Written communication is the foundation of the professional relationship between a property agent and their clients. Every email, letter, status update, and complaint response is an opportunity to build — or damage — client confidence. Yet despite this importance, written communication in property is frequently poor: vague, infrequent, badly structured, and written in a register that either talks down to the client or overwhelms them with jargon. The best property professionals treat every written communication as a professional artefact that reflects their standards — and they invest in developing clear, accurate, empathetic writing skills accordingly.

The principles of effective written communication in property apply to all formats. First: clarity of purpose. Every communication should have a clear purpose that is apparent from the subject line or opening sentence — the reader should know within the first two lines why they are receiving this message and what, if anything, they are required to do. Second: appropriate register. A formal email to a solicitor requires different language from a WhatsApp update to a long-standing landlord client — both should be professional but the tone, vocabulary, and length differ significantly. Third: factual accuracy. Every statement in a professional communication must be accurate — in property, emails and letters are regularly produced as evidence in complaints, disputes, and legal proceedings, and inaccurate or misleading statements create significant professional risk. Fourth: timeliness. A response to a client's enquiry that arrives three days later does not demonstrate professional standards, regardless of how well-written it is.

Structure is as important as content in professional written communication. A well-structured email begins with a clear subject line that accurately describes the content ('Viewing Feedback — 42 Elmwood Road — Week of [date]'); opens with a brief contextual statement that confirms what the email is about; delivers the information in a logical order (most important first, supporting detail following); closes with a clear indication of any action required and when; and ends with a professional sign-off. Lists and bullet points are appropriate where the email contains multiple distinct items (several viewing feedbacks, multiple action points); prose is appropriate for narrative updates or sensitive content. Property professionals who develop a consistent, professional format for their regular communications — weekly marketing updates, post-viewing emails, status updates — can save significant time while maintaining high standards.

Text 2: Managing Difficult Client Conversations — Delivering Unwelcome News and Handling Complaints Professionally
How property professionals can turn difficult conversations into opportunities to demonstrate their expertise and build client trust

Difficult conversations are unavoidable in property. An estate agent will regularly need to tell a vendor that their asking price is too high; advise a buyer that they have been outbid; inform a landlord that their tenant has failed a reference check; or handle a formal complaint about the service provided. These conversations are uncomfortable — but how they are handled is one of the clearest indicators of a property professional's competence and character. A difficult conversation handled with skill, honesty, and empathy often leaves the client relationship stronger than before; one handled with evasion, defensiveness, or poor communication can end it.

The framework for handling difficult conversations in property follows a consistent structure. First: prepare. Never go into a difficult conversation without knowing the facts, having considered the client's likely reaction, and having a clear idea of what you are asking them to do or accept. Second: be direct. State the key message clearly and early — burying difficult news in context or preamble makes it worse and can make the professional appear evasive. Third: use empathetic language without being sycophantic. 'I understand this is disappointing' acknowledges the client's feeling without being patronising. 'I'm so sorry — this is terrible' may be genuine but tips into excessive self-flagellation that serves the professional's anxiety rather than the client's needs. Fourth: give the client space to react. After delivering difficult news, stop talking. Allow the client to process and respond before moving to solutions. Fifth: agree next steps clearly. Before ending the conversation, confirm what has been agreed and what happens next — and follow up in writing.

Handling formal complaints requires all of the above skills plus an understanding of the regulatory framework. The Property Ombudsman Code of Practice requires member agents to acknowledge a complaint in writing within three working days, to provide a full written response within fifteen working days, and to advise the client of their right to refer the matter to the Ombudsman if they remain dissatisfied. Complaint responses should: acknowledge the client's concern without admitting liability unless liability is clear; present the facts as the agent understands them; explain any mitigating circumstances; and set out any remedy offered. A complaint response that is dismissive, delayed, or factually inaccurate is far more likely to result in an Ombudsman referral than one that is prompt, thorough, and empathetic.

Exercise 1 — Grammar 1: Professional Email Openings, Closings, and Structures

Professional property emails use consistent openings, structures, and closings that signal competence and build client confidence.

Formal openings:

  • 'Dear Mr Webb,' / 'Dear Sofia,' (when first name is established)
  • 'I am writing to...' / 'Further to our conversation today...' / 'I write to confirm...'

Structuring the body:

  • Most important information first
  • 'I would like to draw your attention to...' / 'Please note that...'
  • 'As discussed, the next steps are as follows...'

Professional closings:

  • 'Please do not hesitate to contact me should you have any questions.'
  • 'I look forward to hearing from you.' / 'I will be in touch again on [date].'
  • 'Yours sincerely,' (known recipient) / 'Yours faithfully,' (unknown)

Case Study 1: Communication Failures and Their Consequences — Why Poor Client Communication Is the Leading Cause of Property Complaints
The most common communication failures in property, their consequences, and how professional communication practices can prevent them
Analysis of Property Ombudsman complaint data consistently identifies communication failures as the most common cause of complaints against estate agents and letting agents in England and Wales. In a typical year, the Ombudsman receives thousands of complaints — a significant proportion of which relate not to the outcome achieved (the price obtained, the tenant found) but to the manner in which the professional communicated with their client.

The most common communication failures are: failure to pass on information promptly (an offer not communicated on the same day it was received; a survey result not shared with the vendor until a week after the agent received it); failure to provide adequate feedback (vague or infrequent updates that leave the client without the information they need to make decisions); failure to manage expectations at the outset (not explaining clearly what the client can realistically expect in terms of timeline, price, or marketing outcomes); failure to document significant conversations in writing (a verbal agreement about a price reduction or a marketing change that the client later denies having agreed); and failure to respond to complaints promptly and professionally (which almost universally escalates the complaint rather than resolving it).

The financial consequences of communication failures are significant. The Property Ombudsman can award compensation of up to £25,000 in cases of serious maladministration. Industry research suggests that poor communication costs the average agency far more than this in lost repeat business and referrals — a single dissatisfied client who leaves a poor online review may deter dozens of potential future clients. By contrast, agencies that invest in communication training, set clear response time standards, use CRM systems to manage client contact, and hold regular team meetings to review client satisfaction consistently outperform their competitors in client retention, referrals, and revenue.

Case Study 2: Digital Communication in Property — The Rise of WhatsApp, Portals, and AI — and the Risks That Come With Them
How digital channels have transformed client communication in property — and the professional risks they introduce
The past decade has seen a dramatic transformation in how property professionals communicate with clients. Email was once the most significant innovation — enabling instant written communication and replacing the letter for routine correspondence. Today, email competes with — and is frequently supplanted by — WhatsApp for client communication, while property portals have created new expectations around response times and information transparency.

WhatsApp has become the default communication channel for many letting agents and their landlord clients. Its immediacy, ease of use, and informal register make it ideal for quick updates, maintenance notifications, and routine queries. However, its use introduces significant professional risks. WhatsApp messages are personal communications — they are not automatically saved to the agency's client management system, they can be deleted by either party, and they are harder to retrieve in the event of a dispute. An agent who agrees to a maintenance repair over WhatsApp without a written follow-up email creates a record that may be disputed months later. The industry guidance — increasingly reflected in agency training — is clear: WhatsApp is appropriate for brief updates and acknowledgements, but any significant instruction, agreement, or decision must be confirmed in a formal email.

Portal transparency has created new client expectations around marketing data. Vendors can now track how many times their listing has been viewed on Rightmove in real time — and they will notice if viewings are down, if the listing has been viewed significantly less than comparable properties, or if the number of views drops after a price reduction rather than increasing. Agents who fail to address portal statistics proactively — to explain why view counts are what they are and what it means for the marketing strategy — create an information gap that clients will fill with anxiety and speculation. AI-generated email drafting tools are now available to property professionals — they can significantly improve the speed and consistency of client communication but require careful oversight to ensure accuracy, appropriate tone, and compliance with the agency's professional standards.
