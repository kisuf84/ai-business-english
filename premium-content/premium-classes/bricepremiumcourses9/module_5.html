Conveyancing
The legal process of transferring ownership of property from one party to another — encompassing all the legal work carried out by solicitors and licensed conveyancers from the point at which an offer is accepted to the point of completion. In England and Wales, conveyancing involves three main stages: pre-contract (investigation of title, searches, review of the draft contract, mortgage arrangements); exchange of contracts (the legally binding commitment by both parties to complete the transaction); and post-exchange/completion (transfer of funds, registration of the new owner at the Land Registry). The conveyancing process typically takes 8–16 weeks for a straightforward transaction, though leasehold properties, complex title issues, or long chains can extend this significantly. Conveyancing must be carried out by a solicitor or licensed conveyancer — estate agents are not permitted to conduct conveyancing on behalf of clients.

Title Register
The official record maintained by HM Land Registry confirming who owns a property and what rights, restrictions, and charges are registered against it. In England and Wales, the vast majority of land is now registered at the Land Registry, with each registered property having a unique title number. The title register is divided into three parts: the Property Register, which describes the property and any rights it has over other land (such as easements); the Proprietorship Register, which names the registered owner(s) and records any restrictions on the owner's right to deal with the property; and the Charges Register, which records any mortgages, charges, or other encumbrances registered against the property. An official copy of the title register is a key document in any conveyancing transaction and is obtained by the buyer's solicitor at the outset.

Restrictive Covenant
A legally binding obligation imposed on a property's owner — typically by a previous owner, developer, or neighbouring landowner — that restricts how the property can be used. Restrictive covenants 'run with the land', meaning they bind not only the original owner who agreed to them but all future owners of the property. Common restrictive covenants include: prohibitions on building additional structures without consent; restrictions on use (residential use only; no business use; no alterations to the property's external appearance); and obligations to maintain boundary features. Restrictive covenants are recorded in the Charges Register of the title register. If a buyer plans to use the property in a way that may breach a covenant, their solicitor must investigate whether the covenant is enforceable and, if so, whether indemnity insurance or consent from the beneficiary of the covenant can be obtained.
▼ Show definition
🛤️
Easement
A legal right that one landowner has over the land of another — allowing the first party to use the second party's land in a specified way, or preventing the second party from using their land in a way that would interfere with the first party's enjoyment of their own property. Common easements include: rights of way (the right to cross another person's land); rights of light (the right not to have natural light to a window blocked by development on neighbouring land); rights of support (the right to have a building supported by the structure of an adjacent building); and rights of drainage (the right to use another property's drainage system). Easements are either expressly granted (in a deed or conveyance) or arise by implication or long use. They are recorded in the Property Register of the title register and are investigated by the buyer's solicitor as part of the conveyancing process.

🔍
Property Search
An enquiry made as part of the conveyancing process to obtain information about a property and its surrounding area from local and national authorities. The most common searches are: the local authority search (which reveals planning history, road adoption status, tree preservation orders, and other local authority information); the drainage and water search (which confirms whether the property is connected to mains water and sewerage); the environmental search (which identifies flood risk, contaminated land, and other environmental issues); and the chancel repair search (which reveals whether the property is liable for historic church repair contributions). Searches are ordered by the buyer's solicitor on behalf of the buyer and are typically required by the mortgage lender as a condition of the mortgage offer. The results of searches may reveal issues that affect the buyer's decision to proceed or the price they are prepared to pay.
▼ Show definition
🤝
Exchange of Contracts
The moment at which the transaction becomes legally binding in England and Wales — when the buyer's solicitor and the seller's solicitor exchange signed contracts, making both parties legally committed to completing the transaction on the agreed completion date. Before exchange, either party can withdraw without legal liability (although they may lose any holding deposit paid). After exchange, if either party withdraws, they face significant financial penalties: a buyer who withdraws after exchange forfeits their deposit (typically 10% of the purchase price); a seller who withdraws after exchange is liable to pay the buyer's costs and may face a court order for specific performance. Exchange typically happens by telephone: the solicitors read out the terms of the contracts to each other to confirm they are identical, and then post the signed contracts to each other. The completion date is agreed and fixed at exchange.
▼ Show definition
🏠
Completion
The final stage of a property transaction — the day on which the buyer pays the purchase price to the seller (through their solicitors), the seller vacates the property, legal ownership transfers to the buyer, and the buyer receives the keys. On completion day, the buyer's solicitor sends the purchase money (including the mortgage advance from the lender) to the seller's solicitor by electronic bank transfer. Once the seller's solicitor confirms receipt of funds, the transaction is complete and the buyer is the legal owner of the property. The seller must have vacated the property by the time agreed in the contract (typically 1pm). After completion, the buyer's solicitor registers the transfer at the Land Registry and registers the mortgage (if any). The completion date is agreed at exchange of contracts and is typically 1–4 weeks after exchange.

📋
Draft Contract
The initial version of the sale contract prepared by the seller's solicitor and sent to the buyer's solicitor at the start of the conveyancing process. The draft contract contains: the property address and description; the parties' details; the purchase price; the deposit amount; the completion date (left blank at the draft stage, to be agreed and inserted before exchange); the title guarantee (full or limited); special conditions of sale; and any matters subject to enquiry. The buyer's solicitor will review the draft contract, raise pre-contract enquiries (queries about the property, its title, and the seller's obligations), and negotiate amendments to any terms that are unsatisfactory. Once all enquiries have been answered satisfactorily, the searches completed, and the mortgage offer received, the buyer's solicitor will report to the buyer and obtain instructions to exchange contracts.

🗒️
Pre-Contract Enquiries
Written questions raised by the buyer's solicitor to the seller's solicitor before exchange of contracts — seeking information about the property that is not apparent from the title register, the draft contract, or the standard property information forms. Pre-contract enquiries cover matters such as: boundaries and their maintenance; access and rights of way; services and utilities; disputes with neighbours; planning permissions and building regulations approvals for any works carried out; and the seller's knowledge of any defects in the property. The seller's solicitor answers the enquiries on behalf of the seller using the information provided in the standard property information forms (TA6 and TA10) completed by the seller. Buyers are legally entitled to rely on the answers to pre-contract enquiries — a seller who gives false or misleading answers may be liable for misrepresentation.

🏛️
Land Registry
HM Land Registry — the government body responsible for maintaining the register of ownership of land and property in England and Wales. All property transactions in England and Wales must be registered at the Land Registry (compulsory registration applies to all transfers of land, mortgages, and leases of more than seven years). The Land Registry maintains the title register for each registered property, processes applications to register new ownership and new charges, and provides official copies of title registers and title plans on request. Land Registry fees are charged on a scale based on the value of the transaction and are paid by the buyer as part of the completion costs. The Land Register is open to public inspection — anyone can obtain an official copy of any property's title register for a small fee.

💰
Stamp Duty Land Tax
A tax payable by property buyers in England on the purchase of land and property above specified thresholds. SDLT is calculated on a tiered basis: different percentage rates apply to different portions of the purchase price, with higher rates applying to higher value properties. Additional SDLT applies to purchases of additional residential dwellings (second homes and buy-to-let properties), currently at an additional 5% above standard rates. First-time buyers benefit from a relief that raises the threshold at which SDLT begins to apply. SDLT is payable within 14 days of completion — the buyer's solicitor typically handles the SDLT return and payment as part of the completion process. Rates and thresholds change from time to time and buyers should always check current rates with their solicitor or on HMRC's online SDLT calculator.
▼ Show definition
🏦
Mortgage Offer
The formal written commitment from a mortgage lender to provide a loan on specified terms to enable a buyer to purchase a property — confirming the loan amount, the interest rate, the term, and any conditions that must be met before the mortgage can be drawn down. The mortgage offer is a key document in the conveyancing process: without a valid mortgage offer, the buyer's solicitor cannot proceed to exchange of contracts on behalf of a mortgaged buyer. Mortgage offers typically have conditions attached — such as the requirement for a satisfactory valuation of the property, proof of the buyer's income and identity, and the buyer's compliance with any special conditions set by the lender. Mortgage offers are typically valid for three to six months and will expire if exchange does not take place within that period, requiring the buyer to reapply.
▼ Show definition
📄
Transfer Deed
The formal legal document that transfers ownership of a registered property from the seller to the buyer — signed by both parties and submitted to the Land Registry after completion to update the title register in the buyer's name. For registered land, the standard transfer deed is Form TR1 (for transfers of the whole of a registered title) or Form TP1 (for transfers of part). The transfer deed must be signed by the seller (and witnessed) and executed by the buyer (and witnessed), and is submitted to the Land Registry along with the SDLT return, the mortgage deed (if applicable), and the Land Registry application form. Before the Commonhold and Leasehold Reform Act 2002, property was conveyed by an unregistered deed of conveyance — compulsory registration means that all transfers of unregistered land trigger first registration at the Land Registry.

📍
Title Plan
The official plan attached to the Land Registry title register, showing the extent of the registered title — i.e., the boundaries of the property as registered. The title plan is based on the Ordnance Survey map and shows the general extent of the property in red (the 'red edging'). The title plan does not show the precise legal boundary between properties — it shows only the general position of the boundary, and disputes about the exact line of a boundary must be resolved by reference to the title deeds, physical features, and other evidence. Buyers should examine the title plan carefully and check that it accurately reflects the property they are purchasing — any discrepancies between the plan and the physical property (such as a fence in the wrong position, a garage included in the title but not in the plan, or a path excluded from the title) should be raised with the seller's solicitor.

Legal Charge
A legal mortgage registered against a property title — giving the lender (the mortgagee) a security interest in the property as security for the loan. When a buyer uses a mortgage to purchase a property, the lender requires the borrower to grant a legal charge over the property. The legal charge is registered in the Charges Register of the title register and gives the lender the right to take possession of and sell the property if the borrower defaults on the mortgage. Legal charges must be removed (redeemed) when the mortgage is repaid — the lender sends a formal release (DS1 form) to the Land Registry to remove the charge from the register. First charges have priority over second charges — if a property is sold in repossession, the first charge holder is paid first from the proceeds.

🛡️
Indemnity Insurance
Insurance taken out in property transactions to protect against the risk of a specific legal defect in the title or a breach of a covenant or planning condition — where it is impractical or impossible to remedy the defect itself. Common situations where indemnity insurance is used include: lack of building regulations approval for works carried out to the property; breach of a restrictive covenant (such as a building constructed in breach of a covenant restricting development); absence of evidence of planning permission for an extension; and missing title deeds for unregistered land. Indemnity insurance is typically a one-off premium that provides cover for the buyer and their successors in title indefinitely. It protects against the financial consequences of the defect being enforced — it does not remedy the defect itself. Lenders will often require indemnity insurance as a condition of the mortgage offer where a title defect exists.

📝
Completion Statement
A financial statement prepared by the buyer's solicitor before completion, summarising all the money required from the buyer to complete the transaction — including the balance of the purchase price (after deducting the deposit already paid), SDLT, Land Registry fees, and the solicitor's own fees and disbursements. The completion statement tells the buyer the exact amount they need to transfer to their solicitor before completion day. A separate completion statement is prepared by the seller's solicitor, showing the net proceeds of sale the seller will receive after repaying any outstanding mortgage and paying the estate agent's commission and solicitor's fees. Both statements must be reviewed carefully by the respective clients — any discrepancy or unexpected deduction should be queried with the solicitor before funds are transferred.
▼ Show definition
📣
Property Information Form
A standardised questionnaire completed by the seller at the start of the conveyancing process — providing the buyer with detailed information about the property that the seller is aware of. The main residential property information form is the TA6, which covers: boundaries and responsibility for their maintenance; disputes and complaints; notices and proposals; alterations and planning; guarantees and warranties (e.g., NHBC guarantee for new builds, damp-proof course guarantees); services and utilities; rights and informal arrangements; parking; and occupiers. A separate TA10 form (Fittings and Contents Form) is used to record which items in the property are included in the sale and which the seller is taking. The answers to the TA6 and TA10 form part of the contractual documentation — a seller who gives false or misleading answers may be liable for misrepresentation.
▼ Show definition
🔏
Covenant
A legally binding promise in a deed — either to do something (a positive covenant, such as maintaining a boundary fence) or not to do something (a restrictive covenant, such as not building without consent). Covenants are created in the original conveyance, transfer deed, or lease and may 'run with the land', meaning they bind future owners as well as the original parties. Positive covenants typically bind only the party who gave the original promise and do not automatically bind successors in title (although they can be enforced through a chain of indemnity covenants in each subsequent transfer). Restrictive covenants, by contrast, bind future owners of the burdened land as long as they have notice of the covenant (which is ensured by registration). Solicitors investigate all covenants affecting a property as part of title investigation and advise the buyer on their implications.
▼ Show definition
🏗️
Search Result
The information returned by an official search of a government or local authority register — revealing matters affecting the property that are not apparent from the title documents alone. The local authority search result is the most comprehensive: it reveals planning history (all applications and decisions), highways information (whether the road is adopted as public highway), financial charges registered against the property, tree preservation orders, conservation area status, listed building status, and any enforcement notices or breach of condition notices. Environmental search results reveal flood risk (from rivers, surface water, and seas), contaminated land risk, and proximity to landfill sites or mining operations. Search results are crucial to the buyer's decision — they may reveal matters that affect the property's value, the buyer's plans for it, or their lender's willingness to lend.
▼ Show definition
✅
Report on Title
A formal document prepared by the buyer's solicitor summarising the results of all investigations carried out on behalf of the buyer — including the title register, searches, pre-contract enquiries, the draft contract, and any issues arising. The report on title explains the nature of the property, the rights and obligations that come with it, any defects or concerns identified during the investigation, the results of searches, the mortgage conditions, and the financial requirements for completion. It is the solicitor's formal advice to the buyer about the property they are purchasing, and the buyer must read, understand, and sign it before authorising the solicitor to exchange contracts. A solicitor who fails to carry out a proper investigation and prepare an accurate report on title may be liable in negligence to the buyer for any loss resulting from the failure.

Text 1: The Conveyancing Process in England and Wales — From Offer Accepted to Completion
A step-by-step guide to the legal stages of a residential property purchase

The conveyancing process in England and Wales is the legal mechanism by which ownership of property transfers from seller to buyer. Unlike some other jurisdictions where the agreement to buy and sell is binding from the outset, in England and Wales the transaction is not legally binding until contracts are exchanged — a feature of the system that allows either party to withdraw without penalty up to that point, but which also creates the risk of gazumping (the seller accepting a higher offer from a different buyer) and gazundering (the buyer reducing their offer at the last moment before exchange). Understanding the stages of the conveyancing process is essential for anyone working in or around the property market.

The process begins when the seller's solicitor sends the buyer's solicitor a draft contract pack — containing the draft contract, official copies of the title register and title plan, and the seller's completed property information forms (TA6 and TA10). The buyer's solicitor then: obtains official copies of the title register to verify ownership; orders the required searches (local authority, drainage, environmental, and any others specific to the property); investigates the title (examining the register, any rights and covenants affecting the property, and any restrictions on the seller's ability to sell); raises pre-contract enquiries with the seller's solicitor; and reviews the draft contract, negotiating any amendments required. In parallel, the buyer's mortgage lender will commission a valuation and issue a formal mortgage offer once satisfied with the property and the buyer's financial position.

Once all searches have been received, enquiries answered, the mortgage offer obtained, and the buyer's solicitor satisfied with the title, the solicitor prepares a report on title for the buyer. The buyer reads the report, signs the contract, and provides the deposit (typically 10% of the purchase price). The solicitors then agree a completion date and exchange contracts by telephone — at which point both parties are legally committed. On completion day, the buyer's solicitor transfers the full balance of the purchase price to the seller's solicitor. Once funds are confirmed, the seller's solicitor releases the keys (typically via the estate agent), and the buyer's solicitor registers the transfer and any mortgage at the Land Registry. The transaction is complete.

Text 2: Property Title, Covenants, and Easements — Understanding What You Are Buying
How solicitors investigate title and why the rights and restrictions on a property matter as much as the bricks and mortar

When a buyer purchases a property, they are buying not just the physical structure but also a bundle of legal rights — and a set of legal obligations. Understanding the rights and restrictions that come with a property title is one of the most important tasks in conveyancing. A buyer who purchases a property without understanding its covenants may find themselves unable to develop it as they planned. A buyer who does not investigate easements carefully may purchase a property with a right of way crossing the garden, or discover after completion that a neighbouring building has a right of light that prevents any extension. Solicitors investigate title to protect buyers from these risks — and to advise on any issues that arise before the buyer is committed to the purchase.

The title register tells a buyer three things. The Property Register describes the property, its address, and any rights it has over other land — including easements benefiting the property (such as a right of way over a neighbour's land or the right to use a shared access). The Proprietorship Register identifies the owner(s), the class of title (absolute, possessory, qualified, or good leasehold), and any restrictions on the owner's right to deal with the property (such as a restriction requiring the consent of a management company before any sale can proceed). The Charges Register records any mortgages, restrictive covenants, and other encumbrances affecting the property — including covenants imposed by the original developer or a previous owner that restrict how the property can be used.

Easements and covenants are frequently misunderstood — and frequently discovered only when a buyer tries to do something the easement or covenant prevents. A right of way over a garden is not obvious from the title register — it requires careful examination of the wording of the original grant and the plans referred to in it. A restrictive covenant imposed by a developer in the 1930s prohibiting any building on the land without consent may still be enforceable today — and the identity and address of the person entitled to enforce it may be unknown. In these cases, indemnity insurance (taking out a policy that protects the buyer against the financial consequences of the covenant being enforced) is the most common solution. Buyers should ensure their solicitor explains any easements and covenants affecting the property clearly and advises on their practical implications before exchange.

Exercise 1 — Grammar 1: Describing Legal Processes — Sequence, Passive Voice, and Technical Precision

Legal and conveyancing language uses passive voice, sequence markers, and precise technical vocabulary to describe formal processes accurately.

Passive voice for formal processes:

  • 'Contracts are exchanged by telephone.' / 'The deposit must be protected within 30 days.'
  • 'The transfer deed is submitted to the Land Registry after completion.'

Sequence markers:

  • 'Once searches have been received, enquiries are raised...'
  • 'Following exchange, the completion date is fixed...'
  • 'Prior to exchange, the buyer must have a mortgage offer...'

Exercise 3 — Grammar 3: Formal Legal Correspondence — Request, Inform, and Confirm Structures

Property legal correspondence uses formal request structures, information-provision patterns, and confirmation language that follow professional conventions.

Formal request structures:

  • 'We should be grateful if you would provide...'
  • 'Please could you confirm whether...' / 'We write to request...'

Information-provision patterns:

  • 'We write to inform you that...' / 'We are pleased to confirm that...'
  • 'We draw your attention to clause 4.2...'

Confirming and acknowledging:

  • 'We acknowledge receipt of your letter dated...'
  • 'We confirm that contracts were exchanged at 3pm today.'

Case Study 1: Gazumping, Gazundering, and the Gap Between Offer and Exchange — The Case for Reform
Why the period between offer accepted and exchange of contracts creates risk for buyers and sellers — and what has been proposed to address it
The period between offer accepted and exchange of contracts is unique to the English and Welsh conveyancing system — and uniquely problematic. In Scotland, a binding contract is created much earlier in the process (when missives are concluded), significantly reducing the risk of transactions falling through. In England and Wales, by contrast, either party can withdraw at any time before exchange without legal liability — creating the twin risks of gazumping and gazundering that affect thousands of transactions every year.

Gazumping — where a seller accepts a higher offer from a new buyer after already accepting an offer — is more common in rising markets, where the gap between offer and exchange (typically 8-16 weeks) allows the property's perceived value to increase sufficiently to make accepting a new offer worth the reputational damage. The financial consequences for the gazumped buyer can be significant: they may have spent hundreds of pounds on searches, surveys, and legal fees that are not recoverable. Some buyers purchase 'home buyers protection insurance' to cover these costs, but the market for such products is limited.

Gazundering — where a buyer reduces their offer at the last moment before exchange, knowing the seller is vulnerable and may have already committed to an onward purchase — is more common in falling markets. The seller faces the choice of accepting the reduced offer or withdrawing from the transaction and beginning the process again with a new buyer. Property lock-in agreements — where both parties pay a deposit into an escrow account at an early stage, forfeited if they withdraw without valid reason — have been trialled as a solution, but have not been widely adopted. The Law Commission has reviewed the conveyancing process on several occasions, and various proposals for reform — including a binding 'offer to purchase' document, mandatory searches before marketing, and a statutory cooling-off period — have been discussed without resulting in legislative change. The system remains as it has been for decades: binding only at exchange, vulnerable to late withdrawal on both sides, and generating significant uncertainty for buyers and sellers alike.

Case Study 2: Restrictive Covenants in Practice — When Old Obligations Still Bind New Owners
How historical covenants continue to affect modern property transactions — and the legal and practical tools for managing covenant risk
Restrictive covenants are one of the most frequently encountered and least understood legal concepts in residential property transactions. A covenant imposed by a developer when an estate was built in the 1930s may have been largely forgotten by the time a buyer purchases one of the original plots 90 years later — but it may still be legally enforceable by anyone who can establish that they are entitled to the benefit of the covenant.

The legal position on restrictive covenants is complex. For a restrictive covenant to be enforceable against a successor in title, four conditions must be met: the covenant must be negative in nature (it must restrict what the owner does rather than require them to do something); it must have been made for the benefit of other land (the 'dominant tenement'); that dominant land must be identifiable; and the person claiming to enforce it must show they are entitled to the benefit. In many older covenants, one or more of these conditions may be in doubt — the dominant land may be unidentifiable, the benefit may not have been expressly passed to successors, or it may be unclear who owns the dominant land today.

In practice, solicitors and buyers face a range of situations. Some covenants are clearly enforceable — typically on modern housing estates where the developer has retained land nearby and expressly reserved the benefit of the covenants. Others are clearly unenforceable — the dominant land has been developed in a way that makes the covenant irrelevant, or the covenant has been breached consistently for decades without any enforcement action. Many fall in between — and the standard approach in such cases is to obtain indemnity insurance, protecting against the financial consequences of enforcement rather than attempting to resolve the legal uncertainty definitively. For high-value transactions or where the breach is significant, an application to the Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber) can be made to discharge or modify the covenant under Section 84 of the Law of Property Act 1925 — a process that can take many months and is expensive, but which provides a definitive resolution.
