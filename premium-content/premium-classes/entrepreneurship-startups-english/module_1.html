📚 Key Legal Terms
🚀
Startup
An early-stage company designed to grow rapidly — typically by solving a problem at scale using technology or an innovative business model. Unlike a traditional small business, a startup is explicitly built for rapid growth and often accepts losses in the short term in exchange for speed and market share. The term is associated with uncertainty, iteration, and the pursuit of product-market fit before scaling.
👤
Entrepreneur
A person who identifies a market opportunity, takes on the financial and personal risk of creating a business to pursue it, and organises the resources needed to deliver value. Entrepreneurship spans a spectrum from lifestyle businesses (designed to provide income and flexibility) to scalable startups (designed to grow aggressively) to social enterprises (designed to create social or environmental impact). The entrepreneurial mindset — curiosity, tolerance of ambiguity, bias toward action — is as defining as any specific activity.
📋
Business Model
The description of how a company creates, delivers, and captures value — covering who the customer is, what value the company provides, how it reaches customers, and how it makes money. The Business Model Canvas (Alexander Osterwalder, 2010) is the standard framework for mapping a business model across nine building blocks. Investors often scrutinise the business model as rigorously as the product, because a great product with a broken business model will not succeed commercially.
🧪
MVP
Minimum Viable Product — the simplest version of a product that delivers core value to a specific customer segment and enables the team to test critical assumptions before investing in full development. The concept, popularised by Eric Ries in The Lean Startup (2011), is based on the principle that the goal of an MVP is not to build a minimum product but to maximise learning — discovering what customers actually need versus what the team assumed they needed.
🔄
Pivot
A deliberate change in one or more fundamental dimensions of a startup's strategy — the problem being solved, the customer segment, the product, the channel, or the business model — based on learning from market feedback. A pivot is distinguished from abandonment (giving up) and iteration (incremental refinement): it is a significant but informed change in direction. Instagram pivoted from a location-based social game (Burbn) to a photo-sharing app; Slack pivoted from a gaming company to a workplace communication platform.
⏱️
Runway
The amount of time a startup can continue operating at its current burn rate before running out of cash. Runway = cash on hand / monthly burn rate. If a startup has £600,000 in the bank and burns £50,000 per month, it has 12 months of runway. Runway management is one of the most critical financial disciplines in a startup: founders must decide whether to extend runway by cutting burn or to use the runway to reach milestones that justify raising more capital.
💰
Bootstrapping
Building and growing a startup using only personal savings and revenue generated by the business — without raising external investment. Bootstrapped founders retain full equity and control but must grow more slowly and manage cash carefully. Bootstrapping forces commercial discipline: the business must generate revenue early rather than surviving on investor capital. Notable bootstrapped companies include Basecamp (formerly 37signals), Mailchimp, and Craigslist.
😇
Angel Investor
A high-net-worth individual who invests personal capital in early-stage startups — typically at pre-seed or seed stage — in exchange for equity. Angel investors often bring industry expertise, networks, and mentorship alongside capital. Angel rounds are typically smaller than institutional VC rounds (£25,000 to £500,000) and move faster because the decision is made by one person rather than a committee. The UK's Enterprise Investment Scheme (EIS) provides tax incentives that have significantly deepened the angel investment market.
🏦
Venture Capital
Institutional investment in high-growth startups in exchange for equity — provided by funds that pool capital from limited partners (pension funds, endowments, family offices) and deploy it across a portfolio of companies. VC funds operate on the assumption that most investments will fail or return modest multiples, but a small number will return 50x, 100x, or more — generating returns that compensate for the losses. VC investment typically follows seed funding and is structured as Series A, B, C rounds as the company scales.
📊
Pitch Deck
A presentation — typically 10 to 15 slides — used by a startup founder to communicate the business opportunity, team, product, market, traction, and funding ask to potential investors. An effective pitch deck tells a compelling story: here is the problem, here is our solution, here is evidence that people want it, here is how the business makes money, and here is why we are the right team to build it. The Sequoia Capital slide framework and Guy Kawasaki's 10-slide template are widely referenced standards.
📈
Traction
Evidence that a startup is gaining real-world momentum — that customers are adopting the product, revenue is growing, and the business is demonstrating product-market fit. Traction takes many forms: revenue growth, active user numbers, retention rates, customer testimonials, partnership agreements, or media coverage. The form of traction that matters most depends on the stage and business model — for a B2B SaaS startup, monthly recurring revenue (MRR) growth is the most credible indicator; for a consumer app, daily active user growth.
⚖️
Equity
An ownership stake in a company — represented as a percentage of the total shares outstanding. Equity is created when a company is incorporated and distributed among founders, employees (via option pools), and investors. Founders dilute their equity with each funding round as new shares are issued to investors. Managing the cap table (capitalisation table — the record of who owns what percentage) carefully from founding is critical: poor equity decisions early can cause damaging founder conflicts or leave founders with insufficient ownership to remain motivated.
🔥
Burn Rate
The rate at which a startup spends its cash reserves — typically measured monthly. Gross burn rate is total monthly expenditure; net burn rate is monthly expenditure minus monthly revenue. A startup burning £80,000 per month with £20,000 in monthly revenue has a net burn rate of £60,000. Burn rate is a critical metric for investors assessing financial discipline and for founders calculating runway. High burn rates can be appropriate during rapid growth phases but must be accompanied by evidence of return.
💎
Valuation
The estimated financial worth of a company — used to determine how much equity an investor receives in exchange for their capital. Pre-money valuation is the company's value before a new investment; post-money valuation is the value after. If a company has a £4 million pre-money valuation and raises £1 million, the post-money valuation is £5 million and the investor owns 20% (£1m / £5m). Early-stage startup valuations are highly subjective — they reflect the perceived potential of the team and market rather than current financial performance.
🎯
Product-Market Fit
The degree to which a product satisfies a strong market demand — the moment when a startup has found a customer segment that genuinely needs and loves what it is building. The concept, articulated by Marc Andreessen, describes the state where retention is strong, growth is organic, and customers are actively referring others. Before product-market fit, a startup's primary task is to discover it; after it, the primary task is to scale. Many startups raise capital and scale prematurely — before they have achieved genuine product-market fit — and fail as a result.
📏
Scalability
The ability of a business to grow its revenue significantly without a proportional increase in costs. A software company can sell to a million customers using the same product it built for a thousand — costs grow slowly while revenue grows rapidly. A law firm, by contrast, must hire a lawyer for every new client — it does not scale. Scalability is one of the defining characteristics investors look for in startups: the combination of large market size and a scalable business model creates the potential for exceptional returns.
👥
Founding Team
The initial group of individuals — typically two to four people — who create and build a startup through its earliest stage. The founding team is widely considered the most important factor in startup success: investors often say they bet on the team, not the idea. Effective founding teams combine complementary skills (technical, commercial, design), share aligned values and vision, and have the interpersonal resilience to survive the pressures of early-stage building. Founder conflict is among the most common causes of startup failure.
📄
Term Sheet
A non-binding document that outlines the key terms of a proposed investment — including the amount being invested, the pre-money valuation, the equity stake, and any special conditions (board seats, liquidation preferences, anti-dilution protection). The term sheet is the basis for negotiation between founders and investors before a legally binding investment agreement is drafted. Founders should understand the implications of every clause — particularly liquidation preferences, which determine the order in which investors and founders receive proceeds in an exit.
🔁
Iteration
The process of repeatedly testing, learning from, and improving a product or business process based on real-world feedback. Iteration is the operational expression of the build-measure-learn loop from lean startup methodology: build the smallest testable version, measure how users respond, learn what works and what does not, and build again. Successful startups typically iterate dozens or hundreds of times before finding what works — the willingness to iterate rather than defend original assumptions is one of the most important entrepreneurial disciplines.
🗺️
Go-to-Market Strategy
The plan for how a startup will reach its target customers and bring its product to market — covering the target customer segment, the value proposition, the sales and distribution channels, the pricing model, and the initial marketing approach. A strong product with a weak go-to-market strategy will fail to reach customers; a moderate product with a strong go-to-market strategy can achieve significant traction. Go-to-market strategy must evolve as the startup learns more about what channels, messages, and customer segments work.
🦄
Unicorn
A private startup company with a valuation of $1 billion or more — a term coined by venture capitalist Aileen Lee in 2013 to reflect how rare such companies were at the time. As of 2024, there are over 1,200 unicorns globally, making the term more common than its mythological namesake would imply. Unicorn status does not necessarily indicate profitability or sustainable business health — it reflects investor belief in future potential. Some unicorns have collapsed following public listing (Theranos, WeWork) when private valuations proved detached from commercial reality.

Text 1: What Is Entrepreneurship? — Defining a Word That Covers Everything

Entrepreneurship is one of the most used and least precisely defined words in the language of business. At its broadest, it describes anyone who starts and runs their own business. At its narrowest, it describes the specific activity of identifying a scalable market opportunity, assembling resources under conditions of uncertainty, and building an organisation capable of capturing that opportunity at significant scale. Both usages are legitimate — but they describe very different activities, require very different skills, and produce very different outcomes. Conflating them creates confusion about what entrepreneurship actually demands.

The management scholar Howard Stevenson defined entrepreneurship as 'the pursuit of opportunity beyond the resources currently controlled' — a definition that captures the essential tension of the entrepreneurial act: wanting to do something before you have the means to do it. This definition is broad enough to include the street vendor financing stock with a small loan, the software developer building a startup in their bedroom, and the executive launching a new product line within a large corporation (a phenomenon called intrapreneurship). What these activities share is not scale or industry — it is the combination of opportunity recognition, resource assembly under constraint, and tolerance of uncertainty.

Within this broad tent, it is useful to distinguish several categories. Lifestyle entrepreneurs build businesses designed to provide income, autonomy, and a particular way of life — they are not primarily motivated by scale or exit. Small and medium enterprise (SME) owners build and run businesses that may grow substantially but are not explicitly designed for rapid scale. Scalable startup founders are explicitly trying to build large companies quickly — they typically pursue venture capital, accept significant personal risk, and measure success in exits and returns rather than annual profit. Social entrepreneurs prioritise social or environmental impact alongside or instead of financial returns. Understanding which type of entrepreneurship a founder is pursuing matters enormously: the strategies, funding approaches, team structures, and success metrics are fundamentally different.

Text 2: The Startup Ecosystem — Investors, Accelerators, and the Infrastructure of Innovation

Startups do not exist in isolation. They are embedded in an ecosystem of investors, accelerators, mentors, co-working spaces, universities, corporations, and government programmes that collectively determine whether a given geography or sector generates successful companies. Silicon Valley's dominance in global venture-backed technology is not purely the result of founders being better — it reflects the depth of its ecosystem: a density of experienced investors willing to fund early-stage risk, a concentration of technical talent from world-class universities, a culture of knowledge-sharing and network effects that accelerates learning, and a network of service providers (law firms, banks, recruiters) that have optimised for startup clients. Understanding the ecosystem is essential for any founder navigating it.

Funding moves through a startup's lifecycle in stages. At the very earliest stage — pre-seed — founders typically fund themselves through savings, friends and family investments, or small grants. The seed stage (typically £250,000 to £2 million in the UK) is when angel investors and early-stage funds provide capital to allow the startup to validate product-market fit. Series A (typically £3 million to £15 million) is the first significant institutional round — investors expect to see evidence of a working business model and early revenue growth. Series B and beyond finance scaling: hiring commercial teams, entering new markets, and building the infrastructure for rapid growth. At each stage, the investors, their expectations, and the terms of investment change significantly.

Accelerators have become one of the most important infrastructure elements of the modern startup ecosystem. Y Combinator (YC), founded by Paul Graham in 2005, pioneered the model: a cohort of startups is selected twice yearly, given a small amount of capital (approximately $500,000) in exchange for 7% equity, and put through an intensive 3-month programme of mentorship, network access, and preparation for 'Demo Day' — a pitch event where the cohort presents to hundreds of investors simultaneously. YC alumni include Airbnb, Dropbox, Stripe, Reddit, and Coinbase — collectively worth trillions of dollars. The model has been replicated globally: Techstars, Entrepreneur First, and Seedcamp are among the most prominent European equivalents.

Grammar Point 1: Compound Nouns and Noun Phrases in Startup English
Why Startup Language Is Rich in Compound Nouns

Startup and investment language is dense with compound nouns and multi-word noun phrases that name specific concepts precisely. These compounds are fixed collocations — the words must appear in the correct order. 'Product-market fit' is not interchangeable with 'market-product fit'; 'burn rate' is not the same as 'rate of burn'. Learning these fixed phrases is as important as learning their definitions.

'burn rate' (NOT 'rate of burn')
'product-market fit' (NOT 'market-product fit')
'go-to-market strategy' (NOT 'strategy for going to market')
'pitch deck' (NOT 'deck pitch' or 'pitching document')
Hyphenation in Compound Adjectives

When a compound noun functions as an adjective before a noun, it is typically hyphenated. These hyphenated forms are common in startup descriptions and are part of correct professional register.

'a venture-backed startup' (adjective before noun — hyphenated)
'a seed-stage company' (before noun — hyphenated)
'an equity-free grant' (before noun — hyphenated)
'a go-to-market plan' (adjective compound before noun)
Building Accurate Compound Descriptions

Founders must describe their businesses precisely to investors and stakeholders. Compound noun phrases combine multiple descriptors efficiently — reducing the need for longer explanatory sentences.

Instead of: 'We are a software company that operates using a subscription model, aimed at businesses' → 'We are a B2B SaaS startup'
Instead of: 'We provide software as a service and charge monthly recurring fees' → 'We operate a monthly recurring revenue model'
Instead of: 'We are trying to find out if the market wants our product' → 'We are validating product-market fit'
Instead of: 'We are building the least complex version of our product that customers will pay for' → 'We are building our MVP'
📋
Grammar Point 2: Modal Verbs for Potential and Uncertainty in Pitches and Updates
The Certainty Spectrum in Startup Communication

Entrepreneurs must communicate potential without overclaiming. Investors are sophisticated — overconfident language ('will reach £100m in revenue by Year 3') damages credibility; appropriately hedged language ('could reach £100m with the right execution') signals honest realism. The choice of modal verb is the primary signal of confidence level.

Certain (use only when you have strong evidence): 'We will close this round by month-end — all term sheets are signed.'
High confidence: 'With Series A capital, we should reach £5m ARR within 18 months.'
Possible: 'This market could be significantly larger than our initial estimates.'
Uncertain: 'Enterprise adoption might be slower than we have projected in the B2C model.'
'Could', 'May', and 'Might' — Communicating Opportunity and Risk

In investor pitches and board updates, 'could' describes opportunity (what might become true with good execution); 'may' and 'might' describe both opportunity and risk (what might happen, positively or negatively). Both signal appropriate intellectual honesty.

Opportunity: 'This business could become the category leader in European legal tech within five years.'
Opportunity: 'The enterprise tier may generate significantly higher average contract values than we have modelled.'
Risk: 'Customer acquisition costs might increase as we move beyond the early adopter segment.'
Risk: 'Regulatory changes could affect the market in ways that are difficult to predict at this stage.'
'Must' and 'Need To' — Expressing Imperatives in Strategic Communication

Founders use 'must' and 'need to' when describing strategic necessities — actions or achievements that are required, not optional. These are particularly common in investor updates and board papers.

'We must achieve £500,000 in ARR before we can raise a Series A at a credible valuation.'
'The team needs to hire a VP of Sales before we launch the enterprise channel.'
'We must improve retention before scaling acquisition — growth built on a leaky bucket does not compound.'
'The business needs to reach cash-flow breakeven within 24 months to avoid a dilutive down round.'
📋
Grammar Point 3: Verbs for Growth, Change, and Business Activity
Raise, Achieve, and Scale — Core Startup Verbs

Startup English uses specific verb-noun collocations that are not interchangeable. Learning which verbs pair with which nouns is essential for professional fluency.

'raise' capital / funding / a round (NOT 'collect' or 'earn' money)
'achieve' product-market fit / profitability / milestones (NOT 'get' or 'find' — though 'find' PMF is acceptable colloquially)
'scale' the business / the team / a model (NOT 'grow' in the same way — 'scale' implies doing more without proportional cost increase)
'pitch' to investors / the board (NOT 'present' or 'talk' in formal contexts — 'pitch' implies a formal investment ask)
Describing Revenue and Growth — Precise Verbs

Growth metrics in startup communications use precise verbs that carry specific implications about rate, cause, and direction. Using vague verbs ('the business did well') in investor communications signals inexperience.

'Revenue grew from £50k to £600k — a 12x increase over 18 months.'
'Monthly active users declined by 15% following the pricing change.'
'The company expanded into Germany and France in Q3, using a partner-led model.'
'Churn fell from 8% to 3% after the product team improved the onboarding flow.'
Grant, Dilute, Vest — Equity-Specific Verbs

Equity management has its own verb vocabulary. These verbs describe specific legal and financial processes and are not interchangeable with general synonyms.

'grant' options — to give an employee the right to purchase shares at a specific price
'dilute' (equity) — to reduce an existing shareholder's percentage by issuing new shares
'vest' (shares or options) — for shares to become legally owned over time according to a schedule
'exercise' (options) — for an employee to use their option to purchase shares at the agreed strike price

📋 Founding Team Introduction — Write a Founding Team Introduction for a Pitch Deck
The 'Team' slide is often considered the most important slide in a pitch deck. Investors at pre-seed and seed stage are often backing the team as much as the idea — because the idea will almost certainly change, but the founders' capabilities, experience, and resilience will not.

You are writing the Team section of your pitch deck. The section should be 150-220 words and must:

• Introduce each founding team member (you may invent three co-founders for this exercise)
• For each founder: describe their most relevant experience, the specific capability they bring to this startup, and why this startup is the right fit for them personally

• Acknowledge any obvious gap in the founding team's skills and explain how you plan to address it

Co-Founder Conversation: Should We Raise or Bootstrap?
Startup Discussion · B1–B2 Level · approx. 108 sec

Two co-founders, Amara and Kenji, are discussing their funding strategy six months after launching their HR software startup. They have 15 paying customers and £8,000 in monthly recurring revenue. Amara: Okay. We have been building for six months. We have 15 customers, we are at £8,000 MRR, and we have about nine months of runway left at our current burn rate. The question is: do we try to raise a seed round now, or do we keep bootstrapping and try to reach something closer to product-market fit first? Kenji: I keep coming back to the retention numbers. Only 58% of customers who signed up in month one are still active. That is not good enough. If I were an investor and you showed me 58% retention at 90 days, I would pass. Amara: Agreed. But if we raise, we can afford to hire one more engineer and actually fix the onboarding problem. Right now it is just the two of us and we are spending half our time on customer support because the product is too hard to set up. Kenji: So you are saying we need money to get to the metrics that would justify raising money. Amara: Kind of. But I think there is also an argument for being more selective about customers first. We have been saying yes to every inbound enquiry. Some of these customers are not the right fit — they are using us as a workaround for a problem our product was not designed to solve. Kenji: That is a good point. If we tighten our ideal customer profile and focus only on the segment where retention is actually working — which is the medium-sized tech companies — the retention numbers look very different. In that segment, we are at 81%. Amara: 81% is fundable. That is the pitch. We have strong retention in one segment, we have identified product-market fit in that vertical, and we need capital to scale it. Kenji: How much do we raise and at what valuation? Amara: I think we go in at a £2 million pre-money valuation and try to raise £400,000. That gives us 18 months of runway and enough to hire the engineer and a part-time salesperson. Kenji: Let us write the pitch deck and test it on a few angels before we run a formal process.

Mentor Session: Building Your MVP
Mentorship Conversation · B1–B2 Level · approx. 106 sec
0:00

A startup mentor, Priya, is advising a first-time founder, Marcus, who is trying to decide how much to build before launching. Marcus: I have the full product planned out. I know exactly what it needs to do. I want to build it properly before I show it to anyone — I do not want to embarrass myself with something half-finished. Priya: I hear this from almost every first-time founder. And I understand the instinct — you have a vision, and showing something that does not match the vision feels like misrepresenting what you are building. But I want to push back on this thinking. Marcus: Why? Priya: Because the assumption that you know what the product needs to do is almost certainly wrong. Not because you are not smart — but because you have not talked to enough customers yet. Case Study 1: Airbnb — From Air Mattresses to $75 Billion, and the Pivot That Made It Possible
How three broke designers found product-market fit, survived the trough, and built one of the defining companies of the sharing economy

Background

In 2008, Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia were struggling to pay rent in San Francisco when a design conference came to town and all the hotels were full. They bought three air mattresses, set up a simple website called 'Air Bed and Breakfast', and offered accommodation — including home-cooked breakfast — to three conference attendees. The guests paid $80 per night; the hosts covered their rent. The concept worked, but the market turned out to be far narrower than they anticipated: conference accommodation was a specific, recurring but limited demand. The founders, joined by Nathan Blecharczyk as CTO, applied to Y Combinator and were accepted in the Winter 2009 batch — despite Paul Graham initially being sceptical of the concept. At YC, they were challenged to talk to their users obsessively. Chesky famously flew to New York — where most of their bookings were — and stayed with hosts, interviewed guests, and took photographs of listings himself, having noticed that listings with poor photographs attracted fewer bookings.

The Product-Market Fit Discovery

The photography insight was one of the early signals of a broader truth: Airbnb's product was not primarily a booking platform — it was a trust platform. Hosts needed to trust that guests would not damage their homes; guests needed to trust that listings would match their photographs. The team's obsession with understanding these trust dynamics — building reviews, identity verification, host guarantees, and photography services — was the foundation of the product that eventually scaled. By 2010, the company was growing but still small, and had to resort to creative bootstrapping: during the 2008 US presidential election, they sold limited-edition breakfast cereals — 'Obama O's' and 'Cap'n McCains' — to raise $30,000 to survive through the YC programme. The cereal boxes became famous in startup circles as an example of the scrappiness required to survive the early stages.

Lessons for Entrepreneurs

Airbnb's story carries several foundational lessons. First, the initial business model does not have to be the final one — the conference accommodation niche evolved into a global travel platform through iteration and user learning. Second, doing things that do not scale is often the right early approach: Chesky personally photographing listings in New York was not scalable, but it revealed what quality looked like and created the standard for the marketplace. Third, the most important product decisions are often about trust rather than features: Airbnb's success came from solving a fundamentally human problem — trusting strangers — through platform design. Fourth, survival sometimes requires resourcefulness and humour: the cereal box story is an enduring example of founder resilience, creativity, and willingness to do whatever it takes. Fifth, being rejected and staying at YC long enough to be challenged by mentors changed the trajectory of the company — the accelerator's value was in forcing the founders to talk to users before building.

Case Study 2: Y Combinator — How Paul Graham's Experiment Changed How Startups Are Built
The accelerator model that produced Airbnb, Stripe, Dropbox, and Reddit — and why it became the global template for startup support

Background

Y Combinator was founded in Cambridge, Massachusetts in 2005 by Paul Graham, Jessica Livingston, Robert Morris, and Trevor Blackwell. Graham, a successful entrepreneur and essayist, had become convinced that the traditional model of startup funding was deeply inefficient: venture capitalists invested too late in a startup's development, when valuations were already high, and provided capital without the structured mentorship and network access that early-stage founders needed most. YC's founding proposition was to invest very early — before product-market fit, often before a product existed — in exchange for a small equity stake, and to provide intensive support over three months culminating in Demo Day. The first batch in summer 2005 consisted of eight companies and received $6,000 per founder — enough to cover living costs for the summer.

The YC Model and Its Impact

The YC model evolved from that first modest batch into a global institution. The core elements remain consistent: twice-yearly cohorts (W for Winter, S for Summer), a fixed equity stake (now 7% for approximately $500,000), an intensive programme combining weekly dinners with successful founders, office hours with partners, and the pressure of Demo Day as a forcing function for progress. The programme's power comes not primarily from the capital — $500,000 is useful but not transformative at today's startup costs — but from the alumni network, the brand signal, and the forcing function of Demo Day. Being a YC company signals quality to investors, accelerates fundraising, and opens doors to potential hires, customers, and partners that would otherwise take years to access. Alumni companies including Airbnb, Stripe, Dropbox, Reddit, Coinbase, DoorDash, and Instacart are collectively worth hundreds of billions of dollars.

Why the Accelerator Model Works — and Its Limits

The accelerator model works because it compresses time. Three months of intense focus, accountability, and peer pressure from a cohort of equally committed founders produces more progress than most startups make in a year of isolated building. The Demo Day deadline is a forcing function: founders who might otherwise delay launches or over-engineer products are compelled to have something to show. The alumni network — tens of thousands of founders who have been through the same experience — provides ongoing support that extends well beyond the programme. However, the model also has limitations. Accelerators are most valuable at the very earliest stage — before product-market fit; after achieving traction, founders often find the accelerator's structure constraining. The equity taken at entry can be significant if the company becomes very large: 7% of a $10 billion company is $700 million of dilution at the seed stage. And the Demo Day model can create incentives to perform for investors rather than to build for customers.
