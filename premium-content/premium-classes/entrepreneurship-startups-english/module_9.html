📚 Key Legal Terms
🏖️
Beachhead Market
The single, specific, narrow market segment that a startup targets first — chosen because it is small enough to dominate, specific enough to reach efficiently, and strategically positioned so that success there creates a platform for expansion into adjacent segments. The term, taken from military strategy (a beachhead is the first territory captured during an invasion, used as a base to expand), was popularised by Geoffrey Moore in Crossing the Chasm. A beachhead market is not the long-term target — it is the starting point from which to build the credibility, references, and product capabilities needed to expand.
🎯
Ideal Customer Profile
A precise description of the specific type of organisation or individual most likely to buy, use, and derive maximum value from a product — and to become a long-term, high-value customer. An ICP differs from a persona in that it typically describes an organisation (for B2B) rather than an individual, and focuses on firmographic attributes (company size, industry, growth stage, technology stack, budget) and behavioural signals (how they currently solve the problem, where they look for solutions) rather than demographics. A well-defined ICP dramatically improves the efficiency of sales and marketing investment.
📊
Market Segmentation
The process of dividing a broad market into distinct subgroups of customers with common needs, characteristics, or behaviours — and selecting the segments most worth targeting. Segmentation can be based on firmographics (company size, industry, geography), demographics (individual characteristics), behaviour (product usage patterns), or psychographics (attitudes, values, and motivations). Effective segmentation produces segments that are: measurable (can be sized), substantial (large enough to be worth targeting), accessible (can be reached through available channels), differentiable (distinct from other segments), and actionable (the company can serve them).
🏆
Positioning
The way a company defines how its product or service is perceived in the minds of its target customers — relative to competitors and alternatives. Positioning answers: for whom is this product, what category does it belong to, and what makes it uniquely valuable? April Dunford's positioning framework — from Obviously Awesome (2019) — argues that positioning is the context in which customers evaluate everything about a company, and that most startups fail to position deliberately, allowing customers to position the product for them by comparing it to the first thing it reminds them of.
🗺️
Go-to-Market Strategy
The comprehensive plan for how a company will reach its target customers and bring its product to market — covering customer segment, value proposition, distribution channels, pricing, sales process, and marketing approach. A GTM strategy must answer: who will we sell to, through what channels, at what price, using what messages, and in what sequence. Many technically excellent startups fail because of a weak GTM strategy: a product that reaches the wrong customers at the wrong price through the wrong channel will not succeed however good it is.
🌊
Land and Expand
A B2B go-to-market strategy in which a startup enters a large organisation through a small initial purchase — typically one team, one department, or one use case — and then grows within the organisation over time by demonstrating value and expanding to additional teams, departments, and use cases. Land and expand is the dominant GTM model for B2B SaaS companies: it reduces the initial sales barrier (a small trial is easier to approve than an enterprise-wide deployment), demonstrates value before requiring a larger commitment, and produces high net revenue retention as expansion revenue compounds.
📈
Penetration Pricing
A pricing strategy in which a new market entrant sets a price significantly below the market rate to attract early customers quickly, build market share, and create switching costs before raising prices later. Penetration pricing is appropriate when: the market is price-sensitive, the unit economics support initial losses that can be recovered through scale, and the switching costs created by early adoption will retain customers when prices normalise. The risk: if the low price attracts customers who are primarily motivated by price rather than value, retention will collapse when prices increase.
💎
Premium Pricing
A pricing strategy in which a product is priced above the market rate — signalling superior quality, exclusivity, or a fundamentally different category. Premium pricing is appropriate when: the product delivers demonstrably superior value, the target customer segment prioritises quality over cost, and the positioning deliberately excludes price-sensitive customers who would not become loyal long-term customers. Premium pricing can be a self-fulfilling positioning signal: being the most expensive option in a market creates the perception of being the best option, attracting customers who self-select for value orientation.
🔒
Switching Cost
The cost — in time, money, effort, data migration, retraining, and relationship disruption — that a customer incurs when they change from one product or supplier to another. High switching costs create customer retention that is independent of product quality: once deeply integrated into a customer's workflow, data architecture, or supply chain, a product becomes difficult to displace even if a competitor offers a better or cheaper alternative. Building high switching costs is one of the most effective long-term competitive strategies — and one of the most powerful justifications for aggressive early adoption pricing.
🌐
Network Effect
The phenomenon by which a product or service becomes more valuable as more people use it — creating a self-reinforcing cycle of growth that makes the product increasingly difficult for competitors to displace. Network effects are the most powerful competitive moat available to a startup: once a marketplace, social platform, or communication tool reaches critical mass, the network itself becomes the primary value proposition. Direct network effects (each additional user benefits all other users equally) are the strongest; indirect network effects (additional users on one side benefit the other side) are common in marketplace businesses.
🏰
Competitive Moat
A durable structural advantage that protects a company's market position and makes it difficult for competitors to displace it — analogous to the water-filled moat surrounding a castle. Warren Buffett popularised the concept of economic moats in investing. For startups, moats include: network effects, switching costs, proprietary data that cannot be replicated, economies of scale that competitors cannot access, and brand recognition that commands a price premium. A startup building its market entry strategy should consider not just how to acquire its first customers but what structural advantages it will create that will prevent those customers from leaving when competitors emerge.
🔑
Category Creation
A market entry strategy in which a startup defines a new product category rather than competing in an existing one — positioning itself as the reference point against which all subsequent entrants are compared. Salesforce did not enter the 'CRM software' category — it created the 'cloud CRM' category. Hubspot created the 'inbound marketing platform' category. Category creation is high-risk (customers must be educated about the category before they can be persuaded to buy) and high-reward (the category creator typically becomes the category leader, benefiting from research that names them first and from the credibility of having defined the space).
📦
Distribution Channel
The route through which a product reaches its end customer — direct (sold by the company's own sales team, website, or store) or indirect (sold through partners, resellers, distributors, or marketplaces). Channel choice is one of the most consequential market entry decisions: the wrong channel reaches the wrong customers at the wrong cost. Direct channels offer maximum control over customer experience and higher margins; indirect channels offer scale and access to customers the company could not reach efficiently itself. SaaS companies typically begin with direct sales and add channel partners as the product matures.
🎯
Product-Led Growth
A go-to-market strategy in which the product itself is the primary driver of customer acquisition, conversion, and expansion — rather than a traditional sales or marketing team. PLG companies offer free or freemium access to the product, allow customers to discover value through self-serve usage, and convert users to paying customers through the product experience rather than through sales conversations. Slack, Dropbox, Figma, and Notion are canonical examples. PLG is most effective when the product's core value can be experienced quickly without implementation support, and when individual users can make or influence the purchase decision.
🔍
Competitive Displacement
The process of taking a customer from an incumbent competitor — convincing them to switch from their current solution to the startup's product. Competitive displacement is harder than category creation (where there is no incumbent) and requires understanding not just why the startup's product is better but why the customer is dissatisfied with the incumbent and what the switching cost is. The most effective competitive displacement strategy targets customers where dissatisfaction is highest (rather than starting with the competitor's most satisfied customers) and makes the switching cost as low as possible through implementation support, data migration assistance, and risk reduction.
🗓️
Expansion Sequencing
The deliberate ordering of which customer segments, geographies, or use cases to enter and in what order — based on a strategic logic that uses success in earlier markets to enable success in later ones. Amazon entered books first, then music, then video, then electronics, then general retail — each expansion was enabled by the infrastructure, customer trust, and distribution capabilities built in the previous market. Expansion sequencing is the long-term complement to beachhead market selection: the beachhead is where you start; the expansion sequence is the path from there to the full vision.
💰
Customer Acquisition Cost
The total sales and marketing investment required to acquire a single new customer — a critical market entry metric because it determines whether a GTM strategy is economically viable. CAC must be compared to Customer Lifetime Value (LTV): a sustainable business requires LTV significantly above CAC (typically 3:1 or better). CAC varies dramatically by channel and customer segment: enterprise direct sales has very high CAC but very high LTV; PLG has very low CAC but often lower LTV. Understanding CAC by channel enables founders to allocate GTM investment toward the most efficient acquisition routes.
🌍
Geographic Expansion
The deliberate entry into a new country or region — typically pursued once a startup has proven its GTM model in its home market. Geographic expansion is one of the most common causes of startup failure because it consumes resources (capital, management attention, localisation investment) without the operational foundation that made the home market successful. The most common mistake: expanding geographically before proving the model is replicable (not just lucky), before hiring local market expertise, and before understanding the regulatory, cultural, and competitive differences that make each new market unique.
🤝
Channel Partner
A third-party organisation that sells, implements, or refers a startup's product to end customers — in exchange for a commission, reseller margin, or referral fee. Channel partners include: value-added resellers (VARs), system integrators (SIs), independent software vendors (ISVs), and referral partners. Channel partnerships can dramatically accelerate market entry by providing access to established customer relationships and distribution capabilities that would take years to build directly. The risk: channel partners have their own commercial priorities — they will sell the product that is easiest to sell and most profitable for them, not necessarily the product that is best for the customer.
📍
Minimum Viable Segment
The smallest possible market segment that is still large enough to be commercially meaningful and strategically significant — the narrowest beachhead that creates a credible platform for expansion. Inspired by the lean startup concept of the minimum viable product, the minimum viable segment forces founders to resist the temptation to target everyone and instead identify the one type of customer for whom the product is most precisely fit. Dominating a small segment is more valuable than having 1% share of a large segment: deep penetration of a small segment creates references, testimonials, and product specialisation that enable expansion.
🔁
Retention Rate
The percentage of customers or revenue that a business retains over a defined period — the inverse of churn. Retention rate is the most important metric for any subscription or recurring revenue business because it determines whether the business grows over time or shrinks despite new customer acquisition. High retention is a prerequisite for efficient market entry: if customers churn quickly, the business must constantly replace them, CAC compounds without benefit, and the word-of-mouth and referrals that power organic growth never materialise. Retention rate differs by segment and use case, which is why beachhead segment selection matters — some segments have structurally better retention than others.

Text 1: The Beachhead Market — Why Startups Must Go Narrow Before They Go Wide

Geoffrey Moore's observation in Crossing the Chasm (1991) — that most technology startups fail to transition from early adopters to the mainstream market because of the gap between what early adopters need and what mainstream customers need — has become one of the most cited frameworks in startup strategy. But the practical implication of Moore's work is often underappreciated: crossing the chasm requires focusing on a single beachhead market with such concentration that the startup dominates that segment completely before expanding. This is the opposite of what most founders' instincts tell them to do.

Most founders, when asked about their target market, describe it in the broadest possible terms: 'SMEs in Europe', 'HR teams at enterprise companies', 'anyone who uses a computer to manage documents'. These are not target markets — they are market categories. A beachhead market is a specific segment so precisely defined that the startup can dominate it: not 1% of all SMEs in Europe, but the 'sole in-house lawyers at Series A and B SaaS companies in the UK'. This precision matters for several reasons. First, it enables focused messaging: a product that is trying to speak to everyone speaks compellingly to no one. A product positioned for 'sole in-house lawyers at Series A and B SaaS companies' can address their specific workflow, their specific compliance concerns, and their specific budget constraints in a way that a generic product cannot. Second, it enables efficient customer acquisition: if the target is defined precisely enough, the team can identify every potential customer in the segment and approach them systematically — rather than spray-and-pray marketing that reaches millions and converts dozens. Third, it enables reference selling: when the first ten customers in a precisely defined segment are delighted, they are likely to know each other, attend the same events, and trust each other's recommendations.

The criteria for a good beachhead market are specific. The segment must be large enough to be commercially meaningful — too small, and there is no path to growth; too large, and it is not really a beachhead, it is the whole market. It must be reachable — the company must be able to find and communicate with potential customers efficiently. It must be winnable — the startup must have a genuine reason why it can serve this segment better than alternatives. And it must be a launching pad — success in the beachhead must create credibility, references, or product capabilities that make the next adjacent market easier to enter. The beachhead is not the destination; it is the starting position from which to expand.

Text 2: Positioning as Strategy — Why Most Startups Are Positioned by Accident

April Dunford's insight in Obviously Awesome (2019) is elegantly simple but has profound strategic implications: positioning is not something a company chooses whether to do — it is something that always happens. If a company does not deliberately position its product, customers will position it for them — by comparing it to the most familiar thing it resembles. This accidental positioning is almost always damaging: customers who compare a novel product to a familiar alternative will evaluate it on the criteria that matter to the familiar alternative, not the criteria that reveal the novel product's true value.

The consequences of accidental positioning are everywhere in startup failures. A contract management platform compared to Microsoft Word will be evaluated on whether it is better at writing documents — which it is not, and should not be. A messaging platform compared to email will be evaluated on whether it is better at email — it is not. Slack was not better at email; it was a fundamentally different way of organising work communication. But for Slack to be evaluated on that basis, it had to actively position against the concept of email-as-the-default rather than allow itself to be evaluated as an email client. The positioning work — the decision to describe Slack as replacing the endless email chain rather than as an email alternative — was strategic, deliberate, and consequential.

Dunford's framework for deliberate positioning involves five components: alternatives (what customers would do if the product did not exist), unique attributes (what makes the product genuinely different from those alternatives), value (what benefits those attributes create for the target customer), characteristics (which customers care most about that value — the ICP), and market category (the frame of reference in which the product is best evaluated). The most powerful positioning decisions are often about market category: a product positioned as 'a better [existing category]' will always be evaluated against the best in that category; a product positioned as '[new category] for [specific customer]' creates its own evaluation criteria and is harder to benchmark against incumbents.

Grammar Point 1: Causal and Consequential Language in Market Entry Strategy
Expressing What Enables or Allows

Market entry strategy documents explain how choices enable outcomes. 'Enable' and 'allow' describe a relationship where A creates the conditions for B — without claiming A directly causes B. This distinction matters: a beachhead market enables focused messaging but does not guarantee it.

'Focusing on the beachhead enabled the team to create highly specific messaging.' (choice creates conditions)
'The land-and-expand model allowed the startup to grow account value without additional sales headcount.' (model removes constraints)
'High switching costs allowed the company to defend its pricing during competitive pressure.' (structural advantage creates room for action)
'A well-defined ICP enabled the sales team to qualify and disqualify leads efficiently.' (precision creates speed)'
Expressing Direct Causation — Led To, Resulted In, Produced

When market entry choices directly cause specific outcomes, stronger causal language is appropriate. 'Led to', 'resulted in', and 'produced' indicate a direct causal chain rather than a facilitation relationship.

'The decision to enter Germany without local market knowledge led to a costly localisation failure.' (choice → failure)
'Premium pricing resulted in a higher-quality customer base with lower churn than the penetration pricing cohort.' (strategy → customer outcome)
'The network effect produced a self-reinforcing cycle that accelerated growth beyond the rate the sales team could explain.' (mechanism → unexpected outcome)
'Unfocused targeting resulted in inconsistent messaging and inefficient customer acquisition costs.' (strategic error → consequence)'
Expressing Sequential Consequences — Enabling Expansion Sequencing Language

Market entry strategy often involves explaining how success in one area enables the next stage. Sequential causal language builds the strategic narrative from current position to future state.

'Success in the beachhead segment created the references and product capabilities needed to enter the mid-market.'
'The land-and-expand model generated expansion revenue that funded the enterprise sales team hire without additional external capital.'
'Dominating the UK SaaS legal market gave us the credibility to approach comparable European markets with existing customer references.'
'The switching costs created by the first cohort of enterprise customers provided the financial stability to invest in the next product tier.'
📋
Grammar Point 2: Comparative Structures in Market and Competitive Analysis
Comparing Segments, Channels, and Strategies

Market analysis requires precise comparison language. The most common comparison structures: 'X is [comparative adjective] than Y', 'X has [noun phrase] compared to Y', and 'X produces [outcome], whereas Y produces [different outcome]'.

'The enterprise segment has significantly higher LTV than the SME segment — but also 6x higher CAC.'
'Direct sales generates higher margins than channel partner sales, while channel sales reaches a broader customer base at lower cost.'
'Product-led growth has a considerably lower CAC compared to enterprise direct sales, at the cost of lower average contract values.'
'Premium pricing attracts value-oriented customers, whereas penetration pricing attracts price-sensitive customers — fundamentally different segments with different retention profiles.'
Expressing Competitive Advantage Precisely

Competitive analysis requires precise language about the nature and magnitude of advantages. 'Stronger', 'higher', 'more efficient', 'significantly better' — each signals something different about the type and scale of the advantage.

'Our switching cost advantage is substantially greater in the enterprise segment than in the SME segment.'
'The competitor's brand recognition is considerably stronger in the US market than in the UK — which makes the UK the more attractive initial beachhead.'
'Our NRR of 118% compares favourably to the industry benchmark of 105-110% — indicating superior expansion revenue performance.'
'Category creation is inherently riskier than competing in an existing category but offers substantially higher potential returns if the category takes hold.'
Using 'While' and 'Whereas' for Strategic Trade-Off Analysis

'While' and 'whereas' are the most efficient structures for expressing strategic trade-offs — where a choice has benefits in one dimension and costs in another. Strategic memos and investor presentations use these structures constantly.

'While penetration pricing accelerates adoption, it attracts price-sensitive customers who may not remain loyal when prices normalise.'
'Direct sales offers maximum control over the customer experience, whereas channel partners offer scale at the cost of margin and control.'
'Category creation positions the company as the category leader, while requiring significantly more market education investment than entering an existing category.'
'Land and expand reduces the initial sales barrier, whereas a full enterprise sale generates higher initial contract value at the cost of a longer, more complex sales cycle.'
📋
Grammar Point 3: Hedging Language for Strategic Uncertainty in Market Entry
Expressing Projections and Estimates with Appropriate Confidence

Market entry projections are always uncertain. Hedging language signals intellectual honesty — distinguishing between what is known and what is estimated, without abandoning confidence in the strategic direction.

'The beachhead segment is estimated to represent approximately 1,200 companies — though this estimate is based on market interviews rather than a comprehensive survey.'
'The land-and-expand model is projected to grow average account value from £299 to £700 within 18 months — assuming an expansion rate consistent with the pilot accounts.'
'Geographic expansion into Germany could be feasible by Q3 of Year 2, provided the UK milestones are achieved on the current timeline.'
'It is likely that the premium pricing strategy will attract a different customer profile — one that is less price-sensitive and more value-focused.'
Expressing Uncertainty About Competitive Response

Competitors' behaviour is inherently uncertain. Strategic documents acknowledge this uncertainty rather than presenting implausibly confident predictions about how rivals will react.

'The incumbent may respond with aggressive discounting — which would compress margins in the beachhead segment.'
'It is difficult to predict how the market leader will react to a category creation strategy — they may ignore the new category, attempt to replicate it, or acquire a competitor operating in it.'
'Competitive pricing pressure could materialise earlier than modelled if the incumbent perceives us as a material threat before our customer base provides natural protection.'
'It is possible that the competitor will accelerate their own product development in response to our launch — though their current roadmap does not suggest this is imminent.'
Conditional Hedging for Milestone-Dependent Strategies

Many market entry decisions are conditional on achieving specific milestones. Conditional hedging structures link the strategy to the evidence that will validate or change it.

'The expansion into Germany will be pursued in Year 2, provided the UK model achieves £500,000 ARR and demonstrates 85%+ 12-month retention in the beachhead.'
'If the land-and-expand motion produces the projected expansion rate, the premium pricing tier will be introduced in Month 9.'
'Should the beachhead retention rate fall below 75%, we will pause the expansion sequencing and focus on improving retention before entering the adjacent segment.'
'Geographic expansion is contingent on closing the Series A — without the capital to hire local market expertise, the execution risk is too high to proceed.'

Market Entry Discussion: Choosing the Beachhead
Strategy Session · B2 Level · approx. 110 sec
0:00

The ContractIQ founding team — Priya (CEO), Tom (CTO), and their adviser Marcus — are deciding which segment to target first. They have two viable options. Marcus: Let me frame the choice clearly. You have two segments that have both shown interest in the product. Option A: sole in-house lawyers at Series A and B SaaS companies — approximately 1,200 companies in the UK, lower ACV but easier to close. Option B: heads of legal operations at 500-2,000 employee companies — approximately 3,000 companies, higher ACV but a 90-day sales cycle versus Option A's 14 days. Which do you go for first? Priya: My instinct is Option B — bigger segment, higher ACV. Marcus: And that is the wrong instinct, and I want to explain why. The question is not which segment is bigger. The question is which segment you can dominate. You have £500,000 and 18 months. In that time, you need to build enough references, enough product depth, and enough brand recognition in one segment to be able to say: we are the go-to solution for this type of customer. Tom: And we cannot do that in two segments simultaneously. Marcus: Correct. With Option B, you have a 90-day sales cycle. In 18 months, you might close 20 deals. With Option A, you have a 14-day cycle. In 18 months, you could close 80-100 deals, build a reference base of 80-100 companies, and get published case studies, testimonials, and product feedback that shapes the product for the larger adjacent market. Case Study 1: Airbnb's Market Entry Sequencing — How Starting in One City Created a Global Company
How Airbnb used conference events as a beachhead, then expanded city by city — and the disciplined sequencing that prevented premature scaling

Background

Airbnb's market entry strategy in its earliest years was a masterclass in beachhead thinking, even though the founders might not have described it in those terms. The initial concept — renting out air mattresses in your home to strangers attending conferences when hotels are full — was a specific, narrow use case serving a highly defined segment: conference attendees who needed accommodation when hotels in a conference city were sold out. This was a beachhead market in the strictest sense: small enough to serve manually, specific enough to reach systematically (conference attendees are easy to find), and strategically positioned as a proving ground for the broader travel accommodation market.

The Expansion Sequence

From the conference accommodation beachhead, Airbnb expanded in a deliberate sequence. First, they focused intensively on New York — not as a random choice but because New York had the highest density of both potential hosts (people in expensive apartments looking for extra income) and potential guests (the city attracted millions of visitors). Brian Chesky's famous decision to personally visit New York hosts, photograph their listings, and improve the quality of the marketplace was not just a product improvement — it was a focused market entry investment in the first geographic beachhead. From New York, the company expanded to other major US cities, then to Europe (beginning with Paris and London, which had similar density dynamics), then globally. Each expansion was enabled by the infrastructure, brand, and trust mechanisms built in the previous markets.

Lessons for Market Entry Sequencing

Airbnb's expansion sequencing offers enduring lessons. First, the conference event beachhead was not the destination — it was the minimum viable proof that the concept worked, generating the evidence needed to attract YC investment and build toward the real market. Second, geographic expansion was not random — each new city was chosen based on density of potential supply and demand, proximity to existing markets, and cultural similarity to already-proven markets. Third, the company resisted pressure to expand globally before the model was proven — focusing on getting New York right before expanding to other US cities, and getting the US right before expanding to Europe. The discipline to sequence expansion rather than scatter it across multiple markets simultaneously was one of the key differences between Airbnb and many of its competitors who expanded too early and struggled to maintain quality.

Case Study 2: Salesforce and Category Creation — How Marc Benioff Made 'No Software' a Market Entry Strategy
How Salesforce created the cloud CRM category rather than competing in the on-premise CRM category — and won by changing the terms of the competition

Background

When Marc Benioff founded Salesforce in 1999, the enterprise CRM market was dominated by Siebel Systems — a billion-dollar company with established products, existing customer relationships, and significant brand recognition. The rational response to entering such a market would have been to build a product that was better or cheaper than Siebel's and compete directly. Benioff did the opposite: he created a new category — 'cloud CRM' or 'on-demand CRM' — in which Siebel was not the benchmark because Siebel did not have a cloud product. By defining a new category, Salesforce ensured that it would be evaluated on criteria that favoured it (simplicity, speed of deployment, no IT infrastructure required) rather than on criteria that favoured Siebel (feature depth, enterprise integrations, regulatory compliance).

The Category Creation Strategy

Salesforce's category creation strategy had three components. First, it defined the category against the existing alternative rather than against a competitor: the enemy was 'software' (meaning on-premise, complex, expensive software that required months of implementation), not Siebel specifically. The 'No Software' campaign — featuring a red circle with a line through the word 'software' — made the category definition visceral and memorable. Second, it deliberately chose a customer segment where the category advantages were most acute: small and mid-sized businesses that were too small to afford Siebel's implementation costs but had genuine CRM needs. This was a beachhead market from which Salesforce eventually expanded into the enterprise. Third, Salesforce invested heavily in analyst relations and PR to establish the category name — 'on-demand CRM' and later 'SaaS' — before competitors could claim it.

Lessons for Category Creation as a Market Entry Strategy

Salesforce's category creation offers specific lessons for founders considering this strategy. First, category creation requires defining the enemy: the campaign is not 'we are better than competitor X' but 'the old way of doing this is broken, and we represent a fundamentally different approach.' The 'old way' must be sufficiently common that customers immediately recognise the pain. Second, the initial beachhead for category creation is often the segment where the incumbent is weakest — in Salesforce's case, the SMB market that Siebel had neglected because it was too small to be worth their enterprise sales model. Third, category creation requires naming: you cannot create a category without giving it a name that customers, analysts, and journalists can use. Salesforce's investment in establishing 'SaaS' as a recognised category name was as important as the product itself.
