📚 Key Legal Terms
📋
Executive Summary
A concise, self-contained overview of the entire business plan — typically one to two pages — written for senior decision-makers who may not read the full document. An effective executive summary covers: the problem and solution, the target market and its size, the business model and revenue approach, key traction or milestones, the team, and the funding ask. It is written last but placed first. Many investors and lenders read only the executive summary before deciding whether to request the full plan.
📊
Market Sizing
The process of estimating the size of the opportunity a startup is pursuing — typically expressed across three levels: Total Addressable Market (TAM — the total global revenue opportunity if the company had 100% market share), Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM — the portion of the TAM targetable with the current product and geographic reach), and Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM — the realistic portion of the SAM the company can capture in the near term). Credible market sizing uses bottom-up analysis rather than top-down percentages of large market reports.
🔍
Competitive Analysis
A structured assessment of the competitive landscape — identifying existing alternatives, direct competitors, and indirect competitors, and evaluating their strengths, weaknesses, positioning, and market share. A strong competitive analysis does not pretend there is no competition; it demonstrates that the founder understands the landscape, has identified a genuine gap, and has a credible answer to the question: 'Why will customers switch to you rather than staying with what they use today?'
💹
Financial Projections
Forward-looking financial statements — typically covering three to five years — that model the expected revenue, costs, and cash position of the business under stated assumptions. Financial projections communicate two things: the financial opportunity (how large the business could become) and the credibility of the founders (whether their assumptions are realistic and internally consistent). Sophisticated investors read projections primarily to evaluate the quality of the assumptions, not the absolute numbers.
📉
Cash Flow Statement
A financial statement showing the actual movement of cash into and out of the business over a period — distinguishing between operating cash flows (from core business activities), investing cash flows (from purchasing or selling assets), and financing cash flows (from raising or repaying capital). For early-stage startups, the cash flow statement is often more important than the profit and loss statement: a business can be technically profitable and still fail from running out of cash if receivables are slow or expenses are front-loaded.
📈
Income Statement
Also called the Profit and Loss Statement (P&L) — a financial statement summarising revenues, costs, and expenses over a period to show whether the business made a profit or a loss. The income statement shows: revenue minus cost of goods sold equals gross profit; gross profit minus operating expenses equals EBITDA; EBITDA minus depreciation, interest, and tax equals net profit. For startups, the income statement must be accompanied by detailed revenue and cost assumptions.
⚖️
Break-Even Analysis
A calculation that identifies the point at which total revenue equals total costs — the break-even point — below which the business makes a loss and above which it makes a profit. Break-even analysis answers: how many units must be sold, or what monthly revenue must be reached, before the business covers all its costs? It demonstrates financial awareness and helps investors assess whether the business model is viable at achievable sales volumes. Break-even = Fixed Costs / (Price per Unit - Variable Cost per Unit).
🏆
Competitive Advantage
A characteristic or capability that allows a business to outperform competitors — sustainably, not just temporarily. Genuine competitive advantages include: network effects (the product becomes more valuable as more people use it), proprietary data or IP, switching costs (it is painful for customers to leave), cost advantages (producing at lower cost than competitors due to scale or process), and regulatory moats (licences or certifications that are difficult to obtain). Features and price discounts are not competitive advantages — they can be easily copied.
🎯
Target Market
The specific customer segment a business has identified as its primary focus — the group for whom the product is designed and to whom all marketing, sales, and product development is optimised. A well-defined target market is narrow enough to be reachable and large enough to be commercially significant. Business plans that describe the target market as 'anyone who uses the internet' or 'all SMEs' signal a lack of focus that makes customer acquisition strategies impossible to evaluate.
📐
SWOT Analysis
A structured strategic planning tool that assesses an organisation's internal Strengths and Weaknesses alongside external Opportunities and Threats. SWOT is widely used but often poorly executed: generic SWOTs that list obvious observations ('strength: experienced team; weakness: limited capital') add no analytical value. Effective SWOTs make specific, evidence-based claims ('strength: proprietary dataset from 50,000 clinical trials, not replicable by competitors without years of data collection') and draw explicit strategic conclusions from the intersection of each quadrant.
💰
Revenue Forecast
A detailed projection of the revenue a business expects to generate over a future period — typically built bottom-up from assumptions about customer numbers, conversion rates, pricing, and contract values. A credible revenue forecast is accompanied by explicit assumptions that can be tested against market data and comparable companies. Common errors: projecting revenue growth without modelling the sales capacity needed to achieve it, ignoring churn in subscription businesses, and failing to segment revenue by product line or customer type.
🏗️
Operating Plan
The section of a business plan that describes how the company will execute its strategy operationally — covering hiring plans, key activities for each quarter, technology infrastructure, supply chain, and office or physical requirements. An operating plan translates the strategic vision into actionable steps with timelines, responsible owners, and resource requirements. It demonstrates execution capability and shows investors that the founding team understands what it will take to build the business, not just what they want to build.
📏
Key Assumptions
The explicit statements of the underlying beliefs and estimates on which financial projections and strategic plans are based. Key assumptions cover: the size and growth rate of the market, the expected conversion rate through the sales funnel, the average customer lifetime, the cost of customer acquisition, the gross margin, and the hiring plan. Making assumptions explicit is a mark of credibility — it allows investors to evaluate whether each assumption is reasonable and test the sensitivity of the projections to changes in key variables.
📊
Gross Margin
The percentage of revenue remaining after deducting the direct costs of producing the product or delivering the service (cost of goods sold — COGS). Gross margin = (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue. Software companies typically have gross margins of 70-85%; professional services companies have margins of 30-50%; hardware companies may have margins of 20-40%. Gross margin is a fundamental indicator of business model scalability: a high gross margin means each incremental sale generates significantly more profit than it costs to deliver.
🗓️
Milestone Plan
A forward-looking schedule of significant achievements the company plans to reach — typically covering 12 to 24 months — that demonstrates progress toward viability and investment readiness. Key milestones for startups include: first paying customer, product launch, achieving monthly recurring revenue targets, hiring key team members, reaching product-market fit, and closing funding rounds. Milestones in a business plan should be specific, measurable, and linked to the funding ask: investors want to understand what the requested capital will enable the company to achieve.
⚠️
Risk Register
A structured document that identifies, assesses, and describes mitigation strategies for the key risks facing the business. A strong risk register acknowledges market risks (the target customer does not adopt as expected), competitive risks (a well-funded competitor enters the market), operational risks (a key hire cannot be made), financial risks (revenue is slower than projected), and regulatory risks (regulatory changes affect the business model). Business plans that include no risk analysis appear naive; those that show the founder has thought carefully about risks and has mitigation strategies build investor confidence.
💻
Total Addressable Market
The total revenue opportunity available if a company achieved 100% market share of its defined market — representing the theoretical ceiling of the opportunity. TAM is commonly misused: founders frequently quote enormous TAMs from analyst reports ('the global HR software market is $20 billion') to make the opportunity seem large, without demonstrating that their specific product can realistically capture any meaningful portion of it. Credible TAM analysis defines the market precisely and sizes it from first principles rather than citing headline figures from industry reports.
🔢
EBITDA
Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortisation — a measure of a company's core operational profitability, stripping out financing decisions, tax jurisdiction, and non-cash accounting items. EBITDA is widely used as a valuation metric (companies are often valued at a multiple of EBITDA) and as a measure of cash generation potential before capital investment. For early-stage startups, EBITDA is typically negative; investors focus on the trajectory toward positive EBITDA and the projected timeline.
📝
Porter's Five Forces
A strategic framework developed by Michael Porter (1979) for analysing the competitive intensity and attractiveness of an industry — across five forces: threat of new entrants, bargaining power of suppliers, bargaining power of buyers, threat of substitute products, and competitive rivalry among existing competitors. The framework helps founders understand not just who their direct competitors are but the structural dynamics of the industry that determine whether margins are sustainably attractive. A market with high buyer bargaining power and low switching costs is structurally challenging regardless of the quality of a specific product.
📌
Market Penetration
The degree to which a product or service has been adopted within a target market — expressed as the percentage of potential customers who are current users. In business plans, market penetration describes the projected rate at which the company expects to win customers from the available market. Realistic penetration assumptions are critical for credible financial projections: a startup claiming it will capture 10% of a large market in year three without a credible sales and marketing plan will face scepticism from experienced investors.
🏦
Financial Model
A structured spreadsheet (or financial planning tool) that integrates income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement projections with the underlying assumptions that drive them — allowing founders and investors to model different scenarios and test the sensitivity of key outputs to changes in input assumptions. A robust financial model is dynamic: changing the assumed conversion rate or average contract value automatically updates all dependent outputs. The financial model is the analytical foundation behind the financial projections section of a business plan.

Text 1: What Makes an Effective Business Plan — Structure, Credibility, and Audience

A business plan is not a document for the founder — it is a document for the reader. This distinction matters enormously when deciding what to include, how long to make it, and how to present it. The typical reader of a business plan is an investor, a lender, or a potential partner who has read hundreds of similar documents, is highly sceptical of projections, and will spend considerably less time on the document than its author spent creating it. Writing a business plan without a clear model of the reader is like preparing a speech without knowing your audience: it may be technically correct but it will fail to achieve its purpose.

The most effective business plans share several characteristics. First, they lead with a compelling executive summary — one to two pages that allow a busy reader to understand the entire opportunity without reading further, and that create enough interest to make the reader want to read further. Second, they demonstrate market understanding through rigorous analysis rather than assertion: a large market size is not established by citing a headline figure from a market research report but by building a credible bottom-up estimate of who the customers are, how many there are, and how much each will pay. Third, they are honest about competition — acknowledging alternatives rather than pretending the competitive landscape is empty, which experienced readers immediately recognise as a red flag.

Fourth, and most important for credibility, effective business plans make their assumptions explicit. A revenue projection without assumptions is a number without meaning: £5 million of revenue in year three could represent a realistic target or a fantasy depending on the assumptions that underpin it. When founders state their assumptions clearly — 'we assume a 3% conversion rate from trial to paid, a £400 average contract value, and 15% monthly growth in new trials reaching the funnel' — they invite scrutiny rather than avoiding it. This is counterintuitive to many first-time founders, who worry that stating assumptions reveals uncertainty. The opposite is true: investors know that projections are uncertain; what they are evaluating is whether the founder understands the business well enough to have formed intelligent estimates, and whether the estimates are internally consistent and comparable to industry benchmarks.

Text 2: Financial Projections — How to Build Them and What Makes Them Credible

Of all the sections in a business plan, financial projections generate the most anxiety among first-time founders — and the most scepticism among experienced investors. The anxiety is understandable: projecting the future performance of a business that has not yet proven itself is inherently uncertain, and a number on a spreadsheet feels deceptively precise. The scepticism is equally understandable: investors have read too many business plans where year-one revenue of £200,000 magically becomes £5 million by year three without a coherent explanation of what changes to make that growth happen.

The key discipline in building credible financial projections is working bottom-up rather than top-down. A top-down projection starts with the market size and works backward: 'the market is £500 million; we will capture 1% by year three; therefore our year-three revenue is £5 million.' This approach is seductive because it produces large numbers from small percentages — but it says nothing about how the business will actually acquire customers. A bottom-up projection starts with the sales process and works forward: 'we will hire two salespeople in Q2, each capable of closing four deals per month at an average contract value of £5,000, growing to eight salespeople by year-end. This gives us 96 deals in Q3, 128 in Q4, growing to...' This approach produces smaller numbers in the short term but demonstrates genuine commercial understanding.

A complete financial model for a business plan includes three interconnected statements. The income statement shows revenue minus costs and produces gross profit, EBITDA, and net profit or loss. The cash flow statement shows when money actually enters and leaves the bank — critical for startups where customers may pay on 30 or 60-day terms while suppliers demand immediate payment. The balance sheet shows the company's assets, liabilities, and equity at a point in time. These three statements should be linked: changes in revenue and costs flow through the income statement, which affects the cash position in the cash flow statement, which affects the equity in the balance sheet. A financial model where these statements are not linked — or where the cash flow does not reflect the timing differences between revenue recognition and cash receipt — reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of financial modelling.

Grammar Point 1: Formal Language for Presenting Financial Data
Precise Financial Verbs and Expressions

Business plan financial sections use specific verbs and verb phrases for describing numbers and trends. These verbs carry precise meanings that casual synonyms do not — and using the right verb signals financial literacy.

'Revenue is projected / expected / forecast to reach £X by Year 3.' (NOT 'will be' or 'should get to')
'Gross margin improves from X% to Y% as fixed costs are spread over a larger base.' (NOT 'goes up' or 'gets better')
'Fixed costs remain / stand at approximately £X per month throughout Year 1.' (NOT 'stay the same' or 'keep at')
'The break-even point occurs / stands at £X in monthly revenue.' (NOT 'happens at' or 'sits at')
Describing Trends and Changes Precisely

Financial data involves trends — increases, decreases, improvements, and declines. Formal business plan language uses specific verbs and adverbs to describe these trends with precision.

Increase: 'Operating expenses increase by 18% in Year 2, driven primarily by...'
Decrease: 'Customer acquisition cost decreases / falls from £340 to £210 as referral channels mature.'
Improve: 'Gross margin improves from 61% to 74% over the three-year period.'
Remain stable: 'Fixed infrastructure costs remain at approximately £X per month throughout the forecast period.'
Presenting Totals and Summaries

Summarising financial positions — total funding raised, total costs, cumulative revenue — uses specific formal constructions that differ from casual summary language.

'Total funding raised to date stands at / amounts to / totals £420,000.' (NOT 'we have raised')
'The financial model presents / contains / includes three scenarios.' (NOT 'we show' or 'there are')
'Cash-flow breakeven is achieved in Month 18.' (passive — impersonal and formal)
'The company requires a minimum cash reserve of £X to manage the receivables cycle.'
📋
Grammar Point 2: Formal Recommendation and Conclusion Language
Impersonal Recommendation Structures

Business plan recommendations use impersonal structures rather than first-person language ('I think' or 'we believe') to signal that conclusions are based on evidence rather than personal preference. The impersonal structure signals analytical authority.

'It is recommended that the company launch in the UK market first.' (NOT 'We think we should launch in the UK')
'It is proposed that the board review the pricing strategy at Year 1.' (NOT 'We propose reviewing')
'It is advised that the company engage legal counsel before...' (NOT 'We advise getting a lawyer')
'It should be noted that these projections assume...' (NOT 'Note that we have assumed')
Evidence-Based Conclusion Language

Business plan conclusions draw on the analysis presented to reach stated findings. This language signals that conclusions follow from evidence — not from assumption or wishful thinking.

'The analysis concludes that the SME segment offers the most attractive near-term opportunity.'
'The competitive landscape reveals that no current solution addresses...'
'The evidence strongly suggests that onboarding completion drives retention.'
'Based on the analysis above / presented, the company is well-positioned to...'
Reporting Findings Formally

Each section of a business plan presents findings that are then used to inform the overall narrative. These findings should be reported formally — with the section as the subject, not the founders.

'Section 3 outlines the primary competitive risks and the mitigation strategies in place.'
'The financial model indicates that the business will require... '
'The market analysis demonstrates that the target segment is growing at 18% annually.'
'The cash flow projections show that the company will require a bridge facility of £X in Month 16.'
📋
Grammar Point 3: Hedging Language for Projections and Assumptions
Signalling That Projections Are Conditional

Financial projections in business plans are always conditional — they depend on assumptions that may or may not prove accurate. Conditional structures signal this clearly without undermining the credibility of the projection.

'Revenue is projected to reach £X, assuming the following conditions are met: [list assumptions].'
'These figures are contingent on achieving the planned Series A funding by Q3.'
'Year 3 revenue of £6.4 million is dependent on / conditional upon achieving the hiring plan in Section 5.'
'Provided that the conversion rate assumptions hold, the business will reach break-even in Month 18.'
Calibrated Confidence — Choosing the Right Modal

Projections use modals calibrated to the evidence behind them. High-confidence projections (based on contracts or signed letters of intent) use 'will'; probable outcomes use 'should' or 'is expected to'; possible but uncertain outcomes use 'may' or 'could'.

High confidence: 'The signed enterprise contract will contribute £180,000 to Year 1 revenue.'
Expected: 'Customer acquisition cost is expected to / should fall as organic channels mature.'
Possible: 'The US market expansion may be brought forward if UK traction exceeds projections.'
Uncertain: 'A further funding round could be required if growth is slower than modelled.'
Scope and Limitation Language

Formal business plan sections explicitly limit the scope of their claims — preventing overgeneralisation and acknowledging the boundaries of the analysis.

'These projections cover a three-year horizon and should not be taken as forecasts beyond that period.'
'The market sizing analysis is limited to the UK market; international opportunity is not included.'
'It is difficult to project market share with precision at this stage; the figures below are consistent with comparable companies at seed stage.'
'These figures are preliminary and should be reviewed against updated industry data prior to any major strategic decision.'

A seed-stage investor, Leila, is giving feedback to a founder, Marcus, after reading the executive summary of his business plan. The startup builds workflow automation software for financial services firms. Leila: Thank you for sending this over. I read the executive summary carefully and I want to give you honest feedback. Marcus: Please — that is exactly what I need. Leila: You have three problems. First — the problem statement. You say the software helps financial firms 'manage processes more efficiently.' That is not a problem statement — that is a category description. What specifically is broken? What is it costing them? Marcus: We are solving the compliance workflow problem — firms currently use email and spreadsheets to track regulatory tasks, which means they miss deadlines and face regulatory fines. Leila: That is a compelling problem. Why is that not the first sentence? Marcus: I thought I was setting context first. Leila: In an executive summary, context is a luxury you cannot afford. Lead with the problem. Every word in the executive summary is competing for a reader who might stop after the first paragraph. Marcus: Okay. And the second issue? Leila: Your market sizing. You say the market is '$15 billion globally.' That tells me nothing useful. How many financial services firms are there in the UK? How many have compliance teams of more than five people? What would they pay per month for a product like yours? Give me a bottom-up number I can evaluate. Marcus: We have done that analysis — we just did not include it in the executive summary because we thought it was too detailed. Leila: The methodology should be in the body of the plan, but the conclusion should be in the executive summary. Something like: 'Our serviceable market is 2,400 UK financial firms with compliance teams of five or more, at an average contract value of £18,000 per year — a £43 million addressable opportunity.' That I can evaluate. Marcus: And the third issue? Leila: There is no team section. I do not know who is building this, why you are credible to solve this problem, or why I should trust your projections. The team slide is often the most important in a pitch — give it equal weight in the executive summary.

Mentor Session: Building Credible Financial Projections
Mentoring Session · B2 Level · approx. 108 sec
0:00

A startup mentor, Priya, is reviewing the financial projections in a founder's business plan. The founder, Tom, has built a basic financial model for his B2B software company. Priya: Walk me through your Year 1 revenue projection. Tom: We are projecting £480,000 in Year 1. Priya: Okay. What is that based on? Tom: Our market is large and we think we can capture a small percentage. Priya: That is a top-down projection. Let me show you why that is a problem. If your market is large and you capture 1%, that sounds modest — but it says nothing about how you will acquire those customers. An investor looking at this will ask: how many customers does that represent? What is your average contract value? How many salespeople do you need to close that many deals? Tom: Okay. We plan to have two salespeople. Our average contract value is about £24,000. Priya: Good. Now let us build this properly. Two salespeople, each capable of closing how many deals per quarter? Tom: Realistically, maybe four to five deals per quarter each, once they are ramped. Priya: And how long does it take a salesperson to ramp — to reach full productivity? Tom: About three months. Priya: So if you hire them at the start of Q1, they are at full productivity from Q2 onward. Two salespeople, five deals each, per quarter, from Q2 onward — that is 10 deals per quarter, 30 deals in Q2, Q3, and Q4. At £24,000 per deal, that is £720,000. Not £480,000. Tom: That is higher than my projection. Priya: Yes — and that is a credibility problem. If your model shows £480,000 but the actual capacity of your sales team at your deal size implies £720,000, investors will either think your deal size is wrong, your close rate is being modelled incorrectly, or you have not thought through the model carefully. Tom: So which figure should I use? Priya: Neither — yet. Work the model properly. What is your realistic close rate from qualified lead to closed deal? Tom: Maybe 30%. Priya: Then your model needs to show the full funnel — qualified leads needed, expected close rate, and the resulting deal volume. That is the model investors trust.

Amara: About half of you — that is about what I expected. And how many of you wrote: 'Our main competitors are...' followed by a paragraph explaining how you are better than all of them? Amara: Most of the rest. Okay. Both of those are the wrong approaches, and I want to explain why. Claiming there is no competition does two things. First, it tells an experienced investor that you have not done your research, because every problem already has some existing solution — even if that solution is a spreadsheet, a phone call, or an old piece of software. Second — and this is more subtle — it removes the most important question from the competitive analysis: why will customers choose you over what they currently use? That question is the heart of the section. If there is no competition, there is no question to answer. And if there is no question to answer, there is no strategic insight. On the other side — presenting a table of features that you have and competitors do not have. That is a feature comparison, not a competitive analysis. A competitive analysis answers: what is the nature of the competitive dynamic in this market? What determines whether a customer switches to a new solution? What structural advantages — if any — make your business defensible over time? What I want to see in your competitive analysis is three things. First: who do customers use today to solve this problem — not just direct competitors but alternatives. Second: what would make a customer switch, and what would make switching hard. Third: what is your sustainable advantage — not 'we are cheaper' or 'we have more features', both of which are easily copied, but something structural: a proprietary dataset, a network effect, a regulatory advantage, or deep switching costs.

Case Study 1: The BBC iPlayer Business Case — How a Public Organisation Wrote the Business Plan That Changed British Broadcasting
How a rigorous internal business plan convinced a sceptical institution to invest in the technology that would define the next two decades of British television

Background

In the mid-2000s, the BBC faced a strategic challenge that would define its relevance for the next generation: how to deliver television content over the internet. The concept — on-demand video streaming available free of charge to UK licence fee payers — had enormous potential but also significant technical complexity, regulatory risk, and internal opposition. Critics argued that free internet video would undermine the commercial market, expose the BBC to challenges from commercial broadcasters, and require significant investment that the BBC's funding model could not support. The iPlayer project required an internal business case that was rigorous enough to overcome these objections, credible enough to satisfy the BBC Trust (the BBC's regulatory body), and compelling enough to secure the significant engineering and infrastructure investment required.

The Business Case

The iPlayer business case — which was developed over several years before iPlayer launched in 2007 — had to address several dimensions that go beyond typical startup business plans. First, the market analysis had to demonstrate not just that there was demand for on-demand television but that the BBC was the right institution to provide it and that the public interest justified the investment. Second, the competitive analysis had to acknowledge that commercial providers (YouTube, later Netflix) would emerge in this space and explain how the BBC's unique position — public funding, brand trust, and breadth of content — created a defensible position that commercial players could not replicate. Third, the financial model had to demonstrate that the investment was proportionate to the benefit delivered, measured not in revenue (the BBC does not charge for iPlayer) but in reach, engagement, and public value — metrics specific to public broadcasting.

Lessons for Business Plan Writing

The iPlayer case offers several enduring lessons for business plan writers beyond the startup context. First, the audience determines the business plan's structure and metrics: the BBC's regulators cared about public value, not profit; the business case used metrics appropriate to its audience. Second, competitive analysis must be honest about the competitive dynamics — the BBC's business case acknowledged that well-funded commercial competitors would enter the on-demand space and argued that this made a strong BBC presence more important, not less. Third, financial projections must use the right success metrics: for a commercial company, this is revenue and profit; for a public institution, it may be reach, cost efficiency, or social impact. Fourth, the business plan is a persuasion document — the BBC's case was ultimately a persuasion exercise, designed to bring a risk-averse institution to a bold decision by addressing every objection systematically.

Case Study 2: Theranos — What the Most Dangerous Business Plan in History Teaches About Honesty in Financial Projections
How fraudulent projections, overconfident claims, and missing assumptions destroyed a $9 billion company — and what every founder can learn from it

Background

Theranos was a Silicon Valley medical technology company founded by Elizabeth Holmes in 2003. Its central claim — that it could run hundreds of medical diagnostic tests from a single drop of blood using a proprietary device called the Edison — was revolutionary if true. By 2014, Theranos was valued at $9 billion, Holmes appeared on the covers of Forbes and Fortune, and the company had partnered with Walgreens and Safeway to deploy its technology in pharmacies across the United States. In 2015, a Wall Street Journal investigation by journalist John Carreyrou revealed that the technology did not work as claimed — Theranos was running most of its tests on commercially available machines from competitors. Holmes was subsequently charged with and convicted of multiple counts of fraud in 2022.

What the Business Plan Got Wrong — and What This Teaches

The Theranos case is relevant to business plan writing not as an example to emulate but as a cautionary illustration of the consequences of dishonesty in projections and claims. Several specific business plan failures are instructive. First, the financial projections were built on assumptions that did not reflect the actual state of the technology — the technology was not capable of performing what the projections assumed. When projections are disconnected from operational reality, the company must either misrepresent operations or fail to deliver — Theranos chose the former. Second, the competitive analysis implicitly claimed a competitive advantage — proprietary diagnostic technology — that did not actually exist. A robust business plan must be honest about what the competitive advantage is and is not; claiming capabilities that do not exist is fraud. Third, the risk register was absent in the most consequential respect: the plan did not acknowledge that the core technology was unproven. Investors, partners, and regulators were presented with a picture that omitted the central risk to the entire business.

The Positive Lessons

Counterintuitively, the Theranos case teaches the value of honesty in business plans — not through its failure but through its contrast with what honest business plans look like. An honest business plan that stated 'our technology currently performs X tests reliably; we are developing the capability to perform Y and Z tests, which we estimate will take 18 months and £X million to achieve' would have attracted different investors and created different expectations. It might have raised less capital and at a lower valuation — but it would not have been fraudulent. The most durable competitive advantages in business plan history have been real: Stripe's developer experience, Google's algorithm, Amazon's logistics. The most spectacular failures have involved claims that were not grounded in reality. The business plan is a statement of intent and belief — but it must be grounded in honest assessment of what exists, what is possible, and what is uncertain.
