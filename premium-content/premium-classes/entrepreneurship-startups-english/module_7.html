📚 Key Legal Terms
📧
Cold Email
An unsolicited email sent to a recipient with whom the sender has no prior relationship — the startup equivalent of a cold call. Effective cold emails are short (under 150 words), specific (referencing something concrete about the recipient's interests, portfolio, or recent activity), relevant (connecting the startup to something the recipient cares about), and focused on a single small ask (a 20-minute call, not an investment commitment). The cold email is a test of the founder's ability to communicate concisely and compellingly — the same skills that make a good pitch.
🔥
Subject Line
The first line of an email, displayed in the recipient's inbox before they open the message — the single most important factor in determining whether an email is opened at all. Effective subject lines for investor and partner outreach are specific (referencing the company or a shared connection), intriguing without being clickbait, and short enough to display fully on mobile (under 50 characters is ideal). Subject lines that simply say 'Introduction' or 'Investment Opportunity' are among the least effective — they say nothing about why this email is worth opening.
📞
Call to Action
The specific request made at the close of an email — what the sender wants the recipient to do next. A clear, single call to action is one of the most important principles of effective email outreach: the recipient should know exactly what one thing they are being asked to do. For investor outreach: 'Would you be open to a 20-minute call next week?' For partnership outreach: 'Could we arrange a brief call to explore whether this is worth pursuing?' Vague calls to action ('please let me know if you are interested') produce fewer responses than specific, time-bounded ones.
✉️
Forwardable Email
An email written with the explicit intention of being forwarded by one person to another — typically used when asking someone to introduce you to a potential investor or partner. A forwardable email is short, self-contained, and written in the third person (so it can be forwarded without editing): 'I wanted to share a brief note about [Company] — a company I have been advising that I think would be right up your alley...' The person forwarding the email should be able to forward it with zero editing and one line of personal endorsement.
🤲
Double Opt-In Introduction
An introduction approach in which the introducer asks both parties for permission before making the introduction — rather than sending a group email or forwarding without asking. The double opt-in process: 'I know someone who would be a great fit for your fund — can I make an introduction?' Then, separately: 'I know an investor who I think would be very interested in what you are building — would you be open to an introduction?' Only once both sides have said yes is the introduction made. Double opt-in introductions have significantly higher conversion rates because both parties have already expressed interest.
📌
Social Proof
Evidence within an email that reduces a recipient's perceived risk and increases credibility — demonstrated through named investors, notable customers, credible press coverage, or endorsements from respected figures. For early-stage founders, social proof might include: 'We have been backed by [Named Angel] and are part of the Entrepreneur First cohort' or 'Our customers include [Recognisable Company]' or 'The problem was featured in the FT last month.' Social proof signals that others who are capable of evaluating the opportunity have already done so and found it compelling.
🎯
Relevance Hook
An opening sentence that demonstrates why this specific email is being sent to this specific recipient at this specific time — the most powerful tool for breaking through an inbox full of generic outreach. A relevance hook references something the recipient did, wrote, said, or invested in: 'I read your recent piece on the future of legal technology — specifically your point about contract management being the next category to be disrupted by AI. That is exactly what we are building.' A strong relevance hook makes the recipient feel that this email was written for them, not mass-produced.
📈
Traction Line
A single sentence in an investor email that communicates the most compelling metric — the piece of evidence most likely to change an investor's prior from 'probably a waste of time' to 'worth a conversation'. The traction line should be the most credible and impressive number available: '£42,000 MRR growing at 18% month-on-month' or '34 paying enterprise customers in 8 months with 94% net revenue retention'. The traction line should be placed early in the email — in the first or second sentence — before the reader has decided to stop.
🔁
Follow-Up Email
A subsequent email sent to a recipient who has not responded to a previous email — designed to resurface the conversation without appearing aggressive or entitled. Effective follow-up emails are short (one to three sentences), add something new (a metric update, a press mention, a relevant insight), and make it easy to respond ('I am happy to share more detail — or if this is not a fit, no problem at all'). The conventional wisdom: follow up once or twice, then stop. A third follow-up after two silences is rarely appropriate for investor outreach.
🌡️
Warm Email
An email sent to a recipient with whom there is some prior connection — a shared contact, a previous meeting, a mutual event attended, or a prior conversation. Warm emails have significantly higher open and response rates than cold emails because the recipient has a prior reason to engage. The warm email typically references the connection in the opening: 'Following our conversation at the Sifted Summit last month...' or 'Marcus Chen suggested I reach out to you...' The strength of the prior connection determines how warm the email is and how much context-setting it requires.
📋
One-Line Summary
A single sentence that describes the company clearly enough for someone with no prior knowledge to understand what it does and why it matters — the startup equivalent of a dictionary definition. The one-line summary is used in subject lines, email openings, and social introductions. The format: '[Company] is a [product type] that helps [customer] [achieve outcome] by [mechanism].' Example: 'ContractIQ is a B2B SaaS platform that helps in-house legal teams at mid-sized companies automate contract workflows, reducing administration time by 70%.'
📊
Deck Request
An investor's response to an outreach email asking to see the full pitch deck — a positive signal indicating interest in learning more. A deck request is not an investment; it is the first step in a longer evaluation process. When sending the deck in response to a request, founders should: attach a PDF (not a link that requires a login), include a brief covering note that restates the key metrics, and suggest a call to discuss. The email accompanying the deck should be even shorter than the original outreach — the deck is doing the work.
⏰
Time-Sensitive Hook
A reference in an outreach email to a specific event or circumstance that creates a natural reason to connect now rather than later — a funding round closing date, a demo day, a press milestone, or a partnership about to close. 'We are closing our seed round on March 31 and have two slots remaining' is a time-sensitive hook. Used honestly, time-sensitive hooks create urgency without pressure; used manipulatively, they damage credibility. The most credible time-sensitive hooks are verifiable milestones rather than artificial deadlines.
🧭
Investor Thesis
An investor's stated focus — the sector, stage, geography, and business model types they invest in — which determines whether any given startup is a potential fit. Researching an investor's thesis before reaching out is essential for effective outreach: an email to a fintech-focused fund about a legal tech startup, or to a consumer investor about a B2B SaaS company, signals that the founder has not done basic research. The investor thesis is typically available on the fund's website, in their portfolio description, and in the individual partner's LinkedIn and writing.
🔍
Personalisation
The practice of tailoring an email specifically to the recipient's known interests, portfolio, recent activity, or stated thesis — as opposed to sending an identical message to many people. Personalisation exists on a spectrum: at the minimum, using the recipient's name and referencing their fund correctly; at maximum, referencing a specific portfolio company, a recent blog post or talk, or a known investment thesis. The most effective personalisation demonstrates that the sender has done real research — not just found the name in a directory.
📬
Introduction Request
An email asking a mutual contact to introduce the sender to a potential investor or partner. An effective introduction request makes the job as easy as possible for the person being asked: it explains in one or two sentences why the introduction would be valuable for the target ('I know she focuses on B2B SaaS in the legal sector — which is exactly what we are building'), includes a short forwardable note, and makes clear that the sender understands the introducer might not be comfortable and that is perfectly fine.
📝
Follow-Up Sequence
A planned series of follow-up emails sent at defined intervals after an initial outreach — designed to resurface the conversation with new value at each touch rather than simply repeating the original request. A typical investor outreach follow-up sequence: Day 1 initial email; Day 8 follow-up 1 (add a new metric or milestone); Day 21 follow-up 2 (share a relevant press mention or portfolio company reference); then stop. Each follow-up should be shorter than the last and should add something new rather than just asking 'did you see my last email?'
🏷️
Email Signature
The block of identifying information at the bottom of a professional email — typically: name, title, company, website, phone number, and sometimes a brief one-line description of the company or a notable metric. For investor outreach, the email signature should include a LinkedIn profile link and a company website. Some founders include a single standout metric ('£42,000 MRR | 94% NRR') in the signature — a way of reinforcing traction without repeating it in the body. The signature should be clean and minimal — not decorated with images, excessive links, or legal disclaimers that make every email feel like a corporate communication.
🎭
Register
The level of formality and the vocabulary choices appropriate to a specific email context and recipient relationship. Investor and partner emails typically operate in a professional register — specific, direct, and respectful — avoiding both formal corporate language ('I am writing to you to request the pleasure of your acquaintance') and casual language ('Hey! Hope you are good!'). The appropriate register shifts with the relationship: first cold email to a VC partner = professional; third email to an angel who has been responsive = semi-formal; internal team email = casual. Founders who do not calibrate register correctly damage first impressions.
💬
Reply Rate
The percentage of outreach emails that generate a response — the primary metric for measuring cold email effectiveness. Average reply rates for cold investor emails are low (typically 5-15%); highly personalised, specific emails targeting investors whose thesis matches perfectly can achieve 30-40%. Reply rate is affected by: subject line quality, personalisation level, relevance of the thesis match, traction demonstrated, credibility of any social proof, clarity of the call to action, and timing (emails sent Tuesday-Thursday mornings typically outperform weekends). Tracking reply rate across different email versions helps founders improve their outreach over time.
🔗
Mutual Connection
A person known to both the email sender and recipient — the most valuable element of a warm introduction. A mutual connection reduces the perceived risk of the introduction: the investor trusts that the mutual connection has done basic screening, the founder benefits from the endorsement, and the mutual connection's credibility is lent to the interaction. When referencing a mutual connection in an email opening, always name them explicitly: 'Sarah Chen at Hurst suggested I reach out to you' is far more effective than 'We have a mutual connection who thought we should speak.'

Text 1: Why Most Investor Emails Fail — and What the Ones That Work Have in Common

A partner at a mid-sized UK venture fund estimates that they receive between 200 and 400 unsolicited investment enquiries per week. Of those, they typically respond to fewer than ten — and most of those responses are polite declines. The filtering process is rapid and ruthless: subject line irrelevant or generic → delete without opening. First sentence about the founder rather than the opportunity → skim and close. Three paragraphs of company background before any mention of traction → abandon. A request for thirty minutes of time in the first email from someone with no context → ignore. The ratio of emails sent to responses received for poorly crafted investor outreach is not 1 in 10 — it is closer to 1 in 50 or 1 in 100.

What distinguishes the emails that get responses? The evidence — from investor surveys, outreach coaches, and founders who have studied their reply rates — consistently points to the same qualities. First, specificity: the email demonstrates that the sender knows who the recipient is and why this specific investor is a logical fit. A founder who emails a B2B SaaS investor with a consumer app has either not researched the fund or does not care — neither reflects well on their judgment. Second, the traction line: a single compelling metric that changes the reader's prior assessment is the most powerful tool in an outreach email. '£42,000 MRR growing at 18% month-on-month' in the second sentence of an email produces a qualitatively different reading experience than the same information buried in paragraph three. Third, brevity: every sentence that does not add value reduces the probability of a response. Investor emails that work are almost never long.

The third quality — brevity — is worth dwelling on because it runs counter to the instinct of many founders. Founders who have spent months or years building their companies naturally want to explain everything: the technology, the team, the competitive landscape, the financial model, the five-year vision. The investor does not want any of this in a cold email. They want to know three things: is this company in a space I find interesting, does it have evidence that it is working, and is this founder someone worth talking to? These three questions can be answered in four sentences. The goal of the cold email is not to replace the meeting — it is to earn the meeting.

Text 2: The Partnership Proposal Email — Asking for Something Without Sounding Desperate

The most common error in partnership proposal emails is leading with what the startup needs rather than what the potential partner gains. 'We are reaching out because we believe a partnership with your organisation would greatly benefit our growth' is an honest sentence — but it is entirely oriented around the startup's interests. The recipient reads this and thinks: 'You want something from me. Why should I care?' The partnership email that earns a response starts from the opposite position: 'We have 12,000 SME clients who regularly ask us about HR software. We currently send them to a list of generic vendors, but I think your platform would genuinely serve them better than anything else in the market.' This email leads with value — something the recipient immediately cares about.

The structure of an effective partnership proposal email follows a simple logic: attention (why this is relevant to you specifically), interest (what we are offering that benefits you), desire (evidence that this is real and achievable), and action (one specific next step). The attention section — typically the first sentence — must be specific about what the startup brings to the table for this partner. The interest section — one to two sentences — should describe the arrangement in terms of what the partner gains, not what the startup gains. The desire section — again one to two sentences — should provide evidence that makes the proposal credible: a customer count, a relevant reference customer, a previous partnership success. The action section should be a single, low-commitment request: a 20-minute call, not a signed agreement.

The tone of a partnership proposal email matters as much as the structure. Desperation — even subtle desperation — is immediately detected by experienced business development executives. The email should read as an offer between equals rather than a request from a weaker party to a stronger one. This requires founders to genuinely believe that what they are offering has value to the partner — and to communicate from that belief. If the partnership is genuinely good for both sides, the email should reflect that confidence. If the founder is reaching out primarily because they need the partner's distribution rather than because the partner would genuinely benefit, an experienced recipient will detect this — and the email will not receive a response.

Grammar Point 1: Writing Effective Subject Lines
The Subject Line as a Filter — Not a Summary

The subject line's job is not to summarise the email — it is to give the recipient a compelling reason to open it. The most effective subject lines for investor and partner outreach are specific, concrete, and short. They reference something real and relevant — not a generic category.

Effective: 'ContractIQ — £42k MRR | Hurst-backed | 20 mins?' (specific + metric + brief ask)
Effective: 'Re: your piece on legal ops — building exactly what you described' (relevance hook in the subject)
Effective: 'Sarah Chen at Hurst suggested I reach out' (mutual connection named immediately)
Ineffective: 'Investment Opportunity' / 'Introduction' / 'Quick question' (tells the recipient nothing specific)
Subject Line Formulas That Work

Several subject line structures consistently produce better open rates for startup outreach. These are not rigid templates — they are structural patterns that create specificity and relevance.

[Company] — [metric] | [traction proof] | [brief ask]: 'ContractIQ — £42k MRR | Freshfields customer | 20 mins?'
Reference to their work: 'Re: your post on [specific topic] — building [what they described]'
Mutual connection: '[Name] suggested I reach out — [one-word category]'
Follow-up with new information: '[Company] update — [new milestone since last email]'
What to Avoid in Subject Lines

Certain subject line patterns consistently produce low open rates because they are overused, vague, or miscalibrated for the relationship.

'Introduction' — used by everyone; gives the recipient zero signal
'Quick question' — almost always not quick; feels manipulative
'Following up' — the weakest possible follow-up subject line; adds nothing
'Exciting opportunity' — investors read hundreds of 'exciting opportunities' every week; it signals nothing interesting
📋
Grammar Point 2: Structuring Email Bodies for Busy Recipients
The Opening Sentence Does the Most Work

In a cold email, the first sentence either earns the second or loses the reader. The most effective first sentences are relevance hooks — demonstrating that the email was written for this specific person, not mass-produced.

Strong: 'I read your recent investment in Hive Legal — we are building in the adjacent contract workflow space.' (specific reference to their known interests)
Strong: 'Sarah Chen suggested I reach out — she thought our NRR story would resonate with your portfolio thesis.' (warm reference + why it's relevant)
Weak: 'I am writing to introduce ContractIQ.' (tells them nothing they couldn't deduce from any email)
Weak: 'My name is Amara and I am the CEO of ContractIQ, a legal technology startup.' (bio first = wrong priority)
The Evidence Sandwich — Specificity Before Context

Corporate writing puts context before evidence — giving background before the main point. Startup emails should invert this: lead with the most compelling fact (the traction line), then provide the minimum context needed for it to make sense.

Correct order: 'ContractIQ: £42k MRR, 94% NRR, 34 enterprise customers in 8 months. We automate contract workflows for in-house legal teams — reducing admin time by 70%.'
Incorrect order: 'ContractIQ was founded in 2023 to address the contract management problem in legal operations. After extensive product development, we now have 34 enterprise customers and £42k MRR.'
The investor reads left to right and stops when they stop caring — put the thing that makes them care first.
One traction metric with context beats three metrics without context every time.
The Close — One Specific Ask

The final sentence of a cold email should contain exactly one ask — specific, low-commitment, and easy to say yes to. The ask should make it effortless for the recipient to take the desired action.

Strong: 'Would you have 20 minutes next week? I can work around your schedule.' (specific + easy + you do the work)
Strong: 'Happy to send the deck if useful — or if this is not a fit, no problem at all.' (offers an out, reduces pressure)
Weak: 'Please let me know if you would be interested in learning more.' (vague, passive, requires the recipient to define the next step)
Weak: 'I look forward to the opportunity to present our business to you and your partners at a time of your choosing.' (formal, wordy, low probability of action)
📋
Grammar Point 3: Register and Tone for Different Email Relationships
The Register Spectrum for Startup Outreach

Startup email register sits on a spectrum from very formal (never appropriate for cold outreach) to casual (appropriate only with established relationships). The default for first-contact investor and partner emails is professional-direct: respectful, specific, no filler.

Very formal (avoid): 'Dear Sir/Madam, I am writing to respectfully request your consideration...' — feels dated, creates distance
Professional-direct (appropriate for first cold email): 'I noticed your investment in Hive Legal — we are building in the adjacent contract workflow space. [Metric]. Would 20 minutes work?'
Semi-formal (appropriate after first positive response): 'Hi Sarah — thanks for the quick reply. Deck attached as requested + three things I would love your reaction on.'
Casual (appropriate with established relationships): 'Quick update — passed £50k MRR this month. Excited to share more when we catch up.'
Avoiding Desperation and Over-Formality

The two most damaging tone errors in startup email outreach are desperation (signalling need rather than opportunity) and over-formality (creating distance and sounding unlike a real person).

Desperation signals: 'I really hope you will consider this — it would mean a great deal to us.' → Replace with: 'Happy to share more if useful — no pressure if timing is not right.'
Over-formality signals: 'I humbly request your consideration of the enclosed investment memorandum.' → Replace with: 'Deck attached — let me know if you want to talk through the numbers.'
Desperation in follow-up: 'I am very disappointed not to have heard back — I know you must be very busy but this is really important.' → Replace with: 'Following up from three weeks ago — [new metric]. Happy to connect if timing is better now.'
The right tone reads as: confident but not arrogant, specific but not pressuring, persistent but not needy.
Calibrating Warmth with Relationship Depth

The warmth of an email should match the depth of the relationship — and advance it slightly rather than jumping ahead. Premature warmth feels presumptuous; under-warmth with a known contact feels cold.

First cold email: professional-direct, no first name used until they respond
After a positive first reply: use first name, be slightly warmer but remain professional
After meeting in person: reference the meeting specifically ('following our conversation at SaaStock'), be warm and direct
With an ongoing adviser/mentor relationship: casual is appropriate — drop the formality entirely

A founder, Kenji, is discussing investor outreach strategy with his co-founder and adviser, Leila. They are preparing for a seed round. Leila: Before we start sending emails, I want to think about this systematically. How many investors are we targeting and what is our prioritisation? Kenji: I have a list of 80 investors — I pulled it from Crunchbase and sorted by who has invested in B2B SaaS in the legal and compliance space in the last 18 months. Leila: Good. Now segment them. Out of 80, how many have invested in something that is directly relevant — not just adjacent? Kenji: Maybe 15 to 20. Leila: Those are your tier-one targets. They should get a fully personalised email — reference a specific portfolio company, something they have written or said about the category. Not a template. Kenji: And the remaining 60? Leila: Tier two — semi-personalised. Same structure, but the opening references their thesis generally rather than a specific investment. And then tier three — anyone you have thought about adding but they are not obviously a fit. Do not email tier three at all yet. Kenji: Why not? Leila: Because a poorly targeted email can close a door that would otherwise have been open. If you email someone and your thesis is clearly wrong for them, you have burned that relationship without any return. Better to approach them with a warm introduction later when you understand their thesis better. Kenji: What about the follow-up sequence? Leila: One follow-up after 7-10 days if no response. Add a new metric or a new development — not just 'did you see my last email'. If no response after the follow-up, stop. More than two contacts without a response is aggressive and damages your reputation in a small community. Kenji: What reply rate should I expect? Leila: For tier one with proper personalisation — 25 to 35%. For tier two — 10 to 15%. That gets you roughly 8 to 10 positive responses to work with for tier one. That is enough to run a process.

Can you send me the deck?' Yuki: That is a real positive signal. Someone who asks for the deck is spending attention on you. Send the deck today, not tomorrow. Amara: Third — 'We are passed on this one, but you should speak to Marcus Chen at Bolt Ventures — this is exactly his thesis.' Yuki: That is gold. A referral from an investor who passed is one of the best introductions you can get. Reply warmly, thank them genuinely, and ask if they would be willing to make a direct introduction. Do not just ask for the name — ask for the warm intro because the warm intro is what makes the difference. Amara: And the fourth — no response after 14 days to my initial email. Yuki: Send one follow-up. Something like: 'Following up from two weeks ago — we have just signed Freshfields as our first enterprise customer and passed £45,000 MRR. Happy to share more if timing is better.' One sentence of new information, one low-key invitation. If there is still no response, let it go. Amara: How many investors should I be running simultaneously? Yuki: As many as you can manage conversations with — typically 8 to 15 active conversations. Too few and you have no leverage; too many and the quality of engagement drops.

A startup founder, Tom, is getting feedback on a partnership proposal email from a business development expert, Marcus. The email was sent to a potential distribution partner but received no response. Marcus: I read the email. I can see exactly why it did not get a response — and it has nothing to do with the quality of the underlying partnership. The partnership itself is actually pretty compelling. Tom: So what went wrong? Marcus: The email opens with: 'We are reaching out because we believe a partnership with your organisation would be mutually beneficial and could significantly accelerate ContractIQ's growth.' Tom: That is honest. Marcus: It is completely honest. It is also entirely about you. The recipient reads that sentence and thinks: this person wants something from me. Why should I spend time on this? Tom: What should I have opened with? Marcus: What do you bring to them? You told me you have 12,000 SME clients — many of whom ask for HR tool recommendations. That is valuable to them. Open with that. 'We have 12,000 SME clients who regularly ask us for HR software recommendations — and your platform is the only one we consistently refer.' Tom: That is a much stronger opening. Marcus: And here is the other thing — your call to action asks for a 30-minute meeting to explore the partnership in detail. Tom: Is that too much? Marcus: It is borderline. For a cold email — even a partnership email — 30 minutes is a significant commitment to ask for without any prior relationship. I would ask for 20 minutes, and I would offer to work around their schedule entirely — make the logistics as easy as possible for the recipient. Remove every barrier to a yes. Tom: And the length? Marcus: One hundred and ninety words. A partnership email should be under 120 words. Every word you add is another chance for the reader to lose interest. The goal is to get a conversation, not to close the partnership in the email. Tom: So basically — lead with their benefit, ask for less time, be shorter. Marcus: Exactly. Those three changes will probably turn a zero response rate into a forty percent response rate.

Case Study 1: How Justin Kan Emailed His Way Into Y Combinator — and What Great Cold Email Teaches About Founder Communication
The story behind one of the most consequential cold emails in startup history — and the principles it illustrates about earning a response from someone who has no reason to care

Background

Justin Kan, co-founder of Justin.tv (which became Twitch, sold to Amazon for $970 million in 2014), has spoken extensively about the early days of the company and the role that direct, specific communication played in getting through closed doors. In 2006, Kan and his co-founders were a young team with a novel idea — streaming one person's life 24 hours a day on the internet — and essentially no credentials. They applied to Y Combinator's first batch. The application was specific, direct, and clear about what was unusual about what they were building. Paul Graham, who has written extensively about what makes a great founder email, consistently points to clarity, specificity, and the willingness to say something concrete rather than hedging everything with qualifications.

What Makes a Great Founder Email — Paul Graham's Criteria

Paul Graham has outlined, in multiple essays and interviews, what he looks for when founders email him. First, the email should tell him something surprising — something that changes his picture of what is possible or what is happening in a market. Generic 'we are building the next Uber for X' emails tell him nothing surprising. Second, the email should demonstrate that the founder understands the problem at a level that suggests they have genuine insight — not received wisdom from industry reports. Third, the email should be short. Graham has written that the best founder emails he has ever received have been three to five sentences. The signal of writing three clear sentences is that the founder can identify what matters and communicate it — the same skill that makes a good CEO. Fourth, the call to action should be specific and easy to respond to: 'Would you read this three-paragraph description of what we are building?' rather than 'Would you like to schedule a call?'

Lessons for Startup Founders Writing Investor Emails Today

The principles Graham describes map directly onto what works in cold investor outreach in the current era. First, say something surprising: share a counterintuitive market observation, an unexpected metric, or a customer behaviour that subverts the expected. Second, demonstrate genuine insight: reference something specific about the investor's thesis, portfolio, or stated views — not just their general reputation. Third, be short: the length of an email is not correlated with its importance — it is correlated with how much thinking the sender has done. A founder who cannot explain their business in three sentences has not yet fully understood it. Fourth, ask for one specific, small thing: 'Would you have 20 minutes?' is almost always yes or no — which is exactly what you need to know.

Case Study 2: How Notion, Stripe, and Figma Used Email to Build Their First Enterprise Customers
Three case studies in how software companies used targeted, founder-written emails to open their first enterprise doors — without a sales team

Background

Some of the most successful software companies in the world built their initial enterprise customer base not through traditional enterprise sales but through highly targeted, founder-written emails. The pattern is consistent across Notion, Stripe, and Figma: a founder who is deeply immersed in the customer problem writes highly specific emails to decision-makers who have the same problem, drawing on specific observations about that person's company, role, or public statements. These are not mass emails — they are carefully crafted individual messages that happen to share a structure. What distinguished these emails from typical founder outreach was the quality of the insight each email demonstrated about the recipient's specific situation.

Stripe's Approach to Developer Outreach

Patrick Collison and the early Stripe team famously hand-implemented Stripe for their first developer customers — literally sitting with developers while they integrated, watching where they got stuck, and then emailing other developers with highly specific references to the exact integration problems they had observed. These emails were short, specific, and led with a genuine offer of value: 'We noticed you are using PayPal's REST API for recurring billing — we have a solution that handles this in seven lines of code.' This approach was extraordinarily effective not because of any email marketing technique but because the content was genuinely useful and the sender demonstrably understood the recipient's exact problem. The lesson: founder emails that demonstrate genuine understanding of the recipient's specific situation convert at rates that generic outreach cannot approach.

Lessons for B2B Founders Writing Customer Emails

The pattern across Notion, Stripe, and Figma points to consistent principles for B2B founders writing customer emails. First, the most effective opener is a specific observation about the recipient's actual situation — not a general description of the product. 'I noticed your team is using a combination of Google Docs and Trello for project documentation' is more powerful than 'We help teams manage their documentation more effectively.' Second, the email should lead with a concrete offer of value — something the recipient can gain immediately from responding. Third, the ask should be minimal — a 15-minute call, a free implementation, a single-question survey — making it as easy as possible to say yes. Fourth, founder emails work because they signal genuine care about the customer's problem; as soon as they feel like templates, the conversion rate collapses.
