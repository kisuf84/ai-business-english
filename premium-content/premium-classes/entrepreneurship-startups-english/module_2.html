📚 Key Legal Terms
🎨
Value Proposition
The combination of products, services, and experiences that creates value for a specific customer segment — solving a problem or satisfying a need. An effective value proposition is specific (it addresses a precisely defined customer problem), differentiated (it explains why this solution is better than alternatives), and credible (it is supported by evidence). The Value Proposition Canvas (a companion to the BMC) maps the value proposition against the customer's jobs, pains, and gains.
👥
Customer Segments
The distinct groups of people or organisations that a business aims to reach and serve. A customer segment is a coherent group with common needs, common behaviours, and common willingness to pay. Startups that claim to serve 'everyone' have not done the hard work of segmentation — and will struggle to acquire any customer efficiently because their messaging and product are optimised for no one. The most successful early-stage companies identify the smallest viable segment they can dominate before expanding.
📣
Channels
The ways through which a company reaches and delivers value to its customer segments — covering awareness (how customers discover the company), evaluation (how they assess the offering), purchase (how they buy), delivery (how they receive the product or service), and after-sales support. Channels can be owned (the company's own website, sales team, retail presence), partner (distributors, resellers, affiliate networks), or rented (paid advertising, marketplace listings).
🤝
Customer Relationships
The type of relationship a company establishes with each customer segment — spanning a spectrum from fully automated self-service (no human contact) to dedicated personal assistance (an account manager for each major client). The appropriate relationship type depends on the customer's needs, the unit economics of the business, and the competitive context. SaaS products typically offer a mix of self-service onboarding, in-app guidance, and human support for high-value accounts.
💵
Revenue Streams
The sources from which a business generates income from each customer segment. Revenue can be transactional (a one-time payment for a product or service), recurring (a subscription or retainer), or usage-based (charged per transaction, per unit consumed, or per API call). The most valuable revenue streams are recurring — they provide predictability, compound over time, and command higher valuation multiples than transactional revenue of equivalent size.
🏗️
Key Resources
The most important assets a company requires to make its business model work — including physical assets (factories, equipment, property), intellectual property (patents, brands, software, proprietary data), human capital (specific expertise, talent), and financial resources (cash, credit). The nature of a company's key resources shapes its cost structure, its competitive moat, and the strategic difficulty of replication by competitors.
⚙️
Key Activities
The most important actions a company must perform to make its business model function — the things that consume most of its time, attention, and capability. A software company's key activity is product development; a professional services firm's is delivering the service; a marketplace's is managing the platform and ensuring quality on both sides. Identifying key activities clarifies what the founding team must excel at and what can be outsourced or automated.
🔗
Key Partners
The network of suppliers and partners that make the business model work — providing resources, activities, or capabilities that the company does not own or perform itself. Partnerships reduce risk, enable scale, and allow companies to focus on their core. Types: strategic alliances between non-competitors (sharing distribution), coopetition (partnership between competitors for mutual benefit), joint ventures, and buyer-supplier relationships. Over-dependence on a single key partner is a significant business model risk.
💰
Cost Structure
All the costs incurred to operate a business model — fixed costs (rent, salaries, infrastructure) that do not vary with volume, and variable costs (materials, transaction fees, distribution) that do. Businesses are either cost-driven (optimising every element to minimise cost — budget airlines, e-commerce logistics) or value-driven (prioritising the quality and experience of the offering over cost efficiency — luxury brands, premium B2B services). Understanding the cost structure determines the minimum viable price and the path to profitability.
💹
Revenue Model
The mechanism through which a business converts its value proposition into money — the 'how we make money' element of the business model. Common revenue models: subscription (recurring fee for access), freemium (free tier with paid upgrade), transactional (fee per transaction), licensing (fee for the right to use IP), marketplace/commission (percentage of transactions between third parties), advertising (revenue from displaying ads to users), and data monetisation (selling insights derived from user data).
🌱
Freemium
A revenue model in which a product is offered free of charge, with premium features or higher usage limits available for a paid subscription. The logic: a large free user base creates network effects, brand recognition, and a pool of potential paying customers. The economics only work if the conversion rate from free to paid is sufficient to cover the cost of serving free users. Typical freemium conversion rates range from 2% to 5%; the lifetime value of converting customers must significantly exceed the customer acquisition cost.
🏪
Marketplace
A platform business model that facilitates transactions between two or more distinct user groups — typically buyers and sellers — and captures value through a commission, listing fee, or subscription from one or both sides. Marketplaces benefit from network effects: more sellers attract more buyers, and more buyers attract more sellers. The fundamental challenge for new marketplaces is the chicken-and-egg problem: how to attract sellers without buyers and buyers without sellers simultaneously.
📊
Unit Economics
The financial performance of a business on a per-unit basis — typically per customer or per transaction. The two most important unit economic metrics: Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC — the total sales and marketing spend divided by the number of new customers acquired) and Customer Lifetime Value (LTV or CLV — the total net revenue a customer generates over their relationship with the company). A sustainable business model requires LTV to significantly exceed CAC — typically investors expect LTV:CAC of 3:1 or better.
🔄
Recurring Revenue
Revenue that is predictable, contractually committed, and repeats over time — as opposed to one-time transactional revenue. The most common forms: monthly recurring revenue (MRR) and annual recurring revenue (ARR) from subscription businesses. Recurring revenue is highly valued by investors because it is predictable, compounds with net revenue retention, and creates a durable business with high switching costs. SaaS businesses are largely valuable because of the recurring nature of their revenue.
🎯
Jobs to Be Done
A framework for understanding customer needs developed by Clayton Christensen — the insight that customers do not buy products, they hire them to do a specific job in their lives. The 'job' is the underlying functional, social, or emotional progress the customer is seeking. Understanding jobs-to-be-done reveals the true competitive set (competitors are everything a customer could hire to do the same job, not just direct product alternatives) and drives more effective product design and messaging.
🏗️
Customer Acquisition Cost
The total sales and marketing investment required to acquire a single new customer — calculated by dividing total sales and marketing expenditure in a period by the number of new customers acquired in that period. CAC is one of the two fundamental unit economics metrics (alongside LTV). High CAC can be justified by correspondingly high LTV; low CAC with low LTV is equally unsustainable. Startups frequently underestimate CAC by failing to include the full cost of sales team salaries, marketing tools, and content creation.
📈
Customer Lifetime Value
The total net revenue (after variable costs) that a customer is expected to generate over the duration of their relationship with the business. LTV = average revenue per customer per period x gross margin x average customer lifetime. LTV is meaningful only in relation to CAC: a high LTV is attractive only if it significantly exceeds the cost of acquiring that customer. Net revenue retention (the revenue retained from existing customers after churn and expansion) is the primary driver of long-term LTV in subscription businesses.
🌐
Platform Business Model
A business model that creates value primarily by facilitating interactions between two or more distinct user groups — rather than producing and selling a product or service directly. Platform companies (Amazon, Apple, Airbnb, Stripe) benefit from network effects that grow the platform's value with every additional participant. Platform business models are often valued at higher multiples than equivalent-revenue product businesses because the marginal cost of growth is low and the network effects create durable competitive moats.
🔁
Net Revenue Retention
A metric measuring what percentage of recurring revenue from existing customers a company retains over a period — including the effect of churn, contraction (customers downgrading), and expansion (customers upgrading or purchasing more). NRR above 100% means the business grows revenue from its existing customer base without any new customer acquisition. NRR is considered the single most important metric for SaaS business health — companies with NRR above 120% are consistently valued at significant premiums.
🪜
Price Ladder
A structured set of pricing tiers designed to capture different levels of willingness to pay across customer segments — typically free or low-cost entry tier, a core mid-market tier, and a high-value enterprise tier. A well-designed price ladder allows a business to serve a wide range of customers while maximising revenue from each segment. The distance between tiers must reflect genuine differences in value delivered; artificial tier boundaries that do not map to real customer needs create friction and confusion.
📉
Churn
The rate at which customers cancel their subscriptions or stop purchasing from a business — the inverse of retention. Monthly churn rate = customers lost in month / customers at start of month. High churn destroys value even in fast-growing businesses: at 10% monthly churn, a company replaces its entire customer base roughly every 10 months — requiring enormous acquisition investment just to stand still. The causes of churn (poor onboarding, wrong customer fit, competitor alternatives, price sensitivity) must be understood and addressed before scaling acquisition.

Text 1: The Business Model Canvas — A Framework That Changed How Businesses Think About Themselves

The Business Model Canvas, developed by Alexander Osterwalder and published in Business Model Generation (2010), is arguably the most widely used strategic tool in modern management practice. Before the BMC, the standard instrument for describing a business was the business plan — a document of 30 to 100 pages that attempted to capture every aspect of a planned venture, from market analysis to financial projections to operational detail. The business plan had a fundamental problem: it was designed to be read, not worked with. It was static, narrative, and slow to update. It communicated but did not facilitate thinking.

The BMC replaced this with a single page divided into nine building blocks: Value Proposition (what value is created and for whom), Customer Segments (who is served), Channels (how customers are reached), Customer Relationships (the nature of the relationship established), Revenue Streams (how money is made), Key Resources (what is needed to deliver the model), Key Activities (what must be done), Key Partners (who helps), and Cost Structure (what the model costs to run). These nine blocks cover the two sides of a business: the right side (value creation and delivery — the customer-facing elements) and the left side (value capture — the operational and financial elements). Displayed on a single sheet, often using sticky notes so that elements can be moved, updated, and debated, the BMC turned business model design into a collaborative, iterative, visual activity.

The BMC's power comes from several features. First, it forces explicit choices: completing the canvas requires articulating decisions that founders often leave implicit. 'Who is our customer?' is a question many startups avoid answering precisely. The BMC makes avoidance impossible. Second, it enables comparison: two different business model options can be placed side by side, making trade-offs visible in a way that narrative documents cannot achieve. Third, it identifies dependencies: a change in the value proposition propagates through channels, customer relationships, revenue streams, key activities, and cost structure — the canvas makes these ripples visible. Fourth, it supports iteration: in a startup environment where assumptions are being tested and updated constantly, the canvas can be revised in minutes to reflect new learning.

Text 2: Revenue Models — The Most Consequential Business Model Decision

Of the nine building blocks of the Business Model Canvas, the revenue streams block is arguably the most consequential. The choice of revenue model — how a business converts its value proposition into money — shapes not just the financial profile of the company but its entire operating model, its customer relationships, its unit economics, and its potential valuation. Two companies building the same product for the same customer can have dramatically different fates depending on which revenue model they choose. Microsoft Office sold as a perpetual licence for decades; the transition to Microsoft 365 subscriptions transformed the company's revenue predictability, growth rate, and stock market valuation.

The spectrum of revenue models for technology businesses ranges from purely transactional (a one-time payment for a product, as in a traditional software licence) to purely recurring (a subscription with automatic renewal, as in a SaaS product) to usage-based (charged per API call, per message sent, or per gigabyte stored, as in cloud services like AWS). Each model involves trade-offs. Transactional revenue is easy for customers to understand and commit to, but provides no predictability for the vendor and requires constant effort to generate new sales. Recurring revenue is highly predictable and compounds over time through net revenue retention, but requires customers to commit to ongoing payments — creating a higher initial hurdle to purchase. Usage-based revenue scales naturally with the customer's own growth and creates alignment between customer success and vendor revenue, but can be volatile and difficult to forecast.

The freemium model — offering a free tier alongside a paid tier — has become the default go-to-market approach for many consumer and B2B software products. Its appeal is intuitive: remove the friction of payment entirely for early adoption, build a large user base, and convert the most engaged users to a paid plan. The economics, however, are less forgiving than the strategy sounds. Serving free users at scale costs real money — in infrastructure, support, and operational overhead. If the conversion rate from free to paid is too low (typically below 2%), or if the average revenue per paying user is too modest, the free tier becomes a subsidy with no return. The freemium model works best when: the marginal cost of serving free users is genuinely low (software, not physical goods); the free tier creates viral growth through network effects or word of mouth; and the paid tier offers features that a significant proportion of power users are willing to pay meaningfully for.

Grammar Point 1: Prepositional Phrases in Business Model Descriptions
Key Prepositions for Value and Revenue Flows

Business model descriptions require precise prepositions to describe how value and money flow between parties. The choice of preposition is not interchangeable — each carries a specific meaning about direction, mechanism, and relationship.

'generate revenue from customers / from subscriptions / from transactions' (source of revenue)
'charge a commission on transactions / on bookings / on sales' (basis of the charge)
'deliver value through a platform / through a partner network / via a mobile app' (mechanism of delivery)
'designed for enterprise customers / for early-stage founders / for SME owners' (intended recipient)
Describing Dependencies and Relationships

Business model components are interdependent. Precise prepositions describe the nature of these dependencies and relationships accurately.

'depend on key partners for cloud infrastructure' (dependency on external party)
'generate revenue from both sides of the marketplace' (source across multiple parties)
'acquire customers through content marketing / through paid search / via referral' (acquisition channel)
'protect the business model against competitive imitation through IP / through network effects' (defensive mechanism)
Prepositional Phrases for BMC Building Blocks

Each BMC building block has associated prepositional phrases that appear consistently in professional business model descriptions.

Value Proposition: 'designed for [segment]', 'delivers value by [mechanism]', 'addresses the problem of [issue]'
Revenue Streams: 'charged per [unit]', 'billed monthly/annually', 'commission on [transaction]'
Channels: 'delivered through [channel]', 'marketed via [medium]', 'sold through [partner type]'
Cost Structure: 'dominated by [cost type]', 'driven by [activity]', 'fixed costs of [£x]/month'
📋
Grammar Point 2: Defining Relative Clauses for Business Model Concepts
The Standard Definition Pattern

When introducing BMC concepts to non-experts, the defining relative clause pattern provides clarity: [term] is [general category] + that/which/where/who + [defining characteristic]. This pattern is more accessible than technical definitions and avoids circular explanations.

'A value proposition is the bundle of benefits that a company delivers to a specific customer segment.'
'Customer segments are the distinct groups of customers that a business aims to reach with its offering.'
'A marketplace is a platform where two or more user groups transact with each other.'
'Churn is the rate at which customers cancel their subscriptions in a given period.'
Choosing the Right Relative Pronoun

The choice of relative pronoun ('that', 'which', 'where', 'who') depends on whether the antecedent is a thing, a place, a process, or a person. Using the wrong pronoun is one of the most common errors in business model descriptions.

Use 'that' or 'which' for things and concepts: 'a revenue model that charges per transaction'
Use 'where' for processes, environments, or platforms: 'a marketplace where buyers and sellers transact'
Use 'who' or 'whose' for people and organisations: 'customers who subscribe to the premium tier'
Avoid 'what' as a relative pronoun: NOT 'a model what charges per unit' → 'a model that charges per unit'
Extended Definitions for Complex Concepts

For more complex business model concepts, the single relative clause can be extended with additional clauses — building the definition in layers from general to specific.

'NRR is a metric that measures what percentage of recurring revenue a company retains from existing customers — including the effects of churn, contraction, and expansion — over a defined period.'
'The freemium model is a revenue strategy where a free tier of the product serves as the primary acquisition channel, with the expectation that a proportion of free users will convert to a paid plan.'
'LTV:CAC is a ratio that compares the total revenue generated by a customer over their lifetime to the cost of acquiring that customer — with a ratio of 3:1 or above typically considered the minimum for a sustainable business.'
📋
Grammar Point 3: Contrast and Comparison in Business Model Evaluation
'However' and 'That Said' — Introducing Qualifications

Business model evaluations must acknowledge both strengths and weaknesses — alternating between positive statements and qualifications. 'However' and 'that said' introduce the qualification after the positive statement.

'The subscription model provides predictable revenue. However, it requires customers to commit to ongoing payments — raising the initial purchase barrier.'
'The marketplace has strong supply-side traction. That said, buyer demand has not yet reached the threshold needed to create organic search effects.'
'The unit economics are strong at current scale. However, CAC may increase significantly as the business moves beyond the early adopter segment.'
'NRR is impressive at 122%. That said, the business is still growing new ARR more slowly than the cohort data would suggest is achievable.'
'Whereas' and 'While' — Parallel Contrast

'Whereas' and 'while' introduce a contrasting clause in a single sentence — efficient for comparing two options, two sides of a model, or two business model types. They frame the contrast as a parallel observation rather than a sequential qualification.

'A subscription model provides predictability, whereas usage-based pricing creates alignment with customer success.'
'B2C channels offer faster growth, while B2B channels generate higher LTV and lower churn.'
'Platform businesses benefit from network effects, whereas product businesses must compete primarily on product quality.'
'The free tier creates user volume, while the paid tier must generate sufficient revenue to cover the cost of serving both tiers.'
'Despite' and 'Notwithstanding' — Formal Concession

In formal business model documents and investor reports, 'despite' and 'notwithstanding' (followed by a noun phrase) introduce concessions efficiently and formally.

'Despite strong gross margins on the core software product, professional services revenue is dragging blended margins below the target threshold.'
'Notwithstanding the impressive NRR of 118%, the absolute churn volume requires significant new customer acquisition to sustain growth.'
'Despite achieving product-market fit in the SME segment, the path to enterprise requires significant product investment in security and compliance.'
'Notwithstanding the strength of the marketplace model in theory, the coordination cost of managing quality on both sides has proved higher than anticipated.'

📋 Value Proposition Statement — Write a Value Proposition Statement for a Target Segment
A strong value proposition statement is specific, differentiated, and credible. It answers three questions: who is it for, what does it do for them, and why is it better than the alternatives? BMC Workshop: Mapping the Business Model for the First Time
Startup Workshop · B1–B2 Level · approx. 110 sec
0:00

A startup accelerator workshop facilitator, Priya, is guiding a group of founders through their first Business Model Canvas exercise. One founder, Marcus, is working on a B2B HR software startup. Priya: Okay Marcus, let us start with the value proposition block. In one sentence — what specific value do you create for a specific customer? Marcus: We help companies manage their HR processes more efficiently. Priya: That is the category, not the value proposition. 'Efficiently' is not specific. What problem are you solving? Marcus: Our customers — HR managers at companies with between 50 and 200 employees — spend about 40% of their time on administrative tasks that should be automated. We reduce that to under 10%. Priya: Now that is a value proposition. Specific customer, specific problem, specific outcome. Now move to customer segments. Who specifically is the economic buyer — the person who approves the budget? Marcus: The HR Director. Priya: And who is the user — the person who uses the product every day? Marcus: Usually the HR Manager or coordinator. Priya: Good. Those may be different people with different needs. Your value proposition for the economic buyer is about cost savings and compliance. Your value proposition for the user is about ease of use and reducing manual work. Both matter. Marcus: I had not separated those before. Priya: Most founders do not. Now — channels. How do you reach your customer today? Marcus: Mostly through outbound sales — cold email and LinkedIn. And we have had some inbound from content we have published. Priya: What is converting better — outbound or inbound? Marcus: Inbound is converting at about three times the rate of outbound. Priya: That is a critical insight for your key activities block. If inbound converts at 3x, content and SEO should probably be a higher priority than cold outreach. Your canvas should reflect that reality. Marcus: So the business model is an honest map of what is actually working — not what we planned. Priya: Exactly. The canvas should reflect evidence, not aspiration.

Investor Due Diligence: Questioning the Revenue Model
Investor Meeting · B2 Level · approx. 108 sec
0:00

A seed-stage investor, Leila, is conducting due diligence with a startup founder, Tom, whose company offers a B2B SaaS product for supply chain management. The meeting is focused on the revenue model. Leila: Tom, walk me through the revenue model in detail. Tom: We charge a flat monthly subscription — £500 per month per company, regardless of the number of users or the volume of transactions processed. Leila: How did you arrive at £500? Tom: It felt right for the market. Most of our competitors are in the £300 to £700 range. Leila: Have you tested willingness to pay at different price points? Tom: Not formally. We have just been closing customers at £500. Leila: Here is my concern. You have a customer with 5 users and £500,000 in annual supply chain transactions, and you have a customer with 200 users and £50 million in transactions — and you are charging them both £500 per month. The value you deliver to the second customer is roughly a hundred times higher, but you are capturing the same amount. Tom: That is true. We thought about per-user pricing but it seemed complex. Leila: The risk of flat-rate pricing is that you are leaving significant revenue on the table from high-value customers while potentially overcharging smaller ones — which may explain some of your early churn from the SME segment. Tom: We have had some churn in the lower end. I attributed it to product gaps. Leila: It might also be price. When you have similar product quality and churn is segmented by company size, pricing is usually a factor. Have you modelled what your NRR would look like with usage-based or tiered pricing? Tom: Not yet. But based on our transaction volumes, moving to a transaction-fee model could increase average revenue per account by about 40% for our top quartile of customers. Case Study 1: Stripe — How a Payment API Became a Financial Infrastructure Company by Getting the Business Model Right
How Patrick and John Collison built a $95 billion company by understanding that developers were an underserved customer segment with enormous downstream value

Background

Stripe was founded in 2010 by Irish brothers Patrick and John Collison, who were frustrated by how difficult it was to accept payments online. The incumbent payment processors — Braintree, PayPal, Authorize.net — required lengthy applications, complex integrations, and weeks of approval time. Stripe's founding insight was precise: developers were the decision-makers for payment infrastructure in startups and scaleups, but no payment company was designing for developers. The Stripe API could be integrated with seven lines of code; the documentation was beautiful; the developer experience was a first-class product consideration. Stripe launched in 2011 and quickly became the payment stack of choice for YC companies and the broader startup ecosystem.

The Business Model

Stripe's business model is elegant: it charges a transaction fee of 2.9% plus $0.30 per successful card transaction — a usage-based model in which Stripe's revenue grows automatically as its customers' businesses grow. This model has profound unit economic implications. Stripe does not need to sell more to its existing customers; as Shopify processes more transactions, Stripe earns more money. As Lyft grows, Stripe grows. The customer success of Stripe's customers is directly Stripe's revenue growth. This alignment of incentives is one of the most powerful features of a usage-based revenue model in a B2B context. Stripe has since expanded its product suite — Stripe Capital (lending), Stripe Radar (fraud detection), Stripe Connect (marketplace payments), Stripe Treasury (embedded banking) — transforming from a payment API into a full financial infrastructure platform. Each additional product deepens integration and raises switching costs.

Lessons for Business Model Design

Stripe's trajectory carries foundational lessons for business model design. First, customer segment precision creates disproportionate value: developers were underserved and had enormous downstream influence over technology purchasing decisions. Winning developers meant winning the companies developers worked at. Second, the revenue model should align vendor success with customer success: Stripe's usage-based model means that every dollar of customer growth is a dollar of Stripe growth — creating a genuine shared interest in customer outcomes. Third, the most durable business models create ecosystems: each Stripe product increases switching costs and deepens customer relationships, making the bundle harder to replace than any individual product. Fourth, the value proposition must be specific enough to win: 'making payments easy' is too broad; 'seven lines of code to accept payments' is a specific promise that can be evaluated in minutes.

Case Study 2: Facebook/Meta — The Advertising Business Model Hidden Inside a Social Network
How a dorm room project became the world's most profitable advertising platform — and what it reveals about two-sided markets and the true customer in a free product

Background

When Mark Zuckerberg launched Facebook from his Harvard dorm room in February 2004, the business model was not yet defined. The product was free; the company was growing; the question of how it would make money was deferred. This is a common pattern in consumer internet companies: build the product, grow the users, figure out the revenue model later. Facebook began experimenting with advertising in 2004 but the model that would define the company — and make it worth over a trillion dollars — was shaped by a series of insights that took years to fully develop. The core insight, eventually: Facebook was not a social network that also ran ads. It was an advertising platform that used a social network to create an unparalleled targeting dataset.

The Two-Sided Market and the True Customer

Facebook operates as a two-sided market — and understanding which side is the true customer reveals the logic of its business model. Facebook's users receive the social network for free; this is the supply side, whose participation creates the value. Facebook's advertisers pay to reach users with targeted messages; this is the demand side, whose payments generate the revenue. The true customer — the one paying — is the advertiser. The user is the product: their attention, behaviour, and data are what Facebook sells to advertisers. This is not unique to Facebook — it is the business model of every ad-supported media company from newspapers to television. What made Facebook's version extraordinary was the precision of targeting enabled by first-party social data. A newspaper could target by geography; Facebook could target by relationship status, education, employer, interests, and behaviour — creating advertising effectiveness that traditional media could not match.

Lessons for Business Model Design

Facebook's business model trajectory carries several lessons for founders thinking about two-sided markets and ad-supported models. First, 'free' is not a business model — it is an acquisition strategy. The question is always: free to whom, and who pays? In Facebook's case, free to users; paid by advertisers. Second, the value of a user base is only realised once the monetisation mechanism is designed: Facebook grew for years before advertising revenue matched its scale — the business model lagged the user growth. Third, ad-supported business models create structural tensions between user experience and advertiser needs: more ads create more revenue but degrade the user experience; this tension is permanent and must be managed. Fourth, regulatory risk is elevated for businesses where the user's data is the product — GDPR, the California Consumer Privacy Act, and other regulations impose compliance costs and restrict some targeting practices that drove historical revenue growth.
