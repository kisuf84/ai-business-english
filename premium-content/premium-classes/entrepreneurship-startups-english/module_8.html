Study these 21 essential lean startup and MVP terms — the vocabulary that underpins evidence-based product development and iterative learning.

📚 Key Legal Terms
🔄
Build-Measure-Learn
The core iterative cycle of the lean startup methodology — developed by Eric Ries and described in The Lean Startup (2011). The loop begins with building the smallest possible testable artefact (a product, a prototype, a landing page), measuring how customers actually respond to it (not how they say they will respond), and learning from that measurement to inform the next iteration. The critical insight is that learning — not building — is the primary output of each cycle. Teams that build too much before measuring waste the most expensive resource in a startup: time.
🧪
Hypothesis
A specific, testable prediction about customer behaviour, willingness to pay, or market dynamics — the foundational unit of lean startup experimentation. A well-formed hypothesis has three components: the assumption being tested ('we believe that...'), the test to be run ('in order to verify this we will...'), and the success criterion ('we will know we are right if...'). Hypotheses should be falsifiable — it must be possible to discover they are wrong. Hypotheses that cannot be disproved are not hypotheses; they are beliefs.
✅
Validated Learning
Learning derived from real customer behaviour rather than opinion, intention, or assumption — the currency of lean startup progress. Validated learning is distinguished from unvalidated learning by the quality of the evidence: a customer saying 'I would probably use that' is not validated learning; a customer paying for a product is. Eric Ries defines validated learning as the process of demonstrating empirically that a team has discovered valuable truths about a startup's present and future business prospects.
🎪
Wizard of Oz MVP
An MVP type in which the product appears automated to the customer but is actually operated manually behind the scenes — named after the scene in The Wizard of Oz where the impressive wizard turns out to be a man operating levers. The Wizard of Oz MVP allows founders to simulate the experience of a technology product before building the technology, testing whether the value proposition works without the investment in engineering. Zappos used this approach: the founder took photographs of shoes in local stores and posted them online, then bought and shipped the shoes manually when orders came in — proving demand before building a fulfilment system.
🤝
Concierge MVP
An MVP type in which the product's value is delivered manually, one customer at a time, by the founding team — rather than through any form of automation or scalable technology. The concierge MVP allows founders to serve customers with maximum personal attention, learn exactly what they value, and discover the problems with the solution before automating it. Food on the Table, a meal planning app, began by having the founder personally create meal plans and shopping lists for one family — delivering the service manually to learn what was valuable before building the software.
🌫️
Smoke Test
An experiment that measures customer demand before a product is built — typically by presenting potential customers with the product's value proposition and a call to action (such as a sign-up, a pre-order, or a payment) and measuring how many complete the action. The term comes from electronics: when a new circuit is switched on, if smoke comes out, something is wrong. In a startup context, if no one 'signs up' for a product that does not yet exist, something is wrong with the value proposition. Landing page tests are the most common form of smoke test.
📊
Vanity Metrics
Metrics that look impressive but do not reveal whether a business is actually working — total registered users, page views, press mentions, and social media followers are classic examples. Vanity metrics are dangerous because they can provide false confidence: a startup with 100,000 registered users but 5% monthly active users and 2% retention is not growing — it is leaking. Vanity metrics make founders feel good without telling them whether the business model is working. The antidote is actionable metrics — metrics that are directly connected to business outcomes.
🎯
Actionable Metrics
Metrics that directly reveal whether specific actions are causing specific outcomes — the opposite of vanity metrics. An actionable metric enables a founder to say: 'when we changed X, Y changed by Z%'. Conversion rate from trial to paid is actionable; total signups is not. Net revenue retention is actionable; total revenue is only actionable in the context of the cost of acquiring it. Activation rate (the percentage of new users who complete a key onboarding action) is actionable; total downloads is not. The lean startup framework distinguishes between metrics that measure progress and metrics that measure activity.
🗺️
Assumption Map
A structured tool for identifying and prioritising the assumptions that underpin a business model — typically by mapping assumptions along two axes: how important they are to the business model (high impact vs low impact) and how uncertain they are (high uncertainty vs low uncertainty). Assumptions in the high-impact, high-uncertainty quadrant are the ones that must be tested first — they are the ones whose failure would most threaten the business. Assumption mapping forces founders to be explicit about what they believe versus what they know, and to sequence their experiments accordingly.
🔁
Iteration
A single pass through the build-measure-learn loop — producing a slightly improved version of the product, hypothesis, or experiment based on what was learned from the previous version. Iteration is the normal operational mode of a lean startup team: most learning happens not through dramatic pivots but through dozens of small iterations, each informed by the previous one. Fast iteration — the ability to run experiments, measure results, and adjust quickly — is one of the primary competitive advantages of a startup over an established company.
🔀
Pivot
A structured course correction — a significant change to one or more fundamental elements of a business model based on validated learning that the current approach is not working. Pivots are distinguished from iterations (incremental adjustments) and from abandonment (quitting). Types of pivot include: zoom-in (making a single feature the whole product), zoom-out (making the current product a feature of a larger product), customer segment pivot (targeting a different customer), and business model pivot (changing how money is made). The pivot decision should be based on objective data rather than desperation or impatience.
👤
Customer Archetype
A detailed, fictional representation of a specific type of customer — built from real data gathered through customer discovery interviews rather than from demographic assumptions. A customer archetype (or persona) gives the team a concrete reference point for product decisions: 'would Maya — our head of legal at a 200-person SaaS company who manages contracts alone — find this feature useful?' Archetypes should include specific behaviours, frustrations, goals, and decision-making patterns — not just demographics. A persona described only by age, gender, and job title is not yet a useful archetype.
🔬
Experiment
A structured test of a specific hypothesis — designed to generate data that either validates or invalidates the assumption being tested, within a defined time period and with pre-specified success criteria. A startup experiment has three defining characteristics: it tests one variable at a time (to isolate the causal factor), it has pre-specified success criteria (defined before the experiment starts, not after the data is seen), and it generates learnings that inform the next iteration regardless of outcome. Experiments that are designed after the fact to justify a decision that has already been made are not experiments — they are confirmation of prior beliefs.
📉
Pivot vs Persevere
The most consequential decision in the lean startup process: given what we have learned, should we continue with the current strategy (persevere) or make a significant change of direction (pivot)? The lean startup framework argues this decision should be made based on validated learning rather than intuition, elapsed time, or investor pressure. If the current strategy is producing the early indicators that would support a positive long-term outcome — even if current numbers are modest — persevere. If the key assumptions have been tested and found false, pivot.
📈
Innovation Accounting
A framework for measuring startup progress before traditional accounting metrics (revenue, profit) become meaningful — proposed by Eric Ries as an alternative to traditional financial reporting for early-stage companies. Innovation accounting tracks three milestones: (1) establishing the baseline — measuring where the company is now across key early metrics; (2) tuning the engine — running experiments to improve the baseline metrics; (3) deciding whether to pivot or persevere based on whether the experiments are producing the expected rate of improvement.
🚦
Activation
The moment at which a new user or customer first experiences the core value of a product — the 'aha moment' when they understand what the product does and why it matters to them. In SaaS products, activation is typically defined as completing a specific onboarding action: creating a first project, inviting a colleague, or completing a first use case. Activation rate — the percentage of new users who reach the activation milestone — is one of the most important early-stage metrics because it predicts long-term retention: users who activate are significantly more likely to stay than those who do not.
🌱
Early Adopter
A customer who adopts a new product or technology before the mainstream market — willing to tolerate immaturity, bugs, and incompleteness in exchange for being among the first to access the product's benefits. Early adopters are the ideal customers for MVP testing: they have the vision to see past current limitations, the tolerance to work with an imperfect product, and the motivation to provide detailed feedback. Founders who confuse mainstream customer expectations with early adopter behaviour will either over-build their MVP (trying to meet mainstream standards) or misinterpret early adopter frustrations as representative of broader market sentiment.
📋
Product-Market Fit
The degree to which a product satisfies a strong market demand — evidenced by strong retention, organic referrals, and customers who genuinely cannot imagine operating without it. In lean startup terms, achieving product-market fit is the primary goal of the build-measure-learn cycle: all experimentation in the early stage is designed to discover whether, and under what conditions, product-market fit exists. Sean Ellis's test — 'how disappointed would you be if this product no longer existed?' — suggests that 40% or more responding 'very disappointed' is the signal of product-market fit.
🗣️
Customer Interview
A structured one-on-one conversation designed to learn about a potential customer's problems, behaviours, and decision-making — not to pitch a product. Effective customer interviews use open-ended questions, ask about past behaviour rather than hypothetical future behaviour, and follow up on unexpected answers. The classic customer interview mistake is asking 'would you use a product that did X?' — which elicits socially desirable answers rather than honest assessment. Rob Fitzpatrick's The Mom Test recommends asking about the problem rather than the solution and asking about actual past behaviour rather than predicted future behaviour.
🎤
The Mom Test
A framework for conducting customer interviews developed by Rob Fitzpatrick — built around the principle that most customer interviews are conducted badly because entrepreneurs ask questions that their own mother would answer positively just to be supportive. The Mom Test rule: never ask anyone whether your idea is good. Instead, ask about their life, their problems, their current solutions, and how much those problems cost them. Questions that pass the Mom Test: 'How do you currently manage contract renewals?' and 'What happened the last time you missed a renewal date?' Questions that fail the Mom Test: 'Would you use a product that automated contract renewals?' and 'Do you think this is a good idea?'
⚙️
Five Whys
A root cause analysis technique — attributed to Toyota's production system — in which the question 'why?' is asked five times in sequence to trace a problem back to its fundamental cause rather than addressing a surface symptom. In lean startup contexts, the five whys is applied to understand the root cause of customer problems, product failures, and failed experiments. Example: a user churned (why?) → they never completed onboarding (why?) → the onboarding email was going to their spam folder (why?) → the email domain was not authenticated (why?) → no one had set up DKIM records (why?) → there was no onboarding email checklist for new domain configurations.

Text 1: The Lean Startup — Why Eric Ries's Methodology Changed How Companies Are Built

The lean startup methodology, developed by Eric Ries and published in The Lean Startup (2011), emerged from a specific frustration: the recognition that most startups fail not because of technical incompetence or a lack of ambition, but because they build things nobody wants. The traditional product development approach — spend months or years building a complete product, then launch — is optimised for execution rather than learning. If the founding team's assumptions about what customers want turn out to be wrong, all that execution has produced waste rather than value. Ries, drawing on lean manufacturing principles developed by Toyota and on Steve Blank's customer development methodology, proposed a fundamentally different approach: treat the startup as an experiment, and treat every assumption as a hypothesis to be tested.

The lean startup is built on four interrelated principles. First, entrepreneurs are everywhere: the startup, defined as a 'human institution designed to create new products and services under conditions of extreme uncertainty', is not limited to Silicon Valley garages — it applies to any team facing genuine uncertainty about what customers want. Second, entrepreneurship is management: the startup requires a different kind of management adapted to its context of extreme uncertainty — not the management of execution but the management of learning. Third, validated learning: the fundamental unit of progress is not a feature shipped or a line of code written but learning validated by customer behaviour. Fourth, build-measure-learn: the fundamental activity is to turn ideas into products, measure how customers respond, and learn whether to pivot or persevere — as quickly as possible.

The methodology's most controversial and most important principle is the minimum viable product. Ries defines the MVP not as the minimum product but as the product that enables the maximum amount of validated learning with the least effort. This distinction matters enormously. The MVP is not a half-built product — it is a deliberately designed experiment. Its purpose is not to ship something customers can use (though it may do that too) — its purpose is to test the most critical assumptions underlying the business model as quickly and cheaply as possible. A startup that spends six months building a 'minimum' product that tests no specific hypothesis has not adopted lean methodology — it has simply built slowly.

Text 2: Defining the Right MVP — What to Build First and Why

The minimum viable product is widely misunderstood. The most common misunderstanding is treating 'minimum' as an instruction about the amount of functionality to build — leading to half-built software products that are simultaneously too feature-poor to demonstrate value and too expensive in engineering time to be genuinely minimal. The right way to think about 'minimum' is in terms of the minimum investment required to test the most important hypothesis. This reframing immediately opens up a much wider range of MVP types than a half-built software product. If the most important hypothesis is 'will customers pay for this?', the minimum viable test might be a landing page with a payment button — no product at all. If the hypothesis is 'can we deliver this service at a quality level customers will pay for?', the minimum viable test might be delivering the service manually for ten customers.

The MVP type that is appropriate depends on what assumption is being tested. A smoke test (typically a landing page or pre-order form) tests whether customers will express interest or commitment. A concierge MVP tests whether customers value the service when delivered with white-glove, manual attention. A Wizard of Oz MVP tests whether the automated product experience would work — by simulating it manually. A prototype tests whether customers can understand and navigate the intended user experience. A pilot tests whether a product works in the real operational environment rather than a controlled testing environment. Founders who jump straight to building a full software product before running any of these earlier, cheaper tests are building with the most expensive tool available when cheaper tools would have provided the same learning.

The three most common MVP mistakes: first, building too much — spending months on a product that could have been tested with a two-page landing page. Second, measuring the wrong things — tracking total signups rather than the conversion rate that reveals whether the value proposition is working. Third, not defining success criteria before the experiment begins — so that any outcome can be reframed as success. An MVP that has not defined in advance what success looks like cannot generate validated learning — it can only generate data that is interpreted through the lens of the founder's prior beliefs. The discipline of defining 'we will know this hypothesis is correct if X happens' before any experiment begins is what distinguishes lean startup experimentation from wishful thinking.

Grammar Point 1: Writing and Evaluating Hypotheses
The Standard Hypothesis Format

Lean startup hypotheses must be specific, falsifiable, and pre-defined. The standard three-part format ensures all three qualities: the belief, the test, and the success criterion. Using this format prevents founders from reinterpreting results to confirm prior beliefs.

'We believe [specific assumption].' — state the assumption precisely, not vaguely
'To test this, we will [specific experiment].' — describe exactly what will be done
'We will know we are right if [specific, measurable outcome].' — define success before starting
Example: 'We believe in-house legal teams will pay £299/month for contract automation. To test this, we will run a landing page with pricing for two weeks. We will know we are right if 20% of visitors click the sign-up button.'
Quantified Specificity in Hypothesis Language

Effective hypotheses use precise quantitative thresholds rather than vague qualitative predictions. 'Some customers will pay' is not a testable hypothesis. '20% of landing page visitors will click the sign-up button' is.

Vague: 'Most customers will find the product useful.' → Precise: 'At least 60% of users who complete onboarding will log in again within 7 days.'
Vague: 'The new onboarding will improve retention.' → Precise: 'The onboarding redesign will increase 14-day retention from 42% to 55%.'
Vague: 'Enterprise customers will pay more.' → Precise: 'Enterprise customers will pay £999/month — the conversion rate from the £299 tier to the £999 tier will be above 30%.'
Vague: 'The value proposition resonates.' → Precise: 'Activation rate will exceed 35% within 48 hours of sign-up.'
Falsifiability — the Key Test of a Hypothesis

A hypothesis is only valid if it is possible to discover that it is wrong. If every possible outcome can be interpreted as confirmation, the hypothesis is not falsifiable — and the experiment will produce no validated learning.

Falsifiable: 'If fewer than 20% of visitors click the sign-up button, the value proposition is unvalidated.' (a clear failure criterion)
Not falsifiable: 'The experiment will show us interesting things about customer behaviour.' (any outcome is 'interesting')
Falsifiable: 'If 90-day retention is below 30% for the third consecutive cohort, we will pivot.' (pre-defined pivot trigger)
Not falsifiable: 'We will know we are making progress.' (progress can always be claimed)
📋
Grammar Point 2: Reporting Experiment Results with Appropriate Precision
Distinguishing What the Data Shows from What It Suggests

Validated learning requires precise language that distinguishes between what the data directly demonstrates and what it implies or suggests. Overclaiming from limited data is a common error in experiment reporting.

Strong evidence: 'The data shows / demonstrates / confirms that...' (use only with robust evidence)
Moderate evidence: 'The data suggests / indicates / points toward...' (appropriate for most experiment findings)
Weak evidence: 'The data is consistent with...' / 'The early data hints at...' (limited sample or single data point)
Example: 'Three user tests suggest the interface is confusing — but we would need at least 20 tests to be confident this reflects the broader population.'
Reporting Metric Changes Precisely

Experiment results involve metric changes that must be reported with specific, internally consistent language. The change, the direction, the magnitude, and the comparison period must all be clear.

'Activation rate improved from 22% to 38% — a 16 percentage point improvement — in the cohort following the onboarding redesign.'
'14-day retention fell from 58% to 49% in Q3 — a decline driven primarily by three enterprise accounts who churned after the pricing change.'
'Conversion from trial to paid increased by 40% (from 5% to 7%) — not as large as our 10% target but a statistically significant improvement.'
'The cohort activated at 41% — above our 35% threshold, confirming the hypothesis.'
Attributing Causation Carefully

Lean startup experiments rarely have the statistical power to establish causation with certainty. Reporting language must reflect this — distinguishing between what the experiment showed and what it caused.

Appropriate: 'The onboarding redesign coincided with a 16pp improvement in activation — we believe it was causal but are running a second test to confirm.'
Overclaim: 'The onboarding redesign caused activation to improve by 16pp.' (asserts causation from one experiment)
Appropriate: 'The data is consistent with the hypothesis that enterprise users value audit trails — the 67% conversion rate to the enterprise tier suggests this feature is driving upgrades.'
Underclaim: 'We cannot draw any conclusions from this data.' (if the hypothesis was pre-defined, the data does test it)
📋
Grammar Point 3: Discussing Pivot and Iteration Decisions
Distinguishing Pivot, Iterate, and Abandon

Lean startup vocabulary distinguishes three possible responses to experiment results. Using the wrong term signals a misunderstanding of the framework — and can confuse strategic discussions.

Iterate: a small, incremental adjustment to the current approach — the default response when experiments produce partial results. 'We iterated on the onboarding flow three times before activation exceeded our threshold.'
Pivot: a significant change to one or more fundamental elements of the business model — based on validated learning that the current approach is not working. 'We pivoted from a B2C to a B2B model after three cohorts showed consistently poor retention in the consumer segment.'
Abandon: stopping work on the current direction entirely — usually when the fundamental value proposition has been tested and found not to exist. 'We abandoned the contract drafting product after ten customers declined to use the AI output without significant human editing.'
Grounding Pivot Decisions in Validated Learning

Pivot discussions must reference the specific validated learning that drives the decision — not intuition, elapsed time, or investor pressure. The structure: what we learned → why it changes our strategy → what we are pivoting to.

'Three consecutive cohorts showed 90-day retention below 30%. This tells us that the product's core value proposition — productivity improvement — is not compelling enough to sustain engagement after the initial novelty. We are pivoting our focus to compliance value.'
'The customer segment pivot was driven by discovery interviews: lawyers saw the tool as a threat to billable hours, while operations teams saw it as a solution. The same product has a fundamentally different reception in each segment.'
'We are persevering because the key metrics — activation and short-term retention — are improving at the rate our model predicted. The absolute numbers are modest, but the trajectory supports continuing.'
'The decision to persevere is conditional: if 90-day retention does not reach 40% in the next cohort, we will revisit the pivot question with fresh data.'
Zoom-In and Zoom-Out Pivots — Specific Language

Eric Ries identified specific types of pivot that have distinct language patterns. Two of the most common — zoom-in and zoom-out — describe changes to the product scope.

Zoom-in pivot: 'We are making the renewal alert module the entire product — this single feature has 90% of our usage and the highest NPS score in the product.'
Zoom-out pivot: 'We are expanding the product from contract management to full legal operations — the contract module is now a feature of a larger platform.'
Customer segment pivot: 'We are pivoting from individual lawyers to legal operations teams — the same product with different positioning and a different buyer.'
Business model pivot: 'We are moving from a subscription to a transaction fee model — charging per contract processed rather than per month, which aligns our revenue with customer value.'

Product Team Discussion: Designing the Right MVP
Product Meeting · B2 Level · approx. 110 sec
0:00

The ContractIQ product team is discussing what to build for their first external test. The Head of Product, Leila, is facilitating. The CTO, Tom, and the founder, Priya, are also present. Leila: Before we start writing tickets, I want us to agree on what assumption we are testing and what the minimum thing is to test it. Tom, you have been pushing for the full AI drafting feature. Can you articulate the hypothesis? Tom: The hypothesis is that lawyers will use AI to draft contracts faster and it will save them time. Leila: Okay — let me push on that. 'Will use it' and 'saves time' are not measurable. What is the specific, falsifiable version of that hypothesis? Tom: Fair. The hypothesis is: in-house lawyers with fewer than three years of experience will use the AI drafting feature at least three times per week once they have tried it once. Leila: Better. And the success criterion? Tom: If 40% of users who try the feature use it three or more times in week two, the hypothesis is validated. Leila: Now — what is the minimum viable test? Case Study 1: Zappos and the Smoke Test That Proved E-Commerce Could Work
How Nick Swinmurn tested a business idea with zero inventory — and why the Zappos MVP is the most cited example of learning before building

Background

In 1999, Nick Swinmurn had an idea: sell shoes online. The conventional wisdom at the time was that people would never buy shoes online — because they needed to try them on, feel the leather, check the fit. The obvious response to this scepticism was to conduct market research, study industry reports, or commission focus groups. Swinmurn did none of these. Instead, he went to his local shoe stores, photographed shoes that were already for sale, and posted the photographs on a basic website. When orders came in, he went back to the store, bought the shoes at retail price, and shipped them to customers. He was not making money — he was paying retail and selling at retail, covering postage himself. But he was testing a hypothesis: will people buy shoes online?

What the Experiment Revealed

The answer was yes. Customers bought shoes through the website, and — critically — they returned them at lower rates than expected, which indicated that the product photographs and descriptions were accurate enough for customers to make good decisions without physically trying the shoes. Swinmurn had built the minimum viable test of his business: a Wizard of Oz MVP that simulated an online shoe retailer without actually being one. The validated learning from this experiment was that online shoe buying was a real behaviour that real customers were willing to engage in — the most important assumption behind the entire Zappos business model. Armed with this validated learning, Swinmurn was able to approach investors with something far more compelling than a business plan based on market research: evidence that people had already bought shoes online from his website. Tony Hsieh, who would later become Zappos's CEO, was one of the early investors who saw this evidence — and Zappos was eventually sold to Amazon for $1.2 billion in 2009.

Lessons for Founders

The Zappos story illustrates the lean startup principle at its purest: test the most important assumption before building anything. The most important assumption was not whether an inventory management system could be built, or whether logistics could be optimised, or whether the website could scale — all of those are execution problems that can be solved if the fundamental demand exists. The most important assumption was whether customers would buy shoes without trying them on. The Wizard of Oz MVP tested that assumption in weeks rather than years, with minimal cost. The lesson for any founder is to identify the single most important assumption underlying their business — the one whose failure would make everything else irrelevant — and find the minimum viable way to test it before building anything.

Case Study 2: Dropbox and the Demo Video MVP — How Drew Houston Validated a Technical Product Without Building It
How a three-minute video generated 75,000 overnight sign-ups and became the gold standard for testing technical products before they are built

Background

In 2007, Drew Houston was working on Dropbox — a file synchronisation product that would allow users to seamlessly access their files from any device. The product was genuinely technically difficult to build: seamless, reliable file synchronisation across devices and operating systems required solving hard problems that existing solutions had failed to solve well. The conventional approach would have been to build the product, then launch. But Houston faced a classic early-stage dilemma: the product would take months to build, and he had no evidence that people would actually want it enough to use it regularly, change their workflows around it, and — eventually — pay for it.

The MVP: A Demo Video

Houston's solution was remarkably simple: he recorded a three-minute screen capture video demonstrating how Dropbox would work — with the key caveat that the product shown in the video did not yet exist as described. The video showed the seamless file synchronisation experience that Dropbox would eventually provide, narrated by Houston in a conversational, technical style that was clearly aimed at early adopter developers and technical users. He posted the video to Hacker News — a community of technically sophisticated early adopters. Overnight, Dropbox's waiting list grew from 5,000 people to 75,000. This was the validated learning Houston needed: people wanted what he was describing, before it existed. He now had evidence of demand that justified the engineering investment to build the product. Dropbox raised $1.2 million in seed funding shortly afterward and went on to reach a $10 billion valuation.

Lessons for Founders Building Technical Products

The Dropbox demo video illustrates a principle that is particularly valuable for founders building technically complex products: you do not have to build the product to test whether people want it. A well-crafted demonstration of the intended experience — a video, a prototype, a mockup — can reveal whether demand exists at a fraction of the cost of building. The key principles: first, the video must honestly represent what the product will do — misleading demonstrations that promise capabilities the product will not deliver destroy trust when the real product is revealed. Second, the metric that validates the hypothesis must be pre-defined: for Houston, it was a significant increase in waiting list sign-ups. Third, the audience must be the right one — Houston posted to Hacker News because technical early adopters were the target customer, not a generic audience. Targeting the wrong audience in a smoke test produces false data.
