📄 Reading Comprehension

When Nexus Analytics, a data intelligence software company, reviewed its go-to-market strategy in 2022, it faced a challenge that many software founders encounter: it had two equally viable customer types with fundamentally different buying behaviors. On one hand, thousands of individual professionals — analysts, consultants, and freelancers — were using the product and paying £29 per month without ever speaking to a salesperson. On the other, a handful of large enterprises were using it across entire teams and paying between £8,000 and £40,000 annually, with six-month sales cycles involving procurement, security review, and legal sign-off.

The company's founder, Clara Ashworth, recognized that serving both segments with the same team, messaging, and process was creating invisible friction. The marketing that attracted individual users — emotionally driven, feature-focused, conversion-optimized landing pages — was poorly suited to enterprise buyers who needed detailed security documentation, custom pricing models, and evidence of enterprise-grade reliability. Conversely, the white papers and case studies that resonated with enterprise procurement teams were too technical and slow for the impatient individual user who wanted to start immediately.

Clara split the business into two explicit motions. The product-led growth motion served individual users: a generous free tier, frictionless onboarding, and content that demonstrated immediate value within minutes. The enterprise motion was a dedicated team of three account executives supported by a pre-sales engineer, with ABM campaigns targeting 80 specific companies where multiple employees were already using the freemium product — a signal of existing demand within the organization.

The second motion produced an unexpected insight: the freemium individual users were, in many cases, already inside target enterprise accounts. Bottom-up adoption by individual employees was creating champions who could facilitate top-down enterprise deals. Rather than treating B2C and B2B as competing priorities, Nexus had discovered that their consumer motion was their most effective B2B lead generation strategy. Within 18 months, 34% of their enterprise contracts had originated from individual users within the same organization.

📄 Reading Comprehension

In early 2023, Cascade Health, a digital health company generating £4.8M in annual recurring revenue, was preparing its growth budget for the year ahead. Its CFO, Priya Mehta, faced a challenge that will be familiar to many growth-stage businesses: the company had five credible investment opportunities, each supported by a compelling business case, and only enough capital to fund two of them properly. Trying to fund all five partially, she argued to the board, would be worse than funding two fully — a lesson she had learned painfully at two previous companies.

The five options were: expanding the sales team from eight to fourteen to increase market penetration; entering the US market with a local team; investing in product development to launch an enterprise pricing tier; increasing paid marketing spend by 150%; and building a customer success function to improve retention. Each had an estimated cost and a projected return. Each also had different payback periods, risk profiles, and dependencies on other parts of the business.

Priya's framework for the decision was rooted in unit economics. Before approving any growth investment, she required three data points: the current LTV:CAC ratio for the customer segment the investment targeted, the projected payback period on the investment, and the runway impact — how much this investment would shorten the company's cash position before it generated positive return. Two investments immediately qualified: the customer success function (zero incremental revenue but proven retention improvement worth £680,000 annually in reduced churn) and the enterprise pricing tier (an average deal size 4x higher than their current product, with a fully mapped demand pipeline). The US market and increased paid marketing failed the runway test — both required 18+ months to return their investment, while the company's current runway was 22 months.

The resulting budget reflected discipline over ambition. But within the year, the enterprise tier launch generated £1.1M in new ARR, and customer success had reduced churn to 8% annually from 21%. The company entered its next fundraising round with metrics that supported a valuation 40% above management's original estimate. Priya's summary: 'Growth budget decisions are not about what you can afford to do. They are about what you cannot afford to delay.'

📄 Reading Comprehension

In 2021, Norwood Packaging, a mid-sized plastic packaging manufacturer employing 680 people, faced a sustainability challenge that was simultaneously existential and commercial. Three of their top five clients — representing 58% of annual revenue — had publicly committed to eliminating virgin plastic from their supply chains by 2027. Without a credible sustainability transition, Norwood was facing client loss at a scale that threatened its viability. But the transition itself required significant capital investment and operational transformation that, if poorly managed, could destroy the very profitability it was intended to protect.

CEO Marcus Brauer commissioned a materiality assessment that identified four priority issues: virgin plastic content, scope 3 supply chain emissions, product end-of-life recyclability, and social standards in their raw material supply chain. These were the issues most significant to both their clients and their own long-term risk profile. The assessment immediately ruled out a 'sustainability communications' approach — the issues were too material and too measurable to be managed through messaging alone.

The resulting strategy had three components. First, they invested £3.2M in switching 40% of their production to recycled plastic content within 18 months — a capital investment that reduced virgin plastic usage but also, unexpectedly, reduced raw material costs by 12% because recycled feedstock was cheaper than virgin polymer at the volumes they were purchasing. Second, they mapped their full scope 3 supply chain emissions for the first time, identifying two suppliers responsible for 71% of their upstream carbon footprint and beginning a three-year supplier transition program. Third, they launched a product take-back scheme — a circular economy initiative allowing clients to return used packaging for recycling, which became an unexpected sales differentiator.

By 2023, all three major clients had extended their contracts rather than switching suppliers. The sustainability program had attracted two new clients who cited Norwood's verified progress as a selection criterion. And their sustainability-linked refinancing had reduced their annual interest costs by £180,000. Marcus's conclusion at their sustainability review was measured: 'We didn't do this to be good. We did it to survive — and discovered that being good was also the best business decision we had made in ten years.'
