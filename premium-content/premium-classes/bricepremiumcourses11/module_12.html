🏛️   Case Studies

Real-World Cases

Explore the trial that changed practice and the replication that qualified it — how a 2001 single-centre RCT (263 patients) reporting a 16.5 percentage point absolute mortality reduction in septic shock rapidly changed international practice and NHS ICU protocols, despite three important limitations (single-centre design, unusually high control arm mortality, conduct before other sepsis improvements); how three subsequent large multicentre RCTs (ProCESS n=1,341; ARISE n=1,600; ProMISe n=1,260) collectively enrolling 4,200 patients found no mortality benefit; and how clinical consensus eventually concluded that early aggressive sepsis identification remains critical but the specific protocol elements do not confer additional benefit over high-quality usual care — illustrating that single-trial evidence requires replication and must be interpreted in the context of all available evidence; and the coding quality SHMI investigation — how an NHS Trust’s SHMI of 108 triggered an NHS England mortality surveillance alert and a clinical case record review, which found only 2 potentially avoidable deaths in 100 reviewed (consistent with national benchmarks), while the informatics investigation identified three data quality contributors (below-average comorbidity coding for palliative intent/metastatic disease/ESRD, higher-than-average planned end-of-life admissions, hip fracture pathway missing comorbidities), and how a targeted coding improvement programme returned the SHMI to 97 at 12-month re-evaluation — illustrating that mortality statistics are highly sensitive to data quality and require both clinical and informatics investigation before conclusions are drawn.
