Patient-Centred Communication
A model of clinical communication in which the patient's perspectives, values, preferences, and lived experience are placed at the centre of the clinical encounter — rather than the clinician's technical agenda or the institutional requirements of the system. Patient-centred communication, as defined by the NHS's Person-Centred Care framework, requires clinicians to: actively explore the patient's ideas (what do they think is happening?), concerns (what are they most worried about?), and expectations (what do they hope the consultation will achieve?); listen more than they speak; involve the patient meaningfully in decisions about their own care; provide information in a form that the patient can genuinely understand and use; and treat the patient as an individual with a life and context beyond their clinical condition. Research consistently demonstrates that patient-centred consultations produce better adherence to treatment, better clinical outcomes, higher patient satisfaction, and fewer formal complaints — and that the time investment required to shift from clinician-centred to patient-centred communication is smaller than most clinicians assume.

Health Literacy
The degree to which individuals have the ability to find, understand, evaluate, and use health information and services to make informed decisions about their own health. NHS England estimates that approximately half of working-age adults in the UK have a health literacy level that makes it difficult to read and understand standard health information — and that this proportion rises significantly among older adults, people with lower educational attainment, and people for whom English is not a first language. Low health literacy is associated with poorer adherence to medication and treatment plans, higher rates of unplanned hospital admission, difficulty navigating the healthcare system, and poorer clinical outcomes across almost all clinical areas. For healthcare professionals, understanding health literacy means: using plain language rather than medical terminology; avoiding jargon; checking understanding actively through teach-back rather than passively through asking 'do you understand?'; and designing written patient information at an appropriate reading level.

Plain Language
A communication style that prioritises clarity, concision, and accessibility — using familiar, everyday words in preference to technical, Latinate, or jargon-laden equivalents, and presenting information in a logical, straightforward sequence that the reader or listener can follow without specialist knowledge. In a healthcare context, plain language means: saying 'high blood pressure' rather than 'hypertension'; 'heart attack' rather than 'myocardial infarction'; 'water on the knee' rather than 'knee effusion'; 'your body's immune system is attacking your own joints' rather than 'your condition is an autoimmune arthropathy'. NHS England's 'A to Z of conditions' and the Plain English Campaign's health communication guidelines both provide frameworks for translating clinical language into patient-accessible equivalents. Plain language does not mean talking down to patients — it means communicating accurately, clearly, and respectfully in terms that can be genuinely understood and used to make informed decisions.

Active Listening
A deliberate communication technique in which the listener gives their full attention to the speaker — demonstrating engagement not just through the absence of distraction but through specific verbal and non-verbal signals of attention, comprehension, and interest. In clinical communication, active listening involves: maintaining appropriate eye contact (not staring, but not looking away); adopting an open, attentive body posture; nodding and using brief verbal affirmations ('I see', 'I understand', 'go on') to signal attention; not interrupting or completing the patient's sentences; reflecting back what the patient has said ('So what I'm hearing is that the pain is worst in the morning — is that right?'); and tolerating silence rather than rushing to fill it. Research on patient communication consistently finds that clinicians interrupt patients within an average of 11 to 18 seconds of the patient beginning to speak — and that when patients are allowed to speak without interruption, the average uninterrupted consultation length is only 60 to 90 seconds longer. Active listening is both clinically efficient and professionally transformative.

Informed Consent
The legal and ethical process through which a patient, having been given adequate information about a proposed procedure or treatment, exercises their autonomous decision to agree to or refuse it. The standard for informed consent in England and Wales was significantly changed by the Supreme Court judgment in Montgomery v Lanarkshire Health Board (2015), which held that the clinician's duty is to ensure the patient is aware of any material risks of the proposed treatment — a risk being 'material' if a reasonable person in the patient's position would be likely to attach significance to it, or if the clinician is aware that the patient would attach significance to it. This replaced the previous 'Bolam standard' (the clinician disclosed what a responsible body of medical opinion considered appropriate) with a patient-focused standard. For clinical communication, informed consent requires: a genuine two-way conversation in appropriate language; disclosure of material risks and reasonable alternatives; time for the patient to ask questions; and documentation of the consent process, not just the outcome.

Breaking Bad News
The communication process of telling a patient, or their family, information that is likely to cause significant distress — typically a serious diagnosis, a poor prognosis, a treatment failure, or a life-changing change in clinical status. The SPIKES framework (Situation/Setting; Perception — what does the patient already know?; Invitation — how much does the patient want to know?; Knowledge — delivering the news; Emotions — responding to emotional reactions; Strategy and Summary) provides a structured approach to breaking bad news that is widely used in UK clinical training and is endorsed by NHS communication education programmes. Key principles of breaking bad news include: ensuring privacy and the right setting; warning the patient that difficult news is coming ('I'm afraid I have some difficult news to share'); delivering the news clearly and without jargon, pausing after the key statement to allow it to be absorbed; not rushing to information-giving before the emotional response has been acknowledged; and ensuring the patient is not left alone after the conversation and has a clear next step.

Teach-Back
A communication technique for confirming patient understanding in which the clinician asks the patient to repeat back, in their own words, the key information they have been given — rather than simply asking 'do you understand?' or 'is that clear?'. The teach-back technique is based on the well-established finding that patients frequently say they understand information they have not in fact understood, either because they are reluctant to appear ignorant, because they assume they will understand it better when they re-read the leaflet at home, or because they genuinely believe they have understood more than they have. A teach-back prompt sounds like: 'I've given you quite a lot of information today and I want to make sure I've explained it clearly. When you get home and your daughter asks what the doctor said, what will you tell her?' or 'Can you show me how you're going to take this medication when you get home?' If the patient cannot accurately reflect back the key information, the clinician knows their explanation has not been understood — and can rephrase and check again rather than assuming understanding.

Professional Interpreter
A trained, qualified individual who translates spoken language between a patient and a clinician during a clinical consultation — ensuring that clinical information, patient concerns, and the consent process are communicated accurately and completely across a language barrier. NHS guidelines, the GMC's guidance on working with interpreters, and the Equality Act 2010 all establish that patients with limited English proficiency are entitled to professional interpreter services from their NHS provider, and that clinical consultations must not rely on family members or friends as interpreters except in genuine emergencies. The reasons for this are well-evidenced: family interpreters may omit, modify, or add to the clinician's questions and the patient's responses (either unintentionally or to protect the patient from difficult information); family interpreters in certain cultural contexts may influence the patient's responses; children should never be used as interpreters for adults; and clinical information disclosed by the patient during a consultation may be sensitive and should not be shared with a family member without the patient's explicit consent.

ICE
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations — a structured framework for eliciting the patient's own perspective at the start or during a clinical consultation. ICE is a foundational concept in the Calgary-Cambridge communication model, which is widely used in UK medical education and underpins the GMC's expectations for clinical communication. Eliciting the patient's Ideas means asking what they think is causing their problem or what they already know about their condition — this reveals health beliefs, prior knowledge, and potential misconceptions that will affect how the clinician communicates. Eliciting Concerns means asking what is worrying them most — which may be very different from the clinical concern the clinician is focused on (a patient presenting with a headache may be most worried about a brain tumour; the clinician may be most concerned about the patient's blood pressure). Eliciting Expectations means asking what they hope the consultation will achieve — which helps the clinician understand the patient's agenda and meet it, even if the clinical answer is different from what the patient hoped for.

Shared Decision-Making
An approach to clinical decision-making in which the clinician and the patient work together as equal partners — the clinician contributing their clinical knowledge and expertise, the patient contributing their values, preferences, life circumstances, and personal goals — to reach a decision about care that is both clinically appropriate and aligned with what matters to the patient. Shared decision-making is a central principle of the NHS's Person-Centred Care framework and is supported by the NHS NICE guidance on shared decision-making and the 'making decisions together' programme. It differs from paternalistic decision-making (in which the clinician decides what is best and tells the patient) and from uninformed patient-directed decision-making (in which the patient decides without adequate information). The three essential elements of shared decision-making are: the clinician sharing the clinical evidence in accessible language; the patient sharing what matters to them; and both parties reaching a decision together that reflects both sets of priorities.

Chunking and Checking
A communication technique for delivering complex clinical information in manageable portions — presenting one piece of information ('chunk') and then pausing to check understanding before proceeding to the next. Chunking and checking addresses the well-documented phenomenon of 'information overload' in clinical consultations: patients who are given a large amount of clinical information in one continuous delivery typically retain only a small proportion of it. By dividing the information into discrete, logically ordered segments and checking for understanding after each one, the clinician significantly increases the proportion of information the patient retains and can use. An example of chunking and checking: 'Let me explain what we found — [Chunk 1] — 'The tests show that your kidneys are not working as well as they should be. Does that make sense so far?' [Check]. 'The reason for this is probably the ibuprofen you've been taking.' [Chunk 2] 'Is that something you understand?' [Check]. 'What we need to do is stop the ibuprofen — all anti-inflammatory painkillers — and drink more water.' [Chunk 3] 'Are you happy with that plan?'

Safety-Netting
A communication practice in which a clinician who is discharging a patient with uncertainty about their diagnosis or management explicitly tells the patient what to look out for, when and how to seek further help if their condition does not improve or worsens, and in what circumstances they should seek emergency care. Safety-netting is a core requirement of professional clinical practice in UK primary and secondary care: it protects both the patient (ensuring they can access further care if needed) and the clinician (demonstrating that they communicated appropriately about the uncertainty and provided clear guidance for re-presentation). Effective safety-netting is specific and actionable: not 'come back if you're worried' but 'if you develop a fever above 38 degrees, if the pain significantly worsens, or if you feel breathless, please call 999 or come directly to A&E. If your symptoms have not improved within 48 hours, please call the surgery for a telephone review.' Vague safety-netting — 'see how it goes' — is not clinically or professionally adequate.

Empathy
The ability to understand and share the feelings of another person — and, in clinical communication, to communicate that understanding in a way that makes the patient feel genuinely heard, respected, and not alone in their experience. Clinical empathy is distinct from sympathy (feeling sorry for the patient) and from personal emotional identification with the patient's distress (which can impair clinical judgment): it is the professional discipline of acknowledging the patient's emotional experience while maintaining the clinical clarity and objectivity needed to help them. Empathy statements in clinical communication include: 'I can hear how frightening this has been for you'; 'That must have been a very difficult few weeks'; 'I can only imagine how much you were hoping for different news'; 'It makes complete sense that you're feeling overwhelmed by this'. The clinical evidence for empathy is substantial: patients of clinicians rated higher in empathy have better adherence, better outcomes, lower complaint rates, and better recovery times, including from measurable physiological endpoints such as blood pressure control and pain management.

Signposting
A communication technique in which the clinician explicitly tells the patient what is about to happen in the consultation — preparing them for a change of direction, a shift in topic, or the introduction of new information, so they can follow the structure of the conversation and process each stage before moving to the next. Signposting phrases include: 'Now I'd like to ask you some more detailed questions about your symptoms — is that OK?'; 'Before I tell you about the results, I want to ask what you've been thinking about since our last appointment'; 'I'm going to need to examine you now — I'll explain what I'm looking for as we go'; 'There are three things I want to explain to you today, so let me take them one at a time'. Signposting reduces patient anxiety (because they know what to expect), improves attention (because the patient knows what to focus on), and creates a clear structure for both the clinician and the patient to navigate a consultation that may cover several complex topics.

Cultural Competence
The ability to interact effectively with people from different cultural backgrounds — understanding that a patient's cultural context, including their values, beliefs, family structures, attitudes toward illness and authority, and communication norms, may significantly affect how they experience illness, communicate with clinicians, make healthcare decisions, and adhere to treatment. Cultural competence in clinical communication does not require encyclopaedic knowledge of every cultural background — it requires: humility (acknowledging one's own cultural assumptions and the limits of one's knowledge); curiosity (asking the patient rather than assuming); adaptability (adjusting communication style, pace, and approach in response to what the patient tells you); and respect (treating cultural difference as a dimension of the whole person to be understood rather than a problem to be managed). Common areas of cultural difference that affect clinical communication include: the role of the family in healthcare decisions; attitudes to prognosis disclosure (in some cultural contexts, family members may ask that a terminal diagnosis not be shared directly with the patient); varying norms around directness versus indirectness; and different frameworks for understanding the cause and nature of illness.

Reflective Listening
A specific form of active listening in which the clinician mirrors back what the patient has said — either by repeating the key words or phrase ('So the pain is in your chest...'), paraphrasing the content ('It sounds as though the symptoms are worse when you exercise...'), or reflecting the emotional content ('You seem very worried about what this might mean...'). Reflective listening has several clinical functions: it demonstrates to the patient that they have been heard and understood; it allows the clinician to check that they have correctly understood what the patient is communicating; it gives the patient an opportunity to correct a misunderstanding or add nuance; and it encourages the patient to continue sharing information that may be clinically important. In motivational interviewing and person-centred care models, reflective listening is considered a primary clinical skill rather than a secondary communication courtesy — it is both the means of gathering accurate clinical information and the foundation of the therapeutic relationship.

Paternalism
An approach to clinical decision-making and communication in which the clinician assumes the role of authority — deciding what is in the patient's best interests and communicating those decisions to the patient without full exploration of the patient's own values, preferences, and understanding. Paternalism in healthcare has been progressively and deliberately replaced by patient-centred care and shared decision-making in UK NHS policy and professional regulation: the GMC's Good Medical Practice, the NHS Constitution, and multiple Supreme Court judgments (including Montgomery v Lanarkshire) have established that patients are entitled to be treated as autonomous adults capable of making their own informed decisions. The residual presence of paternalism in NHS communication typically manifests as: clinical information provided in language the patient cannot understand; time pressure that prevents adequate information exchange; a focus on the clinician's clinical agenda rather than the patient's concerns; and a culture in which patients who ask questions or decline treatment are treated as 'difficult' rather than as exercising their legal rights.

De-escalation
A communication technique used to reduce tension, anger, or distress in a patient (or their family member) who has become upset, aggressive, or hostile — using language, tone, body posture, and listening skills to lower the emotional temperature of an interaction and restore a productive clinical relationship. NHS guidance on managing conflict and challenging behaviour identifies de-escalation as the first-line professional response to patient aggression, to be used before any consideration of security involvement or formal safeguarding action. Key de-escalation techniques include: remaining calm and speaking in a steady, measured tone; acknowledging the patient's feelings without dismissing them ('I can see you're very frustrated — that's completely understandable'); using the patient's name to create connection; giving the patient space and not crowding them; avoiding confrontational or dismissive language; offering a concrete resolution or next step where possible; and knowing when to pause the consultation and reschedule rather than trying to proceed when the conversation is no longer productive.

Verbal Consent
The spoken agreement of a patient to a clinical procedure, examination, or treatment — an important complement to written consent in contexts where a formal consent form is not required or appropriate. In clinical communication, verbal consent is required for: routine clinical examinations ('Is it OK if I examine your abdomen?'); blood tests and non-invasive investigations; telephone consultations (the clinician should confirm the patient's identity and check they are in a suitable location to speak freely); and day-to-day care activities. The principle is the same as for written consent: the patient must have been given adequate information, must have the capacity to consent, and must agree freely without coercion. For healthcare professionals whose first language is not English, the verbal consent process requires particular attention — ensuring that questions about consent are phrased in a way that genuinely checks agreement (not in a way that makes refusal awkward or difficult), and that the patient's response is a genuine 'yes' rather than a compliance reflex.

Mental Capacity
The ability of an individual to make a specific decision at a specific time — assessed under the framework of the Mental Capacity Act 2005, which specifies that a person has mental capacity to make a decision if they can: understand the information relevant to the decision; retain it long enough to make the decision; use and weigh it in the process of making the decision; and communicate their decision (by any means — verbal, non-verbal, or through an augmentative communication system). The Mental Capacity Act establishes that capacity is presumed unless there is reason to believe otherwise, that capacity is decision-specific and time-specific (a person may have capacity to decide about one thing but not another, or may have capacity on one day but not another), and that an unwise decision does not in itself indicate lack of capacity. For clinical communication, assessment of mental capacity is relevant when a patient declines a treatment, makes a decision that appears against their best interests, or when their cognitive status raises questions about their ability to consent to care.

Motivational Interviewing
A patient-centred, evidence-based communication approach designed to help patients explore and resolve ambivalence about behaviour change — such as adopting a healthier diet, taking regular exercise, stopping smoking, or adhering to a medication regime. Motivational interviewing (MI), developed by William Miller and Stephen Rollnick and widely used in NHS primary care, mental health, and substance misuse services, is built on four key principles: Partnership (the clinician and patient collaborate as equals, with the patient as the expert on their own life); Acceptance (the clinician accepts the patient as they are and respects their autonomy); Compassion (the clinician actively promotes the patient's wellbeing); and Evocation (the clinician draws out the patient's own motivations for change rather than imposing the clinician's agenda). Key MI techniques include: open-ended questions ('What would need to change for you to feel ready to try the medication?'); affirmations ('You've already made some significant changes to your lifestyle — that takes real commitment'); reflections; and summaries.

Text 1: The Evidence for Effective Patient Communication — Why It Matters Clinically
What research tells us about the impact of communication quality on patient outcomes, adherence, and clinical effectiveness

The case for investing in clinical communication skills is not merely ethical or reputational — it is clinical. Decades of research across primary and secondary care settings have consistently shown that the quality of communication between clinicians and patients has a measurable impact on clinical outcomes that is comparable in magnitude to many clinical interventions. Patients of clinicians with higher patient-rated communication quality have significantly better blood pressure control, better glycaemic management, higher rates of cancer screening attendance, higher adherence to medication, and lower rates of unplanned hospital readmission. These are not marginal effects: systematic reviews have found that effective physician communication is associated with a two-fold improvement in patient adherence to recommended treatment, and that patient-centred consultations are associated with significantly lower healthcare resource use — because patients who understand their condition and treatment are less likely to present urgently when they do not need to.

The mechanisms through which communication quality affects clinical outcomes are well understood. When patients understand their diagnosis and treatment plan — genuinely understand, not merely nod during the consultation — they are more likely to adhere to medication, more likely to attend follow-up appointments, more likely to make the lifestyle changes they have agreed to make, and more likely to recognise and respond appropriately to worsening symptoms. Conversely, patients who leave a consultation confused about their diagnosis, uncertain about their medication, or unclear about when to seek further help are more likely to take medication incorrectly, miss follow-up appointments, fail to seek care when they need it, and present as emergencies when the condition has deteriorated beyond primary care management. The quality of the information exchange in the consultation is the proximal driver of these outcomes — and it is primarily the clinician's responsibility.

The NHS's regulatory and educational frameworks have embedded communication quality as a core professional competency. The GMC's Good Medical Practice requires doctors to 'listen to patients, find out about their concerns and preferences, and respond to these'; to 'give patients the information they want or need in a way they can understand'; and to 'respect patients' right to reach decisions with you about their treatment and care'. The Royal College of GPs and the medical Royal Colleges all embed communication skills as a competency framework for postgraduate training, and the Clinical Skills Assessment in MRCGP and the clinical examinations for specialty certificates all test communication quality directly. For internationally trained healthcare professionals joining the NHS, developing the specific communication skills expected in the UK context — including patient-centred consulting, shared decision-making, and breaking bad news — is not a soft skill optional: it is a professional requirement assessed in the highest-stakes professional examinations they will face.

Text 2: Communicating Across Difference — Language, Culture, and Health Literacy in the NHS
The principles and practices of effective patient communication when language, literacy, and cultural background create additional complexity

Effective clinical communication is always challenging. When it takes place across a language barrier, a significant literacy gap, or a cultural difference in health beliefs and communication norms, the challenge is multiplied — and the consequences of failure are proportionally more serious. The NHS serves a population of extraordinary diversity: in London alone, over 300 languages are spoken, and NHS hospitals in major cities manage clinical consultations in dozens of languages on any given day. The commitment to providing safe, equitable care to all patients regardless of language or cultural background is both an NHS constitutional right and an Equality Act requirement — but meeting that commitment demands more than good intentions. It demands specific knowledge, specific skills, and the professional humility to recognise the limits of what a clinician can communicate effectively without appropriate support.

The most important principle governing clinical communication across language barriers is straightforward but frequently violated: the clinician must use a qualified professional interpreter, not a family member, friend, or bilingual staff member who has not been trained as a clinical interpreter. The reasons for this requirement are not bureaucratic — they are clinical and ethical. Family members who interpret may omit information they believe will distress the patient, add reassurances the clinician did not provide, filter questions about sensitive topics such as sexual health, mental health, or domestic violence, and place themselves in an impossible emotional position when bad news is being communicated. Children should never interpret for adult family members — the emotional and psychological burden of interpreting clinical conversations, particularly those involving serious illness or death, is inappropriate for any child. Professional interpreter services — available by telephone, video, or in person in most NHS settings — provide accurate, unmediated, professionally bounded translation that protects both the patient's rights and the clinician's professional position.

Health literacy creates a parallel challenge that is less visible but equally significant. A patient who nods throughout a consultation, says 'yes' when asked if they understand, and leaves with a prescription and a leaflet may have understood very little of what was communicated — particularly if they have low educational attainment, English as a second language, or cognitive impairment from illness or medication. The NHS Long Term Plan and the Health Literacy: Towards a Whole System Approach guidelines emphasise that health literacy is a system responsibility as well as an individual one: health literacy challenges are not primarily a patient problem but a communication design problem — one that requires clinicians to check understanding actively, use plain language consistently, design patient information at appropriate reading levels, and treat each consultation as a communication quality event rather than an information delivery event. The teach-back technique — asking patients to explain back key information in their own words — is the single most evidence-based tool for identifying and addressing health literacy gaps in clinical consultations.

Exercise 2 — Grammar 2: Breaking Bad News and Managing Difficult Conversations

Difficult conversations require a structured approach (warning shot, pause, emotional acknowledgment before information-giving), honest but compassionate language (clear without being brutal), and active support (not leaving the patient with the news and no next step).

Warning shot:

  • 'I'm afraid the news is not what we were hoping for...'
  • 'I have some difficult news to share with you...'

Delivering the news:

  • 'The biopsy has confirmed that there is cancer.' [PAUSE]

Case Study 1: The Consultation That Changed Practice — How Teach-Back and Plain Language Reduced Medication Errors in a Diabetes Service
How a systematic approach to patient communication quality transformed medication safety in an NHS diabetes service
This case study is drawn from a representative composite of communication quality improvement projects documented in NHS England's Getting It Right First Time (GIRFT) programme and the Patient Safety Learning Hub's reports on medication safety in long-term condition management.

A large primary care network in the Midlands was concerned about high rates of hypoglycaemic episodes among its patients with Type 2 diabetes — patients were presenting to A&E after administering incorrect insulin doses, taking their medication at the wrong time, or failing to adjust doses when monitoring showed low blood sugar readings. The clinical team's initial assumption was that the problem was clinical complexity — the patients had difficulty managing their diabetes because their conditions were complicated.

A communication audit told a different story. When patients were asked to demonstrate how they administered their insulin, describe the dose adjustment rules they had been given, and explain what to do if they felt hypoglycaemic symptoms, a significant proportion were unable to do so accurately — despite having been given verbal instruction and written leaflets. The audit identified two consistent patterns: clinicians were using clinical terminology (bolus, basal, titration, hypoglycaemia) without checking understanding; and clinicians were ending consultations by asking 'is that clear?' rather than checking understanding through teach-back.

The improvement programme had two components: all clinical staff received training in plain language communication and teach-back technique; and the consultation protocol was changed to require that all patients starting or changing an insulin regime demonstrate the relevant competencies before leaving the appointment — showing the nurse how they would administer the dose, describing the signs of hypoglycaemia in their own words, and explaining the dose adjustment rule.

Within twelve months, the PCN's rate of A&E presentations for hypoglycaemic episodes had reduced by 38%. There were no changes to the clinical protocols or the complexity of the medication regimes — the only change was how the information was communicated and how understanding was checked.

The lesson: communication quality is not a luxury — it is a direct determinant of medication safety. Assuming patient understanding because a leaflet was given and a 'yes' was received is not sufficient. Checking understanding through active techniques — particularly teach-back — is a clinical standard, not an optional communication enhancement.

Case Study 2: The Interpreter Who Wasn't There — How Communication Failure Caused Harm in a Maternity Setting
How the failure to use a professional interpreter in a clinical consultation contributed to a preventable patient harm incident
This case study is drawn from a representative composite of interpreter-related patient safety incidents documented in NHS England's Maternal Mortality reports, the Healthcare Safety Investigation Branch (HSIB) maternity reports, and NHS Resolution's guidance on communication failures in maternity care.

Mrs Fatima Al-Rashid, a 29-year-old woman in her first pregnancy, attended her antenatal booking appointment at a large NHS maternity unit. Her primary language was Arabic; her English was limited. Her husband accompanied her to the appointment and acted as interpreter throughout, translating the midwife's questions and Mrs Al-Rashid's responses.

During the appointment, the midwife asked a series of standard screening questions — including questions about domestic violence and coercive control, which are asked of all pregnant women as part of the NHS's safeguarding protocol for domestic abuse. These questions are intended to be asked privately, without the presence of a partner. The midwife did not ask the husband to wait outside and did not use a professional interpreter or a telephone interpretation service. All questions, including the domestic violence screening questions, were asked with the husband present and using him as interpreter.

Mrs Al-Rashid denied any concerns about domestic violence — translated to the midwife by her husband. Two months later, following a hospital attendance for injuries consistent with domestic violence, it became apparent that Mrs Al-Rashid had been experiencing abuse throughout her pregnancy. A subsequent investigation found that she had been afraid to disclose this to the midwife — both because her husband was present and interpreting, and because she did not feel she could communicate safely without a private, professional interpretation service.

The investigation identified two root causes: the failure to conduct domestic violence screening privately (a departure from established protocol); and the failure to use a professional interpreter for any part of the booking appointment. The Trust updated its antenatal booking protocol to require a professional interpreter for all patients with limited English proficiency, and to require that domestic violence screening questions be asked in a private setting with professional interpretation — never with a partner or family member present.

The lesson is unambiguous: using family members as interpreters in clinical settings is not merely inadequate — in some clinical contexts, such as domestic violence screening, mental health assessment, and sexual health consultations, it is directly dangerous. Professional interpreter services are a patient safety requirement, not an administrative nicety.
