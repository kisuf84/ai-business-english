Medical Record
The comprehensive, chronological collection of all clinical and administrative documentation relating to an individual patient's healthcare — including clinical notes, letters, investigation results, imaging reports, operation notes, consent forms, prescriptions, and any other information generated in the course of providing care. In the UK, medical records are the legal property of the Secretary of State for Health (for NHS records) but patients have a statutory right of access to their own records under the UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018. Medical records must be: accurate and factual; contemporaneous (made at the time of or immediately after the clinical event); legible (in electronic records, this means clearly structured and written in plain, unambiguous language); complete; and retained for defined minimum periods (typically 8 years from the date of last treatment for adults, and until the patient's 25th birthday or 8 years from death for children, under NHS Records Management Code of Practice guidance). The quality of medical records is both a professional obligation and a patient safety imperative: poor records are a significant contributory factor in clinical errors, delayed diagnoses, and medicolegal claims.

Clinical Note
A contemporaneous entry in a patient's medical record documenting a specific clinical encounter, observation, decision, or action — including inpatient ward round entries, outpatient consultation notes, telephone consultation records, nursing assessments, and any other clinical interaction that generates information relevant to the patient's care. Professional standards for clinical notes (as set by the GMC, NMC, and other regulatory bodies) require that they be: made as close to the time of the clinical event as possible; signed with a legible signature or printed name, professional role, and date and time; factual rather than speculative or judgemental; written in clear English without inappropriate abbreviations; and stored securely. In an electronic patient record (EPR) system, clinical notes are time- and user-stamped automatically, creating an audit trail that is admissible as evidence in medicolegal proceedings. A clinical note that is unclear, incomplete, or made retrospectively without appropriate dating is a significant professional and legal risk.

Discharge Summary
A structured clinical document completed when a patient is discharged from hospital — communicating the key information about the patient's inpatient episode to the receiving healthcare professionals (typically the GP and any community or ongoing care team) who will continue the patient's care after discharge. A professional discharge summary should contain: the patient's identifying information (name, NHS number, date of birth, address); admission date, discharge date, and responsible consultant; the reason for admission (presenting complaint and diagnosis); the investigations performed and their findings; the treatment provided; the outcome (improved, stable, palliative); the discharge medication with any changes from pre-admission medication clearly highlighted; follow-up arrangements (outpatient appointments, community services, further investigations); and clear instructions for any actions the GP should take (repeat prescribing, monitoring, follow-up test results). The NHS requires discharge summaries for emergency admissions to be sent to the patient's GP within 24 hours; for elective admissions, within 48 hours. Delayed or incomplete discharge summaries are a significant patient safety risk — the GP who does not receive a timely summary cannot safely manage the patient's care in the community.

Referral Letter
A formal written communication from one healthcare professional to another — requesting that the recipient assess, investigate, or manage a patient who requires care beyond what the referring clinician can provide. In the NHS, referral letters are most commonly produced by GPs referring patients to secondary or tertiary care, or by hospital specialists referring patients to other specialties or to community services. A high-quality referral letter contains: the patient's identifying information (name, NHS number, date of birth, address, contact number); the reason for referral (the clinical problem to be assessed); the relevant clinical history (background condition, current symptoms, duration, relevant past medical history, medications); examination findings; investigations already performed and their results; the referring clinician's working diagnosis or clinical concern; any specific questions they want the recipient to address; and the urgency (routine, soon, urgent). The quality of a referral letter directly affects the quality of care the patient receives: a vague or incomplete referral may result in the patient being seen by the wrong specialist, the wrong investigation being performed, or the receiving clinician being unable to prepare adequately.

SOAP Format
A widely used structured format for clinical documentation — organising information into four sections: Subjective (what the patient reports — symptoms, concerns, history in their own words); Objective (what the clinician observes or measures — vital signs, examination findings, investigation results); Assessment (the clinician's interpretation — diagnosis, differential diagnosis, clinical reasoning); and Plan (what will be done — investigations to be ordered, treatments to be initiated, referrals to be made, follow-up to be arranged). The SOAP format was originally developed in primary care but is now widely used across hospital and community settings because it imposes a logical, consistent structure on clinical documentation — making notes easier to read, easier to audit, and more likely to include all relevant components. For internationally trained healthcare professionals joining the NHS, adopting a consistent structured documentation format is an important early professional standard — a well-structured SOAP note communicates much more effectively than a narrative-only entry, particularly when being read by a clinician who has not met the patient.

Caldicott Principles
A framework of eight principles governing the use of patient-identifiable information in health and social care organisations in England — developed by Dame Fiona Caldicott in her capacity as the National Data Guardian for Health and Care, originally in 1997 and most recently updated in 2020. The eight Caldicott Principles are: (1) Justify the purpose of using patient information; (2) Use patient-identifiable information only when necessary; (3) Use the minimum necessary patient-identifiable information; (4) Access to patient-identifiable information should be on a strict need-to-know basis; (5) Everyone accessing patient information should be aware of their responsibilities; (6) Comply with the law; (7) The duty to share information can be as important as the duty to protect patient confidentiality; and (8) Inform patients and service users about how their information is used. Every NHS organisation is required to appoint a Caldicott Guardian — typically a senior clinician — who is responsible for protecting the confidentiality of patient information and enabling appropriate information sharing.

Subject Access Request
A formal request from an individual (or their authorised representative) to an organisation for access to the personal data held about them — a right established under the UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018. In healthcare, subject access requests (SARs) most commonly take the form of patients or their representatives requesting copies of their own medical records. NHS organisations must respond to SARs within one calendar month of receiving the request (with a possible extension of two further months in complex cases). The records provided must include all personal data the organisation holds about the individual — including clinical notes, letters, imaging reports, and other records, with the exception of information that would be withheld on legal grounds (for example, information about a third party who has not consented to its disclosure). Healthcare professionals dealing with SARs must understand what information must be disclosed, what can lawfully be withheld, and the process for managing requests accurately and within the required timeframe.

Information Governance
The framework of legal, regulatory, and organisational policies and processes that govern how NHS organisations and their staff collect, store, use, share, and protect patient information — encompassing data protection law (UK GDPR and Data Protection Act 2018), confidentiality obligations, records management standards, and the specific governance requirements of NHS England and NHS Digital (now NHS England). Information governance in the NHS requires healthcare professionals to: handle patient information with appropriate confidentiality; access only the patient information they need to perform their clinical or administrative role; store patient information securely (including secure use of electronic devices); report any breaches of patient data security promptly; complete mandatory information governance training; and comply with their organisation's specific information governance policies. NHS Digital's Data Security and Protection Toolkit (DSPT) is the national assessment framework used by NHS organisations to demonstrate their information governance compliance annually.

Handover Note
A structured clinical communication documenting the key information about a patient's condition, current management, and immediate actions required — produced at the transition between clinical teams (for example, at the end of a shift or when transferring care between departments). Handover notes are one of the highest-risk communication moments in hospital care: inadequate handover is a significant contributory factor in adverse patient events and near misses. Professional standards for clinical handover (informed by NHS Improvement's 'SBAR' framework — Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) require that handover communications be: structured and consistent; complete in terms of the clinically critical information; appropriately urgent (prioritising the sickest and most complex patients); and confirmed by the receiving clinician (closed-loop communication — the receiver confirms they have understood the key information). In the NHS, formal handover processes are supported by tools such as electronic handover systems, standardised handover proformas, and structured ward round documentation.

Contemporaneous
Made at the time of or immediately after the clinical event — the standard of timeliness required for professional clinical documentation. Contemporaneous documentation is a fundamental professional and legal requirement: a clinical note made at the time of an encounter is presumed to be an accurate record of what occurred; a note made days or weeks later is of significantly reduced evidential and clinical value, and its retrospective nature must be explicitly acknowledged if it is added to the record after the fact. The GMC's 'Good Medical Practice' guidance and the NMC's 'Code' both require healthcare professionals to make and keep contemporaneous records. In medicolegal contexts, the question of whether a note was contemporaneous — and whether it is consistent with other contemporaneous records (nursing notes, observation charts, medication records) — is often a central issue in determining what actually occurred and what was known at the time. Healthcare professionals who routinely delay documentation or complete notes retrospectively are at significantly higher medicolegal risk.

Electronic Patient Record
A digital system for creating, storing, retrieving, and sharing patient clinical records — replacing or supplementing paper-based records in NHS hospitals and GP practices. EPR systems used in NHS Trusts include Epic (adopted by several major teaching hospitals including Cambridge University Hospitals, University College London Hospitals, and Leeds Teaching Hospitals), Cerner, and local/regional systems. In primary care, EMIS Web and SystmOne are the predominant EPR platforms. EPR systems offer significant advantages over paper records: they are legible (no handwriting problems); they include time and user stamps that create a clear audit trail; they can be accessed simultaneously by multiple authorised users; they can include decision support and alerts; and they can share information across organisations (through interoperability standards). Challenges include: the need for digital literacy and training; risks of data entry errors (particularly when clinical information is entered by coding or transcription rather than the clinician); system downtime; and the risk of information overload when records are very long or poorly structured.

Clinic Letter
A formal written communication from a hospital consultant or specialist to the patient's GP — documenting the findings, assessment, and management plan from an outpatient consultation, and confirming any follow-up arrangements, investigations ordered, or medication changes. The clinic letter is the primary communication channel between secondary and primary care after a specialist outpatient appointment, and its quality directly affects the GP's ability to manage the patient's ongoing care. A professional clinic letter contains: the patient's identifying information; the date of the consultation; the reason for the referral and the history as presented; the examination findings and investigations performed; the consultant's assessment and diagnosis; the management plan agreed with the patient; any medication changes; follow-up arrangements; and any specific actions requested of the GP. NHS expectations are that clinic letters should be sent to the GP within 14 days of the consultation; in many Trusts, letters should be completed on the day of the clinic using digital dictation or EPR direct entry. Letters that arrive weeks after the appointment, or that are vague or incomplete, are a significant source of GP dissatisfaction and patient safety risk.

Consent Form
A formal legal document recording that a patient has given their informed consent to a specific medical procedure or treatment — having been provided with adequate information about the procedure, its risks and benefits, and the available alternatives, and having had an opportunity to ask questions. Informed consent is both a legal requirement (under the Mental Capacity Act 2005 and established case law, particularly the Montgomery v Lanarkshire judgement) and a professional obligation. A completed consent form should document: the patient's name and identifying information; the procedure to be performed; confirmation that the nature, purpose, risks, and alternatives have been explained; the signature of the clinician who obtained consent (who must be qualified to perform the procedure or have been trained in the consent process); the patient's signature; the date; and any specific additional information relevant to the individual patient. The consent form is a record of the consent process — it does not replace the conversation; it is evidence that the conversation occurred. A consent form completed incorrectly or without genuine informed discussion is legally insufficient and places both the clinician and the organisation at risk.

Clinical Audit
A quality improvement process in which clinical practice is systematically measured against defined standards — and where identified gaps between current practice and the standard are addressed through targeted improvement actions, followed by re-measurement to confirm improvement. Clinical audit is a professional and regulatory requirement in NHS organisations: the CQC expects NHS providers to demonstrate ongoing clinical audit activity as evidence of quality governance; the GMC requires all doctors to participate in audit as part of revalidation; and many clinical standards (including NICE guidelines and Royal College standards) specify audit as the mechanism for monitoring compliance. In a documentation context, clinical audits frequently examine: the completeness and timeliness of discharge summaries; the quality of referral letters; the completion of consent forms; the accuracy of clinical coding; and the standard of documentation of high-risk clinical events. The findings of documentation audits are typically reported to clinical governance committees and translated into targeted education, process changes, or system improvements.

Problem List
A curated summary of a patient's active and significant medical problems — maintained within the electronic patient record as a structured, up-to-date reference for all clinicians involved in the patient's care. The problem list is one of the most clinically valuable features of a modern EPR: when complete and accurate, it provides any clinician — including a covering doctor seeing the patient for the first time — with an immediate overview of the patient's relevant medical history, enabling safe and appropriate clinical decision-making. Problem lists should include: active medical conditions; significant resolved conditions that may affect current care; relevant past surgical history; significant allergies and adverse drug reactions; and any mental health conditions or psychosocial factors relevant to care. Problem lists must be actively maintained — a list that has not been updated for months, that contains duplications, or that omits newly diagnosed conditions, is worse than no list, because it creates a false sense of completeness while leaving gaps that could lead to clinical error.

Clinical Incident Report
A formal record of an unexpected or adverse clinical event — completed by a healthcare professional who witnesses, discovers, or is involved in an incident that has caused or could have caused harm to a patient, a staff member, or a visitor. Clinical incident reporting is a cornerstone of NHS patient safety culture: the NHS encourages a 'just culture' in which staff feel safe to report incidents without fear of blame, recognising that system-level learning from incident reports is more valuable than individual accountability. Incident reports should be: completed as soon as possible after the event; factual and objective (describing what happened, not who was at fault); complete in terms of the sequence of events, the people involved, and the actions taken; and submitted through the Trust's designated incident reporting system (such as Datix, the widely used NHS incident management software). Completed incident reports are reviewed by clinical governance and patient safety teams — serious incidents (SIs) may trigger a formal root cause analysis (RCA) investigation, the findings of which are shared with the relevant teams and, in some cases, with NHS England and the patient.

Information Sharing
The communication of patient-identifiable information between healthcare professionals, organisations, or other relevant parties — governed by the Caldicott Principles, the UK GDPR, and the specific information governance frameworks of NHS organisations. Appropriate information sharing is essential for safe and coordinated patient care: a community nurse who does not receive discharge information from the hospital cannot safely manage the patient's medications; an emergency department that cannot access a patient's GP record is working blind on a potentially critically unwell patient. The NHS has historically been criticised for information sharing that is too restrictive, with clinicians defaulting to confidentiality as a reason not to share information that would benefit patient care. The seventh Caldicott Principle specifically addresses this: 'the duty to share information can be as important as the duty to protect patient confidentiality'. Legitimate reasons for information sharing include: direct patient care (shared with the clinical team involved in caring for the patient); public health and protection (for example, notification of infectious diseases); legal obligation; and, in some circumstances, with explicit patient consent.

Countersigning
The act of a senior clinician adding their signature to a clinical document — most commonly to a note, prescription, or request made by a more junior clinician — to confirm that they have reviewed, approved, and take responsibility for the document. Countersigning is required in several specific contexts: prescriptions written by junior doctors or non-medical prescribers in some settings; clinical notes made by medical students (which must be countersigned by a supervising clinician); discharge summaries in some trusts where the senior clinician reviews and approves the document written by a junior colleague; and certain consent forms or procedural documents that require senior clinical oversight. Countersigning creates a clear accountability structure in clinical documentation: it confirms that a senior clinician has reviewed the document and endorses its content. A senior clinician who countersigns a document without adequately reviewing it — particularly a prescription or a consent form — assumes professional and legal responsibility for its contents.

UK GDPR
The UK General Data Protection Regulation — the data protection law that applies to the processing of personal data in the UK, which retained the European GDPR's core principles and rights after Brexit (through the Data Protection, Privacy and Electronic Communications (Amendments Etc) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019), supplemented by the Data Protection Act 2018. In a healthcare context, patient health data is classified as 'special category data' under UK GDPR — attracting the highest level of protection and requiring a specific legal basis for processing. Key UK GDPR rights relevant to patients include: the right of access (subject access requests); the right to have inaccurate data corrected; the right to be informed about how their data is used; and in some circumstances the right to erasure. Healthcare professionals must understand that making clinical records — whether in an NHS EPR, a paper record, or a personal device — constitutes the processing of special category data under UK GDPR, and must be done in compliance with the organisation's data protection policies.

Operation Note
A detailed clinical record of a surgical procedure — documenting what was done, what was found, and what occurred during an operation, completed by the operating surgeon or a senior member of the surgical team immediately after the procedure. An operation note is a critical clinical and legal document: it is the primary record of what occurred in theatre, and its completeness and accuracy directly affect post-operative care decisions, complications management, and medicolegal risk. Professional standards for operation notes (as set by the Royal College of Surgeons and NHS England's Good Surgical Practice guidelines) specify that they must include: the patient's name and identifying information; the date and time of the operation; the names of the operating surgeon, assistant, and anaesthetist; the type of anaesthesia; the procedure performed (including any deviations from the planned procedure); the findings (both expected and unexpected); any complications during the procedure; the patient's condition at the end of the procedure; post-operative instructions (analgesia, fluids, monitoring, mobilisation); and the surgeon's signature. Operation notes are frequently examined in medicolegal proceedings and should be treated with the same rigour as any other legal document.

Records Retention
The defined minimum period for which medical records must be kept before they can be lawfully destroyed — specified by the NHS Records Management Code of Practice, which sets different retention periods for different types of records based on their clinical, legal, and historical significance. Key retention periods under the current NHS Records Management Code of Practice (England) include: general health records — 8 years from the conclusion of treatment; mental health records — 20 years from the date of last contact; obstetric records — 25 years; records relating to children — until the patient's 25th birthday or 8 years after death if they die before age 17; and records relating to oncology — 30 years. Records must be retained in a form that is retrievable and readable — simply keeping records in a locked archive is insufficient if they cannot be accessed when needed. NHS organisations must have documented records management policies and must train staff in their responsibilities for maintaining and disposing of records appropriately.

Text 1: The Law and Professional Standards Governing Medical Records in the UK
What healthcare professionals are legally and professionally required to do in relation to clinical documentation

Medical records in the UK exist at the intersection of clinical practice, professional obligation, and law. A clinician who makes an entry in a patient's record is doing more than documenting a clinical encounter: they are creating a legal document, a professional record, and a communication tool for the entire care team — all simultaneously. The legal framework governing medical records includes the Data Protection Act 2018 and the UK General Data Protection Regulation (which classify patient health data as 'special category data' requiring the highest level of protection); the NHS Records Management Code of Practice (which specifies minimum retention periods and management standards); the GMC's Good Medical Practice; the NMC's Code; and the common law duty of confidentiality. Understanding this framework is not a specialist legal skill — it is a professional baseline for every healthcare professional who makes, accesses, or uses clinical records.

The fundamental professional standards for clinical documentation are consistency applied across all regulatory frameworks and professional bodies: records must be accurate and factual; they must be made contemporaneously (at the time of or immediately after the clinical event); they must be legible and clearly attributable (signed, dated, and timed); they must be complete without being unnecessarily detailed; and they must be stored securely and only accessed by those with a legitimate clinical or administrative need. These standards exist not as bureaucratic requirements but as patient safety imperatives: an inaccurate record can lead to incorrect clinical decisions; a contemporaneous record provides crucial evidence when a patient's clinical deterioration is later investigated; a secure record protects the patient's confidentiality and dignity. The GMC's guidance 'Good Medical Practice' is explicit: 'You must keep clear, accurate and legible records, and must report concerns about a colleague's conduct, performance or health.'

Patients' rights in relation to their medical records have been significantly strengthened by data protection legislation. Under the UK GDPR, patients have the right to access their personal data — including clinical records — through a subject access request, to which the organisation must respond within one calendar month. Patients also have the right to have inaccurate data corrected. However, the right of access is not absolute: organisations can withhold information that could cause serious harm to the physical or mental health of the patient or another person, or information relating to third parties who have not consented to disclosure. For healthcare professionals, navigating these rights — handling a SAR, deciding what to disclose and what to withhold, and managing the process within the legal timeframe — is an information governance competency that must be actively developed, not assumed.

Text 2: Effective Clinical Documentation — What Good Records Look Like and Why They Matter
The principles and practices of high-quality clinical documentation in NHS and private healthcare settings

The difference between a well-documented patient record and a poorly documented one is not merely aesthetic — it is clinical. A well-written discharge summary can prevent a medication error, trigger a necessary follow-up that would otherwise be missed, or provide a covering GP with the information they need to safely manage a patient over a bank holiday weekend. A poorly written one — vague, incomplete, or sent too late — creates the conditions for clinical error, leaves the community team working blind, and in the worst cases contributes to serious patient harm. The quality of clinical documentation is not a soft skill or an administrative nicety: it is a core clinical competency with direct consequences for patient outcomes, and it should be treated with the same rigour as any other clinical activity.

Effective clinical documentation is built on three foundations. The first is structure: using a consistent format — whether SOAP, SBAR, or a specialty-specific structured template — creates predictable, scannable records that can be read efficiently by busy clinical colleagues. A consultant arriving to see a deteriorating patient at night needs to be able to find the relevant information in seconds, not minutes: a structured record enables this; a stream-of-consciousness narrative does not. The second foundation is precision: using the correct clinical terminology, being specific about doses, routes, and frequencies of medications, specifying the exact procedure performed rather than a generic description, and distinguishing between what was observed, what was reported, and what was inferred. The third foundation is completeness: capturing all the clinically relevant information — including investigations ordered and results received, conversations with the patient and their family, decisions made and the reasoning behind them — without omitting anything that another clinician would need to continue the patient's care safely.

The consequences of poor clinical documentation extend far beyond the individual clinical encounter. Incomplete records are a major driver of clinical coding inaccuracy — if a clinician does not document a significant comorbidity, the clinical coder cannot code it, and the NHS Trust loses tariff income it has earned. Poor documentation is consistently identified as a contributory factor in serious incident investigations: the National Patient Safety Agency's analysis of never events and serious incidents repeatedly finds documentation failures at the root of clinical errors. And in medicolegal proceedings, the maxim 'if it wasn't documented, it wasn't done' is applied with considerable force: a clinician who cannot produce a contemporaneous record of the conversation in which they obtained consent, discussed a treatment risk, or communicated a critical investigation result is in a very difficult evidential position.

Case Study 1: The Discharge Summary That Wasn't — How Documentation Failure Led to a Medication Error
How the failure to complete a timely, accurate discharge summary contributed to a preventable patient harm incident
This case study is drawn from a representative composite of NHS serious incident reports relating to medication errors following hospital discharge, documented in NHS England's National Patient Safety Agency learning reports and the Healthcare Safety Investigation Branch (HSIB) reports on medicines reconciliation.

Mr David Williams, a 74-year-old man with Type 2 diabetes and chronic kidney disease, was admitted to hospital for seven days following an acute kidney injury. During his admission, his metformin was stopped (because metformin is contraindicated in acute kidney injury) and a dose adjustment was made to his gliclazide. He was discharged on a Saturday afternoon.

The discharge summary was not completed before he left the ward. The junior doctor responsible for completing it intended to do so over the weekend but was on call on a different ward and did not return to complete the document. The discharge summary was completed the following Tuesday — four days after discharge.

Mr Williams attended his GP practice on the Monday following discharge — two days after he was discharged. The GP, seeing him for the first time after the admission, had no discharge summary to refer to. Mr Williams was confused about his medications. The GP reviewed his pre-admission medication list (which included metformin) and, in the absence of any discharge documentation indicating that it had been stopped, restarted it. Mr Williams took metformin for eight days before his GP received the discharge summary and identified the error. His renal function had deteriorated again by this point. He required a further hospital admission.

The incident was investigated as a serious incident. The root cause analysis identified two primary causes: the failure to complete the discharge summary before the patient left hospital (a documentation and governance failure); and the failure of the GP to check with the ward team before restarting a potentially nephrotoxic medication in a patient recently discharged from hospital (a communication failure). The junior doctor received additional training and supervision. The Trust implemented a policy requiring a discharge summary to be completed before any patient is formally discharged — with the attending consultant countersigning before leaving the ward.

The lesson is one of the most repeated in patient safety: the discharge summary is not a bureaucratic afterthought — it is the primary clinical communication that enables safe continuity of care. Its absence creates conditions in which preventable errors are not just possible but highly likely.

Case Study 2: The Medicolegal Value of Good Documentation — How Contemporaneous Records Protected a Clinician
How a clinician's thorough, contemporaneous documentation provided the evidence to successfully defend a clinical negligence claim
This case study is drawn from a representative composite of clinical negligence defence cases documented in NHS Resolution (formerly the NHS Litigation Authority) and the Medical Defence Union's annual reports on clinical negligence claims involving documentation.

Dr Amara Osei, a consultant obstetrician, was named in a clinical negligence claim brought by the family of a baby who had suffered a hypoxic injury during labour. The claim alleged that Dr Osei had failed to respond appropriately to concerning cardiotocography (CTG) readings during the labour, and that this failure had caused the hypoxic injury.

The case turned on what Dr Osei knew, when she knew it, and what decisions she made and communicated during the labour. Dr Osei's contemporaneous documentation was exceptional by any professional standard: she had written a detailed entry in the patient's notes at each decision point during the labour — specifying the time at which she was called, the CTG findings she reviewed, her clinical assessment, the management decision she made and the reasoning behind it, and the communication she had with the midwifery team and the patient. Crucially, she had also documented a conversation with the patient and her partner at a specific time in which she explained the CTG concerns, discussed the options (continued monitoring versus expedited delivery), and recorded the patient's informed decision to continue conservative management for a further 30 minutes before reassessment.

The defendant's legal team, reviewing the documentation, advised that the case was very strong. Every allegation in the claim was addressed by a contemporaneous record. The time at which Dr Osei was called was in the notes. The CTG findings she reviewed were documented. Her assessment and reasoning were recorded. The consent discussion was documented. The claim was successfully defended — Dr Osei was not found liable.

NHS Resolution's commentary on this case noted that the quality of Dr Osei's documentation was 'significantly above the average seen in cases of this type' and that her thorough records 'substantially reduced the evidential burden on the defendant'. In contrast, the Resolution commentary noted, many similar cases involving comparable clinical situations had been lost or settled for significant sums primarily because the clinical documentation was insufficient to establish what had occurred.

The lesson is clear, and bears repeating: contemporaneous, structured, and complete clinical documentation is not primarily a bureaucratic requirement. It is the professional foundation of clinical accountability — and when things go wrong, it may be the difference between a justified defence and an indefensible claim.
