National Health Service
The publicly funded healthcare system of the United Kingdom, established by the National Health Service Act 1946 and launched on 5 July 1948 under Health Secretary Aneurin Bevan. The NHS was founded on three core principles: that it be universal (available to all UK residents regardless of ability to pay), comprehensive (covering all medical needs), and free at the point of use (funded through general taxation rather than direct charges at the time of service). The NHS is the largest employer in the UK and one of the largest in the world, with over 1.3 million employees across England alone. It serves a population of approximately 56 million in England — with separate NHS organisations for Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland, each with devolved responsibilities for healthcare. The NHS provides the full range of healthcare services from GP appointments and community health services through emergency care, elective surgery, and specialist treatment to mental health services, maternity care, and end-of-life care. Understanding the NHS's structure, principles, and funding is foundational for any healthcare professional working in the UK.

General Practitioner
A doctor who provides primary care — the first point of contact for patients seeking medical advice or treatment within the NHS. GPs (often referred to informally as 'family doctors') are trained in a broad range of medical areas and are expected to manage the majority of health concerns without referral to a specialist. The GP's role as a 'gatekeeper' is a defining feature of the NHS: patients cannot generally access secondary care (hospital specialists) without a referral from their GP, ensuring that specialist resources are used appropriately and that the GP has coordinated oversight of the patient's overall care. GPs in England typically work in GP practices (primary care settings) that are independent contractor organisations rather than directly employed NHS organisations. They are contracted to provide services under the General Medical Services (GMS) or Personal Medical Services (PMS) contracts. A GP is also responsible for prescribing medications, providing chronic disease management, conducting routine health checks, and coordinating with other primary care professionals (nurses, pharmacists, physiotherapists) within the practice.

Primary Care
The first level of contact with the healthcare system — the everyday, community-based healthcare services that most people use most frequently and that manage the majority of health needs without referral to a specialist. Primary care in the UK includes GP practices, NHS dentists, community pharmacists, opticians, and community health services (district nurses, health visitors, allied health professionals). Primary care is where approximately 90% of all NHS patient contacts take place. It provides: acute care for new illnesses and injuries; chronic disease management (for long-term conditions such as diabetes, hypertension, and COPD); preventive care (vaccinations, screening, health promotion); mental health support; and the coordination of care across the system through referral and communication with secondary and tertiary services. NHS primary care has undergone significant structural change since 2019, with GP practices increasingly working in Primary Care Networks (PCNs) — groups of practices covering a defined population that collaborate to provide a wider range of services and extend their clinical workforce.

Secondary Care
The level of healthcare provided by medical specialists and hospital clinicians — accessed by patients who have been referred from primary care or who present as emergencies. Secondary care encompasses: outpatient clinics and specialist consultations; elective (planned) surgery and procedures; acute medical and surgical admissions; obstetrics and maternity services; paediatric care; accident and emergency services; and diagnostic services including radiology and pathology. Secondary care in England is mainly provided by NHS Trusts, Foundation Trusts, and — for some services — independent sector providers. The referral mechanism from primary to secondary care is a fundamental feature of the NHS's design: a patient with a new health concern will typically be seen by their GP, who will refer them to the appropriate secondary care service if the condition cannot be managed in primary care. This two-tier structure aims to ensure that hospital specialists focus on complex cases while GPs manage the large volume of healthcare needs that do not require specialist input.

Tertiary Care
Highly specialised healthcare services for complex, rare, or multi-system conditions — provided at regional or national specialist centres and accessed through referral from secondary care. Tertiary care encompasses: specialist surgery (neurosurgery, cardiac surgery, transplant surgery, complex orthopaedics); oncology and cancer treatment at specialist cancer centres; rare disease diagnosis and management; complex neonatal care; specialist mental health services; spinal injuries rehabilitation; and nationally commissioned highly specialised services for conditions so rare that they are commissioned at a national level by NHS England (for example, treatments for certain rare cancers or genetic conditions). Tertiary centres are typically large teaching hospitals affiliated with universities, combining clinical care, medical education, and research. Patients requiring tertiary care are referred by their secondary care specialist — a patient with a complex cardiac condition identified in their local hospital's cardiology department, for example, would be referred to a regional cardiothoracic centre for specialist assessment and treatment.

NHS Trust
An organisational unit within the NHS in England responsible for providing a defined range of healthcare services — established under the National Health Service and Community Care Act 1990 and governed by a board of directors. NHS Trusts are accountable to NHS England and to the Care Quality Commission (CQC) which inspects and regulates NHS providers. There are several types of NHS Trust: Acute Trusts (providing hospital services including emergency care, elective surgery, and outpatient clinics); Mental Health Trusts (providing inpatient and community mental health services); Community Trusts (providing district nursing, health visiting, community therapy services); Ambulance Trusts (providing emergency and patient transport services); and NHS Foundation Trusts, which have additional operational freedoms and are accountable to a broader group of governors including local people and staff. Most large NHS hospitals are now Foundation Trusts. NHS Trusts are not-for-profit public bodies — any financial surplus is reinvested in services and capital improvements rather than distributed to shareholders.

Clinical Pathway
A standardised, evidence-based plan describing the key steps in the care of a patient with a specific condition or undergoing a specific procedure — including the sequence of interventions, the professionals involved, the expected timelines, and the outcomes to be achieved at each stage. Clinical pathways (also called integrated care pathways or care bundles) are designed to reduce variation in clinical practice, improve the quality and safety of care, optimise the use of resources, and improve patient experience by making the care journey predictable and coordinated. An example: the NHS stroke pathway specifies that all suspected stroke patients must be given a brain scan within 24 hours of admission, be assessed by a specialist stroke team, and be admitted to a dedicated stroke unit — each step is defined, timed, and audited. Clinical pathways are developed by multidisciplinary teams based on clinical guidelines (such as those produced by NICE), and are regularly reviewed and updated as evidence evolves. In a healthcare professional context, understanding and using the relevant clinical pathway is a professional standard, not an option.

NHS Commissioning
The process by which NHS bodies plan, procure, and fund healthcare services on behalf of their local populations — deciding what services are needed, specifying the standards to which they should be delivered, and contracting with providers (NHS Trusts, Foundation Trusts, and independent sector providers) to deliver them. NHS commissioning in England has undergone several significant structural reforms. From 2013 to 2022, commissioning was primarily the responsibility of Clinical Commissioning Groups (CCGs) — GP-led bodies that held budgets for most NHS services in their local area. From 2022, CCGs were replaced by Integrated Care Boards (ICBs) — larger bodies covering Integrated Care System (ICS) areas, with broader responsibility for both commissioning and strategic planning. NHS England commissions some highly specialised services directly. Commissioning decisions are informed by population health data, evidence of clinical effectiveness, cost-effectiveness analyses from NICE, and local health needs assessments.

Integrated Care System
A partnership arrangement in the NHS in England in which NHS organisations, local authorities, the voluntary sector, and other health and care partners work together within a defined geographic area to plan and deliver health and care services for their local population. Integrated Care Systems (ICSs) were given formal legal status by the Health and Care Act 2022, and replaced Clinical Commissioning Groups as the primary planning and commissioning body. Each ICS covers a population of between 500,000 and three million people, and is governed by an Integrated Care Board (ICB) — responsible for NHS commissioning functions — and an Integrated Care Partnership (ICP), which brings together a broader range of partners including the local authority, the voluntary sector, and public health bodies to develop a joint health and care strategy. ICSs are designed to address the fragmentation that has historically made it difficult to deliver coordinated care for people with complex needs — particularly older people with multiple long-term conditions who require input from both health and social care.

NICE
The National Institute for Health and Care Excellence — an independent arm's-length body of the Department of Health and Social Care that produces evidence-based guidance and quality standards for health and social care in England. NICE was established in 1999 and has become one of the most influential health technology assessment bodies in the world. NICE guidance covers: clinical practice guidelines (recommendations on the treatment of specific conditions, based on systematic reviews of clinical evidence); technology appraisals (assessments of whether new drugs, medical devices, and other technologies represent value for money and should be funded by the NHS); diagnostic guidance; interventional procedures guidance; and public health guidance. When NICE approves a drug or treatment through a positive technology appraisal, the NHS is legally required to fund it within 90 days. NICE uses the QALY (Quality-Adjusted Life Year) as its primary measure of health benefit — typically approving treatments that cost less than £20,000 to £30,000 per QALY gained.

Referral
The process by which a healthcare professional — most commonly a GP — requests that a patient be seen or assessed by another healthcare professional or service, typically at a higher level of care or with a different area of expertise. In the NHS, the GP-to-secondary-care referral is the standard pathway for accessing most specialist hospital services. Referrals can be: urgent (for patients with symptoms that may indicate serious conditions such as suspected cancer — subject to the 'two-week wait' rule, which requires the patient to be seen within 14 days); routine (for non-urgent conditions, subject to the 18-week Referral to Treatment standard); and 'Choose and Book' or 'NHS e-Referral Service' referrals, where patients are given a choice of hospital and appointment date. GPs may also refer patients within primary care (for example, to a community physiotherapist or mental health practitioner) or to social services. A well-written referral letter communicating the relevant clinical information, the reason for referral, and any specific concerns is a critical clinical document.

Waiting List
The queue of patients who have been referred for NHS treatment and are waiting to receive it — a central operational and political issue in NHS management. NHS targets specify maximum waiting times for different types of care: the 18-week Referral to Treatment (RTT) standard requires that patients do not wait more than 18 weeks from GP referral to the start of their treatment; the 2-week wait (2WW) rule requires urgent cancer referrals to be seen within 14 days; the 4-hour emergency department standard requires 95% of patients to be assessed, treated, and either admitted or discharged within 4 hours. Waiting times have been a source of significant political pressure and public concern in recent years, with the COVID-19 pandemic causing a major backlog. As of 2024, the NHS waiting list in England exceeded 7 million patients. Reducing the waiting list is a declared priority of the government and NHS leadership, with strategies including increasing activity at evenings and weekends, expanding the use of independent sector providers, and developing new ways of managing less complex cases in community settings.

Mental Health Services
The range of NHS and social care services that assess, treat, and support people with mental health conditions — including anxiety, depression, psychosis, eating disorders, substance misuse, and personality disorders. Mental health services in England are provided primarily by NHS Mental Health Trusts (or mental health divisions within combined trusts), community mental health teams (CMHTs), Improving Access to Psychological Therapies (IAPT) services (now rebranded as NHS Talking Therapies), and inpatient units. Mental health parity of esteem — the principle that mental health should be given equal priority to physical health in NHS funding and service development — has been a stated policy commitment since 2012, though gaps in access, quality, and funding compared to physical health services remain. The NHS Long Term Plan (2019) made significant commitments to expanding mental health services, including: improving access to talking therapies; expanding community crisis services to reduce inappropriate hospital admissions; and developing specialist perinatal mental health teams.

Community Health Services
A broad range of NHS-funded health services delivered outside of hospital settings — in people's homes, GP practices, care homes, community clinics, and schools. Community health services play an essential role in enabling people to live independently, manage long-term conditions, and avoid unnecessary hospital admission. Services typically included within community health include: district nursing (nursing care provided to housebound or community-dwelling patients); health visiting (support for families with children under five); community physiotherapy, occupational therapy, and speech and language therapy; community podiatry; specialist community palliative care; school health nursing; and community mental health services. Community health services are increasingly seen as a critical lever for reducing pressure on acute hospitals — by managing more complex patients safely in the community rather than in hospital beds. They are typically provided by NHS Community Trusts or as part of larger combined Trusts, and are commissioned by Integrated Care Boards.

🔬
Foundation Trust
An NHS Trust that has been granted additional operational autonomy and freedoms by NHS England in recognition of its good governance and financial performance — introduced by the Health and Social Care (Community Health and Standards) Act 2003 and operating as a unique corporate form known as a 'public benefit corporation'. Foundation Trusts differ from standard NHS Trusts in several important ways: they have an elected Council of Governors drawn from their local community, patients, and staff — giving local people a formal stake in the organisation's governance; they can borrow money commercially without government approval (within limits); they can retain and reinvest financial surpluses; and they are regulated primarily by NHS England (formerly NHS Improvement / Monitor) rather than by the Secretary of State. The intention behind Foundation Trust status was to create a more autonomous and locally responsive NHS — critics argued that it risked fragmentation and inconsistency; proponents argued it incentivised good performance. Most large acute NHS hospitals are now Foundation Trusts.

CQC
Care Quality Commission — the independent regulator of health and social care in England, responsible for inspecting, rating, and where necessary taking enforcement action against NHS providers, independent healthcare providers, dental practices, care homes, and other registered providers. The CQC was established by the Health and Social Care Act 2008 and rates services against five key questions: Are they Safe? Are they Effective? Are they Caring? Are they Responsive? Are they Well-Led? Each provider and each service is rated as Outstanding, Good, Requires Improvement, or Inadequate. CQC inspection reports are publicly available and are a primary source of public information about the quality of specific NHS services and care providers. A CQC rating of Inadequate can trigger enforcement action including the imposition of conditions on registration, suspension, or in the most serious cases cancellation of a provider's CQC registration. CQC inspections are conducted by teams of inspectors — typically including clinical specialists, lay members, and people with lived experience of using health or care services.

Multidisciplinary Team
A group of healthcare professionals from different disciplines working together to provide comprehensive, coordinated care for a patient — typically including doctors, nurses, allied health professionals, pharmacists, social workers, and other relevant specialists. MDTs (multidisciplinary teams) are a core feature of modern NHS clinical practice: the NHS Cancer Plan (2000) made it a requirement that all cancer patients be discussed and managed through an MDT meeting; similar requirements apply in stroke, cardiac, and paediatric services. An MDT meeting (MDT meeting or MDT review) typically involves all members of the team reviewing the clinical information about a group of patients and jointly agreeing the optimal management plan. MDT working improves patient outcomes by ensuring that complex cases benefit from multiple professional perspectives, reduces the risk of important information being missed or siloed within a single speciality, and provides a forum for education and professional development. For an international healthcare professional joining the NHS, participating effectively in MDT meetings — understanding the format, the professional roles, and the decision-making process — is an early professional priority.

Patient Pathway
The sequence of stages, interactions, and services that a patient experiences as they move through the healthcare system from the point of first contact to the completion of treatment and follow-up — encompassing primary care, secondary care, tertiary care, and community services as relevant to their condition. Understanding the patient pathway for common conditions is essential for healthcare professionals working in any part of the system: a consultant cardiologist who understands the full pathway from GP referral to discharge and community follow-up can design their service to reduce unnecessary delays, prevent avoidable readmissions, and improve the overall patient experience. NHS patient pathways are typically mapped by clinical teams and service managers as part of service improvement work, and are aligned with NICE guidelines and NHS clinical standards. The concept of the 'patient pathway' is also used in quality improvement (QI) work — identifying bottlenecks, delays, and quality failures at specific stages of the pathway and testing interventions to address them.

Health Inequality
Systematic, avoidable, and unjust differences in health status and access to healthcare between different population groups — defined by factors including socioeconomic position, ethnicity, geography, age, disability, gender, and sexual orientation. Health inequalities are a longstanding challenge in the UK: life expectancy varies by more than ten years between the most and least deprived areas; people from Black and minority ethnic backgrounds face structural barriers to healthcare access and have poorer outcomes in several clinical areas (including maternal mortality and diabetes management); rural populations often have reduced access to specialist services; and people with learning disabilities have significantly lower life expectancy than the general population. Addressing health inequalities is a legal duty under the Health and Social Care Act 2012 (placing a duty on NHS England and commissioners to consider health inequality in all their decisions) and a stated priority of NHS and government health policy. The Marmot Review (2010) and its update (2020) are the most cited analyses of health inequalities in England.

Digital Health
The use of digital technologies — including electronic health records (EHRs), telemedicine and video consultations, mobile health apps, artificial intelligence, wearable devices, and genomics — to improve health outcomes, enhance the patient experience, and increase the efficiency of health services. Digital health has become a central policy priority in the NHS, with the NHS Long Term Plan setting ambitions for all patients to have access to their health records, to be able to communicate with their GP practice digitally, and to receive care from a fully digitised NHS. NHS Digital (now merged into NHS England) has managed the national digital infrastructure, including the NHS App, NHS login, and the GP IT infrastructure. Electronic Prescribing and Medicines Administration (ePMA) systems, electronic patient record systems (such as Epic, which has been adopted by several large NHS trusts), and AI-based diagnostic tools (in radiology, pathology, and ophthalmology) are transforming clinical workflows. For international healthcare professionals joining the NHS, digital literacy — including familiarity with NHS IT systems and digital communication tools — is an increasingly important professional competency.

Prescribing
The legal authorisation to supply medicines to patients — a regulated activity in the UK in which prescribers must hold an appropriate qualification and operate within their competency. NHS prescribing is a significant area of clinical practice: GPs are the largest prescribers in the NHS, but prescribing rights have been progressively extended to other healthcare professionals. Non-medical prescribers include: independent prescribers (nurses, pharmacists, physiotherapists, paramedics, and others who have completed an approved prescribing qualification and can prescribe any licensed medicine within their competency); and supplementary prescribers (who prescribe within an agreed clinical management plan for a specific patient). The British National Formulary (BNF) is the key reference resource for NHS prescribing — providing information on drug indications, dosages, contraindications, interactions, and costs. NHS prescribing is subject to clinical governance requirements, formulary restrictions (which specify the drugs available in a local NHS area), and prescribing budgets. Appropriate, safe, and cost-effective prescribing is a professional and legal obligation for all NHS prescribers.

Text 1: The Structure of the NHS — Principles, Funding, and the Three-Tier Model
How the National Health Service is organised, funded, and delivered across primary, secondary, and tertiary care

The National Health Service is built on a principle that was radical in 1948 and remains distinctive in global terms today: that healthcare should be available to everyone, based on clinical need rather than the ability to pay, and funded through general taxation rather than private insurance. When Aneurin Bevan launched the NHS on 5 July 1948, he described it as 'the most civilised step any country has ever taken'. More than seventy years later, surveys consistently show the NHS to be one of the most valued public institutions in the UK — even as debates about its funding, structure, and performance generate persistent political controversy. For any healthcare professional working in the UK, understanding these founding principles — and the tensions they create in a modern system under significant financial and demographic pressure — is essential professional context.

The NHS is structured around three tiers of care, each with a defined role and a defined relationship with the others. Primary care — provided by GPs, community pharmacists, dentists, opticians, and a growing range of other professionals working within Primary Care Networks — is where the majority of patient contact occurs and where most health needs are managed without referral. Secondary care — provided by NHS Trusts and Foundation Trusts — handles the more complex cases that cannot be managed in primary care, including emergency presentations and planned specialist treatment. Tertiary care — provided at regional and national specialist centres — addresses rare and highly complex conditions requiring technology, expertise, or infrastructure that is not available at the secondary level. This three-tier model is not merely administrative: it is a system of deliberate rationing designed to ensure that the most expensive and scarce resources are directed to the patients who genuinely need them.

Funding flows through the NHS via a commissioning model: NHS England allocates funding to Integrated Care Boards (ICBs), which commission services from providers — NHS Trusts, independent sector hospitals, and community providers — on behalf of their local populations. This purchaser-provider split was introduced in 1991 and has been maintained (in various forms) through successive government reforms, based on the theory that competitive commissioning produces better quality and value than direct management. In practice, most NHS care is commissioned from NHS providers on long-term block contracts rather than through genuine market competition. The tension between the administrative logic of commissioning and the operational reality of an integrated system is one of the defining debates in NHS management — and understanding it is essential for any professional seeking to influence how NHS services are planned and funded.

Text 2: Private Healthcare and the Independent Sector — The Other Side of UK Healthcare
How private healthcare works in the UK, how it interacts with the NHS, and what healthcare professionals need to know

The UK has a dual healthcare system: the NHS provides universal, tax-funded care on one track, while a significant private healthcare sector operates in parallel. Approximately 10–12% of the UK population holds private medical insurance, typically provided by employers as a workplace benefit. Private healthcare is also accessed on a self-pay basis — patients paying out of pocket for consultations, diagnostics, or procedures without using insurance. The private sector in the UK is not a single entity: it includes large corporate hospital groups (such as BUPA, Spire, and Nuffield Health), independent clinics, private GP services, and a complex web of fee-for-service practitioners operating independently or through partnership models. Understanding the private sector is relevant for most NHS healthcare professionals because the boundaries between NHS and private care are more permeable than many assume.

The most significant intersection between the NHS and the private sector is the NHS's use of independent sector providers (ISPs) to reduce waiting lists. Under NHS commissioning arrangements, NHS commissioners can contract with private hospitals to provide NHS-funded care — allowing NHS patients on waiting lists to be treated in private facilities at NHS cost. This arrangement was significantly expanded during the COVID-19 pandemic recovery and has become a structural feature of NHS capacity planning. A patient treated in an independent sector hospital under an NHS contract receives care that is free at the point of use and is managed under NHS clinical governance standards — from the patient's perspective, the experience may be indistinguishable from a private consultation, but the funding and accountability is NHS.

NHS consultants have traditionally been permitted to undertake private practice alongside their NHS employment — typically under the terms of their NHS consultant contract which allows a defined proportion of their time to be devoted to private work. This 'dual practice' arrangement has been both a valued professional freedom (allowing NHS consultants to supplement their NHS salaries and maintain professional relationships with the private sector) and a source of tension (critics argue that private practice diverts NHS consultant time and attention from NHS patients). The NHS is the dominant healthcare provider in the UK, and the private sector's ability to function depends in part on the supply of NHS-trained professionals, NHS-developed technologies, and NHS-generated clinical evidence. Most healthcare professionals working in the UK will have patient interactions that span both sectors at different points in their careers — understanding the governance, accountability, and ethical dimensions of working across both systems is an important professional competency.

BRIEFING NOTE
To: International Medical Delegation
From: [Name], Health Manager, Ashgrove NHS Foundation Trust
Subject: Overview of the UK National Health Service

The National Health Service, established in 1948, is built on three founding principles: universal coverage, comprehensive services, and care free at the point of use — funded through general taxation rather than private insurance or user charges.

The NHS is structured around three tiers of care. Primary care — delivered by general practitioners, community pharmacists, dentists, and allied health professionals working within Primary Care Networks — is the first point of contact and manages approximately 90% of all patient interactions. Secondary care, provided by NHS Trusts and Foundation Trusts, handles specialist and hospital services accessed through GP referral or emergency presentation. Tertiary care delivers highly specialised services for rare and complex conditions at regional and national centres, accessed through secondary care referral.

Funding flows via a commissioning model: NHS England allocates resources to Integrated Care Boards (ICBs), which commission services from NHS and independent sector providers on behalf of their local populations.

A defining feature of the NHS is the GP gatekeeper role — patients cannot generally access specialist secondary care without a GP referral, ensuring that specialist capacity is directed appropriately.

The NHS currently faces its most significant operational challenge in a generation: a waiting list exceeding seven million patients in England, driven in large part by the COVID-19 pandemic's impact on elective care. Reducing this backlog while managing growing demographic demand is the central operational priority.

We welcome the delegation's questions at our briefing session.

Dear Marcus,

Welcome to the department — I wanted to give you a brief overview of how patients reach us, as this differs meaningfully from many other health systems.

Almost all of your outpatient list will consist of patients referred by their GP — the general practitioner acts as the gatekeeper to secondary care in the NHS. GPs cannot directly refer patients to investigations or procedures in most cases; they refer the patient to us, and we decide on the diagnostic and management plan from there.

The GP referral letter is a key clinical document — it tells you what the GP has done, their working diagnosis, and what they are asking you to address. Please read it carefully before each clinic.

Referrals are made electronically through the NHS e-Referral Service. Patients are given a choice of hospitals and appointment slots. The NHS standard for routine referrals is that patients should be seen within 18 weeks of referral. For urgent referrals — for example, suspected cancer — the target is two weeks.

Unlike many systems, patients here cannot self-refer to a specialist — all outpatient access is via GP referral. This is worth explaining to patients who may find this unfamiliar.

Do come and find me if you have questions.

Best regards,
[Clinical Director]

3. Commissioning and Finance Discussion
Healthcare manager Priya Sharma discusses NHS commissioning and the ICS model with finance director Tom Davies
📄 Show / Hide Transcript
Tom: Priya — you've been working with the ICB a lot recently. Can you help me understand what the shift from CCGs to ICBs has actually changed in practice?
Priya: It's a good question and the honest answer is: less than the architects intended, and more than clinicians expected. Structurally, the CCGs — which were GP-led commissioning bodies — have been replaced by Integrated Care Boards, which have a broader governance base. They have to include NHS Trusts, mental health providers, community providers, and local authorities in their leadership, not just GPs.
Tom: Does that change how our money flows?
Priya: In terms of the mechanics of contract payments — not much. We're still paid on block contracts for most of our activity. What has changed is the planning horizon. ICBs are expected to think about population health across the whole system — not just buy and sell individual services. The idea is that instead of the Trust and the commissioner sitting across the table negotiating contract value, we're meant to be sitting together asking: what does this population need, and how do we jointly design services that meet that need?
Tom: Sounds good in theory. What's your honest view of how it works in practice?
Priya: Mixed. The relationships are better in some cases — I think there's genuinely more collaboration between primary care, community services, and secondary care in planning discussions. But the financial pressures haven't changed — the Trust still needs to hit its control total, and the ICB still needs to balance its budget. When money is tight, collaboration becomes harder. The aspiration is there — the structures are better aligned — but the system is underfunded for the ambition it's trying to achieve.
Tom: What about NICE — do NICE decisions still create funding obligations for us directly?
Priya: Absolutely. That hasn't changed with the ICS reforms. If NICE produces a positive technology appraisal — a new drug, a new device — the NHS has a legal obligation to fund it within 90 days for qualifying patients. That obligation sits with us as a provider, regardless of what the ICB's overall budget position is. It's one of the areas where the system creates a structural tension: NICE approves treatments without reference to the Trust's specific financial situation, and we have to fund them regardless.

Case Study 1: A New International Consultant's First Six Months in the NHS — Navigating the System
How one doctor's experience highlights the key knowledge gaps and learning curves for international medical professionals joining the NHS
This case study is drawn from a representative composite of experiences documented in NHS international recruitment literature and the British Medical Association's guidance for internationally trained doctors.

Dr Aisha Mohammed arrived in the UK from Nigeria in June 2022 to take up a consultant neurologist post at a large NHS Foundation Trust in the Midlands. She had fifteen years of specialist experience and had been trained at leading institutions in Nigeria and the United States. Her clinical skills were not in question — her appointment had been made on the basis of a competitive international selection process.

Her first six months, however, were more challenging than anticipated — not because of clinical complexity but because of system unfamiliarity. Three specific issues stood out.

First, the referral and clinical pathway culture. In her previous settings, patients with neurological symptoms were often assessed directly in outpatient neurology without a GP letter. In the NHS, virtually all her outpatient referrals came from GPs, and the GP referral letter was the foundation of her pre-clinic preparation. She initially found the letters variable in quality — some were detailed and informative, others minimal — and it took several months to develop a system for triaging and responding to referrals in a way that met both her clinical needs and NHS waiting list targets.

Second, MDT culture. Her previous experience with multidisciplinary working had been informal and consultant-led. NHS MDT meetings — particularly the neuroradiology and neurosurgery MDTs — were structured, formally chaired, and expected every participant to prepare and contribute. She described initially feeling 'on the back foot' in meetings where colleagues presented cases with a level of familiarity with each other and with NHS administrative processes that she had not yet developed.

Third, NICE guidelines. In her US training, evidence-based guidelines from multiple sources (AAN, NICE, and others) informed but did not bind clinical decisions. In the NHS, NICE guidelines are the default clinical standard — deviation requires documentation. This was a significant adjustment in documentation practice.

By month six, Dr Mohammed described her NHS experience as rewarding and collegial — and noted that the support she had received from her clinical director, the Trust's international recruitment team, and an NHS buddy system had been essential to her successful transition. She had also joined the NHS GMC register (she had been working under a temporary licence while the registration was processed), completed mandatory training, and was beginning to engage with quality improvement projects within her department.

The case illustrates that international recruitment into the NHS is not simply a matter of clinical credentialing — it requires a structured programme of NHS system orientation, covering the referral pathway, commissioning context, MDT culture, and NICE guidelines, if internationally trained professionals are to integrate effectively and rapidly.

Case Study 2: Commissioning a Service — How an ICB and NHS Trust Redesigned a Cardiology Pathway
How collaborative commissioning between an ICB and an NHS provider transformed a high-demand, low-efficiency outpatient service
This case study is based on a representative composite of NHS service redesign projects documented in NHS England's NHS RightCare and Getting It Right First Time (GIRFT) programmes.

In 2022, an Integrated Care Board in the south of England identified that cardiology outpatient waiting times in its area significantly exceeded the 18-week Referral to Treatment standard, with some patients waiting over 30 weeks for a first outpatient appointment. A joint review by the ICB and the NHS Foundation Trust providing cardiology services identified the root cause: a high volume of GP referrals for undifferentiated chest pain and palpitations, many of which could be managed in primary care without specialist input, was consuming a significant proportion of cardiology outpatient capacity.

The solution was a redesigned clinical pathway. The ICB and the Trust jointly developed a 'Cardiac First Access' service — a community-based diagnostic hub staffed by clinical nurse specialists and advanced cardiac physiologists, supported by a consultant cardiologist who reviewed cases remotely and triaged them to the appropriate level of care. GPs referring patients with chest pain or palpitations were asked to use a standardised referral form specifying the clinical information required for triage. Patients who needed a specialist would be directed to the cardiology outpatient clinic — those who needed diagnostics only (an ECG, a 24-hour tape, an echocardiogram) could have them arranged directly from the community hub without a hospital visit.

Within twelve months, cardiology outpatient referral volumes from primary care had fallen by 22%, waiting times for new outpatient appointments had reduced from an average of 28 weeks to 14 weeks, and patient satisfaction scores had improved significantly — patients who had previously faced a 28-week wait for a consultant appointment they subsequently did not need were now seen and managed quickly in a community setting that was closer to home and easier to access.

The case illustrates several key NHS system principles: that the clinical pathway, not the individual encounter, is the unit of improvement; that ICB commissioning and NHS Trust provision are most effective when designed collaboratively rather than negotiated adversarially; and that moving care closer to home — the principle underpinning much of current NHS strategy — requires investment in community capacity and clinical skill as well as changes in referral behaviour.
