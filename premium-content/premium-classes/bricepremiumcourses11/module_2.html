Outpatient Appointment
A scheduled clinical consultation that takes place in a hospital or specialist clinic setting — where the patient attends for assessment, review, or treatment but is not admitted overnight. Outpatient appointments are the primary mechanism through which NHS patients access secondary care: a patient referred by their GP receives an outpatient appointment with the relevant specialist, who then assesses their condition and determines the appropriate management plan, which may include further investigation, treatment, or planned admission. Outpatient departments (OPDs) are one of the highest-volume clinical settings in the NHS — a large acute Trust may manage hundreds of outpatient clinics each week across dozens of specialties. The efficiency, quality, and patient experience of outpatient services is a significant driver of overall NHS performance and patient satisfaction. Key performance measures for outpatient services include: the proportion of patients seen within the 18-week Referral to Treatment standard; the DNA (Did Not Attend) rate; clinic utilisation (the proportion of available appointment slots that are used); and new-to-follow-up ratios (the balance between first appointments and return visits).

New Patient Appointment
A first clinical consultation between a patient and a specialist or service — typically triggered by a GP referral and representing the patient's initial access to secondary care for a specific condition or concern. New patient appointments are typically longer than follow-up appointments (often 30–60 minutes compared to 10–20 minutes for a follow-up) because the clinician needs to take a full history, review the referring information, conduct a physical examination, order any initial investigations, and agree a management plan with the patient. In NHS outpatient planning, 'new patient' capacity is a critical resource: the volume of new patients that a clinic can accommodate determines how quickly referred patients can be seen, and therefore directly affects NHS waiting times. NHS operational frameworks typically specify target ratios of new-to-follow-up appointments for each specialty, aiming to balance access for new patients against the ongoing review needs of existing patients under active management.

Follow-Up Appointment
A scheduled return visit for a patient who has previously been seen by the same service — to review the results of investigations, assess the patient's response to treatment, modify the management plan, or decide whether ongoing specialist input is still required. Follow-up appointments are typically shorter than new patient appointments (10–20 minutes in most specialties) and are conducted with the assumption that the clinician has familiarity with the patient's history. NHS clinicians and services are encouraged to critically review the follow-up burden on their outpatient services — a large proportion of NHS outpatient activity consists of follow-up appointments, many of which could be conducted by telephone, replaced by open-access arrangements (where patients can refer themselves back if needed), or discharged back to primary care when active specialist management is no longer required. Reducing unnecessary follow-up is a significant lever for improving new patient access and reducing the overall outpatient waiting list.

DNA
Did Not Attend — a term used when a patient fails to arrive for a scheduled appointment without having contacted the service to cancel or reschedule in advance. DNAs represent a significant cost to the NHS: a DNA appointment wastes the clinical time of the specialist and the administrative time of the scheduling team, and the slot cannot typically be reallocated to another patient at short notice. The NHS estimates that DNAs cost over £1 billion per year across the system. NHS Trusts typically have formal DNA policies that specify: at what point a patient is formally recorded as a DNA; whether a patient who DNAs once, twice, or three times is discharged back to their GP; what communication should be sent to the patient following a DNA; and how the GP is informed. Evidence suggests that appointment reminder systems (text messages, telephone calls, or letters sent in advance of the appointment) significantly reduce DNA rates — typically by 30–50% depending on the patient population and the frequency and format of the reminder.

Triage
The systematic process of assessing and prioritising patients — or, in an appointment scheduling context, assessing and categorising referrals — based on the urgency and clinical priority of their condition, to ensure that those who need care most urgently are seen first. In appointment scheduling, triage is applied when a new referral arrives: a clinician or trained administrator reviews the referral information and determines the appropriate urgency category (routine, soon, urgent, or emergency, with definitions varying by specialty and Trust). Clinical triage of referrals allows the scheduling team to allocate slots appropriately — a patient with a two-week wait cancer referral must be offered an appointment within 14 days; a patient with a routine cardiology referral would be offered the next available routine appointment. Triage decisions require clinical knowledge and professional judgement — in many services, referral triage is conducted by a senior clinician (often a consultant or specialist nurse), with the triage outcome documented and communicated to the patient and their GP.

Clinic List
The scheduled list of patients booked to attend a specific clinic session — typically managed by the consultant's secretary or the outpatient booking team, and provided to the clinical team before the clinic begins. A clinic list specifies: the patient's name, NHS number, and date of birth; the appointment time and appointment type (new or follow-up); the reason for attendance or the referral diagnosis; any specific requirements (interpreter, access needs, investigation results that should be available); and often a flag for any administrative actions required (results to chase, letters outstanding). The clinic list is the operational document that governs the day's clinical activity: a complete, accurate, and timely clinic list allows the clinical team to prepare effectively for each patient, reducing delays and improving the quality of the consultation. Managing the clinic list — including dealing with late additions, last-minute cancellations, and urgent walk-ins — is a core competency of NHS medical secretaries and outpatient coordinators.

Appointment Reminder
A communication sent to a patient in advance of their scheduled appointment — via text message, telephone call, letter, or digital notification through the NHS App — to remind them of the date, time, and location of their appointment and to give them an opportunity to confirm attendance or to cancel and reschedule if they cannot attend. Appointment reminders are one of the most evidence-based interventions available to NHS services for reducing DNA rates: systematic reviews consistently show that text message reminders sent 2–7 days before an appointment reduce DNAs by 20–50%, with the greatest effect in populations with historically high DNA rates. The NHS App has become an increasingly important channel for appointment reminders and management — patients can confirm, cancel, or reschedule appointments digitally, and services can send reminders directly through the app. Effective reminder content specifies the appointment details clearly (date, time, location, name of the clinician), provides clear instructions for how to cancel or reschedule, and includes any specific preparation instructions (fasting, bringing medications, wearing specific clothing).

🕐
Slot
A defined unit of appointment time within a clinic session — the basic building block of outpatient scheduling. Appointment slots are created by the clinic template — a pre-defined schedule specifying the total number of slots in each session, the duration of each slot, the appointment types available (new patient or follow-up, or both), and the time intervals between appointments. Slot durations vary by specialty and appointment type: a GP appointment slot is typically 10 minutes; an outpatient new patient appointment may be 20–60 minutes; a complex MDT clinic may allocate 60–90 minutes per patient. NHS outpatient booking systems (such as Bluespier, Meditech, or ICE) allow the booking team to view available slots in real time, allocate them to incoming referrals, and manage cancellations and reallocations. Effective slot management — ensuring slots are filled efficiently while maintaining appropriate flexibility for urgent additions — is a significant operational challenge in high-volume outpatient services.

Telephone Consultation
A clinical consultation conducted by telephone — replacing a face-to-face appointment for cases where a physical examination is not required or where the patient's circumstances make travel difficult. Telephone consultations have grown significantly in the NHS since the COVID-19 pandemic: during the pandemic, the majority of GP consultations and a significant proportion of outpatient reviews were converted to telephone or video format. NHS England guidance following the pandemic endorsed a 'mixed model' of face-to-face, telephone, and video consultations — recognising that remote consultations offer genuine benefits (convenience for patients who can travel only with difficulty, speed of access, efficiency for routine reviews) while also acknowledging their limitations (inability to conduct a physical examination, digital exclusion for some patient groups, reduced quality of communication for some clinical situations). Telephone consultations should be scheduled with the same discipline as face-to-face appointments: a defined time, a confirmed telephone number, and a note in the patient record of what is to be discussed.

Clinic Template
The pre-defined schedule structure for a clinic session — specifying the number of available slots, their duration and type (new patient, follow-up, or both), the start and end time of the session, and any special allocation rules (for example, a first slot reserved for a complex patient, or a fixed break for documentation). Clinic templates are the architecture of outpatient scheduling: they determine how many patients a clinic can see, in what configuration, and with what balance of appointment types. Managing clinic templates requires balancing multiple competing demands: seeing enough new patients to meet referral demand and waiting time targets; allocating sufficient time for follow-up patients under active management; allowing time for urgent additions; and maintaining a realistic schedule that does not regularly overrun. Poor clinic template management — too many patients scheduled into too little time, or too many slots left empty through poor demand forecasting — is a common source of both operational inefficiency and poor patient experience.

Waiting Time
The period between a defined trigger point and the patient receiving the care they have been referred or waiting for — one of the most clinically, operationally, and politically significant metrics in NHS management. NHS waiting time standards include: the 18-week Referral to Treatment (RTT) standard — patients should not wait more than 18 weeks from GP referral to the start of treatment; the 2-week wait (2WW) cancer standard — patients with an urgent cancer referral must be offered an appointment within 14 days; and the 4-hour emergency department standard. From the patient's perspective, waiting time encompasses not only the administrative measurement but the lived experience of living with an undiagnosed or untreated condition while waiting — a dimension that clinical and operational staff should be actively mindful of when communicating about delays. Explaining waiting times to anxious patients requires honesty, empathy, and the provision of useful information about what will happen next, rather than vague reassurance.

Appointment Letter
A formal written communication sent to a patient confirming the details of their scheduled appointment — the standard mechanism by which NHS hospitals communicate appointment information to patients. An NHS appointment letter typically contains: the patient's full name and address; the date and time of the appointment; the location (hospital name, department, floor or room number, and how to find it); the name of the clinician they will be seeing (where known); the reason for the appointment (typically the specialty and a broad description, not the detailed diagnosis); any preparation instructions (fasting, bringing medications, wearing specific clothing); contact details for cancellation or rescheduling; parking and transport information; and information about accessibility (how to request an interpreter, wheelchair access details). The clarity and quality of the appointment letter significantly affects whether patients arrive at the correct location, at the correct time, and adequately prepared — a confusing or incomplete appointment letter is a preventable cause of DNAs and poor patient experience.

Rescheduling
The process of changing a patient's appointment from one date, time, or location to another — initiated either by the patient (who cannot attend their original appointment) or by the service (because of clinic cancellation, staffing change, or operational need). Rescheduling from the patient's side is a routine part of appointment management: NHS services should make it as easy as possible for patients to reschedule rather than simply failing to attend (DNA), both because the rescheduled appointment ultimately benefits the patient and because an early notification of non-attendance allows the slot to be reallocated to another patient. Rescheduling by the service — for example, when a consultant's clinic is cancelled due to leave — requires timely and clear communication to affected patients, an apology for the inconvenience, and a new appointment offered promptly. Repeated or late-notice service-initiated rescheduling creates negative patient experience, erodes trust, and can contribute to patients opting for private care.

Urgent Referral
A referral from a GP or other healthcare professional that indicates the patient requires an appointment significantly sooner than the routine waiting time — because of clinical urgency, the risk of rapid deterioration, or a suspicion of a serious underlying condition. In the NHS, urgent referrals include: the two-week wait (2WW) cancer referral — for patients with symptoms that may indicate cancer, requiring an appointment within 14 days; 'urgent' outpatient referrals — for patients whose condition warrants priority but not the two-week urgency of a cancer pathway; and same-day urgent referrals — for patients whose condition requires assessment on the day of referral, typically managed through acute assessment units or emergency departments. Scheduling urgent referrals requires a specifically reserved capacity — urgent slots that are protected from routine booking and kept available for same-day or short-notice allocation. Managing the balance between protected urgent capacity and routine booking is an important aspect of outpatient scheduling governance.

Clinic Utilisation
The proportion of available appointment slots in a clinic session that are used — typically expressed as a percentage. A clinic that has 20 available slots and sees 16 patients has a utilisation rate of 80%. Clinic utilisation is a key operational metric for outpatient services: very low utilisation (below 70–75%) suggests that booked appointments are not being converted into attendances (due to DNAs or late cancellations) or that clinic templates are not calibrated to realistic demand. Very high utilisation (above 95%) suggests there is no capacity buffer for urgent additions, running late, or complex patients who take longer than their allocated slot. NHS operational guidance typically targets utilisation in the range of 85–92%, maintaining efficiency while preserving the clinical flexibility needed for safe and high-quality care. Clinic utilisation data is used by department managers, medical directors, and the outpatient booking team to identify clinics that are underperforming and to make evidence-based decisions about capacity changes.

Pre-Assessment
A clinical review conducted before a planned procedure or operation — designed to ensure that the patient is medically fit and prepared for the intervention, that all necessary investigations have been completed, that the patient has given informed consent, and that any anaesthetic or procedural risks have been identified and mitigated. Pre-assessment appointments are standard practice before most NHS elective procedures and are typically conducted by a specialist nurse or anaesthetic practitioner in a dedicated pre-assessment clinic. The pre-assessment typically includes: a clinical history and examination; review of relevant investigations (blood tests, ECG, imaging); medication reconciliation; allergy documentation; consent; and patient education (what to bring, fasting instructions, post-procedure care). Patients who fail to attend their pre-assessment appointment are typically unable to proceed with their planned procedure — a significant clinical risk and a waste of the procedure time that has been allocated. Pre-assessment DNA rates are monitored separately from outpatient DNA rates because of their direct impact on theatre utilisation.

Referral Triage
The systematic review and categorisation of incoming referrals by a clinician or trained clinical administrator — to determine the appropriate urgency level, the most suitable clinic or service, and any additional information required before an appointment can be offered. Referral triage is a critical function in high-volume secondary care services: without effective triage, urgent referrals may wait in a queue with routine ones; referrals that could be managed in primary care may consume secondary care capacity unnecessarily; and referrals may be allocated to the wrong specialist or service. Triage outcomes include: 'accept for urgent appointment' (within 2 weeks); 'accept for soon appointment' (within 4–6 weeks); 'accept for routine appointment' (next available slot within the 18-week standard); 'return to referrer' (the referral does not meet the service's criteria, or the patient could be managed more appropriately in primary care, with advice sent to the GP); and 'advice only' (where the triage clinician can provide guidance to the GP without a formal appointment being needed).

NHS e-Referral Service
The electronic referral system used in England through which GPs and other primary care professionals refer patients to secondary care outpatient services — replacing the previous Choose and Book system and enabling patients to choose their provider and appointment directly from the GP's system or online. The NHS e-Referral Service (e-RS) connects GPs to the appointment booking systems of secondary care providers, showing patients the available appointment slots at their chosen hospital and allowing them to book, cancel, or rebook online or by telephone. From the GP's perspective, e-RS allows the referral to be sent with supporting clinical information electronically, avoiding delays associated with paper referral letters. From the patient's perspective, e-RS provides choice and some control over their appointment timing. From the hospital's perspective, e-RS provides a standardised incoming referral pathway, allows referral triage to be conducted electronically, and integrates with the 18-week RTT clock management function. Not all NHS services are available on e-RS — emergency, maternity, mental health, and some highly specialised services use different referral mechanisms.

Patient Choice
The NHS policy giving patients the right to choose where they receive their NHS treatment — typically at the point of GP referral, where patients can select from a list of providers (NHS Trusts, Foundation Trusts, and approved independent sector providers) who offer the service they need. Patient choice in outpatient referrals is implemented through the NHS e-Referral Service, which presents patients with the available providers and their waiting times, allowing the patient to balance factors including proximity, waiting time, and any known reputation. NHS patient choice also extends to choice of GP practice, choice of hospital for elective surgery in some circumstances, and some choices within mental health services. Choice is not unlimited — patients can choose from approved NHS providers and some independent sector providers who have contracts with the ICB, but not from any provider they prefer. Understanding patient choice is important for healthcare professionals who discuss referral options with patients, ensuring that patients are aware of their rights and the options available to them.

Digital Appointment Management
The use of digital technologies — including the NHS App, patient-facing websites, text messaging systems, and automated telephony — to manage the full appointment lifecycle from booking confirmation and reminder through attendance confirmation, rescheduling, and patient feedback. The NHS App, launched in 2018 and significantly expanded since, allows patients to view their GP health record, manage prescription requests, and in many areas book and manage GP appointments and view outpatient referrals and appointment details. Digital appointment management reduces the administrative burden on clinical reception teams, improves the patient experience by providing convenient access to appointment information, and reduces DNA rates through automated reminders. NHS digital inclusion is an important consideration: digital appointment management must not create barriers for patients who lack access to digital devices or connectivity, who have limited digital literacy, or who have specific accessibility needs — all NHS services must maintain non-digital channels alongside digital ones.

Access Equity
The principle that appointment systems and scheduling processes should be designed to ensure that all patients — regardless of their literacy level, language, disability, socioeconomic status, digital access, or working patterns — have genuinely equal access to NHS appointments. Access equity is a legal requirement under the Equality Act 2010 (which requires NHS services to make reasonable adjustments for patients with disabilities) and an ethical requirement of the NHS's founding universal principle. Common access equity issues in appointment scheduling include: appointment letters written in complex English that are not accessible to patients with limited English or low health literacy; appointment times during working hours that are inaccessible to patients who cannot take time off work; telephone-only reminder systems that exclude patients with hearing impairments; digital appointment systems that exclude patients without smartphones or internet access; and appointment systems that do not offer interpreter services or Braille or audio appointment letters. Achieving access equity requires proactive design — building inclusive features into scheduling systems from the outset rather than making adjustments reactively when problems are identified.

Text 1: NHS Appointment Management — The Operational Challenge and the Human Stakes
Why managing appointments well is one of the most important operational functions in the NHS

An NHS appointment is not simply a diary entry. It is the operational expression of a clinical need — the point at which a patient's health concern meets the system's capacity to address it. When appointments are managed well, patients receive timely care, clinical time is used efficiently, and the NHS meets the targets that govern its performance. When appointments are managed poorly — slots wasted by DNAs, urgent patients delayed in long routine queues, cancellations communicated late, letters arriving after the appointment date — the consequences extend far beyond operational metrics. Patients experience anxiety, deterioration, and loss of confidence in a system they have been taught to depend on. The human stakes of appointment management are as real as any clinical decision, and they deserve the same professional seriousness.

The scale of the challenge is formidable. The NHS in England conducts over 100 million outpatient appointments each year, making outpatient scheduling one of the largest logistics operations in the country. Against this backdrop, the NHS faces: a growing mismatch between the supply of appointment capacity and the demand from an ageing, increasingly complex population; a persistent DNA rate of approximately 6–8% nationally, representing millions of wasted clinical encounters annually; significant variation in waiting times between specialties, Trusts, and geographic areas; and the challenge of managing a waiting list that, following the COVID-19 pandemic, exceeded seven million patients in England. These are not problems that will be solved by a single intervention — they require sustained, systematic improvement in the management of every stage of the appointment cycle.

The most effective NHS services approach appointment management as a continuous improvement discipline rather than an administrative function. They monitor their DNA rates and test interventions — reminder formats, booking lead times, partial booking models — to reduce them. They review their clinic templates regularly to ensure the balance of new and follow-up slots reflects actual demand. They triage incoming referrals systematically so that urgent patients are seen quickly and routine patients are managed in the appropriate sequence. They communicate clearly and empathetically with patients about waiting times, providing honest information and practical support rather than vague reassurance. And they treat the administrative staff who manage appointments — medical secretaries, outpatient coordinators, booking clerks — as clinical support professionals whose work directly affects patient outcomes, rather than purely clerical workers whose role is incidental to care.

Text 2: Communicating About Appointments — Language, Tone, and Professional Standards
How healthcare professionals communicate with patients and colleagues about scheduling — and why getting it right matters

Healthcare appointment communication sits at the intersection of clinical care and customer service — and it is one of the most frequent points of contact between NHS patients and the system. For many patients, the appointment letter, the booking phone call, or the text message reminder is the clearest signal of how the NHS views them: as an individual whose time, circumstances, and health anxiety matter, or as a unit of activity to be processed. The language used in appointment communications — the tone of a reminder, the clarity of a letter, the empathy of a phone call about a long waiting time — shapes patient trust, affects attendance rates, and can either reassure or distress people who are already navigating a health problem.

Professional appointment communication requires three qualities. The first is clarity: appointment letters, reminder messages, and telephone communications must give the patient all the information they need to attend successfully — the right date, the right time, the right location, with clear instructions for any preparation required. Ambiguity in an appointment letter is not a minor inconvenience: it is a preventable cause of patients arriving at the wrong place, missing their appointment, or failing to follow preparation instructions that matter clinically. The second quality is empathy: patients waiting for NHS appointments are often anxious — they may be waiting with undiagnosed symptoms, a recent concerning diagnosis, or a condition that is affecting their quality of life significantly. Communication that acknowledges this reality — 'I understand this wait has been difficult' — creates a very different interaction from communication that is purely procedural. The third quality is accuracy: the information provided to patients must be correct — an incorrect appointment time, a wrong clinic location, or a telephone number that does not connect are not just administrative errors; they undermine the patient's ability to access care and their trust in the system.

For NHS staff communicating with colleagues about scheduling — in booking team emails, in clinic list notes, in MDT coordination calls — the standards are similar but the register is different. Internal scheduling communication should be efficient and precise rather than empathetic — using the correct clinical and operational terminology, specifying the relevant details without ambiguity, and communicating the implications of scheduling changes for colleagues who need to plan their clinical day. A medical secretary who emails 'the patient in slot 3 might not come, just wanted to flag' has communicated inadequately compared to one who writes 'Mr J. Whitfield (DOB 14.03.62, NHS No. 123 456 7890) — booked in slot 3 at 10:30 — called this morning to say he may be late; asked him to call back to confirm; awaiting confirmation; please be aware clinic may run short at that slot'. The precision of internal scheduling communication is itself a patient safety issue — clinical teams making decisions based on incomplete or inaccurate scheduling information are at higher risk of errors.

Case Study 1: From 78% to 92% — How One NHS Trust Transformed Its Outpatient DNA Rate
How systematic appointment management changes reduced DNA rates and improved clinic utilisation across a major NHS Trust
This case study is drawn from a representative composite of NHS outpatient improvement projects documented in NHS England's Outpatient Transformation Programme and Getting It Right First Time (GIRFT) programme reports.

In 2019, a large acute NHS Foundation Trust reviewed its outpatient DNA data and identified that its overall DNA rate was 78% clinic utilisation — meaning that 22% of all booked outpatient slots were not being attended. Across the Trust's 300,000 annual outpatient appointments, this represented approximately 66,000 wasted clinical appointments — equivalent to a full year's clinic activity for a significant number of specialties. The clinical and financial cost was substantial.

The Trust's improvement team analysed the data by specialty, clinic type, patient demographic, and appointment lead time. Several clear patterns emerged: DNA rates were significantly higher for new patient appointments than for follow-ups; rates were highest in clinics with an average appointment lead time of more than six weeks; patients under 30 had DNA rates nearly double those of patients over 60; and specialties that used text message reminders had DNA rates approximately 35% lower than those that did not.

Based on these findings, the Trust implemented a phased improvement programme: text message reminders were standardised across all outpatient services, sent at both 7 days and 2 days before the appointment; a partial booking pilot was introduced for four high-DNA specialties, reducing appointment lead times significantly; a targeted telephone outreach programme was introduced for patients who had previously DNA'd more than once; and clinic templates in the highest-DNA specialties were redesigned to include a 'buffer' slot that could be filled at short notice from a standby list.

Within 18 months, the Trust's overall clinic utilisation rate had risen from 78% to 92%. This represented approximately 42,000 additional appointments per year being attended — without any increase in clinical capacity. The Trust estimated the financial benefit at approximately £3.5 million in avoided wasted capacity per year, and the clinical benefit in terms of patients receiving earlier care was significant.

The lesson: DNA reduction is not a matter of patient behaviour change alone — it is primarily a systems design question. When appointment systems make it easy to attend, easy to cancel, and easy to reschedule; when reminders are timely and clear; and when appointment lead times are managed to reduce the likelihood of life changes intervening between booking and attendance — DNA rates fall predictably and substantially.

Case Study 2: The Appointment Letter That Caused Confusion — How Poor Communication Created a DNA and a Delayed Diagnosis
How an unclear appointment letter led to a patient attending the wrong location — and the lessons for communication quality in NHS scheduling
This case study is drawn from a representative composite of NHS patient safety and patient experience incidents related to appointment communication failures, compiled from NHS England's patient safety reporting data and NHS Ombudsman case summaries.

Mr George Hartley, a 71-year-old man from a rural area, received an NHS appointment letter for a new patient consultation at his local NHS Trust's respiratory department. He was referred by his GP on an urgent basis — his GP had identified persistent cough and unintentional weight loss as symptoms warranting urgent investigation. The referral was marked as a two-week wait cancer referral.

Mr Hartley's appointment letter specified 'Respiratory Outpatients, Level 3, Main Building'. It did not specify which of the Trust's two sites (the main hospital and a satellite clinic eight miles away) was intended. The Trust had recently reorganised its outpatient services, and the respiratory outpatient department had moved from the satellite clinic to the main hospital site — but the letter template had not been updated to reflect this, and did not include a postcode or detailed address for the appointment location.

Mr Hartley travelled to the satellite clinic — the site he had attended for previous appointments. He arrived on time but was told that the respiratory outpatient department was no longer based there. He was distressed and confused. A member of staff at the satellite clinic helped him telephone the main hospital, where a receptionist confirmed that his appointment was indeed at the main site and that the clinic had already started. By the time the situation was resolved and an alternative was arranged, the clinic was no longer able to accommodate him, and his appointment was rescheduled — for three weeks later.

The three-week delay in this patient's urgent respiratory assessment was investigated as a patient safety incident. The investigation found that the appointment letter template had not been updated following the service reorganisation; no postcode or detailed address was included in the letter; and no process existed to verify that appointment letter content was accurate before letters were sent.

The Trust made three immediate changes: letter templates were audited and updated within 48 hours of the investigation; all appointment letters were required to include the full address and postcode of the location; and a quarterly letter quality audit was introduced as part of the outpatient governance framework.

The lesson: appointment letter quality is a patient safety issue, not merely an administrative one — particularly for patients with urgent clinical needs. Every element of the letter that prevents a patient from arriving at the right place, at the right time, and prepared appropriately, is a failure that has real clinical consequences.
