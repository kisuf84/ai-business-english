Premium
The regular payment made by an individual or employer to a private health insurance provider in exchange for health coverage — typically paid monthly or annually. The premium is the cost of maintaining health insurance cover regardless of whether the insured person makes a claim. Premium levels are determined by a range of risk and market factors: the insured person's age (older patients typically pay higher premiums reflecting their higher expected healthcare utilisation); their medical history (whether pre-existing conditions are covered or excluded affects the premium significantly); the level of cover chosen (comprehensive cover including mental health, physiotherapy, dental, and optical costs more than basic acute cover); the chosen excess (a higher excess reduces the premium); the hospital network included in the policy (cover at a wider range of hospitals is more expensive); and the insurance provider's underwriting approach. For UK employees, private health insurance is often provided as a workplace benefit — a group scheme — at a lower premium than an individually purchased policy. For healthcare professionals dealing with insured patients, understanding how premiums are structured helps in communicating why coverage varies between patients with apparently similar conditions.

Excess
The amount that the insured patient agrees to pay out of their own pocket toward the cost of a healthcare claim before their insurance cover activates — expressed as a fixed monetary amount (for example, £100 or £500 per policy year). The excess is a cost-sharing mechanism: it reduces the premium cost for the patient (because the insurer's risk is reduced by the amount the patient bears) and reduces low-value claims (because patients are less likely to make small claims that would not exceed their excess). In private healthcare billing, the excess creates a common source of patient confusion and complaint: a patient who presents for a procedure believing they are 'fully covered' may be surprised to receive a bill for the excess amount after their treatment. Healthcare billing teams and clinical staff who discuss finances with patients should understand the excess concept and be able to explain clearly that the excess is the patient's own contribution to the cost, not a billing error by the provider.

Pre-Authorisation
The formal approval from a private health insurer that must be obtained before a patient undergoes a planned procedure, investigation, or course of treatment — confirming that the proposed care is covered under the patient's policy and that the insurer will pay the associated costs up to the authorised limit. Pre-authorisation (also called prior authorisation or pre-approval) protects both the patient and the provider: the patient knows their treatment is funded before it takes place; the provider knows the insurer will pay the claim; and the insurer can control what it funds. Without pre-authorisation, a patient who undergoes a procedure may find their insurer declines to pay the claim on the grounds that it was not approved in advance. For healthcare providers managing private patients, establishing a robust pre-authorisation process — confirming authorisation before every procedure, checking the authorisation number and limit, and ensuring the clinical team is aware of any conditions attached to the authorisation — is a critical financial and clinical governance responsibility.
▼ Show definition

Benefit Limit
A cap on the amount of money a private health insurer will pay for a specific type of treatment, condition, or category of care within a defined policy period — after which the patient becomes responsible for all further costs. Benefit limits vary significantly between insurance policies: basic policies may have low annual benefit limits for mental health treatment, physiotherapy, or specialist drugs; comprehensive policies may have higher or unlimited limits for inpatient care but limits on outpatient consultations. In a healthcare billing context, benefit limits create significant financial risk for patients with chronic, complex, or long-term conditions: a patient with a serious musculoskeletal condition who reaches their physiotherapy benefit limit part way through a course of treatment must either self-fund the remaining sessions or return to NHS care. Healthcare professionals discussing treatment plans with privately insured patients should routinely check whether the proposed plan will exceed the patient's benefit limit and communicate the financial implications proactively rather than leaving the patient to discover a shortfall at the billing stage.

📋
Schedule of Fees
A published list of the charges that a consultant, hospital, or other healthcare provider makes for their specific services — specifying the fee for each procedure, consultation type, or clinical activity, typically referenced by procedure code or description. In private practice, the schedule of fees is the commercial document that governs what a provider charges and what an insurer or self-paying patient can expect to pay. Insurers publish their own fee schedules — the amounts they will reimburse for specific procedures — and consultants who set their fees above the insurer's schedule create a 'shortfall' for which the patient may be liable. Some insurers require consultants who wish to treat their policyholders to agree to the insurer's fee schedule (recognising them as 'recognised consultants' or 'fee-assured practitioners'), which protects patients from shortfalls. Transparency about fees — providing patients with a clear, written schedule of what they will be charged before treatment — is an ethical and regulatory requirement for private healthcare providers in the UK under the Competition and Markets Authority's private healthcare market study recommendations.

NHS Tariff
The nationally determined prices that NHS commissioners pay to NHS providers (and approved independent sector providers) for specific healthcare activities — set by NHS England and forming the financial basis of most payment transactions between commissioners and providers for acute hospital care. The NHS national tariff (now formally the 'NHS Payment Scheme') sets prices for specific Healthcare Resource Groups (HRGs) — the clinical groupings used to categorise inpatient stays, outpatient attendances, and other healthcare activities for payment purposes. The tariff price for a specific HRG reflects the national average cost of delivering that type of care, adjusted for complexity, patient characteristics, and local market factors. Providers whose actual costs are below the tariff retain the surplus; those whose costs exceed the tariff must absorb the difference. Understanding the tariff is important for any healthcare professional involved in clinical coding, service planning, or financial management — because the tariff price determines the NHS revenue generated by each clinical activity, and accurate coding is essential to ensure the correct tariff is applied.
▼ Show definition
📊
Healthcare Resource Group
A standardised clinical grouping — abbreviated as HRG — used in the NHS to classify episodes of care into clinically meaningful and financially manageable categories for the purpose of payment, benchmarking, and activity reporting. HRGs group together episodes of care that are clinically similar and have similar resource implications — so that the NHS can apply a single tariff price to all episodes within the same group rather than pricing each episode individually. HRGs are defined by NHS England and updated annually to reflect changes in clinical practice and coding. The HRG applied to a patient's episode is determined by the clinical coding team based on the diagnoses and procedures recorded in the patient's clinical record — making clinical coding accuracy a direct financial and informational issue. An inaccurately coded episode may be assigned to the wrong HRG, resulting in underpayment (if the episode is assigned to a lower-value group than it should be) or overpayment with potential audit risk (if it is assigned to a higher-value group). HRGs are the building block of NHS payment and the foundation of healthcare cost and activity analysis.
▼ Show definition
💻
Clinical Coding
The systematic translation of clinical information from a patient's medical record — diagnoses, procedures, complications, and other clinical findings — into standardised alphanumeric codes from recognised classification systems. In the UK NHS, the two primary coding systems are: ICD-10 (International Classification of Diseases, 10th revision) — used to code diagnoses, symptoms, and health conditions; and OPCS-4 (Office of Population Censuses and Surveys Classification of Surgical Operations and Procedures, 4th revision) — used to code procedures and operations. Clinical coding is performed by trained clinical coders based on the information recorded in the patient's clinical record — and its accuracy depends on the completeness and clarity of the clinical documentation. Clinical coding has multiple functions: it determines the HRG assigned to a patient's episode (and therefore the NHS tariff payment received); it generates the activity data used for national benchmarking, healthcare planning, and research; and it produces the Patient Level Information and Costing System (PLICS) data used for cost modelling. Inaccurate or incomplete clinical coding results in financial loss, misleading activity data, and risk of audit.
▼ Show definition
🤝
Pre-Authorisation Number
A unique reference number issued by a private health insurer when they grant pre-authorisation for a specific procedure, course of treatment, or outpatient episode — confirming that the care is approved and funded under the patient's policy up to the authorised limit. The pre-authorisation number is the key administrative document in private healthcare billing: every invoice submitted to a private insurer for a procedure or treatment that was pre-authorised must quote the correct pre-authorisation number, without which the insurer may decline to process the claim or reject it on administrative grounds. Healthcare billing teams should record the pre-authorisation number on the patient's administrative record before any procedure takes place, and should verify with the insurer that the authorisation covers the specific procedure, the specific consultant, and the specific hospital — since authorisations can be specific to the clinical team and the facility. A mismatch between the authorised details and the billed details is a common cause of claim disputes and delays.

📈
Payment by Results
A NHS payment model — abbreviated as PbR — in which NHS providers receive a set tariff payment for each episode of care they deliver, based on the activity they perform rather than receiving a fixed block payment regardless of the volume of care provided. Payment by results was introduced in England in 2003–04 as a mechanism to incentivise NHS providers to increase activity, improve efficiency, and compete for patients. Under PbR, a Trust that treats more patients receives more income; a Trust that treats fewer receives less — creating a direct financial incentive to maximise throughput. PbR contrasts with the block contract model, in which a commissioner pays a fixed annual sum to a provider regardless of the volume of activity delivered. Following the Integrated Care System reforms of 2022, NHS payment has moved toward a more blended model — combining activity-based tariff payments for some services with population-based block payments for others — reflecting the ICS's goal of managing population health rather than simply maximising activity.

Self-Pay
A payment model in which a patient pays directly for their healthcare from their own funds — without using NHS funding or private health insurance. Self-pay patients include: individuals who prefer not to wait on the NHS waiting list and can afford to fund their care privately; individuals who lack private health insurance but want private hospital care for its benefits (choice of consultant, single room, shorter waiting times); individuals whose insurance policy does not cover their specific procedure; and overseas visitors who are not entitled to NHS-funded care and are not insured. Self-pay pricing in private healthcare is typically published as a fixed-price package — covering the surgeon's fee, the anaesthetist's fee, the hospital facility fee, and post-operative follow-up — allowing the patient to know the total cost before committing to treatment. NHS hospitals with private patient units also offer self-pay services, which generate additional income for the Trust but must be managed within a regulatory framework that ensures NHS patients are not disadvantaged. Clear, proactive communication about self-pay costs — including what is and is not included in the quoted price — is an ethical requirement and a practical protection against billing disputes.

📝
Invoice
A formal financial document issued by a healthcare provider to a patient, insurer, or commissioner — requesting payment for services rendered, specifying the services provided, the relevant codes, the charges, and the payment terms. In private healthcare, invoices are issued directly to patients (for self-pay care or for the excess and shortfall elements of insured care) and to insurers (for the insurer-funded element of a patient's treatment). A professional healthcare invoice should contain: the provider's name, address, and VAT registration number (if VAT-registered); the patient's name and date of birth; the date and nature of the service (with procedure codes where applicable); the pre-authorisation number (for insured patients); the total amount charged; the amount funded by the insurer; the patient's personal liability (excess, shortfall, and any non-covered elements); payment terms and methods; and a contact for billing queries. NHS invoices between providers and commissioners follow standardised formats determined by NHS finance guidance. Accuracy, clarity, and timeliness of invoices directly affects both the provider's cashflow and the patient's financial experience.

Shortfall
The difference between the fee charged by a consultant, hospital, or other healthcare provider and the amount the patient's private health insurer will pay for that service — for which the patient is personally liable. Shortfalls arise when: a consultant's fees exceed the insurer's fee schedule (the insurer pays the schedule amount and the patient pays the remainder); a procedure attracts additional charges not covered by the insurer's authorisation (such as consumables, specialist implants, or drugs); the patient's benefit limit has been reached and the insurer no longer funds the care; or the treatment is partially excluded under the policy. Shortfalls are a significant source of financial surprise and distress for patients who believed they were fully covered. UK regulatory guidance — including the Competition and Markets Authority's private healthcare recommendations and the Private Healthcare Information Network's transparency standards — emphasises that consultants and providers should inform patients of the potential for a shortfall before treatment, not after the invoice is received. A culture of proactive financial communication is both a legal and ethical requirement in private healthcare.

ICD Code
International Classification of Diseases code — a standardised alphanumeric code from the World Health Organization's ICD coding system (currently ICD-10 in UK NHS use, with ICD-11 being progressively introduced) used to classify a patient's diagnosis or health condition for clinical records, billing, and statistical reporting. ICD codes provide a universal language for describing health conditions that allows consistent communication across clinical teams, hospitals, countries, and data systems. In NHS billing, ICD-10 diagnosis codes are combined with OPCS-4 procedure codes by clinical coders to determine the Healthcare Resource Group (HRG) that generates the tariff payment for an episode of care. In private healthcare billing, ICD codes are required by most major insurers on invoices to confirm the clinical indication for the treatment claimed. Accurate ICD coding depends on the quality of the clinical documentation in the patient's record — a diagnosis that is not clearly documented cannot be coded, and an incorrectly documented diagnosis will generate an incorrect code with downstream financial and statistical implications.
▼ Show definition
🌐
Recognised Insurer
A private health insurer with whom a consultant or hospital has a formal agreement — typically specifying the fee schedule the provider will accept and the administrative processes they will follow — and which the insurer's policies formally acknowledge as a reimbursable provider for its policyholders. In the UK private healthcare market, the major recognised insurers include BUPA, AXA Health, Aviva, Vitality, and WPA. A consultant who is 'recognised' by an insurer agrees to charge fees within the insurer's schedule (or within a negotiated arrangement), which protects the patient from shortfalls. A consultant who is not recognised by the patient's insurer — or who charges above the recognised fee schedule — may result in a shortfall for the patient. For internationally trained medical professionals joining UK private practice, understanding which insurers their hospital or practice group is recognised with, and what administrative processes apply to each insurer (pre-authorisation requirements, invoicing formats, claim submission deadlines), is an important early orientation priority.

NHS Funded Care
Healthcare provided to eligible patients at no direct cost at the point of use — funded through general taxation via NHS England's commissioning framework, Integrated Care Boards, and NHS provider Trusts. Eligibility for NHS-funded care in England is based primarily on ordinary residence — UK residents are entitled to free NHS care for most services (with some exceptions including dental treatment, prescriptions for adults in England, and some optical services). International visitors to the UK may be entitled to NHS-funded care in emergency situations, and EEA nationals may be entitled to a broader range of NHS treatment under reciprocal health agreements, but non-UK residents receiving planned care in NHS hospitals are generally subject to overseas visitor charging. For healthcare professionals dealing with international patients — whether at NHS trusts with international private patient units or in private hospitals treating medical tourists — understanding eligibility for NHS-funded care and the charging rules for overseas visitors is an important legal and financial competency.

Electronic Prior Authorisation
A digital system through which pre-authorisation for private healthcare procedures or investigations is requested and granted electronically — replacing paper-based or telephone-based authorisation processes. Electronic prior authorisation (EPA) systems, used by major UK private health insurers including BUPA and AXA Health, allow clinical secretaries, billing coordinators, and consultants to submit pre-authorisation requests through an online portal or integrated clinical system, receive authorisation decisions within minutes or hours rather than days, and manage the authorisation and claims process digitally. For healthcare billing teams, EPA reduces the administrative burden of managing pre-authorisations by phone, reduces the risk of authorisation being overlooked before a procedure takes place, and creates a digital audit trail of every authorisation request and decision. Electronic authorisation is increasingly integrated with the billing workflow — ensuring that every invoice submitted has a corresponding authorisation record, reducing disputed claims.

Overseas Visitor Charging
The NHS's system for recovering the cost of healthcare from overseas visitors — people who are not ordinarily resident in the UK and are therefore not entitled to receive free NHS-funded care. The NHS charges overseas visitors for most healthcare services, with the significant exception of accident and emergency treatment (which is free for all at the point of emergency presentation), treatment of certain communicable diseases (to protect public health), and some other categories of care. Overseas visitor charging is administered by NHS Trusts under the National Health Service (Charges to Overseas Visitors) Regulations 2015, and requires Trusts to make enquiries about the residency status of patients and to charge those who are not entitled to free treatment at 150% of the NHS national tariff. Healthcare professionals who interact with international patients — in admissions, clinical settings, or billing — should be aware of the overseas visitor charging regulations and the Trust's process for identifying, assessing, and billing overseas patients.

Disputed Claim
A billing claim submitted by a healthcare provider to a private health insurer that the insurer has declined to pay in full, disputed on clinical or administrative grounds, or returned for further information or amendment. Disputed claims are an inevitable feature of private healthcare billing and can arise from: administrative reasons (missing pre-authorisation number, incorrect patient details, billing deadline exceeded); clinical reasons (the insurer argues the treatment was not medically necessary, or that it relates to a pre-existing condition not covered by the policy); coding reasons (the procedure code used does not match the insurer's schedule or the pre-authorised clinical indication); or fee disputes (the consultant has charged above the insurer's recognised fee schedule). Managing disputed claims requires systematic processes: a designated billing contact who handles disputes; a record of all communications with the insurer; a clear escalation pathway for disputes that cannot be resolved at the operational level; and an understanding of the patient's position — since a disputed claim may result in the patient receiving a bill for the disputed amount if the insurer ultimately declines to pay.

Explanation of Benefits
A formal document issued by a private health insurer to a policyholder (and sometimes to the provider) following the processing of a claim — summarising what was claimed, what the insurer is paying, what is being excluded or reduced, and what the patient's personal financial liability is. The Explanation of Benefits (EOB) is often the first document a patient receives that tells them exactly how their claim has been handled, and it can be a source of confusion or distress — particularly when the EOB shows a shortfall, an exclusion, or a reduction from the amount claimed. Healthcare billing teams and patient-facing staff should be familiar with how to read and explain an EOB to a patient: identifying the claim reference, the procedure claimed, the insurer's payment amount, any deductions (excess, benefit limit exhaustion, fee schedule reduction), and the patient's remaining balance. Clear communication about the EOB — and about the patient's options for querying the insurer's decision — is an important component of patient financial support.

Medical Insurance Liaison
A specialist administrative role in healthcare organisations — particularly private hospitals, NHS private patient units, and large medical practices — responsible for managing all aspects of the relationship between the provider and private health insurers: including pre-authorisation management, claims submission, payment chasing, dispute resolution, and insurer relationship management. Medical insurance liaison staff are the operational interface between the clinical team and the insurance companies: they ensure that pre-authorisation is obtained before every procedure, that invoices are submitted accurately and on time, that payments are received within agreed terms, and that disputed claims are followed up systematically. For clinical staff dealing with insured patients, the medical insurance liaison team is the first point of contact for financial queries and the key resource for understanding the specific requirements of different insurers' processes. Building a good working relationship with the insurance liaison team — sharing complete and accurate clinical documentation, communicating planned procedures in advance, and flagging potential claim issues early — significantly reduces the administrative burden on all parties.

Text 1: NHS Funding and Private Health Insurance — How UK Healthcare Is Paid For
The mechanics of NHS funding, the role of private health insurance, and the financial landscape of UK healthcare

Understanding how healthcare is funded in the UK requires holding two parallel models in mind simultaneously. The NHS — funded through general taxation, free at the point of use for eligible UK residents — is the dominant model, accounting for approximately 80% of all healthcare expenditure in the UK. The private sector — funded through premiums paid to health insurers, direct self-pay payments, and corporate healthcare budgets — accounts for the remaining 20% and operates alongside the NHS, complementing it in some areas (providing faster access to elective care) and competing with it in others (for the time and expertise of NHS-trained consultants). For healthcare professionals working in the UK, understanding both models — and the financial interface between them — is not optional: the majority of consultants interact with insured and self-pay patients as well as NHS patients, and the administrative processes that govern each type of care are fundamentally different.

Private health insurance in the UK operates on a risk-pooling principle: policyholders pay premiums into a common fund, from which claims are paid when policyholders need healthcare. The premium is calculated to reflect the insured person's expected risk — meaning that older policyholders, those with pre-existing conditions, and those seeking more comprehensive cover pay more. Key policy features include the excess (the amount the policyholder pays toward each claim before insurance activates), benefit limits (maximum payments for specific types of care), exclusions (categories of care not covered, typically including pre-existing conditions and some chronic conditions), and the hospital and consultant networks covered. The major private health insurers in the UK — BUPA, AXA Health, Aviva, and Vitality, among others — each have their own specific policy terms, fee schedules, and pre-authorisation requirements, creating an administrative landscape that healthcare billing teams must navigate with precision.

NHS funding flows from central government through NHS England to Integrated Care Boards, which commission services from NHS Trusts, Foundation Trusts, and approved independent sector providers. Providers receive payment for most acute hospital activity through the NHS Payment Scheme — a system of nationally determined tariff prices that specifies the amount paid for each type of episode, grouped into Healthcare Resource Groups (HRGs). The tariff price reflects the national average cost of delivering that type of care, creating financial incentives for efficient providers and financial pressure for those whose costs exceed the tariff. Beyond the tariff system, NHS organisations also generate income from: training and education funding; research income; overseas visitor charges; private patient income from NHS hospitals' private patient units; and commercial partnerships. Understanding this income landscape — and the financial pressures and incentives it creates — is important context for any professional working within the NHS's financial system.

Text 2: Medical Billing, Coding, and Financial Administration — Precision, Compliance, and Communication
The principles and practices of medical billing in NHS and private healthcare contexts

Medical billing is the operational process through which healthcare providers convert clinical activity into financial payment — translating what clinicians do into codes, invoices, and claims that generate the income required to sustain a healthcare organisation. In the NHS, billing is primarily an internal process between providers and commissioners, governed by the national tariff, clinical coding standards, and NHS finance regulations. In the private sector, billing is a more visible and complex process that directly involves patients and their insurers, requires detailed pre-authorisation management, and operates within a competitive commercial environment. In both settings, the quality of billing — its accuracy, completeness, and timeliness — has direct financial and reputational consequences for the organisation.

Clinical coding is the foundation of NHS billing. Every episode of inpatient or outpatient care generates a clinical record — and that record must be translated into standardised codes (ICD-10 for diagnoses, OPCS-4 for procedures) by trained clinical coders. The codes assigned determine the Healthcare Resource Group (HRG) for the episode, which determines the NHS tariff payment. Inaccurate coding costs the NHS money in two directions: under-coding generates less revenue than the episode deserves (a loss for the provider); over-coding generates more revenue than it should (creating liability for repayment and reputational risk in external audit). Clinical documentation quality is the upstream driver of coding accuracy — a clinician who documents diagnoses clearly, completely, and with the appropriate clinical terminology produces records that can be coded accurately; one who leaves ambiguous or incomplete notes creates the conditions for coding error.

In the private sector, billing accuracy and the communication of financial information to patients are both commercial and ethical imperatives. The Competition and Markets Authority's review of the private healthcare market in 2014 identified a systemic failure of transparency in fee communication — patients were routinely receiving unexpected bills for shortfalls between consultant fees and insurer schedules, without having been warned of the possibility in advance. The CMA's remedies, implemented progressively since 2014, require consultants to publish their fees and their insurer recognition status, to notify patients of the potential for shortfalls before treatment, and to provide itemised bills upon request. For billing teams and clinical staff alike, the lesson is clear: financial communication in private healthcare is not an afterthought to clinical care — it is an integral component of professional practice, and its quality has direct consequences for patient experience, trust, and the organisation's commercial reputation.

Case Study 1: The Coding Audit That Recovered £1.8 Million — How Improving Clinical Documentation and Coding Accuracy Transformed an NHS Trust's Financial Position
How a systematic coding audit and clinical documentation programme recovered significant NHS tariff income
This case study is drawn from a representative composite of NHS clinical coding improvement projects documented in NHS England's GIRFT programme and NHS Providers' finance improvement literature.

A mid-sized NHS Foundation Trust in the north of England had been experiencing a persistent gap between its clinical activity data and its NHS tariff income. Despite treating a high-complexity patient population — with above-average rates of comorbidities including diabetes, COPD, and heart failure — its case mix index (a measure of the average clinical complexity of its patient population) was consistently below the national average for trusts with similar patient demographics.

A clinical coding audit commissioned in 2021 identified two systematic issues. First, clinicians in the orthopaedic and respiratory departments were regularly failing to document secondary diagnoses — comorbidities such as diabetes and COPD that, when coded, would move an episode into a higher Healthcare Resource Group with a correspondingly higher tariff payment. The documentation of these conditions was present in the nursing notes but absent from the consultant's discharge summary — the primary clinical document used by the clinical coding team. Second, clinical coders were defaulting to lower-specificity diagnosis codes when documentation was ambiguous, rather than querying the clinical team for clarification.

The improvement programme had three components. A clinical documentation education programme was delivered to all medical and surgical consultants — explaining the financial implications of incomplete discharge summaries and providing specific guidance on how to document secondary diagnoses. A coding query process was established — requiring coders to formally query ambiguous records with the relevant clinician rather than defaulting to a lower code. And a monthly coding dashboard was introduced — reporting specialty-level case mix index, coding accuracy, and HRG distribution to clinical directors and the finance team, creating visibility and accountability.

Within twelve months, the Trust's case mix index had risen from 0.87 to 0.96 (against a national average of 0.95), and the increase in tariff income attributable to the improved coding was estimated at £1.8 million per year. No additional clinical activity was generated — the same patients were treated, but their clinical complexity was now accurately captured and funded.

The lesson: clinical coding and clinical documentation are not administrative functions separate from clinical care — they are the mechanism through which clinical complexity is translated into appropriate funding, and the quality of both has direct and quantifiable financial consequences for the organisation.

Case Study 2: The Shortfall That Wasn't Communicated — How a Failure of Fee Transparency Cost a Patient and Damaged a Practice
How a failure to proactively communicate a consultant's shortfall left a patient with an unexpected bill — and the consequences for the practice
This case study is drawn from a representative composite of private healthcare billing disputes documented in the Private Healthcare Information Network's transparency reporting and the NHS Ombudsman's guidance on private patient financial communication.

Mr William Hartley, aged 58, was referred by his GP to a private orthopaedic consultant for a knee replacement procedure. He had private health insurance through his employer's group scheme — Aviva, comprehensive cover — and assumed that his insurance would cover the entire cost of his treatment. At no point during his consultations, his pre-operative assessment, or his admission did any member of the clinical team, the consultant's secretary, or the hospital billing team mention the possibility of a shortfall.

Following his procedure, Mr Hartley received an invoice from the hospital for £1,650 — described as 'patient liability balance'. He also received a separate invoice from the orthopaedic consultant for £750, described as 'excess over Aviva schedule'. The combined unexpected charge of £2,400 — in addition to his policy excess of £250 — was not something Mr Hartley had anticipated or budgeted for.

Mr Hartley complained formally to the hospital. The complaint was reviewed by the patient services team, who confirmed that: the orthopaedic consultant's fees were above the Aviva fee schedule (the consultant was Aviva-'recognised' but had opted to charge above the standard schedule for complex procedures); the hospital's own charges for certain consumables were not included in Aviva's standard procedure authorisation; and neither of these shortfalls had been communicated to Mr Hartley before the procedure in the pre-treatment financial discussion that CMA guidance required.

The hospital upheld the complaint in full. Mr Hartley's shortfall charges were written off by both the hospital and the consultant. The consultant agreed to participate in a practice improvement programme covering pre-treatment financial communication. The hospital updated its admissions process to require a signed patient financial disclosure document — confirming that all potential shortfalls had been explained — before any private procedure took place.

The financial cost to the hospital and the consultant: the £2,400 written off, plus the staff time and management resource consumed by the complaint. The reputational cost: the case was cited in the hospital's CQC inspection report and featured in the Private Healthcare Information Network's annual transparency report.

The lesson is clear: proactive, upfront communication of all potential financial liabilities — excess, shortfall, and non-covered elements — before a private procedure is not merely good practice. It is a regulatory and ethical requirement. Failure to comply creates financial loss, reputational damage, and patient harm.
