🏛️   Case Studies

Real-World Cases

Explore the Jehovah’s Witness refusal case — how Mrs Fatima Al-Rashid’s post-partum haemorrhage created a direct, acute tension between the clinical team’s beneficence obligation (without transfusion she would likely die) and her legally valid autonomy (a signed, witnessed ADRT explicitly refusing blood transfusion even at risk of life, reaffirmed on admission) — how the legal and ethics teams confirmed that overriding a valid ADRT constitutes unlawful assault regardless of benevolent intent, and how the clinical team responded by exhausting every alternative intervention to manage the haemorrhage without transfusion — illustrating that patient autonomy is a legal right that constrains clinical action; and the whistleblower who was not heard — how Dr Maria Santos raised a concern about a consultant’s surgical practice through internal channels that failed to respond, eventually made a GMC referral as legally entitled, which confirmed a pattern of substandard practice with conditions placed on the consultant’s registration — while Dr Santos experienced hostility and isolation despite being legally protected under PIDA — illustrating two dimensions: the professional duty to raise concerns and escalate, and the organisational failure when an NHS Trust is not genuinely open to hearing them.
