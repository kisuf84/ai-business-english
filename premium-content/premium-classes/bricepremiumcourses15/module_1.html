Reading Text

Over the past decade, remote work has shifted from a rare privilege to a widespread reality for millions of workers around the world. Before 2020, only a small percentage of employees regularly worked from home. However, the global pandemic forced companies to adapt quickly, and many discovered that productivity did not necessarily suffer when people worked outside the office. Today, a growing number of organisations offer flexible or fully remote arrangements as a standard benefit. For employees, this change has brought both exciting opportunities and genuine challenges.

On the positive side, remote workers often report higher levels of job satisfaction. They save time and money by avoiding daily commutes, which in large cities can easily consume two or three hours each day. Working from home also gives people greater control over their environment, allowing them to create comfortable, personalised workspaces. Parents, in particular, appreciate the flexibility to be more present for their families. Despite these advantages, remote work is not without its drawbacks. Many people struggle with feelings of isolation, since casual conversations with colleagues — the kind that happen naturally in an office — disappear when everyone is working from different locations. Collaboration can become more difficult, and some workers find it hard to separate their professional and personal lives when both happen in the same space. There is also evidence that career progression can slow for remote workers, as they may be less visible to their managers. Ultimately, the best arrangement depends on the individual, the job role, and the company culture.

Platforms have taken some steps to address this problem, such as labelling disputed content or reducing the spread of known misinformation. However, many experts believe that these measures are insufficient and that greater media literacy — the ability to evaluate sources critically — is essential for healthy democratic societies.

The Power of Daily Habits
SocietyLifestylePsychology
▼
Reading Text

It is a common belief that our lives are shaped by major decisions — the career we choose, the person we marry, the city we live in. While these choices undoubtedly matter, researchers who study human behaviour argue that our everyday habits may have an even greater influence on our wellbeing and success. A habit is a behaviour that has become automatic through repetition, and neuroscientists have found that a significant proportion of our daily actions — some estimates suggest around forty percent — are carried out on autopilot, without conscious decision-making.

The power of habits lies in their efficiency. Once a behaviour is habitual, the brain requires less energy to perform it, freeing up mental resources for more complex tasks. This is why routines can be so valuable: a person who exercises every morning without having to motivate themselves afresh each day has turned a beneficial behaviour into an effortless part of their life. Psychologist Charles Duhigg, in his influential book on habits, describes how each habit consists of three components: a cue that triggers the behaviour, the routine itself, and a reward that reinforces it. Understanding this loop is the key to both building positive habits and breaking negative ones.

However, changing habits is not as simple as it might seem. Research shows that it takes an average of sixty-six days for a new behaviour to become truly automatic, though this varies greatly between individuals and types of behaviour. Many people fail in their attempts to change their habits because they focus too heavily on willpower and motivation, which are unreliable and can be depleted by stress or fatigue. More successful approaches tend to involve designing the environment to make the desired behaviour easier, setting specific implementation intentions such as 'I will do this at this time and in this place,' and celebrating small wins to keep motivation alive.

As societies evolve, attitudes towards these living arrangements continue to shift, and there is growing recognition that neither choice is inherently superior.

The phrase 'work-life balance' has become so common in recent years that it risks losing its meaning. Yet the concept it describes — the idea that people need a sustainable equilibrium between their professional and personal lives — has never been more relevant. In many high-income countries, working hours have not necessarily decreased despite advances in technology that were supposed to make us more efficient. Instead, smartphones and email have blurred the boundaries between work time and personal time, creating a culture of constant availability that many workers find exhausting and dehumanising.

The consequences of poor work-life balance are well documented by health researchers. Chronically overworked individuals face significantly higher risks of cardiovascular disease, depression, anxiety disorders, and burnout — a state of complete mental and physical exhaustion that can take months or even years to recover from. Companies also suffer when their employees are overworked: productivity falls, creativity declines, absenteeism rises, and skilled workers are more likely to leave for employers who treat them better. The economic cost of burnout and work-related stress runs to hundreds of billions of dollars globally each year.

Achieving better work-life balance requires changes at both the individual and structural level. Individually, people can benefit from setting clear boundaries around their working hours, learning to say no to excessive demands, and investing time in activities and relationships that provide genuine restoration. At the structural level, companies and governments have an important role to play. Some countries, including France and Germany, have introduced policies that discourage email contact outside working hours. Others are experimenting with four-day working weeks, with encouraging early results suggesting that productivity often remains stable or even improves when working hours are reduced. Ultimately, creating a healthier relationship with work requires a cultural shift as much as a legal one.

Coffee is one of the most widely consumed beverages on the planet, with an estimated two billion cups drunk every day. For many people, it is far more than a hot drink: it is a ritual, a social activity, a source of comfort, and a performance enhancer. The global coffee industry generates revenues of well over one hundred billion dollars annually, and coffee shops have become some of the most important social spaces in modern urban life. Understanding why coffee has such a powerful hold on so many people requires looking at both its biochemical effects and its cultural dimensions.

The scientific explanation for coffee's popularity centres on caffeine, a naturally occurring stimulant found in coffee beans. When you consume caffeine, it blocks adenosine receptors in the brain — adenosine is a chemical that builds up over the course of the day and makes you feel tired. By blocking these receptors, caffeine prevents the fatigue signal from reaching the brain, creating the alert, focused feeling that coffee drinkers value. This effect typically begins within fifteen to thirty minutes of consumption and can last for several hours. Research also suggests that regular coffee consumption is associated with a reduced risk of several health conditions, including type 2 diabetes, Parkinson's disease, and certain types of depression.

Beyond the chemistry, coffee holds a special cultural significance in many societies. In Italy, ordering and drinking a coffee is a highly ritualised social act governed by precise rules about which types of coffee are appropriate at which times of day. In Ethiopia, where coffee originated, elaborate multi-hour ceremonies involving roasting, grinding, and brewing are still practised in homes and communities. In the contemporary urban environment, coffee shops have become what sociologists call 'third places' — spaces that are neither home nor workplace but serve a vital social function. For many people, the act of making and drinking coffee provides a small but reliable source of pleasure and routine in their daily lives.

Making friends as a child or teenager often seems effortless — shared classrooms, sports teams, and neighbourhoods naturally bring young people together. Adults, however, frequently report that forming new friendships is surprisingly difficult, even when they live in busy cities surrounded by thousands of other people. Understanding why adult friendships are harder to form — and what can be done about it — is an increasingly important social question.

Psychologist Jeffrey Hall has identified three conditions that tend to produce friendship: proximity (being near someone regularly), repeated interaction (encountering them more than once), and a setting that encourages informal, unplanned interaction. These conditions exist naturally in school and university but must be deliberately created in adult life. Many adults make the mistake of expecting friendships to develop purely through professional networking, which is typically too formal and goal-oriented to allow the kind of relaxed, spontaneous connection that true friendship requires. Joining clubs, taking classes, and participating in regular group activities are more effective routes to friendship because they provide all three of the conditions Hall identifies.

Technology presents a mixed picture. On one hand, social media can help people maintain long-distance friendships and reconnect with people from their past. On the other hand, research suggests that online interaction is a poor substitute for in-person connection and that heavy social media use may actually increase feelings of loneliness and social comparison. Perhaps the most important insight from the research is that friendships require investment of time and vulnerability — sharing personal thoughts and feelings rather than simply exchanging pleasantries. For adults who want to expand their social world, the key is to be patient, to show genuine interest in others, and to be willing to make the first move, even when it feels uncomfortable.

It is also essential to keep overheads extremely low in the early stages, which often means working from home or shared workspaces rather than renting office space. Many entrepreneurs also recommend developing a marketable skill — such as graphic design, web development, or writing — that can generate income while the core business is being developed. Patience and the willingness to learn from early mistakes are perhaps the most valuable assets of all.

Leadership is one of the most studied and debated topics in business and organisational psychology. Despite decades of research, there is no single agreed definition of what makes a great leader. Different cultures, industries, and historical periods have valued different leadership qualities, and what works in one context may be ineffective or even counterproductive in another. Nevertheless, researchers have identified certain traits and behaviours that are consistently associated with effective leadership across a wide range of settings.

Another key quality is intellectual humility — the willingness to acknowledge the limits of one's knowledge and to actively seek out different perspectives. Leadership style also matters enormously. Studies on management approaches have distinguished between 'transactional' leaders, who rely primarily on rewards and punishments to motivate employees, and 'transformational' leaders, who inspire their teams through a shared vision and genuine concern for personal development. The evidence strongly favours transformational leadership in most modern work environments: employees who feel inspired and genuinely valued tend to be more creative, more committed, and more willing to go beyond their basic job requirements. Great leaders also demonstrate authenticity — they behave consistently with their stated values, acknowledge mistakes, and treat people at all levels of the organisation with equal respect.

For workers and educators, the message is clear: invest in human skills, embrace lifelong learning, and be prepared for a career that involves continuous reinvention.

During the interview itself, body language and tone of voice matter as much as the content of your answers. Research consistently shows that interviewers form impressions in the first few minutes of a meeting — sometimes in the first thirty seconds — based on non-verbal signals such as posture, eye contact, and handshake. Listening carefully and responding thoughtfully, rather than rushing to speak the moment the interviewer finishes, also creates a positive impression. At the end of an interview, asking intelligent, specific questions about the role or company demonstrates curiosity and initiative. Finally, sending a brief, professional follow-up message after the interview is a simple but often overlooked gesture that can reinforce a positive impression.

Many people who have lived abroad for extended periods report that working through culture shock was one of the most personally valuable experiences of their lives, despite the difficulty it involved.
