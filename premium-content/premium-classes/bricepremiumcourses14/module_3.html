Missing the Bus in the Morning
▼

📖 Reading Text

On a Thursday morning, Grace left her flat at the usual time, but as she turned the corner onto the main road, she saw the number nine bus pulling away from the stop. She ran, but it was already too far. She stood on the empty pavement, slightly out of breath, and checked the time on her phone: seven fifty-one. The next bus was in twenty-two minutes, which would make her at least fifteen minutes late for work.

She had two options: wait for the next bus and be late, or take a different route. She checked the transport app and discovered she could take the number fourteen bus in four minutes, get off one stop earlier than usual, and walk the last twelve minutes. This would get her to work only four minutes late. She walked quickly to the stop for the fourteen, boarded it, and sent a quick message to her manager explaining the situation.

She arrived at four minutes past nine. Her manager had already seen the message and simply nodded when Grace walked in. Grace made herself a coffee and sat at her desk. Later, she adjusted her alarm by eight minutes and added a reminder to herself: the nine bus left at seven fifty on the dot, not seven fifty-two as she had always assumed. She had been relying on her own incorrect memory for over a year.

5. How late would the alternative route make her?

6. What did she do before boarding the number fourteen bus?

7. How did her manager react when she arrived?

8. What mistake had she been making for over a year?

'Lesson learned,' she told a colleague, 'the expensive way.'

📖 Reading Text

On a Wednesday night, Lea was trying to sleep when the music from the flat above hers became too loud to ignore. It was eleven forty-five. The music – a heavy beat she could feel through the ceiling – had been going since nine and had gradually increased in volume. She lay there for twenty minutes hoping it would stop. It did not.

She knocked on the ceiling once, then twice. No response. She considered calling the building's management company, but it was too late for a non-emergency call. She went upstairs and knocked on the door of the flat above. A man she had seen but never spoken to answered. She said, politely but directly: 'I work early tomorrow and the music is very loud through my ceiling – would you mind turning it down?' He apologised immediately. He had not realised how late it was. The music stopped within two minutes.

Back in her flat, Lea reflected on the conversation. She had been anxious about knocking but the exchange had been short and entirely civil. She fell asleep at quarter past twelve. The next morning, the man from upstairs knocked on her door while she was making coffee and apologised again. She appreciated it. She thought: the problem with avoiding small uncomfortable conversations is that they keep you awake longer than the conversation itself would have.
