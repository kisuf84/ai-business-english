A Busy Morning Routine
▼

📖 Reading Text

Miriam had spent years trying to make her mornings work. She was not a natural early riser, but her job as a nurse required her to be at the hospital by seven fifteen, which meant leaving the flat no later than six forty. She had tried every combination of preparation: laying out clothes the night before, making breakfast the night before, even going to bed earlier. In the end, what worked was a decision to accept that the mornings would be functional, not pleasant.

She rose at five fifty. She showered immediately, because she had found that delaying the shower led to delays everywhere else. She dressed quickly, ate a banana and yogurt standing at the kitchen counter, packed her bag which she had prepared the night before, and left. She spent no time looking at her phone before leaving the flat. She had discovered that the phone was the single most reliable cause of being late and had applied a strict rule: no phone until she was outside.

On the bus to the hospital, she allowed herself fifteen minutes of phone time. She checked messages, read one news headline, and put it away. By the time she arrived at the hospital at seven ten – five minutes early – she felt as ready as the morning could make her. Her colleagues sometimes asked how she was always so composed in the early shift. She said: 'I did all my thinking the night before.'

6. How long did she spend on her phone on the bus?

7. What time did she arrive at the hospital?

8. What did she say to colleagues who asked how she was always composed?

📖 Reading Text

Vera had been avoiding a check-up for two years. She was not ill and had no specific symptoms, but she had a family history of high blood pressure and her doctor had been asking her to come in for a routine check since her last visit. He gave her four recommendations: a consistent bedtime, no screens for forty-five minutes before sleeping, a cooler room, and no coffee after two in the afternoon. He said the blood pressure and cholesterol were both good and that she should come back in a year. She thanked him and went home. She moved her last coffee to one thirty that afternoon. Two weeks later she was sleeping better than she had in years. She thought: she should have come in sooner.
