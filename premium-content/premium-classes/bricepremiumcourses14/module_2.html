A Visit to the Bank
▼

📖 Reading Text

Last Tuesday, Hannah went to the bank to open a new savings account. She had been meaning to do this for several months but had never found the time. When she arrived, there were four people waiting ahead of her. She took a number from the machine by the door – number forty-seven – and sat down on one of the plastic chairs near the window. She spent the wait reading a leaflet about the bank's different account options.

After about twelve minutes, her number appeared on the screen. She went to counter number three, where a man called Mr. Osei helped her. Hannah explained that she wanted to open a savings account and asked about the interest rates. Mr. Osei showed her two options: a standard savings account with easy access but a lower rate, and a fixed-term account that paid more but required her to leave the money untouched for twelve months. She chose the standard account.

The whole process took about twenty-five minutes. She needed to provide her passport, a recent utility bill, and her current bank account details. Mr. Osei photocopied her documents and told her the new account would be active within three working days. Hannah left with a printed confirmation and a small booklet explaining the account terms. On the way home, she decided she would transfer fifty euros a month into the new account, starting that Friday.

The keys were hanging from the handle of one of them – he must have hooked them there automatically.

Tom was relieved but also slightly annoyed with himself. He was now twelve minutes late. He sent a quick message to his manager explaining the delay and ran to the bus stop. On the bus, he decided to buy a small hook for the inside of his front door so that hanging up his keys would become automatic. He ordered one on his phone during the journey. It arrived the following day. He has not lost his keys since.

📖 Reading Text

On a Thursday afternoon, Lily opened her phone and noticed she had not spoken to her friend Jonas for nearly three weeks. She did not want to send something too short, which might seem dismissive, but she also did not want a long message that could wait for a long reply. She read it back twice, decided it was right, and pressed send.

Jonas replied forty minutes later: 'This made me smile. Job is good, actually. Surviving. Let's actually talk – are you free Saturday?' Lily was. They arranged a phone call for Saturday afternoon that lasted an hour and forty minutes. Lily thought about how easy it had been to reconnect – one short message had been enough to restart a friendship that had drifted through no one's fault. She put a note in her calendar to message him again in two weeks, just in case.
